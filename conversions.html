Hostile Witnesses

Conversion Stories

Skeptics, scientists, a former Muslim, and a record-setting trial lawyer who set out to examine the evidence — and changed their minds.

In a courtroom, the most persuasive testimony often comes from a witness who has no reason to help your case — or every reason to hurt it. The same instinct applies to faith. When someone embraces Christianity who was raised to believe it and never seriously doubted it, that is a quiet witness. But when an atheist journalist sets out to disprove the resurrection, or a homicide detective applies the methods he uses to convict murderers, or a geneticist who once dismissed faith as wishful thinking finds himself unable to look away — that carries a different weight.

None of these stories is an argument on its own. A changed life proves only that a person was changed, not that what changed them is true; that case has to be made on its own merits, and the Evidence Library is where we try to make it carefully. But these accounts do something an abstract argument cannot. They show real people — trained skeptics, accomplished professionals, people with a great deal to lose — doing the honest work of looking at the evidence and following it where it led.

What follows are seven such people. They came from very different places: a feminist English professor, a devout Muslim, an Oxford don, the most successful defense attorney in recorded history. We tell their stories with respect for who they were before — never as trophies, and never with a sneer at the convictions they once held with sincerity. Each, in their own way, was a hostile witness who found the evidence harder to dismiss than they expected.

LS
Lee Strobel
1952 – present · American
Atheist journalist Resurrection

Strobel was an avowed atheist and the Yale-trained legal-affairs editor of the Chicago Tribune when, in 1979, his wife Leslie became a Christian. Alarmed and irritated, he set out to use the investigative and legal skills of his profession to demolish the foundation of her new faith — the resurrection of Jesus — and win her back. The investigation ran for the better part of two years. He interviewed scholars, weighed the manuscript and medical evidence, and tested the alternative theories. To his own surprise, he concluded the case for the resurrection was stronger than the case against it. He placed his trust in Christ on November 8, 1981. He later distilled that inquiry into The Case for Christ (1998), structured as a series of cross-examinations of leading scholars, which became one of the best-selling apologetics books of its era.1

“I’ll admit it: I was ambushed by the amount and quality of the evidence that Jesus is the unique Son of God.”2
Key book
  • The Case for Christ (1998)
Came to faith 1981 Investigative journalist
NQ
Nabeel Qureshi
1983 – 2017 · American
Former Muslim Gospels & History

Qureshi grew up in a loving, devout Ahmadi Muslim family and was a committed defender of Islam — able to argue his faith ably and proud of his heritage. As a pre-med student at Old Dominion University he befriended a Christian classmate, David Wood, and the two began a years-long, genuinely respectful dialogue over the historical claims of both faiths: the reliability of the Gospels, the evidence surrounding Jesus’ death and resurrection, and the textual history of the Qur’an. Qureshi did not want to leave Islam; by his own account he loved his community and resisted the conclusion at great personal cost to his family ties. After extended study, prayer, and what he described as dreams and visions he could not dismiss, he came to faith in Christ. He told the story in Seeking Allah, Finding Jesus (2014), a New York Times bestseller. He died of stomach cancer in 2017, at 34.3

Following the gospel, Qureshi knew, would cost him nearly everything — his family and the Muslim community that had raised him.4
Key book
  • Seeking Allah, Finding Jesus (2014)
Came to faith 2005 Ahmadi background
LL
Sir Lionel Luckhoo
1914 – 1997 · Guyanese-British
Record-setting lawyer Resurrection

Luckhoo was, by the measure of the Guinness Book of Records, the most successful advocate in history — credited with 245 consecutive murder-case acquittals. A Guyanese barrister, diplomat, and statesman, he was knighted twice by Queen Elizabeth II and once served simultaneously as ambassador for two sovereign nations. He spent his career weighing evidence and persuading juries, and he applied that same forensic discipline when he turned, later in life, to the historical claim at the center of Christianity. As an evangelical Christian he examined the case for the resurrection with a trial lawyer’s skepticism — and concluded that it met the highest evidentiary bar he knew. His verdict, often quoted, carries the weight of a man who had cross-examined a great deal of testimony in his time.5

“I say unequivocally [that] the evidence for the resurrection of Jesus Christ is so overwhelming that it compels acceptance by proof which leaves absolutely no room for doubt.”6
Key writing
  • What Is Your Verdict? (booklet on the resurrection)
Came to faith later in life 245 acquittals
CL
C.S. Lewis
1898 – 1963 · British
Atheist scholar Literature

Lewis was a brilliant young atheist and a literary scholar at Oxford (later Cambridge) when his unbelief began to give way. Grief, reading, and above all long late-night conversations with his Christian colleagues — chief among them J.R.R. Tolkien and Hugo Dyson — wore down his objections. He moved first to belief in God in 1929, reluctantly, and then to Christianity in 1931 after a now-famous nighttime walk and talk with Tolkien and Dyson about myth, fact, and the Gospels. His trained literary instinct mattered here: the Gospels, he judged, did not read like the myths he had spent his life studying, but like reportage. He went on to become the most widely read Christian apologist of the twentieth century, author of Mere Christianity and the spiritual autobiography Surprised by Joy.7

“In the Trinity Term of 1929 I gave in, and admitted that God was God... perhaps, that night, the most dejected and reluctant convert in all England.” — Surprised by Joy8
Key books
  • Surprised by Joy (1955)
  • Mere Christianity (1952)
Came to faith 1931 Oxford · Cambridge
FC
Francis Collins
1950 – present · American
Geneticist Science & Faith

Collins is one of the most accomplished scientists of his generation: a physician-geneticist who led the international Human Genome Project to its completion and later directed the U.S. National Institutes of Health. As a young man he was a confident atheist who regarded faith as sentimentality. Two things unsettled that confidence during his medical training in his twenties. The first was watching dying patients face death not with despair but with a steady peace — and one of them simply asking him what he believed, a question he found he could not answer honestly. The second was a Methodist pastor who handed him C.S. Lewis’s Mere Christianity. Lewis’s argument from the moral law dismantled the case he thought he had. Around age 27 he became a Christian, and he later set out his view that rigorous science and Christian faith belong together in The Language of God (2006).9

Reading Lewis, Collins recalls, he realized his own atheism had itself been a choice — one he had never honestly examined.10
Key book
  • The Language of God (2006)
Came to faith c. age 27 Human Genome Project
JW
J. Warner Wallace
1961 – present · American
Cold-case detective Gospel reliability

Wallace spent his career as a Los Angeles-area homicide detective, specializing in cold cases — murders solved years or decades later, often built entirely on the careful evaluation of old eyewitness statements with no living witnesses to cross-examine. He was a committed atheist into his thirties who considered Christianity a fable. At around age 35, almost on a dare to himself, he began reading the Gospels not as scripture but as witness statements, applying the same forensic-statement-analysis techniques he used to test the reliability of testimony in court: internal corroboration, incidental detail, the presence or absence of late legendary embellishment. To his surprise, the Gospel accounts bore the marks he associated with true eyewitness reports. He became a Christian in 1996 and later wrote Cold-Case Christianity (2013).11

Wallace concluded the Gospels bear what he calls “the earmarks of trustworthy testimony” — the same markers he weighed in any cold-case witness.12
Key book
  • Cold-Case Christianity (2013)
Came to faith 1996 Homicide detective
RB
Rosaria Butterfield
1962 – present · American
English professor Friendship & faith

Butterfield was a tenured professor of English and women’s studies at Syracuse University, specializing in queer theory and critical theory. She was in a committed lesbian relationship, an activist, and by her own description deeply skeptical of — even hostile toward — Christianity, which she associated with cruelty toward people like her. In 1997 she wrote a newspaper piece criticizing the religious right; among the responses was a warm, non-defensive letter from a local Reformed Presbyterian pastor, Ken Smith, who invited her to dinner. What followed was not a debate but a years-long friendship: she ate at Ken and Floy Smith’s table, read the Bible repeatedly out of professional curiosity, and slowly found her objections giving way. She converted in 1999 at real cost to her career and relationships, and recounted it in The Secret Thoughts of an Unlikely Convert (2012). She has asked that her story be told without caricature of the life she left.13

She has described her conversion as feeling, at the time, like “a train wreck” — the upheaval of an entire life, not a tidy decision.14
Key book
  • The Secret Thoughts of an Unlikely Convert (2012)
Came to faith 1999 Syracuse University
