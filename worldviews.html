A scholarly examination of Islam

Islam is the world's second largest religion and the most theologically engaged alternative to Christianity. These 12 arguments address Islam's truth claims — the Quran's preservation, the reliability of the Hadith, what the Quran itself says about Jesus, and the prophecy claims — using the same historical standards applied to all ancient documents. The Islamic Dilemma

01
The Islamic Dilemma — The Quran, the Bible, and a Two-Horned Trap
The Quran affirms the Torah and Gospel as revelation from God. Whether a Muslim says the Bible is reliable or corrupted, the Quran is undone either way.
🌿 Beginner
Free
The argument in one sentence

Popularised by Christian apologist David Wood, the Islamic Dilemma observes that the Quran repeatedly affirms the inspiration, preservation and authority of the Torah and the Gospel — which forces the Muslim onto one of two horns, both of which count against Islam.

Step 1 — What the Quran says about the earlier scriptures
  1. The Quran calls itself musaddiq — "confirming" the scriptures that came before it (Surah 2:97, 3:3, 5:48, 35:31, and many more).
  2. Surah 5:46-47 — the Gospel contains "guidance and light," and the People of the Gospel are commanded to "judge by what Allah has revealed therein."
  3. Surah 5:43-44 — the Torah, "in which is the judgement of Allah," is treated as a present, usable authority for the Jews of Muhammad's day.
  4. Surah 5:68 — Jews and Christians "have nothing to stand on" unless they uphold the Torah and the Gospel.
  5. Surah 10:94 — if Muhammad himself is in doubt, he is told to "ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you."
  6. Surah 6:115, 18:27, 10:64 — "None can change His words." If the Torah and Gospel are God's words, then on the Quran's own logic they cannot have been textually corrupted.
Step 2 — The two horns
  1. Horn 1 — the Bible is corrupted. Then the Quran affirmed, confirmed and commanded obedience to scriptures that (on this view) were already false — and sent its own prophet to consult them. A God who points people to a corrupted book as guidance is either mistaken or deceptive, so the Quran's author is not God.
  2. Horn 2 — the Bible is reliable. Then it stands as a true witness — and it contradicts the Quran on the deity of Christ, the crucifixion and the resurrection. If the Bible is trustworthy where it contradicts the Quran, the Quran is false.
  3. No third option. The Bible is either reliable or it is not. Reliable → it refutes the Quran. Corrupted → the Quran endorsed a corrupted book. Either road ends with the Quran failing.

This is the argument David Wood (Acts 17 Apologetics) calls "the Islamic Dilemma," and it has been debated extensively in Christian-Muslim dialogue, including against Muslim interlocutors such as Daniel Haqiqatjou. Its force comes entirely from the Quran's own posture toward the earlier scriptures: it never calls itself their abrogator, only their confirmer (musaddiq). The strongest form of the argument is not "the Bible says X and the Quran says Y," but the internal tension between the Quran affirming the Torah and Gospel as God's preserved word while later Islam must treat them as corrupt to avoid the contradictions. The card below states the argument, then gives the strongest Muslim and academic responses and assesses them honestly — because anyone using this in real conversation will meet those responses.

The strongest responses — stated fairly, then weighed
Q"The Quran affirms the original Torah and Gospel, not today's Bible." This is the most common reply: Allah confirmed the true revelation given to Moses and Jesus, but what Jews and Christians hold now has been altered. Assessment: the difficulty is that the Quran addresses scriptures physically present in the 7th century — it tells Christians to "judge by" the Gospel they had and tells Muhammad to consult readers of "the Scripture before you." Manuscripts predating Islam by centuries (the Dead Sea Scrolls; the 4th-century codices Vaticanus and Sinaiticus) show that the text in 7th-century hands is essentially the text we have. So the "lost original" must be a book for which there is no manuscript, quotation, or historical trace — which is a heavy claim to carry.
Q"The Quran supersedes (abrogates) the earlier scriptures rather than depending on them." This is the more sophisticated, scholarly reply (and the thrust of academic responses to Wood): the Quran is the final, governing revelation; it confirms what was authentic in the Torah and Gospel while standing as the muhaymin (guardian, Surah 5:48) over them. Assessment: this is a serious answer and the best available. But the historian Martin Whittingham (A History of Muslim Views of the Bible, 2021) notes that early Muslim writers did not generally treat the previous scriptures as abrogated; the "supersession" reading hardened later. And even as guardian, the Quran still commands present-day obedience to the Gospel (5:47) — supersession does not remove the tension so much as relocate it.
Q"Tahrif means corruption of meaning, not text." Many Muslims and a number of scholars hold that the Quran accuses Jews and Christians of misinterpreting and concealing scripture (Surah 3:78, 2:42), not of rewriting it. Assessment: this actually concedes the heart of the argument. If only the meaning was twisted while the text stayed intact, then Christians still possess the genuine text — which lands the Muslim back on Horn 2, since that genuine text contradicts the Quran.

How to explain this simply

Ask your Muslim friend to read Surah 10:94 aloud: if Muhammad is in doubt, he is to ask those who have been reading the Scripture before him. Then ask gently: if that Scripture was already corrupted, why would Allah send his own prophet to it for reassurance? You are not attacking — you are asking an honest question that the verse itself raises.

In conversation: "Can you show me one verse where the Quran says the text of the Bible was changed? Not misread, not misinterpreted — actually rewritten. I have looked and I cannot find one. The Quran seems to call the Torah and Gospel God's word. Can you help me understand that?"

Does the Quran actually say the Bible is corrupt? What the research shows

This is the pivot of the whole argument, so it is worth stating carefully and accurately. The popular belief that "the Bible has been changed" (tahrif al-nass — corruption of the text) is not stated anywhere in the Quran. This is not merely an apologist's claim; it is the finding of academic specialists. Martin Whittingham's A History of Muslim Views of the Bible (De Gruyter, 2021) — the standard scholarly survey — concludes that textual-corruption claims were marginal in classical Islamic texts, and that the dominant early charge was corruption of interpretation (tahrif al-ma'na), not of the text. Whittingham locates the watershed at Ibn Hazm of Cordoba (d. 1064), who systematised and popularised the textual-corruption doctrine some four centuries after Muhammad. Specialists such as Ilka Lindstedt (Helsinki) state plainly that the Quran nowhere asserts the corruption of the Jewish or Christian scriptures, only their misinterpretation.

What the Quran actually charges is narrower than later polemic assumes: concealing parts of scripture (Surah 2:42, 2:159, 3:71), distorting it with the tongue in recitation (Surah 3:78, 4:46), and misinterpreting its meaning. Crucially, every one of these presupposes an intact text — you cannot conceal or twist the reading of a book that no longer exists in its true form. So even the Quran's criticisms assume Jews and Christians still held the genuine scripture.

A working list of confirming verses to know: 2:41, 2:89, 2:91, 2:97, 2:101, 2:136, 3:3, 3:81, 3:84, 4:47, 4:136, 5:46, 5:47, 5:48, 5:68, 6:115, 10:37, 10:94, 35:31, 46:12. The repeated Quranic word is musaddiq / tasdiq — "confirming." Academic work has even proposed tasdiq (confirmation), rather than naskh (abrogation), as the Quran's own self-understanding in relation to the Bible (see the 2023 study "The Qur'an and the Bible: Abrogation or Confirmation?" in Religions).

The hadith reinforce it — Muhammad treated the Torah as authentic

The point is not only Quranic. In a narration found in Sahih al-Bukhari (6819; also 4556) and Sahih Muslim (1699), Jews bring an adulterous couple for judgement; Muhammad calls for the Torah and judges by its command of stoning, and when a reader tries to cover the relevant verse with his hand, it is uncovered and read aloud. Muhammad treats the physical Torah present before him as a reliable, authoritative source. In Sunan Abi Dawud (4449), the Torah is brought, Muhammad places it on a cushion and says, "I believe in you and in Him who revealed you." This is not how one treats a book believed to be textually corrupt.

Classical authorities reflect the same. Major early figures — including al-Tabari and al-Razi — leaned toward corruption of meaning rather than text, and al-Bukhari himself is cited for the view that no one can remove the actual words of God's books, only misinterpret them. The wholesale "the text was changed" position is, historically, the later innovation.

Evidence for Horn 1 — the Bible was not corrupted after Muhammad

Horn 1 says: if the Bible is corrupt, Allah affirmed a corrupt book. The Muslim's usual escape is "it was corrupted, just not the part Allah affirmed" — so the strength of this horn rests on showing the biblical text was demonstrably stable around and before Muhammad's time (7th century). The manuscript evidence is decisive here. For the Old Testament, the Dead Sea Scrolls (discovered at Qumran, dated roughly 250 BC to AD 70) pushed our manuscript record back over a thousand years and showed the Hebrew text — including a virtually complete scroll of Isaiah — is essentially the text Jews held later and hold today. For the New Testament, the great codices Vaticanus and Sinaiticus (both 4th century, c. AD 325–360) preserve the same four Gospels Christians read in the 7th century and now, with earlier fragments such as P52 (a fragment of John) dated to around AD 125.

The logical force is this: the Bible Muhammad's contemporaries possessed in the 7th century is, by the manuscript evidence, the Bible we possess. There was no window in which it could have been secretly rewritten after Allah affirmed it, because copies were already scattered in their thousands across three continents — in Hebrew, Greek, Latin, Syriac, Coptic and more, in the hands of rival communities who would never have coordinated an identical forgery. As classical Muslim commentator al-Razi himself reasoned, the copies of the Torah were too numerous and too widely distributed for any conspiracy to alter them all without detection. So Horn 1 cannot be escaped by saying "it was corrupted later" — the text did not change.

Evidence for Horn 2 — the Bible contradicts the Quran on the essentials

Horn 2 says: if the Bible is reliable, it refutes the Quran — because the two contradict on the central claims, and both cannot be true (the law of non-contradiction). The contradictions are not minor; they are precisely the doctrines the Gospel hangs on:

The deity of Christ. The Bible affirms it directly — "In the beginning was the Word… and the Word was God" (John 1:1); Thomas calls the risen Jesus "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28); see also Philippians 2:6-11 and Hebrews 1:8. The Quran flatly denies it: Jesus was "no more than a messenger" (Surah 4:171), and it pronounces a curse on those who call Christ the Son of God (Surah 9:30; cf. 5:72-73, 112:1-4).

The crucifixion. The Bible makes Jesus' death by crucifixion the hinge of everything — "Christ died for our sins… was buried… was raised on the third day" (1 Corinthians 15:3-4), attested in all four Gospels and even by hostile sources (Tacitus, Josephus). The Quran denies it: "they neither killed him nor crucified him — it was only made to appear so" (Surah 4:157-158).

The resurrection. The Bible presents the bodily resurrection as the foundation of the faith, such that "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Corinthians 15:17). The Quran, by denying the crucifixion, has no death to be raised from and no resurrection account — it teaches instead that Jesus was taken up alive (Surah 4:158).

Here the trap closes. The Quran (Surah 3:3, 5:46) calls the Gospel God's revelation, and (Surah 6:115, 18:27) says God's words cannot be changed — yet the Gospel teaches exactly what the Quran denies. By the law of non-contradiction, a later book that both affirms an earlier revelation as God's unchangeable word and contradicts it cannot itself be from that same God. This is the formulation used by Christian apologists and even acknowledged in outline by Muslim respondents: the affirmation and the contradiction cannot both stand.

The strongest counter-argument, taken seriously

Honesty requires stating the best Muslim response at full strength. The most serious reply is the supersession / abrogation answer, developed by thoughtful Muslims and reflected in academic pushback on Wood (e.g. papers arguing the Quran "supersedes" the earlier scriptures while confirming what was authentic in them). On this view, the Quran is the final criterion (5:48 calls it muhaymin, guardian, over previous scripture); it endorses the true kernel of the Torah and Gospel while correcting accretions; and so affirming the earlier revelation in principle does not commit the Muslim to the present canonical Bible in every detail. A related move, associated with figures like Yasir Qadhi, revives the older "corruption of meaning" reading as a respectable, even preferable, position.

This is a genuine answer and should not be waved away. But two pressures remain. First, historically: Whittingham's research indicates that the abrogation-of-previous-scripture framework is itself a later development, not the Quran's own categorisation, which is confirmation (tasdiq). Second, logically: even as guardian, the Quran still issues present-tense commands to obey the Gospel (5:47) and still sends Muhammad to consult existing scripture (10:94). Supersession explains why a Muslim need not become a Jew or Christian; it does not dissolve the question of why Allah would affirm, confirm, and command obedience to books he knew to be corrupted. So the dilemma is narrowed and pressured by the best response — but not cleanly escaped.

Where the argument is weakest — so you don't overstate it

Use this argument with precision, not triumphalism. Two honest limits. (1) The claim "the Quran nowhere hints at corruption" is too strong; a few verses (e.g. 2:75, 2:79, 3:78) were read by some later exegetes as gesturing at textual tampering, even if the mainstream early reading was misinterpretation. State it as "the Quran does not teach textual corruption and overwhelmingly affirms the scriptures," not "there is not a single verse anyone could appeal to." (2) The argument shows an internal tension within the Islamic position; it does not, by itself, prove Christianity true — that case rests on the historical evidence for the resurrection. The Islamic Dilemma clears the ground; it is not the whole building.

Using this argument graciously (1 Peter 3:15)

The goal is never to corner someone but to open an honest question. Lead with curiosity: ask your friend to read the verses with you rather than announcing a "gotcha." Expect the supersession and "corruption of meaning" replies, and meet them with respect — they are reasonable moves, and treating them as such earns you a hearing. Remember that your Muslim friend loves their scripture and their prophet; you are engaging their own text honestly, not mocking it. The aim is to plant a question the Holy Spirit can water, not to win an exchange.

Go deeper

For the apologetic case: David Wood's "The Quran, the Bible, and the Islamic Dilemma" (Acts 17 Apologetics) is the standard short presentation, and The Islamic Dilemma by Christopher W. Frost (foreword by Dr David Wood) is the accessible book-length treatment. For the scholarly history that underlies the key claim — that textual-corruption doctrine is a later development — the authoritative work is Martin Whittingham, A History of Muslim Views of the Bible: The First Four Centuries (De Gruyter, 2021). For the Muslim side, see the responses arguing supersession/abrogation and the revived "corruption of meaning" position. Engaging the best of both sides is what makes your use of the argument credible.

Jesus in the Quran — What Isa Actually Says About Himself
The Quranic Jesus makes claims that undermine the Islamic account of who he is
🌿 Beginner
Free
What the Quran says about Jesus
  1. Surah 3:45 — Jesus is called the Messiah (al-Masih), the Word of God (Kalimatullah), and highly honoured in this world and the next.
  2. Surah 4:171 — Jesus is called 'a Word from Him and a Spirit from Him' — unique titles given to no other prophet in the Quran.
  3. Surah 3:46-47 — Jesus was born of a virgin with no human father. He spoke from the cradle as an infant.
  4. Surah 5:110 — Jesus raised the dead, healed lepers and the blind, and created birds from clay — unique miracles not attributed to Muhammad.
  5. Surah 19:33 — Jesus himself says 'Peace be upon me the day I was born, the day I die, and the day I am raised alive' — affirming his resurrection.
  6. Surah 4:157-158 — Muslims deny the crucifixion, claiming it only appeared that Jesus was killed.

The Quran's portrayal of Jesus (Isa) is remarkable and often unknown to both Muslims and Christians. The Quran affirms the virgin birth, that Jesus performed miracles including raising the dead, that he is the Messiah, the Word of God, and a Spirit from God. It denies that he died on the cross. Christian apologists argue that this Quranic portrait of Jesus raises more questions than it answers — and that the New Testament provides the most coherent account of who this extraordinary figure was.

Common objections
QDoesn't the Quran say Jesus was just a prophet? — The Quran calls Jesus a prophet — but also much more than a prophet. No other prophet in the Quran is called the Word of God, the Spirit of God, and the Messiah. The Quran gives Jesus attributes it does not give Muhammad. This unique status requires explanation. The New Testament provides the only coherent account of why Jesus is so uniquely elevated.
QThe Quran says Jesus himself denied being God (Surah 5:116)? — Surah 5:116 asks whether Jesus told people to worship him and his mother as gods — a reference to folk Catholic practice, not biblical Christianity. Biblical Christianity has never taught the worship of Mary as a goddess. Jesus's denial of that specific claim in this verse is not a denial of his divine nature as the second person of the Trinity. The question assumes a version of Christianity the Quran is addressing, not orthodox Trinitarian faith.

How to explain this simply

Ask your Muslim friend: the Quran calls Jesus the Word of God and a Spirit from God — but it never calls Muhammad by those titles. Why does the Quran give Jesus attributes it does not give your own prophet? What does it mean that Jesus is the Word of God? That question alone can open a deep conversation.

In conversation: I have been reading the Quran and I am genuinely curious — it calls Jesus Kalimatullah, the Word of God. It gives him that title but not Muhammad. What do you think that title means? Because in the Bible, the Word of God becoming flesh is the whole explanation for who Jesus is.

Quranic titles of Jesus — and what they mean for apologetics

The title Kalimatullah — Word of God — is the most significant Quranic title given to Jesus and the most useful for Christian apologetics. It appears in Surah 3:45: 'O Mary, indeed Allah gives you good tidings of a word from Him, whose name will be the Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary.' And in Surah 4:171: 'The Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, was but a messenger of Allah and His word which He directed to Mary and a soul from Him.' The Word of God is not merely a word spoken by God — it is a Word that came from God and was directed to Mary, and which became the person of Jesus. This language is extraordinarily close to John 1:14: 'The Word became flesh and dwelt among us.'

The title Ruh min Allah — Spirit from God — appears in Surah 4:171 alongside Kalimatullah. Jesus is called both the Word from God and a Spirit from God in the same verse. Muslim commentators have struggled to explain what these titles mean without inadvertently affirming something close to the Christian position. If Jesus is the Word of God and the Spirit of God in a unique sense not applied to any other prophet — including Muhammad — then the question of his unique ontological status cannot be dismissed by saying 'he was only a prophet.'

The title al-Masih — the Messiah — is used eleven times for Jesus in the Quran. Yet Islam has no theology of what Messiah means. In the Jewish and Christian tradition, the Messiah is the anointed one who fulfils the promises of the Old Testament — who suffers, dies, and rises to establish God's kingdom. The Quran uses the title but strips it of its content by denying the crucifixion. This is apologetically significant: the Quran keeps the title but removes the event that gives the title its meaning. Christian apologists can ask: if Jesus is the Messiah, what is he the Messiah of? What does his messiahship accomplish if he did not die for sins and rise again?

Surah 19:33 — Jesus predicts his own death and resurrection

Surah 19:33 is one of the most remarkable verses in the Quran for Christian apologetics. In it, Jesus himself says: 'And peace be upon me the day I was born, the day I will die, and the day I am raised alive.' This is the Quranic Jesus speaking in the first person — affirming that he will die and be raised alive. The parallel with the Christian account of the resurrection is impossible to miss.

Muslim commentators respond in one of two ways. Some say the verse refers to a future death and resurrection at the end of times — Jesus will return, live, die a natural death, and then be raised in the general resurrection. Others interpret 'the day I die' as referring to a spiritual death. Neither interpretation is satisfying. The first concedes that Jesus will die and resurrect — which raises the question of why the New Testament account of exactly this is rejected. The second imports a meaning into the Arabic that is not there.

Surah 19:33 also contains a perfect parallel with Surah 19:15, which uses almost identical language about John the Baptist: 'And peace be upon him the day he was born and the day he dies and the day he is raised alive.' John the Baptist died — this is affirmed in Islamic tradition. If the same grammatical structure is used for John (who died) and Jesus (who the Quran says was not killed), the parallel is awkward for the Islamic interpretation. Christian apologists can note this parallel and ask what explains the difference in how the same language is applied.

The crucifixion denial — Surah 4:157-158 examined

Surah 4:157-158 is the Quranic basis for the Islamic denial of the crucifixion: 'And their saying, Indeed, we have killed the Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, the messenger of Allah. And they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but it appeared to them so. And indeed, those who differed over it are in doubt about it. They have no knowledge of it except the following of assumption. And they did not kill him, for certain. Rather, Allah raised him to Himself.' This verse is the foundation of the Islamic position that someone else was substituted for Jesus on the cross.

The historical problem with this position is severe. The crucifixion of Jesus under Pontius Pilate is one of the most historically certain events of antiquity. It is attested not only in all four Gospels and Paul's letters (written within 25 years of the event) but also in Tacitus (Annals 15.44), Josephus (Antiquities 20.9.1), and the Babylonian Talmud. All of these sources were written independently and with no motive to support Christianity. The crucifixion is also confirmed by the standard Roman practice of crucifying political criminals — Jesus had been accused of claiming to be King of the Jews, a capital offence under Roman law.

The substitution theory — that someone else died in Jesus's place — also faces the problem of identifying the substitute. The Quran does not name him. Various Islamic traditions have proposed Judas, Simon of Cyrene, or a random person created to look like Jesus. None of these alternatives has any historical evidence. The disciples who were present at the crucifixion — and who were in the best possible position to know whether Jesus was crucified — unanimously affirm that it was Jesus who died. They then claimed he rose from the dead. The Islamic account requires us to believe that the people closest to Jesus were completely deceived about what happened, while the Quran — written 600 years later — has the correct account.

Does the Quran Guarantee Its Own Preservation?
The preservation argument cuts both ways — and the Quran's textual history is more complex than Muslims are often taught
📚 Intermediate
Free
Key facts
  1. Surah 15:9 says Allah will preserve 'the Reminder' — but al-dhikr is used for divine revelation generally in the Quran.
  2. The Quran endorses the Bible as divine revelation (Surah 5:46-47, 3:3-4) — if al-dhikr is preserved, this includes the Bible.
  3. Uthman burned variant Quran manuscripts c. 650 AD — confirming multiple versions existed within 20 years of Muhammad's death.
  4. Ten accepted variant readings (Qira'at) of the Quran exist — some differing in meaning, not just pronunciation.
  5. The Sanaa manuscripts (discovered 1972) show pre-Uthmanic text that differs from the standard Quran.

Muslims frequently argue that Surah 15:9 — 'Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder, and indeed, it is We who are its Guardian' — guarantees the perfect preservation of the Quran. They use this to contrast the Quran with the allegedly corrupted Bible. But the verse uses the word al-dhikr (the Reminder), which in the Quran refers to divine revelation generally — not exclusively to the Quran. And the Quran's own textual history, including Uthman's burning of variant manuscripts, complicates the simple preservation claim.

Common objections
QDoesn't Surah 15:9 specifically mean the Quran? — Many classical Muslim commentators did interpret al-dhikr as the Quran specifically. But the word is used 38 times in the Quran for divine revelation generally. And even if the verse applies only to the Quran, it does not say the Bible was corrupted — it only claims Allah will preserve the Quran. The corruption claim requires additional evidence the Quran does not provide.
QAren't the Qira'at just different pronunciation traditions? — Some variants are pronunciation-based. Others affect meaning. Surah 2:222 differs between Hafs and Warsh readings in ways that affect Islamic law on ritual purity. Surah 5:6 has a variant that affects whether feet must be washed or wiped in ablution — a significant practical difference. These are not trivial.

How to explain this simply

When a Muslim says the Quran is perfectly preserved but the Bible is not, ask them to read Surah 15:9 with you. The verse says Allah preserves al-dhikr — the Reminder. Then ask: does the Quran anywhere say the Bible is not al-dhikr? Because the Quran calls the Torah and Gospel divine revelation too. If Allah preserves divine revelation, that includes the Bible.

In conversation: I am genuinely curious about Surah 15:9. The verse says Allah preserves the dhikr. But the Quran also calls the Torah and Gospel revelation from Allah. Does Surah 15:9 only apply to the Quran — and if so, where does it say that?

Surah 15:9 in its Quranic context

Surah 15:9 reads: 'Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder and indeed, it is We who are its Guardian.' The Arabic word translated Reminder is al-dhikr (the definite article al- plus dhikr). To understand what this verse means, we need to see how dhikr is used elsewhere in the Quran. In Surah 21:7: 'And We sent not before you except men to whom We revealed the message, so ask the people of the message if you do not know.' The people of dhikr here are Jews and Christians — the People of the Book. In Surah 16:44: 'We sent them with clear proofs and scriptures. And We have sent down to you the message that you may make clear to the people what was sent down to them and that they might give thought.' Here dhikr encompasses what was sent down to the people of the Torah and Gospel.

The apologetic use of Surah 15:9 as a proof of Quranic preservation therefore depends on a specific interpretation — that al-dhikr means only the Quran. This is a common Muslim assumption but it is not the only reading supported by the Quran's own usage of the word. If al-dhikr means divine revelation broadly, then Surah 15:9 is a claim that Allah preserves all divine revelation — which would include the Torah and Gospel and would work against the corruption claim.

Uthman's burning — the preservation problem within Islam

The canonical account in Sahih Bukhari (6:61:510) records that Caliph Uthman collected all variant Quran manuscripts and burned them, standardising a single text around 650 AD — approximately 18 years after Muhammad's death. The fact that this burning was necessary is itself historically significant: it confirms that multiple different versions of the Quran were in circulation within living memory of the prophet. If the Quran had been perfectly preserved in a single form from the beginning, there would have been no variants to burn.

The Sanaa manuscripts, discovered in a mosque in Yemen in 1972, provide physical evidence of the pre-Uthmanic textual diversity. German scholar Gerd-R. Puin examined photographs of these manuscripts and identified differences from the standard Uthmanic text — including different verse ordering and in some places different words. The Yemeni authorities restricted full access to these manuscripts for decades. What has been published suggests the Quran's textual history is more complex than the standard Muslim narrative of perfect singular preservation acknowledges.

Key facts about Qira'at
  1. Islamic scholarship officially recognises ten canonical Qira'at named after ten scholars.
  2. The most widely used are Hafs an Asim (most of the world) and Warsh an Nafi (North and West Africa).
  3. Surah 2:222 differs between readings in ways affecting Islamic law on ritual purity.
  4. Surah 5:6 differs between readings on whether feet must be washed or wiped in ablution.
  5. Surah 1:4 — in the Fatiha itself, recited in every prayer: Hafs reads maaliki yawmi-d-deen (“Owner/Master of the Day of Judgement”) while Warsh reads maliki (“King of the Day of Judgement”). Different words, different meanings — in Islam’s most-recited verse.
  6. Surah 37:12 — Hafs reads bal ‘ajibta (“rather, you marvel”, addressed to Muhammad) while Warsh reads bal ‘ajibtu (“rather, I marvel” — Allah speaking). The variant changes who is speaking — and raises the theological question of whether Allah can be surprised.
  7. Uthman burned variant manuscripts c. 650 AD — confirming multiple versions existed.

How to explain this simply

The deepest question in Christian-Muslim dialogue is not about manuscript variants or the age of traditions. It is this: is God's ultimate communication to humanity a book or a person? Islam says a book. Christianity says a person — Jesus, who is himself the Word of God made flesh. The Quran actually gives Jesus more extraordinary titles than any other prophet. The question is whether those titles point toward the truth that the New Testament reveals about him.

In conversation: I want to ask you the deepest question I know in this conversation. The Quran calls Jesus Kalimatullah — the Word of God. What do you think the title Word of God means when applied to Jesus?

The theology of divine speech — Allah's word vs God's Word

Islamic theology holds that the Quran is the uncreated, eternal speech of Allah (kalam Allah). This was the position that emerged victorious after the great Mutazilite controversy of the 9th century, which debated whether the Quran was created or uncreated. The Mutazilites — who argued the Quran was created — were eventually condemned. The mainstream Sunni position is that the Quran is literally the eternal word of God, existing alongside Allah from eternity.

Christian theology holds that the eternal Word of God is not a text but a person — the second person of the Trinity, who became flesh in Jesus of Nazareth (John 1:1-14). The New Testament, the creeds, and the church councils did not create this theology — they described and defended what the earliest Christians, including Jewish monotheists who knew Jesus personally, believed about him. Larry Hurtado's scholarship demonstrates that the worship of Jesus as divine was not a late development but an explosion that occurred within the first two decades of Christianity among people who understood perfectly well that worship belonged to God alone.

The Quranic title Kalimatullah — Word of God — for Jesus is therefore deeply significant from a Christian perspective. If Jesus is the Word of God in the sense that John 1 describes — the eternal divine Logos who became flesh — then the Quran is inadvertently affirming the Christian claim about Jesus's unique ontological status. Muslim commentators have always acknowledged the difficulty of explaining what Kalimatullah means without implying something close to the Christian position.

The resurrection as the decisive evidence

Ultimately, the Christian-Muslim debate about the nature of revelation comes down to one historical question: did Jesus rise from the dead? If the resurrection happened — if the tomb was genuinely empty and Jesus genuinely appeared to hundreds of people over forty days — then Jesus's own claims about himself are validated and the Christian account of who he is demands the most serious consideration. The resurrection is not just one Christian doctrine among many; it is the event that Paul describes as the hinge of the entire faith (1 Corinthians 15:14-17).

The Islamic account requires that the crucifixion did not happen (Surah 4:157-158) — that Allah caused it to appear that Jesus was crucified when he was not. This means that the entire chain of witnesses to the crucifixion — the disciples who fled, the women who stayed, Joseph of Arimathea who provided the tomb, Pilate who issued the death warrant, the soldiers who carried out the execution, Tacitus and Josephus who recorded it as historical fact — were all completely deceived. The Islamic account of the crucifixion requires a divine act of mass deception unprecedented in any religious tradition.

The minimal facts approach — using only evidence accepted by virtually all critical scholars regardless of theological position — produces a powerful case for the resurrection that the Islamic account cannot adequately address. Jesus died by crucifixion (affirmed even by hostile sources). The tomb was empty (the Jewish authorities never disputed this, only explaining it away). The disciples genuinely believed they saw the risen Jesus (no serious historian disputes this). Paul converted after an appearance. James — the sceptical brother of Jesus — converted after an appearance. The resurrection hypothesis accounts for all five facts. The Islamic hypothesis requires explaining all five without it.

The Eternal Word Dilemma — Is Allah's Word Created or Uncreated?
The Quran calls Jesus the Word of God — and Islam's own greatest theological crisis shows why that title cannot be tamed.
🎓 Advanced
Free
The dilemma stated
  1. Surah 4:171 calls Jesus "His Word, which He bestowed on Mary, and a Spirit from Him" — kalimatullah, the Word of God.
  2. Islam itself was torn apart over this exact question: is Allah's Word (the Quran) created or uncreated? The Mu'tazilites said created; mainstream Sunni orthodoxy, after the Mihna inquisition (833–848 AD) and Ahmad ibn Hanbal's resistance, settled that Allah's Word is eternal and uncreated.
  3. Horn one: if Allah's Word is eternal and uncreated, then something exists eternally that is fully divine yet in some sense distinct from Allah's essence — which is precisely the conceptual structure of the Logos that Muslims reject in John 1:1.
  4. Horn two: if Allah's Word is created, then Allah existed "before" His Word — a God who was once without speech, and the Quran becomes a creature, the very position Sunni Islam condemned as heresy.
  5. Now apply Surah 4:171: Jesus IS the Word. If the Word is eternal, the Quran has called Jesus eternal. If the Word is created, orthodox Islam's own doctrine of the uncreated Word collapses.

Christians did not invent this problem and import it into Islam — Islam discovered it on its own. The question of whether God's Word is created or eternal produced the deepest theological crisis in Islamic history, complete with an inquisition, imprisonments, and a reversal of imperial policy. Sunni orthodoxy concluded that God's Word must be eternal and uncreated, because a God who acquired speech would have changed. But that conclusion concedes the central category of John 1:1 — an eternal, divine Word that is God's and yet distinguishable — sixteen centuries after John wrote it. And the Quran itself hands the title to Jesus.

Common objections
Q"Word" just means the command "Be!" by which Jesus was created — Two problems. First, Surah 4:171 does not say Jesus was created by a word; it calls him "His Word" — the title belongs to Jesus himself. Every human is created by Allah's command, yet no one else in the Quran bears this title. Second, if the command "Be!" is itself created speech, the created-Quran problem returns in full force; if it is uncreated, the dilemma stands.
QAllah's attributes are eternal without being distinct persons — This is the Ash'ari formulation: attributes "not Him, nor other than Him." But notice what that concedes: something eternal, fully divine, neither simply identical to the essence nor separable from it. That is no longer an objection to Trinitarian grammar — it is a parallel construction of it. The Christian simply adds what the Quran itself suggests in 4:171: the Word is personal, and entered history.

Tawhid and the Problem of Eternal Love
If Allah is an absolute solitary unity, who did he love before creation? The Trinity is the only way "God is love" can be eternally true.
🎓 Advanced
Free
The argument stated
  1. Love, by its nature, requires a beloved — an object. Love with no one to love is not love.
  2. On Islamic tawhid (Surah 112), Allah is an absolute, solitary unity — one person, alone from eternity, before any creation existed.
  3. Therefore, before creation, Allah had no one to love. Either his love began when he created — meaning love is not essential to his nature but a new, acquired state (change in the changeless) — or from eternity he loved only himself.
  4. The Quran's own love-language is consistently conditional: "Allah loves not the transgressors" (2:190), "loves not the unbelievers" (3:32), "loves not the proud" (4:36). Love is a response to human performance, not the ground of the divine nature.
  5. Christianity claims something structurally different: "God IS love" (1 John 4:8) — and only the Trinity makes that possible. The Father loved the Son "before the foundation of the world" (John 17:24). Love is not something God began to do; it is what God eternally is, within himself.
  6. The consequence is enormous: the Triune God creates from the overflow of love he already enjoys; a solitary god creates to acquire what he lacked. And the Christian God "demonstrates his own love for us in this: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" (Romans 5:8) — love prior to performance, the exact reverse of the Quranic pattern.

This is not a gotcha — it is a serious metaphysical question that Muslim theologians have felt the weight of. A being who is one solitary person cannot have love as his eternal essence, because eternal love requires an eternal beloved. He can become loving when he makes something to love, but then love is an accident of his nature, dependent on creatures. Only if there is eternal relationship within God — Father, Son, and Spirit — can love be what God is rather than merely something God does. The doctrine Muslims find most objectionable about Christianity turns out to be the only thing that secures the claim both faiths want to make: that God is supremely loving.

Common objections
QBut Allah is al-Wadud — "the Loving" — The name exists, and Muslims rightly cherish it. The question is not whether the Quran uses love-language but whether love can be essential to a being who was eternally alone. A name describes; it does not solve the metaphysics. Before creation, toward whom was al-Wadud loving? The name either describes a capacity that waited for creation to be exercised — acquired love — or it is grounded in something Islam denies: eternal relationship within God.
QGod is beyond such human categories — Then the Quran should not apply the category — but it does, repeatedly, including in the negative ("Allah loves not..."). You cannot use love-language when it commends Allah and retreat to mystery when its logic is examined. And note which conception is actually greater: a God whose love depends on creatures, or a God whose love is so complete in himself that creation was its overflow rather than its occasion.
