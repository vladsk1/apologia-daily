The Islamic Dilemma

01
The Islamic Dilemma — The Quran's Confirmation of the Bible
The Quran calls the Bible the Word of God. If the Bible is corrupted, the Quran's testimony is unreliable.
🌿 Beginner
Free
The dilemma in brief
  1. The Quran repeatedly affirms the Torah and Gospel as the Word of God (Surah 5:46-47, 3:3-4, 10:94).
  2. The Quran tells Muhammad to consult the existing Bible when he is in doubt (Surah 10:94).
  3. Muslims claim today's Bible is corrupted (tahrif) and unreliable.
  4. But the Quran never says the Bible was corrupted — it affirms it as authoritative scripture.
  5. This creates an irresolvable contradiction within the Islamic worldview.

The Quran itself — in dozens of verses — calls the Torah and the Gospel the Word of God, given by Allah to Moses and Jesus. It instructs Muhammad to consult the Bible when in doubt. It calls Jews and Christians 'People of the Book' — implying they possess genuine divine scripture. But modern Muslims say today's Bible is corrupted. This creates an irresolvable dilemma: if the Bible is corrupted, why does the Quran treat it as authoritative?

Developed by Christian apologist David Wood, the Islamic Dilemma uses the Quran's own testimony to show that the Muslim claim about biblical corruption contradicts the Quran itself. If the Bible was corrupted before Muhammad, the Quran is endorsing corrupt scripture. If it was corrupted after Muhammad, then Christians and Jews must have conspired globally to change their scriptures simultaneously — an implausible historical claim.

Questions Muslims ask
QDoesn't tahrif mean the meaning was corrupted, not the text? — This is the most common Muslim response. But the Quran says 'judge by the Torah' (5:43-44) and 'ask those who have been reading Scripture before you' (10:94). If only the meaning was corrupted, why would Allah direct Muhammad to consult it? A meaningfully corrupted text would give wrong guidance. The response fails to account for the Quran's repeated endorsement of the Bible as a guide.
QWasn't the original Bible lost and replaced? — There is no historical evidence for a wholesale replacement of the Bible. We have thousands of manuscripts — Jewish, Christian, and Gnostic — from the first and second centuries. They agree on the core content. No ancient source describes a sudden replacement of all biblical manuscripts. The manuscript evidence for the Bible's preservation is overwhelming.

How to explain this to someone

The Islamic Dilemma is one of the most powerful arguments in Christian-Muslim dialogue — because it uses the Muslim's own scripture against their claim. Here is how to explain it simply: 'The Quran says the Bible is the Word of God in dozens of places. It tells Muhammad to consult it. But Muslims say the Bible has been corrupted. So either the Quran was endorsing a corrupted book — which means Allah didn't know — or the Bible wasn't corrupted when Muhammad lived, which means Muslims today are wrong about it.'

In conversation: "Can you show me a single verse in the Quran that says the Bible has been changed? Not the meaning — the text. Because the Quran only affirms the Bible. It never says it was altered."

The Quranic evidence — what the Quran actually says

The Islamic Dilemma rests entirely on the Quran's own testimony about the Bible. The evidence is extensive and unambiguous. Surah 5:46-47 states: 'We sent, following in their footsteps, Jesus, the son of Mary, confirming that which came before him in the Torah; and We gave him the Gospel, in which was guidance and light and confirming that which preceded it of the Torah as guidance and instruction for the righteous.' Three verses later (5:47), Allah commands: 'And let the People of the Gospel judge by what Allah has revealed therein. And whoever does not judge by what Allah has revealed — then it is those who are the defiantly disobedient.'

Most decisive for the Islamic Dilemma is Surah 10:94: 'So if you are in doubt, [O Muhammad], about that which We have revealed to you, then ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you.' Muhammad is explicitly told to consult the existing Torah and Gospel as an authoritative reference. If those Scriptures were corrupted, this command would direct Muhammad toward corrupted guidance — a blasphemous implication.

Surah 3:3-4 is equally clear: 'He has sent down upon you, [O Muhammad], the Book in truth, confirming what was before it. And He revealed the Torah and the Gospel. Before, as guidance for the people.' The present tense matters here — Allah is describing the Torah and Gospel as current, living guidance for the people. This is not a reference to an original, now-lost revelation. It is a statement about what exists in Muhammad's time.

The Quran also titles Christians and Jews 'People of the Book' (Ahl al-Kitab) — approximately 54 times. This title affirms that they possess an actual Book. If the Book had been so corrupted as to be unreliable, the title would be a cruel mockery. The consistent Quranic position is that Jews and Christians possess authentic divine scripture which they should follow.

The tahrif debate — what kind of corruption do Muslims actually claim?

When confronted with the Islamic Dilemma, Muslim scholars typically distinguish between two types of corruption: tahrif al-lafz (corruption of the words) and tahrif al-ma'na (corruption of the meaning). Classical Islamic scholars were divided on this question. Most modern Sunni Muslims claim only corruption of meaning — that the words are largely preserved but have been misinterpreted.

But this response creates its own problem. The Quran never specifies tahrif al-ma'na. And if only the meaning was corrupted by misinterpretation, why does the Quran repeatedly command people to 'judge by what Allah has revealed therein' (5:44, 5:47)? Directing people to a misinterpreted text for authoritative guidance makes no sense. The Quran clearly treats the Bible in Muhammad's time as genuine, reliable scripture.

The historical argument against wholesale textual corruption is also powerful. If Jewish scribes corrupted the Torah to remove prophesies of Muhammad, they would have had to coordinate globally — in Palestine, Egypt, Babylon, and Rome simultaneously. Jewish communities in Alexandria, Rome, and Persia were not in communication. Yet they all have the same Torah. And Christians, who had every motive to preserve prophesies of Jesus, would need to have simultaneously removed those same prophesies from their scriptures. The logistics of this conspiracy are impossible.

The Dead Sea Scrolls — discovered in 1947 and dated to 125 BC — contain Hebrew manuscripts of the Old Testament books that are virtually identical to what we read today. They predate Christianity entirely. If the Torah was corrupted to remove Muhammad's name, that corruption would have had to happen after 125 BC. The manuscript evidence leaves no room for the corruption claim.

David Wood's argument — examining every Muslim response

David Wood, former atheist and now one of the leading Christian apologists on Islam, has spent years developing the Islamic Dilemma in public debates with Muslim scholars. His central argument is that every Muslim response to the dilemma fails on Quranic grounds.

The 'original revelation was lost' response: Muslims sometimes say that Muhammad was referring to the original Torah and Gospel, which were later lost. But Surah 10:94 tells Muhammad to 'ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you' — present tense, living people, accessible scripture. There is no reference to an original revelation that has been lost. The command is to consult the scripture that exists.

The 'the Quran supersedes the Bible' response: Some Muslims argue that the Quran supersedes and replaces the Bible, making its preservation irrelevant. But the Quran itself never says this. It says it 'confirms' what came before it (3:3). It commands people to judge by the Torah and Gospel. A supersession argument contradicts the Quran's own statements about the Bible's ongoing authority.

The 'there are errors in the Bible' response: Muslims often cite contradictions or errors in the Bible as evidence of corruption. But alleged errors and deliberate corruption are different things. The Quran never accuses Jews and Christians of deliberately changing their scripture — it accuses them of misinterpreting it, hiding parts of it, and mispronouncing it. These accusations are about interpretation and oral recitation, not textual alteration.

The Islamic Dilemma stands: the Quran's testimony about the Bible is incompatible with the Muslim claim that the Bible has been corrupted. Either the Quran is wrong about the Bible — a devastating admission for a Muslim — or the Bible has not been corrupted in the way Muslims claim.

Using this argument graciously in conversation

The Islamic Dilemma is a powerful argument but it must be used with care. Many Muslims have never examined the Quranic evidence for themselves. They have inherited the corruption claim as a given without verifying whether the Quran actually says what they have been told.

The most effective approach is curiosity rather than confrontation. Ask your Muslim friend to show you where in the Quran it says the Bible has been corrupted. Most cannot find a verse — because there isn't one. This opens a genuine conversation rather than creating defensiveness.

It is also essential to be clear that this argument is not an attack on Islam as a religion or on Muslims as people. It is an internal consistency argument — asking whether the Islamic worldview is coherent on its own terms. Muslims who engage honestly with this question deserve respect for their intellectual seriousness.

When a Muslim responds that scholars have found errors in the Bible, acknowledge those discussions and invite them to engage with the manuscript evidence and the textual scholarship. The conversation about biblical reliability is separate from the Islamic Dilemma argument and deserves its own treatment.

Does the Quran Promise to Preserve the Bible?
Muslims claim Allah promised to preserve the Quran — but the same Quran affirms the Bible was preserved before it
📚 Intermediate
Free
The preservation argument
  1. Surah 15:9 promises Allah will preserve 'the Reminder' — but classical scholars disagree on whether this means only the Quran.
  2. The Quran affirms the Torah and Gospel as true and authoritative in Muhammad's time — implying they were preserved.
  3. Muslims claim the Bible was corrupted after Muhammad — but there is no historical evidence of this.
  4. The manuscript evidence shows no global corruption of biblical texts in the 7th century.
  5. Allah directing Muslims to corrupted scripture would contradict divine wisdom.

Muslims often argue that Allah promised to preserve the Quran (Surah 15:9) but never made this promise for the Bible — implying the Bible could have been corrupted. But Surah 15:9 says: 'Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder, and indeed, it is We who are its Guardian.' Classical Muslim scholars debated whether 'Reminder' referred only to the Quran or to all divine revelation. More importantly — if Allah affirmed the Torah and Gospel as true and authoritative in Muhammad's time, then by implication they were being preserved. Otherwise Allah was endorsing corrupted guidance.

The preservation argument is secondary to the Islamic Dilemma but adds historical depth. If Allah endorsed the Bible as scripture in Muhammad's time, it was necessarily being preserved. And the manuscript evidence shows no corruption either before or after Muhammad.

Questions Muslims ask
QDoesn't Surah 15:9 only apply to the Quran? — This is the standard Muslim interpretation. But the verse uses 'the Reminder' (al-dhikr) — a term used in the Quran for divine revelation generally, not exclusively for the Quran. And the argument does not depend on 15:9: if the Quran endorses the Bible as authoritative in Muhammad's time, corruption would contradict the Quran's endorsement regardless of 15:9.
QWhat about the thousands of Bible manuscript variants? — The variants in biblical manuscripts are overwhelmingly trivial — spelling differences, word order, minor scribal errors. No Christian doctrine depends on a textual variant. Bart Ehrman, the most prominent critic of New Testament reliability, acknowledges this. The core content of the Bible is preserved with extraordinary fidelity across 5,800+ Greek manuscripts.

How to explain this to someone

When a Muslim says Allah only promised to preserve the Quran, ask them: 'If the Bible was corrupted in Muhammad's time, why does the Quran command people to judge by it and follow it? Would Allah direct his prophet to a corrupted book for guidance?' The preservation question is secondary — the endorsement question is primary.

In conversation: "Show me the historical evidence for a global corruption of the Bible in the 7th century. We have thousands of manuscripts from before and after Muhammad. They agree. Where did the corruption happen?"

The Quran's endorsement implies preservation

The core argument is simple: if the Quran endorses the Bible as authoritative scripture for Jews, Christians, and even Muhammad, then the Bible was necessarily preserved at the time of the endorsement. Allah would not direct his prophet to corrupted guidance — that would be a serious theological problem for the Islamic view of divine wisdom.

The Quran was written in the 7th century AD. At that time, thousands of biblical manuscripts existed across the Middle East, North Africa, and Europe. These manuscripts — held by Jews, by Eastern Orthodox Christians, by Coptic Christians, by Roman Catholics — all agree on the core content. For the Bible to have been corrupted after Muhammad, all of these widely dispersed communities would have had to simultaneously change their manuscripts. This is historically impossible.

The claim that the Bible was corrupted before Muhammad is equally untenable. If it was corrupted before Muhammad, Allah was directing his prophet to consult a corrupted book in Surah 10:94. This contradicts the Islamic view that Allah is all-knowing and perfectly wise.

The manuscript evidence for biblical preservation

We have approximately 5,800 Greek manuscripts of the New Testament and thousands more in Latin, Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, and other languages. The earliest fragments date to within decades of the original writing. Textual scholars have compared these manuscripts exhaustively. Their conclusion: the text of the New Testament is preserved with extraordinary reliability.

The Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered in 1947, contain Old Testament manuscripts dated to approximately 125 BC. Comparing them with medieval Hebrew manuscripts shows virtually identical text — despite 1,200 years of copying. The Isaiah scroll is particularly remarkable: 1,000 years of copying has produced a text that is 95% identical word for word, with the remaining 5% being obvious scribal slips.

For the biblical text to have been corrupted, every known manuscript would have had to be changed simultaneously — in Alexandria, Rome, Constantinople, Antioch, Carthage, and Jerusalem. No historical source records this event. No Muslim scholar has identified when, how, or by whom the corruption was carried out. The corruption claim is asserted without historical evidence.

The Quran's own textual history

An important consideration is the Quran's own textual history. According to Muslim tradition, the third Caliph Uthman ordered all variant copies of the Quran burned and had an official version produced (approximately 650 AD). This means that variant Qurans were in circulation within 20 years of Muhammad's death — and were deliberately destroyed.

Modern scholarship has identified Qira'at — variant readings of the Quran — that survive despite Uthman's burning. These variants affect meaning in some places. The Quran has its own textual history that is more complex than the standard Muslim narrative of perfect preservation suggests.

This is not to undermine the Quran but to point out that appealing to perfect divine preservation as a reason to distrust the Bible — while ignoring the textual history of the Quran — is inconsistent. Both documents have textual histories that deserve honest examination.

The Variant Readings of the Quran — Qira'at
The Quran has multiple accepted variant readings that differ in meaning — a fact rarely discussed in Muslim communities
📚 Intermediate
Free
Key facts about Qira'at
  1. Islamic scholarship officially recognises ten accepted variant readings of the Quran (Qira'at).
  2. Some variants differ not just in pronunciation but in meaning — affecting theology.
  3. The Prophet is recorded in Hadith as having taught different communities different versions.
  4. Uthman's burning of variant manuscripts (c. 650 AD) confirms multiple versions existed within 20 years of Muhammad.
  5. The Hafs reading used in most of the world today is one of ten — not the only 'authentic' Quran.

Muslims frequently appeal to the Quran's perfect preservation as evidence of its divine origin — often contrasting it with the Bible's textual variants. But Islamic scholarship has always recognised the Qira'at — ten officially accepted variant readings of the Quran. These are not merely different ways to pronounce the same words. Some Qira'at differ in meaning, affecting theological interpretation. This internal evidence challenges the claim that the Quran was perfectly preserved in exactly one form.

The Qira'at are not a fringe argument — they are taught in every serious institution of Quranic studies. The mainstream Muslim response is that the Prophet himself authorised multiple recitations. This is an important concession: it acknowledges that the Quran exists in multiple authorised versions, which significantly weakens the 'perfect preservation' argument used against the Bible.

Questions Muslims ask
QDidn't the Prophet himself authorise multiple recitations? — This is the standard Muslim response — and it is important. If the Prophet authorised multiple recitations, then the Quran intentionally exists in multiple versions. But this concession undermines the argument that the Bible's variants prove corruption while the Quran's unity proves divine origin. Both have variant traditions. The question is which underlying text is more reliable — and on that question, the New Testament's manuscript tradition is vastly larger.
QAren't the variants minor and unimportant? — Some are. But others affect meaning. For example, Surah 2:222 in the Hafs reading: 'until they are pure' (yatahhurna) vs Warsh reading: 'until they purify themselves' (yatahharna) — the legal implications differ. In Surah 9:100, a single word creates a different theological meaning between the two main reading traditions. The variants are not all trivial.

What are the Qira'at and why do they matter?

The Qira'at (plural of Qira'a, meaning 'recitation') are the multiple authorised ways of reading and reciting the Quran. Islamic scholarship recognises ten canonical Qira'at, named after the scholars who codified them: Nafi, Ibn Kathir, Abu Amr, Ibn Amir, Asim, Hamza, al-Kisa'i, Abu Ja'far, Ya'qub, and Khalaf. Of these, the most widely used in the world today are the Hafs an Asim reading (used in most printed Qurans) and the Warsh an Nafi reading (used in North and West Africa).

The significance of the Qira'at is that they represent genuine variant traditions of the Quranic text. The Muslim tradition explains this by saying Muhammad himself recited the Quran in different ways to accommodate different Arab tribes' dialects. The Hadith literature records a famous tradition where Muhammad taught different companions different recitations — and both were confirmed as authentic.

What this means for the preservation argument is significant: the Quran does not exist in one single perfect form. It exists in multiple authorised forms, some of which differ in meaning. The printed Quran you pick up depends on which reading tradition was used in its publication. This is analogous to the textual traditions of the Bible — and the existence of variants does not, by itself, imply corruption.

Meaningful variant differences between Qira'at

While most Qira'at differences are phonological (how letters are pronounced), some create genuine differences in meaning. The verse most frequently cited is Surah 2:222, which deals with women during menstruation. The Hafs reading (yatahhurna) means 'until they become pure' — a passive construction. The Warsh reading (yatahharna) means 'until they purify themselves' — an active construction. The legal implications for Islamic jurisprudence differ: one suggests the state of purity, the other an active ritual.

Another significant variant is in Surah 5:6 (ablution before prayer). The Hafs reading uses the word 'arjulakum' (your feet) in the accusative, suggesting feet must be washed. The Ibn Kathir reading uses 'arjulikum' (of your feet) in the genitive, which some scholars interpret as permitting wiping the feet rather than washing them. Shia Muslims cite this genitive reading in support of their different ritual practice.

These are not catastrophic differences — both Qira'at traditions are internally coherent and produce a consistent overall message. But they demonstrate that the claim of perfectly preserved, singular textual unity is more complex than the standard Muslim presentation acknowledges.

Uthman's burning — why it matters historically

According to Islamic tradition, the third Caliph Uthman ibn Affan (r. 644-656 AD) became concerned about disagreements over Quranic recitation in different parts of the rapidly expanding Islamic empire. Around 650 AD — approximately 18 years after Muhammad's death — he commissioned a standardised text and ordered all variant manuscripts burned.

This historical event is remarkable for two reasons. First, it confirms that multiple different versions of the Quran existed within 20 years of Muhammad's death — different enough that Uthman felt compelled to burn them. If the Quran had been perfectly preserved in one form, burning would have been unnecessary. Second, information about the burned variants is lost. We do not know exactly how they differed from the standardised text Uthman produced.

Ibn Masud, one of the Prophet's most prominent companions and a recognised Quranic scholar, refused to hand over his copy for burning and disputed Uthman's authority to standardise the text. His version is recorded as having had differences from the Uthmanic text. This internal Islamic evidence — not a hostile external source — confirms that Quranic textual history is more complex than simple perfect preservation.

Applying this consistently to both scriptures

The purpose of examining the Qira'at is not to attack the Quran but to call for intellectual consistency. The standard Muslim argument is: the Bible has variants, therefore it has been corrupted; the Quran has no variants, therefore it is perfectly preserved. The first part of this argument is true. The second part is overstated.

If textual variants prove corruption, then the Quran's variant reading traditions would also require explanation. If textual variants do not prove corruption — if they are a normal feature of the transmission of any ancient document — then Bible variants do not prove corruption either. The consistent position is the second: variants are normal in manuscript transmission and do not by themselves prove corruption.

The honest conversation is: both scriptures have textual histories that deserve examination. The question is which text is better supported by manuscript evidence, historical documentation, and internal consistency. On that question, the New Testament's manuscript tradition — 5,800+ Greek manuscripts, multiple independent textual families — provides the strongest foundation of any ancient document.

04
Uthman's Burning of Competing Manuscripts
The third Caliph destroyed variant Qurans — what was in them, and why does it matter?
🎓 Advanced
Free
Key historical facts
  1. Uthman burned variant Quran manuscripts approximately 18-20 years after Muhammad's death.
  2. Multiple companions had their own codices that differed from the standardised text.
  3. Ibn Masud refused to surrender his codex and openly disputed Uthman's authority.
  4. The Sanaa manuscripts (discovered 1972 in Yemen) reveal pre-Uthmanic text that differs from the standard Quran.
  5. The burned manuscripts cannot be recovered — we do not know exactly what they contained.

Around 650 AD, the third Caliph Uthman ordered all variant Quran manuscripts collected and burned, leaving only his standardised version. The Islamic tradition presents this as a positive act of preservation. But it raises important questions: Why were variants numerous enough to require this action within 20 years of Muhammad's death? What was in the burned manuscripts? And what does this say about the claim that the Quran was perfectly preserved from the moment Muhammad received it?

The burning of variant Qurans is an established fact of Islamic history — reported in Sahih Bukhari and other major Hadith collections. The question is what to make of it apologetically. It does not prove the Quran is false. But it does challenge the claim that the Quran was perfectly preserved in a single form from the beginning.

The historical sources — what Sahih Bukhari records

The burning of variant Qurans is not a controversial reconstruction by hostile scholars — it is recorded in Sahih Bukhari, one of the two most authoritative Hadith collections in Sunni Islam. Bukhari 6:61:510 records that Uthman sent a message to Hafsa (one of the Prophet's widows who kept an early manuscript) asking to borrow it to have copies made. He then 'had all other Quranic materials, whether written in fragmentary manuscripts or whole copies, burnt.'

The scope of the burning was comprehensive. It was not merely a collation of oral recitations into written form. It was the active destruction of written manuscripts that differed from the standardised text. The reason Uthman gave was that different communities were reciting different versions and arguing about which was correct — a problem serious enough to require military action within 20 years of the Prophet's death.

The burning raises an obvious historical question: what was in the destroyed manuscripts? We know from the Hadith that different companions had different codices. Abdullah ibn Masud had a codex that he believed was more complete than Uthman's version. Ubay ibn Ka'b had a codex known to have included Surahs not in the standard text (notably the 'Surah of Suckling' mentioned in other Hadith). These are Islamic sources reporting these facts — not hostile external critics.

The Sanaa manuscripts — pre-Uthmanic evidence

In 1972, workers renovating the Great Mosque of Sanaa in Yemen discovered a cache of ancient Quran manuscripts hidden in the walls. The find included what appears to be pre-Uthmanic manuscripts — texts dating to before Uthman's standardisation. German scholar Gerd-R. Puin was given access to photograph the manuscripts and reported that they show significant variations from the standard Uthmanic text.

The Yemeni authorities restricted access to these manuscripts for decades, preventing full publication. This has naturally generated curiosity about what they contain. The available photographs and partial analyses suggest that some manuscripts have different verse ordering, different verse divisions, and in some places different words from the standard text.

This is significant because it provides physical evidence of the variant tradition that Uthman's burning was designed to eliminate. The Sanaa manuscripts are not proof that the Quran is false — they are evidence that the Quran's textual history is more complex than the standard narrative of perfect singular preservation suggests.

What this means for the apologetics conversation

The Uthman burning argument must be handled carefully and respectfully in conversation. It is not a knockout argument — Muslims have responses to all of these points, and the responses deserve to be heard. The goal is not to destabilise someone's faith but to invite honest examination of the evidence.

The most important apologetic use of this argument is to challenge the double standard applied to the Bible and the Quran. When Muslims argue that the Bible's textual variants prove corruption, the Uthman burning shows that the Quran has its own textual history. Consistency requires applying the same standards to both documents.

The broader point is this: the question of which scripture is more reliable cannot be settled by simple appeals to perfect preservation. Both scriptures have histories of transmission that deserve careful scholarship. The New Testament, despite its manuscript variants, has the most extensively attested text of any ancient document. Examining both honestly is the only intellectually serious approach.

The Missing Verses — What the Hadith Says Was Lost
Multiple Hadith record that parts of the Quran were lost, forgotten, or abrogated after Muhammad's death
🎓 Advanced
Free
What the Hadith record
  1. Sahih Muslim records that a companion forgot entire Quranic verses — and when he recited to the Prophet, he confirmed they were correct but had 'been withdrawn from my memory.'
  2. Sahih Bukhari records Umar bin al-Khattab stating: 'Let no one of you say that he has acquired the whole of the Quran, for how does he know that it is all? Much of the Quran has been lost.'
  3. The Stoning Verse (Rajm): Multiple Hadith record that a verse prescribing stoning for adultery was in the Quran but was lost when the person who memorised it was killed.
  4. Sahih Muslim records a 'Surah of Suckling' (Rada') that apparently prescribed ten breastfeedings — Aisha reported it was 'revealed and was in the Quran' but was lost when the parchment was eaten by a goat.
  5. These are mainstream Hadith — not fringe traditions — recorded in the collections Sunni Muslims regard as the most authoritative after the Quran.

The Islamic doctrine of naskh (abrogation) acknowledges that some Quranic verses were abrogated — replaced by later revelations. But Hadith literature goes further: multiple traditions record that actual Quranic text was forgotten or lost after Muhammad's death. These are not hostile external sources but the most authoritative Hadith collections in Sunni Islam.

The missing verses argument does not undermine the Quran's spiritual value but significantly challenges the claim of perfect, complete preservation. These are internal Islamic sources — not external critics — acknowledging that the Quran as we have it may not contain everything Muhammad received.

QDoesn't abrogation explain lost verses? — Naskh (abrogation) explains verses that were intentionally superseded. But the tradition of the goat eating the parchment and Umar's statement about lost Quran are not abrogation — they are records of unintentional loss. The distinction matters.

The Stoning Verse — analysis of the tradition

The Stoning Verse (Ayat al-Rajm) is arguably the most significant missing verse tradition in Islamic literature. Multiple strong Hadith in Bukhari and Muslim record that Umar ibn al-Khattab — the second Caliph and one of the most authoritative figures in early Islam — repeatedly said: 'Among the things which Allah revealed was the verse of rajm (stoning). We read it, understood it, and memorised it. The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) passed judgement in accordance with it, and we did so after him. But I am afraid that as time goes by, someone may say, 'We do not find the verse of rajm in the Book of Allah,' and will go astray by abandoning an obligation that Allah has revealed.'

Umar's statement does several things simultaneously. It confirms that a stoning verse existed. It confirms it was 'revealed' — authoritative Quranic revelation. It confirms it was 'understood and memorised' by leading companions. And it expresses anxiety that future generations would deny its existence because it could no longer be found in the physical Quran. This is precisely what happened: the stoning verse is not in the standard Uthmanic Quran.

Islamic law (Sharia) prescribes stoning for adultery — derived from the Hadith, not from the Quran. If the Stoning Verse was genuine Quranic revelation, its absence from the current Quran is a significant question for the perfect preservation claim. Muslim scholars have various responses, but all must grapple with why one of the Prophet's most trusted companions believed Quranic revelation had been lost.

Aisha's tradition and the goat

Sahih Muslim 8:3421 records: 'Aisha reported that it had been revealed in the Holy Quran that ten clear sucklings make the marriage unlawful, then it was abrogated by five sucklings, and Allah's Apostle died and it was before that time (found) in the Holy Quran.' She adds in another version of the tradition: 'The parchment was under my bed. When Allah's Messenger died and we were preoccupied with his death, a goat came in and ate it.'

This Hadith is in Sahih Muslim — the second most authoritative collection in Sunni Islam. Aisha, the Prophet's wife and one of the most important transmitters of Hadith, records that a Quranic verse was lost because a goat ate the parchment it was written on. This is not a hostile report from an enemy of Islam — it is transmitted by the Prophet's own household.

Muslim scholars have various responses to this tradition. Some say it was abrogated in recitation (not just in rulings). Some question the chain of transmission. Some simply acknowledge the complexity. What none can do is simply dismiss it — it is in Sahih Muslim with a sound chain.

Hadith Science — Is It a Reliable Historical Method?
The Islamic science of Hadith reliability uses criteria that, applied consistently, weaken rather than strengthen many traditions
🎓 Advanced
Free
Key questions about hadith reliability
  1. Hadith were not written down systematically until 150-250 years after Muhammad's death.
  2. The isnad (chain of transmitters) system depends on oral transmission across multiple generations.
  3. Even the most authoritative Hadith collections (Bukhari, Muslim) disagree with each other on numerous points.
  4. The Mutazilites and many early Muslim scholars questioned the reliability of the Hadith tradition.
  5. By the same standards that Muslims apply to reject the Gospels, the Hadith tradition is even more vulnerable.

Hadith — the traditions recording the sayings and actions of Muhammad — are the second most important source in Islam after the Quran. Islamic scholarship developed a sophisticated science (ilm al-hadith) to evaluate their reliability, based primarily on the chain of transmitters (isnad). When Christians apply similar historical methods to the Gospels, Muslims often object that the Gospels are not reliable. This argument examines whether the hadith science actually provides reliable historical access to Muhammad.

The New Testament documents were written within 20-60 years of Jesus's death by people in direct contact with eyewitnesses. The earliest Hadith collections were compiled 200-250 years after Muhammad's death. By secular historical standards, the Gospels have far stronger claim to reliability than the Hadith. This argument uses Islamic methodology against itself.

Questions Muslims ask
QBut Bukhari and Muslim are sahih (sound) — the highest standard! — The terms sahih, hasan, and da'if are judgements made by 9th-century scholars about 7th-century oral traditions — reconstructing chains of transmission from centuries earlier. The criteria are: was the chain unbroken, were the transmitters known for reliability and good memory? But we cannot verify memory across oral chains of 5-6 people over 200 years.
QIsn't the isnad system uniquely rigorous? — The isnad system is sophisticated and more developed than anything in Greco-Roman historiography. But its weaknesses are documented within Islamic scholarship itself. The Hadith contain contradictions, forgeries, and later insertions acknowledged by Muslim scholars. Al-Bukhari reportedly examined 600,000 Hadith and accepted only about 7,000 as authentic — meaning he rejected over 98%.

How to explain this to someone

When a Muslim says the Gospels are unreliable because they were written decades after Jesus, point out that the Hadith were written down 200+ years after Muhammad. If decades make the Gospels unreliable, what do centuries make the Hadith?

In conversation: "I find the Hadith science really interesting. Can I ask — if someone applied the same criteria to the Hadith that you apply to the Gospels, how do you think they would hold up? I'm not trying to undermine the Hadith — I'm genuinely curious about the comparison."

The timeline problem — when were the Hadith written?

The Quran was compiled within decades of Muhammad's death (the Uthmanic codex, c. 650 AD). The Hadith were different. While some Hadith were written down during Muhammad's lifetime, systematic compilation did not begin until well into the 2nd century AH (8th century AD). The major Hadith collections — Bukhari, Muslim, Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud, Nasa'i, Ibn Majah — were compiled between approximately 846 and 915 AD. This is 210-280 years after Muhammad's death in 632 AD.

By comparison, Paul's letters were written 15-25 years after Jesus's crucifixion. The Gospel of Mark was written approximately 35-40 years after Jesus. Even the latest Gospel (John) was written approximately 60 years after Jesus. All of the New Testament documents were written while living memory of Jesus was still accessible — and while people who knew Jesus were still alive to correct errors.

The Hadith compilers were reconstructing oral traditions from 8-10 generations earlier. Al-Bukhari reportedly spent 16 years collecting and evaluating Hadith, traveling across the Islamic world. He examined approximately 600,000 Hadith and accepted only 7,275 as sahih (authentic). This means he rejected over 99% of the traditions he examined as unreliable. The sheer scale of fabrication he was filtering out is significant.

The isnad system — strengths and weaknesses

The isnad (chain of transmitters) is the foundation of Hadith evaluation. A Hadith records not just what Muhammad said or did, but the chain of people who transmitted the information: 'A told B, who told C, who told D, who heard from Muhammad.' The reliability of each person in the chain (their memory, piety, and trustworthiness) is evaluated by the science of rijal criticism (evaluation of transmitters).

The isnad system is genuinely sophisticated. Nothing comparable exists in Greco-Roman historical methodology. The Muslim scholars who developed it were serious about distinguishing authentic traditions from fabrications. The criterion that every transmitter must be known and their reliability verifiable is stringent.

However, the system has acknowledged weaknesses. First, it depends on oral transmission for the first 150-200 years — a long period during which fabrication was rampant (acknowledged by Muslim scholars themselves). Second, the evaluation of transmitters was itself done centuries after the fact, relying on written sources about people who may not have been evaluated contemporaneously. Third, the system cannot detect sophisticated fabrications that included plausible chains.

The Gospels vs Hadith — applying consistent standards

When Christians present the Gospels as historical testimony to Jesus, Muslims frequently object: they were written decades after Jesus, they have variants, they were compiled by a community with theological interests. These are legitimate historical questions that deserve serious engagement.

But the consistent application of these criteria produces an uncomfortable result for the Hadith tradition. If decades of distance from the events are disqualifying, centuries of distance are worse. If community theological interest is disqualifying, the Hadith compilers — who were deeply embedded in 9th-century Islamic political and theological debates — faced the same problem. If variants are disqualifying, the acknowledged contradictions between Bukhari and Muslim require explanation.

The strongest argument is not that the Hadith are unreliable (they contain much that is historically valuable) but that a double standard is being applied. The Gospels, written within living memory of Jesus by communities that included people who had seen Jesus, deserve at least as much credibility as traditions compiled 200+ years after Muhammad by scholars who had no living connection to their subject.

The Sira — How Reliable is Muhammad's Biography?
The earliest biography of Muhammad was written 150 years after his death and relies on sources it admits are uncertain
🎓 Advanced
Free
Key facts about the Sira
  1. The earliest Sira (Ibn Ishaq) was written approximately 130-140 years after Muhammad's death.
  2. Ibn Ishaq himself included stories he acknowledged hearing without reliable chains of transmission.
  3. The original Ibn Ishaq text no longer exists — we have only Ibn Hisham's edited version from 60 years later.
  4. Ibn Hisham explicitly says he omitted things that 'would be distressing to certain people.'
  5. Many details of Muhammad's life are known only from the Sira — not from the Quran or the more rigorously evaluated Hadith.

The Sira (biography of Muhammad) is the primary historical source for the details of Muhammad's life beyond the Quran. The earliest and most authoritative Sira is by Ibn Ishaq (d. 767 AD), revised by Ibn Hisham (d. 833 AD) — written 135-200 years after Muhammad's death. Ibn Ishaq himself acknowledged including traditions he could not verify and stories he had heard without reliable chains. By the historical standards Muslims apply to the Gospels, the Sira faces far greater challenges.

The reliability of the Sira matters because many Islamic practices and theological claims depend on details found only there. If the Sira is as reliable as Muslims claim, it should be evaluated by the same standards applied to the Gospels — which, on those standards, come out significantly better.

Questions Muslims ask
QIsn't the Sira supported by the Hadith? — Some details in the Sira are supported by Hadith. But many are not — and the Sira itself often lacks the rigorous isnad chains required by Hadith science. Ibn Ishaq was criticized by his contemporaries, including Malik ibn Anas (founder of the Maliki school), for including unreliable material and even fabrications.
QThe Sira isn't scripture — why does it matter? — The Sira is not the Quran. But it is the primary source for the details of Muhammad's life that Muslims regard as authoritative models for conduct. Many Islamic practices and theological claims depend on Sira details. Its reliability matters for the same reason the reliability of Acts matters for early Christian history.

Ibn Ishaq and the historical sources for Muhammad's life

Muhammad ibn Ishaq ibn Yasar (d. 767 AD) wrote the first systematic biography of Muhammad approximately 130-140 years after the Prophet's death in 632 AD. His work, the Sirat Rasul Allah, is the foundation of all subsequent Siras. However, the original text of Ibn Ishaq does not survive — we have it only through the edited version produced by Ibn Hisham (d. 833 AD), who explicitly says in his introduction that he has omitted material 'which it is disgraceful to discuss' and things that would 'distress certain people.'

This immediately presents a problem: the primary biography of Muhammad was edited by someone 60 years later who admits to removing content he found inconvenient. We do not know what was removed. The most authoritative biography of Muhammad is a second-hand redaction of a 130-year-old text.

Ibn Ishaq's methodology is also acknowledged to have limitations. He is known to have included isra'iliyat — traditions borrowed from Jewish and Christian sources — without always indicating their origin. He also included poetry attributed to pre-Islamic and early Islamic figures that modern scholars suspect was composed later and attributed to historical figures. His contemporary Malik ibn Anas refused to include Ibn Ishaq's traditions in his own legal work and called him a liar, though this was partly a legal dispute about the inclusion of poetry as legal evidence.

Comparing the Sira to the Gospels historically

The most important comparison is one of proximity to the events. The Gospel of Mark was written approximately 35-40 years after the crucifixion. The Gospel of Luke explicitly claims to be based on eyewitness accounts (1:1-4). Paul's letters were written 15-25 years after the crucifixion and contain personal testimony from someone who had met Peter and James, the brother of Jesus. The earliest Gospel traditions are within living memory of Jesus.

Ibn Ishaq wrote approximately 130-140 years after Muhammad. He could not have spoken to anyone who personally knew Muhammad — the gap was too large. He was working from traditions, documents, and accounts that had themselves been transmitted for 4-5 generations before reaching him.

By the historical criteria Muslims typically apply to the Gospels — proximity to events, access to eyewitnesses, independence of sources — the Sira is in a weaker position than the Gospels. This does not make the Sira worthless. It contains much valuable historical material. But intellectual consistency requires applying the same standard to both.

What this means for Christian-Muslim dialogue

The purpose of examining the Sira's reliability is not to attack Muhammad or Islam but to call for intellectual consistency. The same critical tools that historians apply to the Gospels — questions of dating, provenance, authorship, and transmission — should be applied to Islamic sources with equal rigour.

When Muslim scholars examine the Gospels using modern historical-critical methods, they typically conclude that the Gospels are late, redacted, and theologically motivated — and therefore unreliable as historical sources. But this methodology, applied consistently to the Sira and Hadith, produces equally uncomfortable results for Islamic historical sources.

The most honest conversation is: both traditions have historical sources that were written after the events, transmitted through communities with theological interests, and subject to critical historical examination. The question is not which tradition is immune to criticism but which tradition has the strongest overall historical case. Christian apologists argue that the Resurrection evidence — the minimal facts approach, the early creed in 1 Corinthians 15, the empty tomb — provides a stronger historical foundation than the Islamic case for Muhammad's prophethood can provide.
