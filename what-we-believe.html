What We Believe

What We Believe

Apologia Daily stands within historic, creedal Christianity — the faith summarized in the Apostles' and Nicene Creeds and held in common, for nearly two thousand years, across the great branches of the Church.

We are deliberately a "mere Christianity" project, in C. S. Lewis's sense: we defend the faith that Catholics, Orthodox, and Protestants share, rather than any one tradition's distinctives. Our doctrinal commitments are the historic essentials.

The faith we affirm

With the whole Church, we confess the Apostles' Creed:

I believe in God the Father Almighty, Maker of heaven and earth:

And in Jesus Christ his only Son our Lord, Who was conceived by the Holy Ghost, Born of the Virgin Mary, Suffered under Pontius Pilate, Was crucified, dead, and buried: He descended into hell; The third day he rose again from the dead, He ascended into heaven, And sitteth on the right hand of God the Father Almighty; From thence he shall come to judge the quick and the dead.

I believe in the Holy Ghost; The holy Catholick* Church; The Communion of Saints; The Forgiveness of sins; The Resurrection of the body, And the life everlasting. Amen.

And, with the councils of the undivided Church, the Nicene Creed (Constantinople, AD 381):

We believe in one God, the Father Almighty, Maker of heaven and earth, and of all things visible and invisible.

And in one Lord Jesus Christ, the only-begotten Son of God, begotten of the Father before all worlds (æons), Light of Light, very God of very God, begotten, not made, being of one substance with the Father; by whom all things were made; who for us men, and for our salvation, came down from heaven, and was incarnate by the Holy Ghost of the Virgin Mary, and was made man; and was crucified also for us under Pontius Pilate; he suffered and was buried; and the third day he rose again, according to the Scriptures; and ascended into heaven, and sitteth on the right hand of the Father; and he shall come again, with glory, to judge the quick and the dead; whose kingdom shall have no end.

And in the Holy Ghost, the Lord and Giver of life, who proceedeth from the Father,† who with the Father and the Son together is worshiped and glorified, who spake by the prophets. In one holy catholic and apostolic Church; we acknowledge one baptism for the remission of sins; we look for the resurrection of the dead, and the life of the world to come. Amen.

* "catholic" / "Catholick" here means universal — the whole Church across time and place, not a single denomination. (Creed texts are the public-domain Schaff and 1662 Book of Common Prayer translations.)
† We give the shared AD 381 wording, "who proceedeth from the Father." The Western churches later confess "from the Father and the Son" (the filioque). This is a genuine, historic East–West difference, and—true to our neutrality—we do not adjudicate it.

In short, with the historic creeds we hold: one God, eternally Father, Son, and Holy Spirit; the full deity and full humanity of Jesus Christ; his bodily death, resurrection, and future return; the inspiration and authority of Scripture; humanity's need of rescue; and salvation as God's gift, received by grace. These are the convictions every argument on this site is meant to serve and stay within.

Where we stay neutral

On the questions that have long and sincerely divided faithful Christians — the mode and meaning of baptism, church government, the Lord's Supper, predestination and free will, the details of the end times, the age of the earth, and more — Apologia Daily takes no party line. Where these come up, our aim is to present the historic options fairly so you can think them through, not to settle them for you. Reasonable, devout believers land in different places on these matters, and we respect that.

How this shapes the content

Every argument we publish is written to fit within this creedal orthodoxy and to avoid adjudicating intra-Christian disputes. It also shapes our tone: because we believe the truth can bear scrutiny, we are free to represent critics fairly and to admit the genuine difficulties — always, as 1 Peter 3:15 puts it, "with gentleness and respect."

Faithfulness before reach

Faithfulness to Jesus Christ and to this historic, creedal orthodoxy matters more to us than the platform's reach, growth, or success — every time. Where the two ever pull apart, orthodoxy wins. Every essay, answer, and reel on this site passes an independent doctrinal review against this statement before it is published.
