The HANDS Framework

The deep dive explains the argument. This track makes you able to wield it — rebuild it from memory, meet its hardest objections, and stay clear and calm in a real conversation.

The argument in brief

The HANDS framework, from Robert Bowman and J. Ed Komoszewski's book Putting Jesus in His Place, organizes the New Testament's case for the deity of Christ into five categories that, in Jewish monotheism, belong to God alone. HANDS is an acronym: the Honors due to God (worship, prayer, doxology), the Attributes of God (eternity, omnipresence, unchangeableness), the Names of God (YHWH/kyrios, theos, "the First and the Last"), the Deeds of God (creating, sustaining, forgiving, judging, saving), and the Seat of God's throne.

The argument is that the New Testament deliberately attributes all five to Jesus. He is worshipped and prayed to; he is called eternal and unchanging; he bears the divine name in passages that quote YHWH texts; he creates and sustains all things, forgives sins, and judges the world; and he is seated on God's own throne (Revelation, Hebrews 1). Second-Temple Judaism drew one absolute line — between the Creator and everything created — and HANDS shows Jesus placed on the Creator's side of that line at every checkpoint Judaism itself used.

Honest scope: any single line can be debated, and the framework presupposes (rather than re-proves) the wider Trinitarian and incarnational picture in which Jesus is fully God and fully man, distinct from the Father yet one God. Its strength is cumulative: five independent lines, each hard to square with a merely human Jesus, converging on one conclusion — that the New Testament identifies Jesus within the unique identity of the one God.

The argument, formally

Five fingers, one grip: Jesus shares what God alone is.

P1

In Jewish monotheism, five things belong to God alone: the Honors due God (worship, prayer, doxology), the Attributes of God (eternity, omnipresence, unchangeableness), the Names of God (YHWH/kyrios, God, the First and the Last), the Deeds God does (creating, sustaining, forgiving, judging, saving), and the Seat of God's throne.

P2

The New Testament — across its earliest and latest documents alike — deliberately attributes all five to Jesus: he is worshipped, eternal, named with the divine Name, creator and final judge, and seated on God's own throne.

∴ C

Therefore the New Testament identifies Jesus within the unique identity of the one God — not as a promoted creature, but as one who shares everything that makes God God.

The framework (Bowman & Komoszewski) is cumulative by design: any single line can be quibbled; five independent lines converging — each individually fatal to a merely-human Jesus — is a case that must be answered whole.

The key idea — identity, not just titles.

Second-Temple Jews drew one absolute line: the Creator versus everything created, marked by what God alone does and receives. HANDS does not collect proof-texts; it shows Jesus placed on the Creator side of that line at every checkpoint Judaism itself used to draw it. That is why the framework convinces scholars who distrust isolated verses.

Defending the premises

Why each premise is more plausible than its denial

Support for P2 — each finger, with receipts

H + A + N

Honors: the risen Jesus receives proskunesis (Matt 28:17), prayer (Acts 7:59), and the doxology of every creature (Rev 5:13). Attributes: 'before Abraham was, I AM'; 'the same yesterday, today, forever' (Heb 13:8). Names: kyrios in YHWH-citations (Rom 10:13/Joel 2:32); 'the First and the Last' — Isaiah's exclusive self-title for YHWH — spoken by Jesus (Rev 1:17). And Jesus claims the honor in his own words: all judgment is given to the Son “that all may honor the Son JUST AS they honor the Father” (John 5:23) — the same honor, willed by the Father, while the persons stay distinct (the Father sends, the Son is sent). In a faith where God gives his glory to no other, equal honor is equal deity.

D + S

Deeds: all things made through him (John 1:3, Col 1:16), the universe sustained by his word (Heb 1:3), sins forgiven in his own person (Mark 2), all judgment given to the Son (John 5:22). Seat: 'sit at my right hand' (Ps 110) — and in Revelation 22 there is one throne, 'the throne of God AND of the Lamb,' with one set of servants worshipping.

Support for the inference — why five lines settle it

Judaism's own test

Bauckham showed Second-Temple Judaism marked God's uniqueness precisely by creation, sovereign rule, the Name, and exclusive worship. HANDS simply audits Jesus against Judaism's own checklist — and he passes every item no creature was permitted to pass.

Early and everywhere

The five lines are not a late stratum: the Honors and Names sit in Paul (50s AD) and in Aramaic prayer (marana tha); the Deeds open Mark; the Seat is the most-quoted OT verse in the NT. The pattern is the floor of the tradition, not its ceiling.

The hard part — objections, steelmanned

Each one stated as its strongest defender would put it

Each objection gets its strongest form first — never a strawman — then the reply.

"Worship just means homage — people bowed to kings too" Common

Strongest form

Proskuneo is used of bowing to human authorities throughout the ancient world. Honors prove etiquette, not deity.

Reply

Context is the test — and the NT runs the test for us: Peter refuses the same gesture from Cornelius ('I too am a man'), and Revelation's angel refuses it twice ('worship God!'). The risen Jesus accepts it, in heaven, from every creature, alongside the One on the throne. The same documents that police the line for men and angels watch Jesus stand on the far side of it.

"An exalted agent can carry the Name and the throne — Judaism allowed it" Trickier

Strongest form

Texts like the Exagoge put Moses on God's throne; angels bear the Name in Exodus 23. Agency, not identity, explains your five lines.

Reply

Each line alone, perhaps — that is why the case is cumulative. No agent figure receives all five: the Exagoge's Moses is a dream-scene never worshipped; the Name-bearing angel refuses cultic devotion. And the NT closes the agency door explicitly: the Son is contrasted WITH the angels (Hebrews 1), creates rather than is created, and receives the worship agents must refuse. Agents represent God's rule; Jesus shares God's throne while the seraphic test of Isaiah 6 — glory seen — is applied to him (John 12:41).

"Each letter has disputed verses — a chain of weak links is weak" Trickier

Strongest form

Granville Sharp constructions, textual variants at John 1:18, contested punctuation in Romans 9:5 — every pillar wobbles when pushed. Five wobbly pillars are not a fortress.

Reply

The framework was built for exactly this objection: no letter rests on one verse. Strike Romans 9:5 and the Names still have Romans 10:13's YHWH-citation; strike a variant and 'the First and the Last' remains. Five independent lines, each with multiple supports across multiple authors, is the structure of a cumulative case — the rebuttal must explain the CONVERGENCE, and 'every author independently wobbled in the same direction' is not an explanation.

"The Seat disproves it — sitting at God’s right hand means NOT being God" Common

Strongest form

Psalm 110 has the LORD speaking TO David's lord and seating him beside himself. Two figures, one God, one honoured guest.

Reply

Two persons, certainly — that is Trinitarianism, not a problem for it. The question is what the seat MEANS in Jewish thought: God shares his throne, like his glory, with no creature ('I will not give my glory to another'). Yet the Lamb is on the throne itself (Rev 7:17, 22:1-3), receiving the worship of every creature — the seat that distinguishes the persons simultaneously places the Son inside the prerogatives of the one God.

"This is theology imposed on texts — a clever acronym is not exegesis" Hard

Strongest form

HANDS is a mnemonic marketing device. Arrange any figure's honorifics into categories and you can manufacture divinity — the framework creates the pattern it claims to find.

Reply

Run the falsification test: apply HANDS to anyone else in the literature. Moses, Enoch, Michael, the high priest — each scores one partial line at most, always with the worship-line failing. The categories are not invented for Jesus; they are Judaism's own markers of the Creator/creature divide (Bauckham's divine-identity work, recognised across the scholarly spectrum). A framework that only one figure in the entire corpus satisfies is not manufacturing a pattern — it is reporting one.

"Then the NT contradicts itself — the same Jesus grows, prays, dies, and does not know the hour" Hard

Strongest form

Your five lines collide with five counter-lines: ignorance, fatigue, prayer, death. Honest reading says the NT is conflicted about Jesus, and you have curated one half.

Reply

The NT itself holds both halves ON PURPOSE — often in the same breath: the Creator of all things 'became flesh'; the one in the form of God 'emptied himself'; the upholder of the universe 'made purification for sins' (Hebrews 1:3 — one verse, both truths). That is not editorial confusion; it is the doctrine of the incarnation, stated before any council named it. The counter-lines describe what the Son took on; the HANDS lines describe what he never ceased to be — and a reading that keeps every text beats a reading that must delete half of them.
