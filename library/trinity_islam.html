Answering Islamic Objections to the Trinity

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Five times a day, from minarets on every inhabited continent, the same confession rings out: La ilaha illa Allah—there is no god but God. It is the first half of the Muslim profession of faith, and it expresses something a Christian should admire rather than resent: a fierce, jealous love for the oneness of God. To the Muslim ear, the Christian doctrine of the Trinity sounds like an assault on that oneness—a smuggling of partners into the throne room of the one Creator. When a devout Muslim recoils from the Trinity, he is not being obtuse. He is defending what he takes to be the most important truth there is. Any Christian who wants to be heard must begin by honoring that instinct, not mocking it.

And yet the disagreement is real, and it is not merely a misunderstanding to be cleared up with a clever phrase. This essay tries to do two things at once, both demanded by 1 Peter 3:15—to answer “with gentleness and respect.” First, to state the Muslim objections to the Trinity at full strength, in the words of the Qur'an and of mainstream Islamic theology, without caricature. Second, to show what orthodox Christianity actually claims—and, crucially, what it does not claim—so that the real doctrine, not a phantom, is the thing under discussion. Some of the Muslim objections, it turns out, land on a heresy that the Church condemns as firmly as Islam does. One of them, the deepest, lands on Nicaea itself, and that one we must take seriously to the end.

The Charge: Tawhid and the Sin of Shirk

At the center of Islam stands tawhid—the absolute, indivisible oneness of God. God is one in essence, one in will, one in worship; He has no peer, no partner, no offspring, no internal composition. The gravest sin a person can commit, in Islamic theology, is shirk: associating anything with God, setting up a partner alongside the Creator or ascribing divinity to a creature. The Qur'an warns that God may forgive any sin but this one: “Indeed, Allah does not forgive association with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills” (Surah 4:48, Sahih International).1 Shirk is, in a sense, the photographic negative of the whole faith—the one thing Islam exists to abolish.

It is from this conviction, and not from mere stubbornness, that the Qur'an rejects the Trinity. To many Muslims the doctrine reads as a textbook case of shirk: three divine beings worshiped where there should be one, a man (Jesus) and even a woman (Mary) exalted to the rank of God. The objection deserves to be put in its strongest form before any reply, because a refutation aimed at a weak version persuades no one who actually holds the strong one.

The Three Qur'anic Texts

Three passages do most of the work in the Islamic case, and they should be read in full.

The first is Surah 4:171. Addressing Christians directly, it says: “O People of the Scripture, do not commit excess in your religion…The Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, was but a messenger of Allah and His word which He directed to Mary and a soul [created at a command] from Him. So believe in Allah and His messengers. And do not say, ‘Three’; desist—it is better for you. Indeed, Allah is but one God. Exalted is He above having a son.”2 Here is the explicit command—do not say ‘Three’—set against the bare affirmation that God is one.

The second is Surah 5:73: “They have certainly disbelieved who say, ‘Allah is the third of three.’ And there is no god except one God.”3 And alongside it the most revealing verse of all, Surah 5:116, in which God questions Jesus on the Day of Judgment: “And [beware the Day] when Allah will say, ‘O Jesus, Son of Mary, did you say to the people, Take me and my mother as deities besides Allah?’”4 The triad named here is unmistakable: God, Jesus, and Mary.

The third cluster concerns sonship. Surah 19:35, in the chapter named for Mary, declares: “It is not [befitting] for Allah to take a son; exalted is He! When He decrees an affair, He only says to it, ‘Be,’ and it is.”5 And the great creed of divine unity, Surah 112 (al-Ikhlas), just four lines long and beloved by every Muslim child: “Say, He is Allah, [who is] One, Allah, the Eternal Refuge. He neither begets nor is born, nor is there to Him any equivalent.”6 To “beget” or “be begotten”—to have a walad, an offspring—is, the Qur'an insists, beneath the dignity of the Creator.

What Christians Do Not Claim

Now the first and most important clarification. A good part of the apparent conflict dissolves once we see precisely which doctrine the Qur'an is describing—and that on its surface, in at least one decisive verse, it is not the Christian doctrine at all.

Surah 5:116 names the rejected trinity as God, Jesus, and Mary. But no Christian creed, council, or catechism has ever placed Mary in the Godhead. There is no branch of Christianity—not Catholic, not Orthodox, not Protestant—in which Mary is a person of the Trinity. The honor paid to Mary in the older traditions is the honor of a creature, the mother of the incarnate Lord; it is categorically not worship of her as God. So when God in the Qur'an asks Jesus whether he told people to take “me and my mother as deities besides Allah,” the honest Christian answer is an emphatic no—and we condemn that idea as you do. Whatever Surah 5:116 is rejecting, it is not the doctrine the Church confesses.

The same goes for the “three gods” framing of 5:73, “the third of three.” If the Trinity meant three separate deities—three centers of divine power standing side by side—Christians would be tritheists, and tritheism is a heresy the Church has anathematized for nearly two thousand years. The Athanasian Creed, the most explicit ancient statement of the doctrine, hammers the point: “So the Father is God, the Son is God, and the Holy Spirit is God; and yet they are not three Gods, but one God.”7 The Christian does not believe in three gods. He believes in one God. On that, tawhid and the Creed agree.

Here a careful word about Islamic intellectual history is owed, because charity requires precision about why the Qur'an describes the doctrine this way. Many Western scholars—and a fair number of Muslim ones—hold that the seventh-century Arabian environment contained Christian groups whose popular devotion to Mary had drifted toward something like cultic veneration; the so-called Collyridians, described much earlier by Epiphanius, are sometimes invoked as background.8 On that reading the Qur'an may be correcting a genuine folk distortion it encountered, not inventing a strawman. The identification is debated and far from certain. But it matters for tone: the Christian should not say the Qur'an is simply ignorant. He should say that whatever distortion it addresses, the Nicene faith is innocent of it—and then move the conversation to the doctrine the Church actually holds.

What Christians Do Claim

So what is the doctrine? The Nicene Creed (325, expanded 381) confesses one God, the Father Almighty; one Lord Jesus Christ, “the only-begotten Son of God, begotten of the Father before all worlds…begotten, not made, being of one substance (homoousios) with the Father”; and the Holy Spirit, “the Lord, the giver of life.” Three persons (Greek hypostaseis), one being (ousia). Not three gods who happen to cooperate, and not one God wearing three masks in turn (the ancient error of modalism, also condemned). The doctrine threads a needle: God is numerically one in essence and eternally three in personal relation.

This is not, the Christian must insist, the association of a creature with the Creator. Shirk, as the Qur'an describes it, is the elevation of something outside God—an idol, a prophet, a saint—to a rank alongside God.9 The Trinity adds nothing to God from outside. It does not place a second being beside Him. It is a claim about what the one God eternally is in His own inner life: not a bare, solitary monad, but an eternal communion of Father, Son, and Spirit in a single undivided essence. Whether that claim is true is the live question. But it is a claim about God's interior life, not about adding partners—and that distinction is the hinge of the whole conversation.

It is worth adding that the earliest Christians did not arrive at this by importing Greek philosophy onto a simple Jewish monotheism, as is sometimes alleged. The New Testament scholar Richard Bauckham has argued at length that the highest possible Christology—the inclusion of Jesus within the unique identity of the one God of Israel—was present in the church before any New Testament book was written, expressed in the categories of Second Temple Jewish monotheism itself.10 The first worshipers of Jesus were strict Jewish monotheists who recited the Shema (“the Lord our God, the Lord is one,” Deuteronomy 6:4). They were not careless about God's oneness. They came to believe that the one God's identity included the Son.

“How Can God Have a Son?” The walad Question

Now to the sonship objection, which is sharper than it first appears and turns partly on the Arabic. The Qur'an's denial that God has a walad (Surah 112:3; 19:35) is a denial of physical, biological offspring—a son produced through procreation, as a consort might bear a child. On that point the Christian agrees without reservation. The idea that God took a wife and fathered a child is pagan, blasphemous, and utterly foreign to the Gospel. If the doctrine of the Son meant that, it would deserve the Qur'an's rebuke.

Here the Arabic distinction, noted by Christian Arabophone tradition for centuries, does real work. Arabic has two words. Walad connotes a biological child, the fruit of procreation. Ibn can be used far more broadly and figuratively. Tellingly, Arabic-speaking Christians have historically never called Christ walad Allah—which would imply literal biological sonship—but ibn Allah, “Son of God” in the relational, non-physical sense.11 When the Qur'an denies that God has a walad, it denies something Christians also deny.

What the Creed means by “begotten” is something else entirely: eternal generation. The Son is “begotten, not made”—not created in time, not the product of any act, but eternally derived from the Father as light is derived from a source, sharing the one undivided essence. Athanasius, the doctrine's great fourth-century defender, insisted precisely that this generation is timeless and non-physical: the Son is eternally from the Father, never a creature, never originating at a moment.12 “There was never a time when the Son was not” was the Nicene answer to Arius. The begetting is an eternal relation within the Godhead, not an event in a divine biography. So the question “how can God have a son?” rests, charitably understood, on a picture of sonship—biological, temporal, the bringing of a new being into existence—that Nicene Christianity also rejects.

The Strongest Objection, Stated at Full Strength

A thoughtful Muslim will not be satisfied, and rightly so, because the replies above clear away the misdescriptions without yet touching the real disagreement. The best Muslim objection does not depend on the Mary-triad of 5:116 or on a crude biological reading of sonship. It presses past all of that to the doctrine itself.

Put in its strongest form, by a Muslim who has understood Nicaea exactly: Grant everything. Grant that you do not worship Mary. Grant that you mean one essence and three persons, not three gods. Grant that “begotten” means eternal generation, not procreation. You still call a man—Jesus of Nazareth, who ate and wept and died—God. You still introduce real distinction into the One whose very perfection is to be undivided and simple. The deepest meaning of tawhid is not arithmetic; it is that God is utterly unique, without internal composition, beyond all the categories of creaturely multiplicity. The Trinity, however carefully you state it, makes the Absolute complex and ascribes deity to a creature. That is shirk in its most refined and dangerous form—all the more dangerous for being so subtle.

This is the objection that must be answered, and it should not be answered glibly. It is held by serious people—classical theologians in the tradition of al-Ghazali and the Ash'ari school, and contemporary Muslim philosophers—and it rests on a genuine and weighty intuition about divine transcendence. The critic gets the last word before the reply, and that word is: your refined Trinity is still a creature called God, and the One cannot be complex.

The Reply—and an Honest Concession

Two things should be said in response, the first conceding more than apologists sometimes do.

First, the concession. The Muslim is right that this is the real disagreement, and right that it is not a mere confusion. Whether the divine unity can be triune—whether perfect oneness is compatible with real internal relation—is a genuine metaphysical dispute, argued by able philosophers on both sides, and it is not settled by quoting a verse or a creed. The Christian who pretends the Muslim objection is simply a misunderstanding has stopped listening. There is a hard question here, and it deserves the name.

Second, the reply. The Muslim objection assumes that divine oneness must mean absolute, undifferentiated simplicity—that any internal distinction is automatically a division, and any division a denial of unity. But that assumption is exactly the point in dispute; it is asserted by tawhid, not demonstrated by it. To quote “God is one” against the Trinity is to presuppose that “one” excludes triune relation—which is the very thing the Christian denies. And here, interestingly, Islam's own theological history shows the question is harder than the slogan suggests. The classical Sunni schools wrestled for centuries over God's attributes—His knowledge, will, power, and above all His Word. Are these eternal realities identical to the divine essence, or distinct from it? The dominant Ash'ari answer was that the attributes are eternal and subsist in God, “not He, nor other than He.”13 The disanalogy must be conceded at once, and it is real: these attributes are not persons—hypostases who know, will, and love one another—and they cannot become incarnate; so the parallel is partial, and it is not on its own evidence for three persons. The narrow point it carries is this and no more: that the thesis “the divine One admits no internal distinction whatever” is itself contested within Islamic theology rather than a self-evident axiom—for the Ash'aris allowed real, eternal distinctions of some kind within the one undivided God. Once that much is granted, the bare claim that “the One can have no internal distinction at all” is no longer Islam's settled possession; it is a contested premise.

This does not prove the Trinity. The Christian should be candid about scope: the parallel with the divine attributes shows that pure, distinction-free simplicity is not an undisputed axiom even within Islam; it does not, by itself, establish that God is three persons. That further claim rests on revelation and on the historical case for who Jesus was—on whether the carpenter from Nazareth said and did what only God can say and do, and was vindicated by resurrection. Those are separate arguments, argued on their own ground. What the present argument achieves is narrower and still worth a great deal: it moves the conversation off the caricature—three gods, a deified Mary, a procreated child—and onto the doctrine the Church actually confesses, where the disagreement is honest and joined on its real merits.

The State of the Question

Where does the live debate stand? On the textual side, scholars of the Qur'an broadly agree that Surah 5:116 names a God-Jesus-Mary triad that does not match Nicene orthodoxy, though they differ on whether the verse rebuts a real heterodox group or functions rhetorically; the Notre Dame Islamicist Gabriel Said Reynolds and others have explored how the Qur'an's Christology engages the Christianities of late antiquity, not a textbook Nicene definition.14 On the theological side, the deep disagreement is exactly where it has stood for thirteen centuries: a real, unresolved dispute about whether divine unity can be internally relational. Able Muslim philosophers continue to argue that it cannot; able Christian philosophers—in the analytic-theology revival of recent decades—argue that coherent models of the Trinity are available. Neither side has produced a knock-down proof, and honesty requires saying so. This is not a debate the Christian wins by volume or by scoring points off the Qur'an. It is one to be had slowly, with the texts open and the heart engaged.

Conclusion: What This Argument Does and Does Not Establish

It is worth being exact about what has been shown. This essay does not prove the Trinity true; that case rests finally on the identity and resurrection of Jesus, argued elsewhere. It does not show that Muslims are foolish or insincere—the love for God's oneness that animates the objection is something Christians share and should honor. And it does not dissolve the deepest Muslim worry, which is a serious metaphysical conviction about divine transcendence, not a mere confusion.

What it does establish is twofold. First, that several of the Qur'an's explicit anti-Trinitarian statements—the God-Jesus-Mary triad of 5:116, the “three gods” of 5:73, the denial of a procreated walad—describe a doctrine the Church condemns as heresy, not the doctrine it confesses. On those points the Christian can say, gladly and without evasion, I agree with you: that would be false, and I reject it too. Second, that the one objection which does reach Nicaea—that the One cannot be internally distinct—rests on a premise that is contested rather than proven, and contested even within Islam's own debate over the divine attributes—so it does not, as it stands, refute the Trinity. That is not victory. Frequently asked questions

Isn't the Trinity a form of shirk, associating partners with God?

The Trinity adds nothing to God from outside and places no second being beside him. Doesn't the Qur'an refute the Trinity when it condemns worshiping God, Jesus, and Mary?
