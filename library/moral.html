The Moral Argument for God: Where Do Right and Wrong Come From?

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Through 1943 and 1944, a young Lithuanian Jew named Avraham Tory kept a secret diary in the Kovno ghetto — later buried in crates beneath an unfinished building and recovered after the war — recording what the Nazis were doing to his people. He did not write that the murders violated a German statute or offended his preferences. He wrote as though something had been broken in the fabric of the world itself — as though the killing of innocents was wrong whether or not anyone agreed. Most of us read such testimony and feel the same thing. We do not think the Holocaust was wrong for the Allies and acceptable for the SS. We think it was simply, objectively wrong.

That intuition is the engine of the moral argument for God's existence. The argument does not claim that unbelievers are wicked or that one must read Scripture to know that cruelty is evil. It asks a narrower and stranger question: if such moral facts are real — true independently of anyone's opinion — what kind of universe must we live in for that to be so?

The Argument and Its History

In its most widely circulated form, associated with the philosopher and apologist William Lane Craig, the argument is a tidy syllogism:

1. If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist.
2. Objective moral values and duties do exist.
3. Therefore, God exists.1

It is valid: if both premises are true, the conclusion follows. Everything rides on the premises. Craig draws a useful distinction. Values concern what is good or bad; duties concern what is right or wrong — what we are obligated to do. And by objective he means, precisely, independent of human opinion.2 The claim is not that moral views vary across cultures (they do), but that beneath the variation some things are really, mind-independently wrong.

The argument is old. Immanuel Kant, in the Critique of Practical Reason (1788), argued that practical reason commits us to the "highest good" — perfect virtue crowned with proportionate happiness — and that since we are obligated to pursue it, we must postulate God and immortality as the conditions that make its realization possible. Kant was careful: he did not claim God is the basis of moral obligation, which for him rested on the autonomy of reason itself.3 The contemporary argument is bolder than Kant's, and its most sophisticated grounding comes not from Craig the popularizer but from the analytic philosopher Robert Merrihew Adams, whose 1999 book Finite and Infinite Goods is the scholarly anchor of the whole project.4

Premise 2: That Some Things Are Really Wrong

Begin with the second premise, which is in some ways the easier. Do objective moral values and duties exist?

Most people, on reflection, are far more certain that torturing a child for fun is wrong than they are of any philosophical theory that might be marshaled to deny it. This is the method of reflective intuition: just as we trust our sensory experience of the physical world absent a defeater, we are within our rights to trust our moral experience absent a defeater. We apprehend the wrongness of the Holocaust the way we apprehend the reality of the past — not by argument but by a basic, non-inferential moral perception.

This is not a uniquely religious claim. It is shared by a large camp of secular moral realists — philosophers like Erik Wielenberg, Russ Shafer-Landau, and David Enoch — who agree wholeheartedly that some acts are objectively evil. So premise 2 is, if anything, common ground. The interesting fight is over premise 1: granting that morality is objective, what grounds it?

Premise 1: What Grounds the Moral Law?

Here we must be scrupulously clear, because this is where the argument is most often caricatured. The claim is not that atheists cannot be good, cannot know right from wrong, or lack moral sincerity. They plainly can and do — often putting believers to shame. The claim is about ontology: about what, at the deepest level, makes moral facts true. An atheist can read the moral law as fluently as anyone; the question is who, if anyone, wrote it.

The theist's answer comes in two layers. Values are grounded in God's own nature: God is not a being who happens to be good but Goodness itself — the standard, "the Good" in person, after which all finite goods are patterned. Duties arise from the commands that flow from that nature. This is the heart of Adams's modified divine command theory. In his 1973 essay "A Modified Divine Command Theory of Ethical Wrongness" and again in "Divine Command Metaethics Modified Again" (1979), Adams argued that moral wrongness consists in being contrary to the commands of a loving God — not the commands of just any conceivable deity.5 In Finite and Infinite Goods he developed the value side: finite things are good insofar as they resemble the infinite Good, which is God.6 On this picture, the moral order is neither an accident nor an invention but a reflection of the character of the One who is.

The Euthyphro Dilemma

No discussion is honest without Plato's ancient trap. In the Euthyphro, Socrates asks: is the holy loved by the gods because it is holy, or holy because the gods love it? Translated to monotheism: Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?

Both horns wound. Grab the first — good because commanded — and morality looks arbitrary: had God commanded gratuitous cruelty, cruelty would be good, which seems monstrous. Grab the second — commanded because good — and goodness is a standard above God, to which even He answers; God becomes a moral middleman, and the atheist's question returns: why not skip God and go straight to the standard?

The standard theistic reply, sharpened by Adams and others, is that the dilemma is false because it omits a third option. God neither invents the good by fiat nor consults a standard outside Himself. God's own necessary nature is the Good. His commands are not arbitrary, because they necessarily express His character — He cannot command gratuitous cruelty, not because a higher law forbids it, but because such a command would contradict what He essentially is. And the good is not independent of God, because it just is God's nature. The dilemma assumes the standard must be either God's will or something external; the theist locates it in God's being.7

This is a strong reply, and it is the standard move among theists. But it is not cost-free, and honesty requires saying so. Critics such as Wes Morriston press that "God's nature is the Good" may quietly relocate rather than dissolve the dilemma: why is God's nature good? If the answer is "because being loving, just, and so on is good," then goodness seems definable apart from God after all; if the answer is "because it is God's nature," the charge of arbitrariness creeps back in.8 The theist can respond that "good" and "God's nature" are necessarily, not contingently, identical — like "water" and "H₂O" — so the question "why is God's nature good?" is as confused as "why is water H₂O?" The exchange continues; it is live, not settled.

The Strongest Objection: Wielenberg's Godless Normative Realism

The most formidable secular challenge today comes from Erik Wielenberg of DePauw University, who debated Craig in 2018. In Robust Ethics: The Metaphysics and Epistemology of Godless Normative Realism (Oxford, 2014), Wielenberg argues that objective moral truths exist as brute, necessary, non-natural facts — they require no foundation outside themselves, God included.9 Moral properties, on his view, are objective, non-natural, irreducible, and causally inert; that "torturing the innocent for fun is wrong" is simply a necessary truth, true in every possible world, full stop.

Let us steelman this. Wielenberg presses a tu quoque the theist must feel. The theist says, "moral values are grounded in God's nature, and that's where explanation stops." Wielenberg says, "moral values are grounded in necessary moral facts, and that's where explanation stops." Both bottom out in something brute and unexplained. If the theist may help himself to God as a necessary stopping point, why may the atheist not help himself to moral facts as necessary stopping points? Indeed, Wielenberg argues, his view is more economical — one fewer entity.10

The theist's honest reply is twofold. First, on parsimony: a single personal ground (God) that unifies values, duties, moral knowledge, and the obligatory force of morality may explain more than a sprawling plurality of free-floating ethical facts — and explanatory power, not bare entity-counting, is the right measure. Second, and more pointedly, it is hard to see how an impersonal, causally inert fact could generate a genuine obligation or accountability. Duties seem to be owed to someone; commands require a commander. Wielenberg's reply — that obligations can be grounded in the value of persons without a commander — is serious, and the theist must concede that he has a coherent account. What the theist claims is not that Wielenberg's view is incoherent but that it is less explanatorily satisfying: it asks us to accept obligations that float, owed to no one. Reasonable philosophers disagree about whether that cost is fatal. This is the honest state of the debate, and Wielenberg gets the better of the parsimony point more often than apologists admit.

The Darwinian Dilemma — Which Cuts Both Ways

A second objection targets premise 2 by attacking our access to moral truth. In her landmark paper "A Darwinian Dilemma for Realist Theories of Value" (Philosophical Studies 127, 2006, pp. 109–166), Sharon Street argues that our moral intuitions were shaped by natural selection to promote reproductive fitness, not to track mind-independent moral truth.11 If so, the realist faces a dilemma: either deny that evolution shaped our values (empirically untenable) or admit it did, in which case the staggering coincidence that fitness-tracking intuitions also happen to track moral reality cries out for an explanation the realist cannot give. Our confident sense that the Holocaust was evil might be just a well-engineered survival heuristic.

Two things must be said. First — and this is crucial — Street's argument is not an argument for theism; she is herself an anti-realist, and her dagger is aimed at secular moral realism above all. It therefore wounds Wielenberg at least as badly as it wounds anyone, since his causally inert moral facts cannot have shaped our evolving brains. Second, the theist has a reply ready to hand: a God who designed our cognitive faculties to apprehend the moral order He authored gives a non-coincidental reason why our intuitions track truth. (Wielenberg himself answers Street with a "third-factor" account, on which our faculties both track moral facts and help explain why those facts are instantiated; so the honest claim is not that no secular reply exists, but that a designing mind explains the fit more economically.) That is a point in theism's favor. But candor demands the concession that the theist who leans on this reply has not proved moral realism; he has shown that if realism is true, theism explains our access to it better than naturalism does. The realism itself still rests on the intuition of premise 2 — an intuition the debunker has at least dented.

What the Argument Does, and Does Not, Establish

Suppose the argument succeeds. What has been shown? Not the Trinity, not the resurrection, not the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob in any detail. The conclusion is thinner: there exists a transcendent ground of objective morality — a moral lawgiver whose nature is the Good. That is a long way from the full Christian God, and the careful defender says so plainly. The moral argument is one strand in a cumulative case; it points toward a personal, good, necessary being, and leaves to other arguments — cosmological, historical, revelational — the work of filling in the portrait.

Nor does the argument impugn the goodness of unbelievers. Let it be said once more, emphatically: atheists can be morally excellent, morally wise, and morally sincere. The question was never whether one can be good without believing in God, but what makes goodness real for believer and unbeliever alike. A child can read by sunlight without knowing what the sun is.

What remains, after every concession, is the original intuition — and its strange weight. When we say the murder of innocents is wrong, we mean it is wrong in a way that does not bend to opinion, culture, or power. The theist offers an account of why that should be so: because reality is, at its root, personal and good. The secular realist offers a rival account: because some truths simply are. Between these the argument does not coerce; it invites. As 1 Peter 3:15 counsels, the invitation is best extended "with gentleness and respect" — confident that the One who is the Good has nothing to fear from an honest question.
