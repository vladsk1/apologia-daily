Wahy vs Incarnation: Two Different Ideas of How God Reaches Us

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Begin with a single question that both Muslims and Christians care about more than almost any other: how does God reach us? Not whether he exists—both faiths take that as settled—but what he is willing to do across the immense distance between the eternal Creator and his small, mortal, often wayward creatures. How does the gap get crossed? Islam and Christianity give two of the most beautiful and serious answers human beings have ever offered, and they are not the same answer. One says: God sends down a book. The other says: God comes down himself.

This essay is not a polemic. It is a comparison made in the spirit of 1 Peter 3:15—“with gentleness and respect”—and it tries to do something harder than scoring a point. It tries to state the Islamic vision of revelation so fairly that a thoughtful Muslim would recognize it and nod, to honor its real strengths, and then to lay the Christian vision beside it so that the contrast can be felt rather than merely argued. The aim is not to defeat Islam but to show what Christianity is actually claiming—which is, as we will see, far more than most people on either side assume.

Wahy: God Speaks From Behind a Veil

In Islamic theology, the act by which God communicates with humanity is called wahy—revelation, the “sending down” of guidance to chosen prophets. The Qur'an itself describes the modes available to God with striking precision: “And it is not for any human being that Allah should speak to him except by revelation or from behind a partition or that He sends a messenger to reveal, by His permission, what He wills” (Surah 42:51, Sahih International).1 Notice the architecture of that verse. God may speak by inspiration, or from behind a veil, or through a sent messenger—the angel. What God does not do, in this account, is appear in person. The veil is not an accident; it is intrinsic to how the Qur'an pictures divine address.

The concrete form wahy takes is tanzil—literally a “sending down.” The mainstream Sunni picture is layered and deliberate: the divine speech, eternally inscribed on the heavenly “Preserved Tablet” (al-Lawh al-Mahfuz, Surah 85:22),2 is conveyed by the angel Gabriel to the Prophet Muhammad, who recites it; and the recited Arabic Qur'an is that very speech of God brought to earth. The word qur'an means “recitation.” This is why, in Islam, the Qur'an is not primarily a book to be skimmed but a sound to be received—memorized, chanted, carried in the body. The Islamicist Daniel Madigan has shown how acutely the Qur'an is aware of its own status as God's authoritative “writing” and address, his self-communication to a listening community.3

What does wahy give the believer? A great deal. It gives clear commands and a whole way of life; warnings and promises; a map of the world’s meaning and the soul’s destiny. Above all it gives the believer the actual words of God—not a human report about God, but, on the Muslim view, the very speech of the Creator on human lips. To open the Qur'an is, for a Muslim, to hear God speak.

The Real Strength of the Islamic Model

It would be a failure of both honesty and charity to rush past how much this model has going for it. Three strengths in particular deserve to be named plainly, because they are the reasons a sincere person finds the Islamic vision compelling.

First, it guards the transcendence of God without compromise. The God of Islam is utterly beyond the creation—no image can capture him, no creature can be confused with him, nothing can be “associated” with him. The deepest instinct of tawhid, God's absolute oneness, is that the Creator must never be dragged down into the category of created things. Wahy honors that instinct perfectly: God reaches us without ever ceasing to be wholly other. The veil stays intact.

Second, it provides a stable, verifiable text. A message in words can be memorized, recited, and—Muslims believe—preserved letter for letter. There is something clean and reassuring about a revelation you can hold and check, as against a life that must be interpreted. (Whether the Qur'an's transmission is in fact pristine is a separate historical question, examined in our essay on Qur'anic preservation; here we are describing the model on its own terms, as it understands itself.)

Third, and most importantly, it appears to keep monotheism simple and pure. There is no incarnation to explain, no eternal Son to reconcile with God's oneness, no apparent “partner” raised beside the Creator. The line between God and everything else is bright and unbroken. To a mind that prizes that clarity above all, the Christian doctrine of God-made-flesh can look not like good news but like a contamination of the one truth that matters. This is a serious conviction held by serious people, and any Christian who wants to be heard must feel its force before answering it.

Incarnation: God Crosses the Veil in Person

Now lay the Christian vision beside it. Christianity affirms everything the Muslim affirms about God's transcendence—the Creator is infinitely beyond us, holy, uncontainable. And then it says something that, on first hearing, sounds almost scandalous: this transcendent God did not stay behind the veil. “In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God…. And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory” (John 1:1, 14, ESV).4 The eternal Word—God's own self-expression—did not merely send a message across the gap. He became a man and crossed it in person.

The structural difference could hardly be sharper. In the Islamic model, what crosses the distance between God and humanity is words: information, commands, guidance. In the Christian model, what crosses the distance is God himself: a life, a face, a presence. The name the Gospel of Matthew gives the child of Bethlehem is Immanuel—“God with us” (Matthew 1:23). When Philip asks Jesus to show the disciples the Father, the answer reframes the whole question of revelation: “Whoever has seen me has seen the Father” (John 14:9).

This yields a revelation of a different kind, not merely a different degree. A book can convey God's will—his statutes, his preferences, his demands. But only a life can reveal God's character: how he responds to a leper, what he does at the grave of a friend, how he treats the woman caught in adultery, what he says to those crucifying him. You cannot learn mercy from a legal code the way you learn it from watching mercy enacted. If the deepest thing you want to know is not what God commands but what God is like, then—the Christian claims—a person reveals more than any text could.

The Word of God: One Title, Two Homes

Here the comparison becomes genuinely illuminating rather than merely oppositional, and it turns on a striking observation made by the comparative-religion scholar Wilfred Cantwell Smith. Christians, Smith argued, instinctively misread Islam when they treat the Qur'an as the Muslim equivalent of the Bible. Once that is seen, a shared vocabulary comes into focus—though, as we will see, the two traditions mean different things by it. Both faiths confess a “Word of God” that comes down to us. For Islam, that Word is the Qur'an. For Christianity, that Word is Jesus (John 1:1). And—this is the part that opens a real conversation—the Qur'an itself applies the title “Word” not to a book but to Jesus: “The Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, was but a messenger of Allah and His word which He directed to Mary and a soul [created at a command] from Him” (Surah 4:171, Sahih International).6 The Qur'an gives Jesus a title—kalima, “a Word from God,” along with “a spirit from Him”—that it gives to no other prophet.

Intimacy, Knowability, and Salvation

The two models imply two different relationships, and it is worth drawing the difference out without caricature. In the Islamic vision, the believer is preeminently 'abd—a servant, even a “slave,” of God—and this is a title of honor, not degradation; the highest dignity is faithful submission (islam) to the Lord of the worlds. The relationship is real and warm in its own register: gratitude, obedience, reverent love. But it is mediated by a text, and it preserves a certain distance. God gives his will; he does not give himself.

The Christian vision claims something more intimate, and stakes a great deal on it. Because the Word came in person and dealt with sin at the cross, the relationship offered is reframed from servanthood to sonship: “you have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, ‘Abba! Father!’” (Romans 8:15, ESV).7 The God who once spoke “from behind a partition” is, on the Christian account, the God who came out from behind it—and who now dwells, by his Spirit, within his people. Knowability and intimacy are not add-ons to this model; they are its point.

And here the difference touches salvation itself. A God who sends a book can show the way home. A God who comes in person can carry us home. The cross, in Christian theology, is not God instructing us about the road from a safe distance; it is God walking the road himself and bearing its cost in his own body. That is why the Incarnation and the atonement belong together: a revelation that stops at information can tell you what to do, but a revelation that becomes flesh can do for you what you cannot do for yourself.

The Strongest Muslim Objection, at Full Strength

The deepest Muslim response to all of this is not a quibble; it is a profound theological conviction, and it must be stated at full strength. The objection is that Incarnation is beneath the dignity of God. For the transcendent Creator to enter flesh—to hunger, tire, weep, bleed, and die—is, on this view, not a glory but a degradation, a category error that drags the Uncreated into the mud of the created and so violates the very oneness and majesty that make God God. The Qur'an's instinct is exactly here: “It is not [befitting] for Allah to take a son; exalted is He!” (Surah 19:35).8 Wahy, on this reading, is not a poorer way of reaching us than Incarnation; it is a purer one, precisely because it reaches us without compromising who God is. To many Muslims, the Christian claim sounds like loving God so much that you forget how great he is.

This objection has been pressed by Muslim theologians for fourteen centuries, and it is sincerely felt by ordinary believers today. It deserves the last word before any reply—so let it stand in its full force: if God is truly transcendent, would he not reach us in the way that guards his transcendence rather than the way that risks it? Is not a God who keeps the veil more worthy of worship than a God who tears it?

The Christian Reply

The reply does not deny the premise that God is transcendent; it denies the assumption hidden inside the objection—that condescension diminishes God. The Christian claim is the reverse: a love willing to stoop is greater, not lesser, than a love that stays aloof. Consider a king who, hearing that his people are dying in a plague district, could send a perfect proclamation of the cure from the safety of his palace—or could walk into the district himself, touch the sick, and bear the danger in his own person. The second king has not lowered his kingship; he has revealed how high it truly is. Greatness measured by distance is a lesser greatness than greatness measured by love. The question the Incarnation poses is not “would a dignified God do this?” but “what is the highest love capable of?”

There is also a concession the Christian should make honestly. The Muslim is right that the Incarnation, if it happened, is the most astonishing thing imaginable—and right that it cannot simply be assumed from the elegance of the idea. Whether the eternal Word actually became flesh is not settled by which model of revelation is more beautiful. It rests on a historical question: did Jesus claim and demonstrate such a thing, and did God vindicate the claim by raising him? That case stands on its own evidence—the resurrection, Jesus' own words and deeds—and is argued elsewhere in this library, not here.9 This essay shows what is at stake between the two visions; it does not, by itself, prove the Christian one true.

What the Christian reply does establish is narrower and still significant: that the objection “Incarnation is beneath God” is not self-evident—it depends on a particular theory of what divine greatness consists in, and the Christian offers a rival theory in which self-giving love is the supreme divine perfection. A God whose holiness is so secure that he can enter suffering without being diminished by it is, the Christian claims, more transcendent, not less.

The State of the Conversation

Among scholars of comparative religion, Smith's insight—that the Qur'an, not the Bible, is the true analogue of Christ—is widely accepted as a clarifying frame, and it has reshaped how thoughtful Christians and Muslims talk to one another.5 It does not by itself adjudicate which faith is true; it simply ensures the two are compared at the right level. The most fruitful Christian-Muslim engagement of the last century—associated with figures such as the Anglican bishop and Islamicist Kenneth Cragg—has worked along exactly this seam, taking the Qur'an's own honoring of Jesus as “a Word from God” as an invitation rather than an embarrassment.10

Two honest caveats keep the comparison fair. First, this is a contrast that clarifies and commends; it is not a proof that Islam is false. A Muslim can fully understand the Christian vision and still, in good conscience, prefer the model in which God reaches us without crossing the veil. Presenting “Incarnation is more profound” as a refutation would overstate the case and, worse, fail the charity the subject demands. Second, the Christian must resist any temptation to flatten Islam into mere “distance and law.” Muslim devotion knows real warmth, real love of God, real intimacy in prayer—the Sufi tradition above all. The contrast drawn here is structural and theological, not a claim that Muslims do not love God or are not loved by him.

Conclusion: Two Loves, and What This Essay Settles

So return to the question we began with: how does God reach us? Islam answers with a vision of awesome purity—the transcendent One sends down his Word as a book, guarding his majesty, giving his will, calling his servants home. Christianity answers with a vision of awesome nearness—the transcendent One sends down his Word as a person, crossing the veil, sharing our flesh and our suffering, calling not servants but children home. Both are answers given in love. The honest Christian does not say the first is loveless; he says the second is love taken to its furthest possible length.

What this essay establishes is modest and real: that the difference between wahy and Incarnation is not a quarrel about which book is correct, but a difference about what God is willing to do—and that the Christian claim, far from being a lesser or corrupted monotheism, is the claim that God's transcendence is so secure he could enter our condition without losing himself in it. What this essay does not establish is that the Incarnation actually occurred; that turns on history, on Jesus' claims and his resurrection, and the conversation continues there. But it has, perhaps, shown the size of the question. The Muslim and the Christian are not arguing over a detail. They are asking whether the God who is great enough to stay behind the veil might also be loving enough to come out from behind it—and that is a question worth asking together, gently, to the end.
