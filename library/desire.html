The Argument from Desire: Does Our Deepest Longing Point to God?

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

It usually arrives unbidden. A bar of music, a smell of woodsmoke on an autumn evening, the sight of distant hills under a clearing sky—and for a moment the heart leaps toward something it cannot name. The feeling is not quite happiness; it carries an ache. And the strange thing is that no object in the scene actually contains what the heart reached for. We turn back to the music or the hills, and they are only music and hills. The longing has already moved on, pointing past them toward a country we have never visited.

C. S. Lewis spent much of his life chasing that experience. He gave it a name—Joy—and built around it one of the most beloved, and most disputed, arguments for God in the modern apologetic tradition. It is not a proof in the manner of a geometry theorem. It is something humbler and, to many readers, more haunting: an argument that our restlessness is itself a clue.

The argument stated

In Mere Christianity, in a chapter titled "Hope," Lewis gives the argument its most famous compression. "Creatures are not born with desires unless satisfaction for those desires exists. A baby feels hunger: well, there is such a thing as food. A duckling wants to swim: well, there is such a thing as water. Men feel sexual desire: well, there is such a thing as sex. If I find in myself a desire which no experience in this world can satisfy, the most probable explanation is that I was made for another world."1

Notice the careful hedging. Lewis does not say "therefore God exists." He says "the most probable explanation." The argument is offered as an inference to the best explanation, not a demonstration. The philosopher Peter Kreeft later tightened it into a syllogism. Major premise: "Every natural, innate desire in us corresponds to some real object that can satisfy that desire." Minor premise: "But there exists in us a desire which nothing in time, nothing on earth, no creature can satisfy." Conclusion: Therefore there exists something beyond time and earth that can satisfy it—"this something is what people call God and life with God forever."2

Everything turns on a distinction Kreeft draws sharply: between natural (or innate) desires and artificial (or externally conditioned) ones. Natural desires—hunger, thirst, sexual longing, the wish for knowledge, for friendship, for beauty—arise from within us, appear universally across the species, and reliably track real objects. Artificial desires—for a sports car, for political office, for the land of Oz—come from without, from advertising or fiction, and vary from person to person. Some of their objects exist; some do not. The claim is not that every wish has a real object, but that every natural one does. And then the empirical wager: "No one has ever found one case of an innate desire for a nonexistent object."3

The longing and its history

Lewis's own name for the desire was Joy, which he defined in his autobiography Surprised by Joy as "an unsatisfied desire which is itself more desirable than any other satisfaction."4 It is, he insisted, sharply distinct from both happiness and pleasure: a stab of longing that, paradoxically, we would not trade for any contentment the world offers. He explicitly linked it to the German Romantic concept of Sehnsucht—an "inconsolable longing," the wistful ache for a far-off and half-remembered home.

He gave it its most lyrical statement in a sermon, "The Weight of Glory," preached at the University Church of St Mary the Virgin, Oxford, on June 8, 1941. There he confessed a "certain shyness" in speaking of "this desire for our own far-off country," which he called "the inconsolable secret in each one of you—the secret which hurts so much that you take your revenge on it by calling it names like Nostalgia and Romanticism and Adolescence."5 The desire, he argued, is so easily mistaken for nostalgia or restlessness precisely because we keep aiming it at finite things—a place, a person, a moment—that cannot bear its weight.

The root of all this is far older than Lewis. It reaches back at least to Augustine, who opened his Confessions (written around 397–401) with a line that has echoed through sixteen centuries: "You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our heart is restless until it rests in you" (fecisti nos ad te et inquietum est cor nostrum donec requiescat in te).6 The Latin preposition ad—"toward"—captures the structure exactly: the heart is built with a direction, a tilt toward something it does not yet possess. The medievals systematized the intuition. Thomas Aquinas argued that "it is impossible for a natural desire to be empty, since nature does nothing in vain"—and that humanity's natural desire for perfect happiness must therefore be capable of fulfillment, in the vision of God.7 Lewis inherited a venerable tradition; his contribution was to clothe it in autobiography and Romantic feeling so that modern readers felt the ache before they were asked to weigh the logic.

Why the argument has force

The argument's strength is partly phenomenological. It begins not with an abstraction but with an experience nearly everyone can recognize once it is named. The defender does not have to prove that you ought to feel a transcendent longing; he need only point, and trust that you have already felt it. That is why the argument tends to move readers who are unmoved by cosmological or design arguments. It speaks to the inner life.

Its logical engine is the principle that natural kinds of desire are not free-floating. Evolution, the body, the mind—each produces appetites calibrated to a real environment. Eyes presuppose light; lungs presuppose air; hunger presupposes food. There is something genuinely odd, on this view, about a species-wide hunger pointed at nothing whatever—an appetite with no possible object anywhere in reality. If the longing for the transcendent is a natural desire of that universal, recurrent kind, then its having no real object would make it a strange exception to an otherwise reliable rule. The argument asks us to take that oddity seriously as evidence.

The objections, stated at full strength

Here the honest work begins, because the argument has drawn sharp and able critics, and they deserve their best case.

First, the logical gap. Wanting something does not make it exist. This is the most immediate objection, and it is devastating if the argument is read as a proof. People desire all manner of impossible things: to be immortal, to be young forever, to fly unaided, to find a flawless and undying lover, to undo the past. The desires are real; the objects are not. If desire reliably delivered its object, the world would be very different. So the bare inference—I long for X, therefore X exists—is plainly invalid. A defender must concede this at once and retreat to the narrower claim about natural desires. But that retreat is exactly where the second critic strikes.

Second, John Beversluis. In C. S. Lewis and the Search for Rational Religion (Eerdmans, 1985; revised, Prometheus, 2007)—still the only book-length philosophical critique of Lewis's apologetics—Beversluis mounts the most sustained attack on the argument from desire.8 His charges are several and pointed. He argues that the crucial distinction between "natural" and "artificial" desires is doing all the work but is never independently established; the categories are drawn precisely so that the inconvenient counterexamples (immortality, the perfect lover) get filed under "artificial." That risks circularity: a desire counts as natural only if it has a real object, which is the very thing to be proved. He presses a propositional content objection: if Joy is really a desire for some object, we ought to be able to say something about what that object is—yet Lewis insists Joy is precisely the longing whose object we cannot name or describe, which makes it doubtful that it is a desire "for" any determinate thing at all. And he detects an equivocation: the word "desire" slides between an ordinary appetite for an experienceable object (food, water) and a mysterious metaphysical yearning whose alleged object lies outside all possible experience; the analogy between them, on which the whole inference rests, is therefore weak. Beversluis concludes that none of Lewis's arguments succeed, this one included.

Third, the naturalistic account. A critic need not even attack the logic; he can simply offer a rival explanation of the longing. Evolutionary psychology supplies one: a restless, never-quite-satisfied disposition could be adaptive, driving organisms to keep striving, exploring, reproducing rather than settling into contented stagnation. The "ache for elsewhere" might be a byproduct of a large, story-telling, future-modeling brain that can imagine states better than any it will ever inhabit. On this view the longing is real and even universal, but it points to nothing transcendent; it is a feature of the kind of animal we are, fully explicable without God. The atheist philosopher, here, grants Lewis the experience and denies only his interpretation of it.

Replying—while conceding what must be conceded

The first objection should simply be granted. The argument from desire is not a proof, and any defender who treats it as one has overreached. Lewis himself said "the most probable explanation," not "the necessary conclusion." Properly understood, this is an abductive, cumulative-case consideration, not a deduction.

To Beversluis, defenders such as Robert Holyer have replied at length. In a 1988 essay in Faith and Philosophy, Holyer reformulates the argument and contends that it deserves more serious philosophical treatment than its dismissers grant.9 The natural/artificial distinction, defenders argue, is not merely stipulated: there really does seem to be a difference in kind between appetites that arise spontaneously and universally from human nature and those manufactured by culture or fantasy. We do not have to be taught to long; we have to be taught what to call it. As for impossible desires—to fly, to be immortal—defenders reply that these are often desires for a genuine good (mobility, continued life) misdirected onto an impossible mode. But this much must be conceded plainly: pointing to a spontaneous, universal felt difference does not, by itself, answer Beversluis’s circularity charge—for “arises spontaneously and universally” is not the same criterion as “has a real object,” and the desire for earthly immortality looks spontaneous and universal yet has no such object. (To reply that immortality is available, in God, would simply assume what the argument is trying to show.) The honest defender therefore treats the natural/artificial distinction as suggestive but not load-bearing, and rests the case on the phenomenological point that follows.

Yet candor requires the concession: the major premise is the contested hinge, and it cannot be proved. "No one has ever found an innate desire for a nonexistent object" is an inductive generalization that the critic can always say has simply met its first exception in the very case under dispute. The defender believes the premise is more plausible than its denial; the critic believes the reverse; and there is no neutral instrument to settle the matter. To the naturalistic account, the theist's strongest reply is not refutation but a question about adequacy: an evolutionary story explains why we keep striving, but it is less clear that it explains the specific shape of Joy—a longing that feels like homesickness for a place we have never been, and that intensifies rather than diminishes precisely when every earthly good has been secured. Why would a desire aimed at survival present itself as a desire that no survival, no pleasure, no human love could ever satisfy? That phenomenological surplus is what the argument trades on. Whether it amounts to evidence, or only to a feeling awaiting interpretation, is exactly where honest people divide.

The state of the question

Among philosophers of religion the argument from desire is treated as serious but minor—rarely anyone's primary case for theism, frequently a contributor to a cumulative one. Few think it succeeds as a stand-alone proof; many grant that it has genuine evidential and persuasive weight, especially as a "pointer" within a larger argument. The conversation continues to develop: William Lauinger has worked to ground a desire-based argument in a theory of well-being and in the Thomistic "nature does nothing in vain" tradition, and recent work has even attempted a Bayesian reconstruction, asking how much the existence of such a longing should raise one's probability of theism.10 These are live and contested projects. No consensus exists that the argument is sound; no consensus exists that Beversluis has buried it. It remains, fittingly, an open longing.

What it does, and does not, establish

The argument from desire does not prove that God exists. It does not, on its own, deliver the God of any particular creed—Kreeft is explicit that what it points to is an unknown "X" whose direction is known (more beauty, more joy, more reality) but whose full nature it cannot supply. It does not refute the naturalist, who can coherently reinterpret the same experience.

What it does, at its best, is something quieter. It takes a near-universal feature of human inner life—the recurring sense that we are made for more than the world contains—and argues that this is at least as well explained by the hypothesis that we are made for more as by the hypothesis that we are mistaken. It is suggestive, experiential, and confirmatory rather than coercive. For the person already wrestling with the question, it can be the consideration that turns a private ache into a reason to look further. Augustine did not argue that the restless heart proves God; he confessed that the heart was restless and that he had found its rest. Lewis, characteristically, put the whole thing as a hope rather than a Q.E.D.: if there is within us a desire that no experience in this world can satisfy, the most probable explanation—not the only one, but the most probable—is that we were made for another world. The argument cannot compel that conclusion. It can only ask, with disarming honesty, what we make of the longing that will not let us alone.

Frequently asked questions

Doesn't the argument from desire fail because wanting something doesn't make it exist?

The essay grants this immediately and says any defender treating the argument as a proof has overreached. People desire impossible things, to fly or be immortal, so the bare inference "I long for X, therefore X exists" is invalid. Lewis said "the most probable explanation," not the necessary conclusion. Properly understood it is an abductive, cumulative-case consideration, a suggestive pointer, not a demonstration, and it retreats to the narrower claim about natural desires.

Isn't the 'natural desire' distinction just rigged so the inconvenient examples get excluded?

The essay presents this as Beversluis's circularity charge and takes it seriously: a desire counts as natural only if it has a real object, which is the very thing to be proved. It concedes plainly that "arises spontaneously and universally" is not the same as "has a real object," and the desire for earthly immortality looks spontaneous and universal yet has no such object. So the honest defender rests little weight on that distinction and shifts to the phenomenological point instead.

Couldn't our restless longing just be an evolutionary byproduct rather than a signpost to God?

The essay grants this is a serious rival: a never-satisfied disposition could be adaptive, driving striving and exploration, a byproduct of a large future-modeling brain. The theist's reply is not refutation but a question about adequacy, whether evolution explains the specific shape of Joy, a longing that feels like homesickness for a place we have never been and intensifies when every earthly good is secured. Whether that surplus is evidence or a feeling awaiting interpretation is where honest people divide.
