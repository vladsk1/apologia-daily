Post-Resurrection Behaviour: Reading Backward From What the Church Became

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

An Argument That Runs Backward

Most defences of the resurrection run forward: they begin with the reports of an empty tomb and appearances and ask whether those reports are reliable. This essay runs the other direction. It sets the appearances themselves to one side — those are argued elsewhere — and starts instead with a cluster of facts that almost no historian disputes: that within a generation of a crucifixion outside Jerusalem, a movement had appeared that behaved in ways with no good precedent. Terrified followers became public proclaimers who would not recant under threat of death. A community of strict Jewish monotheists began worshipping a crucified man. The day of worship moved. New rites appeared. And all of it grew fastest in the very city where the founder had been executed.

These are effects. The argument from post-resurrection behaviour treats them as a body of evidence and asks the historian's ordinary question: what cause best explains this pattern? It is a form of inference to the best explanation, the same reasoning a coroner or a detective uses. The claim is not that any single one of these facts proves Jesus rose. It is that, taken together, they form a shape — and the shape fits the disciples' own stated cause, a risen Jesus, better than the alternatives on offer. What follows lays out the data, defends the inference, and gives the strongest sceptical replies their full due.

The First Datum: Men Who Would Not Take It Back

The Gospels are candid — embarrassingly so — that at the arrest the inner circle "all left him and fled" (Mark 14:50), that Peter denied even knowing him, and that the survivors met behind locked doors "for fear of the Jews" (John 20:19).1 Whatever one makes of the resurrection, the portrait of a collapsed, frightened band is not the kind of thing a movement invents about its own heroes. It is preserved because it was remembered.

What requires explanation is the reversal. Within weeks the same men were preaching publicly in Jerusalem that God had raised the Jesus their hearers had crucified, and they kept preaching it through arrest and flogging. The earliest layers of the tradition show this conviction translating into a willingness to suffer. Here a careful distinction matters, and the honest apologist insists on it: the disciples' willingness to die does not prove the resurrection true. Sincere people die for false beliefs all the time. What martyrdom rules out is deliberate fraud. People will die for what they wrongly believe to be true; they do not generally die for what they know to be a lie they themselves manufactured. As the New Testament historian Sean McDowell concludes in the most thorough modern study of the question, the strong historical evidence reaches only a handful of the apostles' deaths (Peter, Paul, James the brother of Jesus, and probably James son of Zebedee), yet what we can establish is that the apostles "were willing to suffer and die for their belief" — which secures their sincerity, not the truth of what they sincerely held.2

So this first datum is modest but real: the founding witnesses were transformed from frightened deserters into proclaimers who staked their lives on a specific, checkable claim. Sincerity is established; the truth of their conviction must be argued on other grounds. The behaviour is the effect to be explained.

The Second Datum: Mutations in a Conservative Religion

The more striking evidence is not psychological but sociological. Religions, and especially ancient ones, are conservative; their core practices change slowly and only under pressure. Yet within a remarkably short span the early Jewish followers of Jesus introduced several departures from their inherited religion that have no obvious precedent — departures that, like fossils, preserve a record of the force that produced them. N. T. Wright, in his major study The Resurrection of the Son of God, catalogues a series of such "mutations" within the Jewish belief in resurrection and argues that they are inexplicable apart from something having happened at Easter.3

Resurrection moved from the edge to the centre

In the Judaism of the day, resurrection — where it was believed at all — was a hope for all the righteous at the end of history, a corporate event on the last day. It was a peripheral doctrine, the subject of dispute between Pharisees and Sadducees. In early Christianity it leaps to the centre of everything and takes an unprecedented form: the resurrection of one man, in the middle of history, ahead of everyone else. As Wright stresses, no Jewish text anticipated the Messiah dying and rising bodily in advance of the general resurrection; the category did not exist to be borrowed.4 A movement that placed exactly this at its heart was not extending a familiar hope but minting a new one — and it claimed a particular event as the mint.

Worshipping a crucified man — the devotional explosion

The most extraordinary mutation is devotional. First-century Jewish monotheism was uncompromising: the Shema — "the Lord our God, the Lord is one" — was confessed daily, and to render worship to any figure beside God was idolatry, the gravest sin. Yet our earliest Christian documents, Paul's letters of the 50s, already show Jewish believers directing to Jesus the devotion reserved for God: praying to him in the Aramaic cry Marana tha, "Our Lord, come" (1 Corinthians 16:22); singing what most scholars regard as a pre-Pauline hymn that bends every knee to him in the words Isaiah reserved for YHWH (Philippians 2:6–11); applying the divine name to him (Romans 10:13, citing Joel 2:32).5

The historian who has charted this most exhaustively is Larry Hurtado. In Lord Jesus Christ: Devotion to Jesus in Earliest Christianity he argues that this reverence was not a slow, centuries-long deification borrowed from pagan god-men but a sudden development — a "veritable explosion of devotion to Jesus" that he describes as a binitarian mutation within Jewish monotheism, in place within the first two decades.6 Crucially, Hurtado insists the cause cannot be a gradual evolutionary drift; the speed and intensity demand a precipitating force. Richard Bauckham presses the same data toward a sharper thesis in God Crucified: the earliest Christians did not slowly promote Jesus up a ladder of divinity but included him from the outset within the unique identity of Israel's one God, so that "the earliest Christology was already the highest Christology."7 For a monotheist to do this for a man crucified as a criminal — the most shameful death Rome could impose, and under the law a sign of God's curse (Deuteronomy 21:23) — is on its face inexplicable. Something overturned the verdict of the cross. The believers said it was the raising of Jesus.

The day of worship moved; new rites appeared

Two further mutations are liturgical. Sabbath observance — the seventh day — was among the most identity-defining of all Jewish practices, anchored in the creation account and the Ten Commandments. Yet the followers of Jesus came to attach a special significance to the first day of the week. That day already carries weight in the New Testament — Paul has the Corinthians set funds aside "on the first day of every week" (1 Corinthians 16:2) and Acts describes a gathering to break bread on it (20:7) — though the full transfer of the day of worship away from the Sabbath becomes clear only by the early second century, when Ignatius of Antioch can speak of Christians "no longer keeping the Sabbath but living by the Lord's Day," with the Roman governor Pliny reporting (c. AD 112) that they met "on a fixed day before dawn" to "sing a hymn to Christ as to a god."8 The natural explanation for choosing that day is the one the sources give: it was the day of the resurrection. Alongside this came new sacraments tied not to the agricultural calendar but to specific events — a baptism administered "in the name of Jesus," and a Lord's Supper that Paul says he "received" and "delivered" (the technical language of fixed tradition) as a re-enactment of a particular meal, proclaiming "the Lord's death until he comes" (1 Corinthians 11:23–26).9 Communities do not build weekly rites around the execution of a failed leader. They build them around a death they believe was vindicated.

The Third Datum: Growth in the Wrong City

Finally, the geography. A fabricated resurrection has a natural strategy: proclaim it far from the scene, where the tomb cannot be inspected and the authorities who buried the body cannot produce it. The early movement did the opposite. Its public proclamation began, and grew fastest, in Jerusalem — within walking distance of the tomb, before the very council that had secured the execution, among a population that had recently watched Jesus die. This is the worst possible venue for a known falsehood and a natural one for a conviction the proclaimers believed could survive scrutiny. The relevant fact here is not a precise count of converts — the figures in Acts (2:41; 4:4) are debated — but the location and the survival of the claim in it. A resurrection preached in Jerusalem could in principle have been ended by one produced corpse. It was not.

Putting the Inference Together

Each datum, alone, admits of more than one reading. Their force is cumulative. We have a transformed band of sincere witnesses; a peripheral doctrine vaulted to the centre in an unprecedented form; the worship of a crucified man by monotheists, erupting within years; new rites built around a specific death; a first-day-of-the-week observance that hardened into a wholesale change of the holy day (though, as noted above, the full transfer is clear only by the early second century); and explosive proclamation in the one city where the claim was most exposed. A fair-minded skeptic will rightly note that these effects are not really six unrelated puzzles: one naturalistic cause — the disciples sincerely came to believe Jesus had been raised — already unifies the behaviour, since people who hold that belief will worship, change their rites, and preach boldly. Granted. The honest force of the argument is therefore not that the behaviour resists a single cause, but that it presses the question back one step: what caused that belief, so suddenly, so early, and so resistant to the ordinary engines of grief, expectation, and legend? The resurrection itself answers that question directly; its competitors — cognitive dissonance, hallucination, social drift, borrowing — must each be made to bear weight, and even then struggle with the speed and the convergence. Those rival accounts of the belief's origin are taken up in the companion essays on the appearances, the empty tomb, and the disciples' transformation. That is the heart of the argument: not that any one effect is decisive, but that the whole pattern drives us to ask what lit the fire — and the resurrection makes sense of the answer where its competitors make sense of it only in pieces.

The Strongest Objections

Gerd Theissen and the sociology of cognitive dissonance

The most respectable naturalistic reply is sociological. Drawing on Leon Festinger's famous study of a failed-prophecy cult, When Prophecy Fails, scholars have proposed that the disciples' bold behaviour is what cognitive-dissonance theory predicts: a group whose hopes are shattered by an unexpected disconfirmation (here, the crucifixion) does not collapse but rationalises the failure and proselytises all the harder to recruit social support for the threatened belief.10 On this view the boldness, far from evidencing a real resurrection, is the textbook response of a disconfirmed movement.

This is a serious model and deserves a fair hearing; dissonance-reduction is a genuine phenomenon. But the analogy strains at the decisive points. Festinger's cult survived disconfirmation precisely by reinterpreting a still-future prophecy, not by claiming a concrete, checkable, public event had already occurred. Dissonance theory can explain a group that says "the end is merely delayed"; it is far weaker at explaining a group that says "we saw him, the tomb is empty, come and check" in the city of the burial. As N. T. Wright notes, disappointed messianic movements in this period typically either dissolved or found a new leader — they did not declare the executed leader risen and divine.11 The dissonance model explains persistence; it does not explain the content of what persisted — bodily resurrection of one man, mid-history, worshipped as Lord — which is the very thing that needs a cause.

Bart Ehrman: high Christology grew, it did not erupt

Bart Ehrman grants the transformation and the early devotion but resists the conclusion that it was sudden or that it requires a miracle. In How Jesus Became God he argues for a development — from an initial belief that God had exalted Jesus to heaven, toward fuller incarnational claims over time — and traces the disciples' conviction itself to visionary experiences, including the well-documented "bereavement visions" of the grieving.12 On Ehrman's reading the behaviour follows naturally from sincere belief in such visions; no empty tomb or bodily rising is needed.

One should concede what is true here: there was theological development in the decades that followed, and visionary experience is a real human phenomenon. But two points blunt the objection as an account of the behaviour. First, the data Hurtado and Bauckham marshal show that the highest devotional practices — prayer, hymns, the divine name — are already present in our earliest sources, not merely at the end of a long curve; even Ehrman locates a high view "remarkably early," and the dispute is over how high and how fast, not whether it was swift.13 Second, bereavement visions, however real, are private and do not move a man to abandon the Sabbath, reorganise his community around a new rite, or worship the deceased as God in defiance of the Shema. Visions might explain a feeling of presence; they are a thin cause for an institutional revolution. The behaviour outruns the proposed mechanism.

The history-of-religions reply: it was all borrowed

An older objection, still heard, holds that the dying-and-rising savior, the sacred meal, and the divine man were stock features of the surrounding pagan world, so the church merely assembled its novelties from available parts. The reply, now widely accepted even among non-confessional scholars, is that the parallels are late, loose, or imposed. The closest analogues to a Lord-cult are pagan, but the worship of a recently executed contemporary by strict monotheists has no genuine pagan or Jewish precedent — that is precisely why Hurtado calls it a "mutation" rather than an importation, and why Wright treats the resurrection form as unprecedented in the Jewish sources. Borrowing explains a detail here or there; it does not explain why monotheists crossed the one line their whole religion forbade.

The State of the Scholarship

It is important to mark the contested seams honestly. The facts in this essay are very widely granted: the disciples' sincere transformed conviction, the early and intense devotion to Jesus, the shift to Sunday, the new sacraments, the Jerusalem proclamation. These are mainstream, affirmed across the spectrum from Wright to Hurtado to Ehrman. What is contested is the inference from the facts to a bodily resurrection as their cause. Sceptical scholars — Ehrman, the late Gerd Lüdemann, Maurice Casey — accept the data and reach for naturalistic causes (visions, dissonance, gradual development). The argument from behaviour does not pretend this debate is settled. Its claim is narrower and, in its place, strong: that the convergence of these effects is better explained by the cause the witnesses named than by any single naturalistic substitute, and that the rivals succeed only by multiplying separate explanations for what one event explains together. Even a critic should grant that the pattern is real and demands a cause; the live question is which cause.

What the Argument Does and Does Not Establish

Honesty about scope is the price of a serious argument. This one does not independently prove the empty tomb, nor does it prove that the disciples' visions were veridical — those belong to the sibling arguments about the appearances and the origin of the Easter belief. By itself it does not compel assent from someone who holds, on prior grounds, that a bodily resurrection is impossible; such a person will pay the price of stacking naturalistic explanations, and the argument's job is to show that the price is higher than it first appears. Nor does the disciples' willingness to die prove their belief true; it proves they were not knowing frauds.

What the argument does establish is considerable. It shows that the rise of Christianity is not a free-floating story to be taken or left but a set of hard historical effects — behavioural, devotional, liturgical, geographical — that genuinely require a cause; that the cause the first Christians named accounts for all of them with a single explanation; and that the leading naturalistic rivals, fairly stated, account for them only in fragments and strain at the speed. Reasoning backward from what the church demonstrably became to what could have made it so, the resurrection remains the explanation that does the most work. That is not the whole case for Easter, but it is a real and weighty part of it — offered, as the apostle who once fled and then would not stop preaching urged, "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15).
