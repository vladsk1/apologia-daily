The Trinity in the New Testament

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Short on time? Read the quick answer →

Around the year 112, the Roman governor Pliny the Younger wrote to the emperor Trajan to report on a strange new sect he had been interrogating in northern Asia Minor. The Christians, he explained, were in the habit of meeting before dawn and singing “a hymn to Christ, as to a god” (carmen Christo quasi deo).1 It is a small, almost incidental line in a bureaucrat’s memo—but it is one of the earliest outside descriptions of Christian worship, and it captures the puzzle that would occupy the church for the next three centuries. Here were people who insisted, with every fiber of their Jewish inheritance, that there is only one God. And yet they sang to a crucified man as though he were that God.

The doctrine of the Trinity is the church’s attempt to say, with precision, what those first worshippers were already doing. It is often treated as a piece of late, abstract philosophy—Greek metaphysics smuggled into a Hebrew faith, or a committee decision finalized at Nicaea in 325. This essay argues something different and, I think, more interesting: that the Trinity is not imposed on the New Testament but is the only formulation that keeps all of its data at once. The vocabulary is later. The pressure that produced it is on nearly every page.

What the Doctrine Claims—and What It Does Not

Stated in its classical, creedal form, the doctrine holds that there is one God who exists eternally as three persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—each of whom is fully and equally God, yet who are genuinely distinct from one another. One being; three persons. This is the confession of the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed of 381, the shared inheritance of Eastern Orthodox, Roman Catholic, and Protestant Christians alike.

It is essential to see what the doctrine is not, because most popular objections are really objections to a heresy the church itself rejected. It is not tritheism—three gods. It is not modalism—one person wearing three masks, now Father, now Son, now Spirit. It is not Arianism—a supreme God plus a created, lesser Son. And it is not partialism—three thirds that add up to one God. The doctrine threads between all of these: the three are not parts of God, nor roles God plays, nor a ranking of greater and lesser divinities. What God is is one; who God is is three.

The argument from the New Testament can be stated almost as a syllogism. First given: there is exactly one God. Second given: the Father is God, the Son is God, and the Holy Spirit is God. Third given: the Father, Son, and Spirit are really distinct—they address one another, send one another, relate to one another. The Trinity is simply the doctrine that holds all three givens together without dropping any. The work of the argument is to show that each given is genuinely taught, and that no rival reading can keep all three.

The First Given: The New Testament Keeps Strict Monotheism

It would be a serious mistake to think the New Testament softens Jewish monotheism to make room for Jesus. If anything, it sharpens it. “There is no God but one,” Paul writes (1 Corinthians 8:4). The demons, James notes acidly, believe that God is one—and shudder (James 2:19). Jesus himself, asked for the greatest commandment, recites the Shema: “Hear, O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is one” (Mark 12:29). Whatever the early Christians were claiming about Jesus, they were not abandoning the conviction that the God of Israel is the sole creator and the only proper object of worship.

This is the hinge that makes the whole argument work. Were the New Testament casually polytheistic, the deity of Christ would be unremarkable—one more god in a crowded pantheon. It is precisely because the monotheism is uncompromising that the worship of Jesus becomes so startling, and the doctrine of the Trinity so necessary. The historian Larry Hurtado built his career on documenting this tension: from astonishingly early—within the first two decades after the crucifixion—Christians directed to Jesus the cultic devotion that Jews reserved for God alone, producing what Hurtado called a “binitarian” pattern of worship that was, in his words, an “unprecedented” innovation within Jewish monotheism rather than a drift away from it.2

The Second Given: The Son and the Spirit Are God

The deity of the Son does not rest on a single proof text but on a convergence of many. John’s Gospel opens with it: “In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God” (John 1:1)—and closes with Thomas falling before the risen Jesus and confessing, “My Lord and my God!” (John 20:28), a confession the narrative presents approvingly (Jesus’ next words pronounce a blessing, not a correction). Paul calls believers to await “our great God and Savior Jesus Christ” (Titus 2:13). The letter to the Hebrews has the Father address the Son directly as God—“Your throne, O God, is forever and ever”—and then applies to him a Psalm originally sung to YHWH the unchanging creator who founded the earth (Hebrews 1:8–12).

The most penetrating recent work locates Jesus not merely among lofty titles but within the identity of the one God. Richard Bauckham argues that Second Temple Jews defined their one God by what only he does—he alone created all things, he alone rules all things—and that the New Testament writers deliberately place Jesus on the divine side of that line: the one through whom all things were made and to whom every knee will bow. Bauckham’s thesis, in its often-quoted summary form, is that the earliest Christology was already the highest Christology—a fully divine Christology in which Jesus is intrinsic to the unique identity of God.3 The clearest case is Paul’s reworking of the Shema itself in 1 Corinthians 8:6: “for us there is one God, the Father…and one Lord, Jesus Christ.” As N. T. Wright has argued, Paul has taken the central Jewish confession of the one Lord God and, astonishingly, distributed it between the Father and Jesus—not adding a second god, but expanding the Shema from within to include the Son.4

The deity of the Spirit is established more by what the Spirit is and does than by the word “God” applied as a label. The decisive text is Acts 5, where Peter tells Ananias, “You have lied to the Holy Spirit”—and in the next breath, “You have not lied to men but to God” (Acts 5:3–4). To lie to the Spirit simply is to lie to God. Paul calls Christians the temple of God precisely because the Spirit dwells in them (1 Corinthians 3:16). Gordon Fee’s exhaustive study of the Spirit in Paul concludes that for Paul the Spirit is no impersonal force but a fully personal divine presence—“God’s empowering presence”—to whom personhood applies as much as it does to the Father and the Son.5

The Third Given: The Three Are Distinct

If the first two givens push toward unity, the third forbids collapsing the three into one another. The Son prays to the Father in Gethsemane, submitting his will to another’s (Luke 22:42). The Father sends the Son into the world (John 3:17), and the Son promises to send “another Advocate”—the Spirit—a phrase that distinguishes the Spirit from himself even as it puts the Spirit in his own class (John 14:16). At Jesus’ baptism all three are present and active at once: the Son in the water, the Father’s voice from heaven, the Spirit descending as a dove (Mark 1:9–11). One who prays to another, is sent by another, and sends another is not a mask the others wear. The persons relate; modes cannot.

The Triadic Texts

What makes the New Testament data more than a loose collection of high claims is that the three are repeatedly named together, in formulas the church did not have to invent because it inherited them from worship. The most famous is the baptismal command of Matthew 28:19: to baptize “in the name”—singular—“of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.” One name; three who share it. Paul’s benediction in 2 Corinthians 13:14 invokes “the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ and the love of God and the fellowship of the Holy Spirit.” Peter greets his readers as chosen “according to the foreknowledge of God the Father, in the sanctification of the Spirit, for obedience to Jesus Christ” (1 Peter 1:2). And twice Paul lays out the church’s gifts and unity in a threefold pattern: “the same Spirit…the same Lord…the same God” (1 Corinthians 12:4–6), and “one Spirit…one Lord…one God and Father of all” (Ephesians 4:4–6). None of these is a developed dogma. But they are the raw triadic grammar—Father, Son, Spirit, named as equals in the same breath—that the doctrine of the Trinity was later coined to explain.

The Objection That Sounds Decisive: “The Word Is Not in the Bible”

The most common challenge is also the easiest to answer, though it deserves a respectful hearing. The word “Trinity” (Latin trinitas, first used by Tertullian around AD 200) appears nowhere in Scripture; neither do “person,” “essence,” or “consubstantial.” The doctrine’s technical vocabulary is unmistakably post-biblical. If the teaching were so central, the objection runs, surely the apostles would have stated it plainly.

The reply is that absence of a label is not absence of a thing. The word “monotheism” is not in the Bible either; nor is “omnipotence,” nor “incarnation,” nor even the word “Bible.” Summary terms exist to name what a body of texts teaches, and the only question worth asking is whether the data the term summarizes is present. We have seen that it is: one God; Father, Son, and Spirit each treated as God; the three genuinely distinct. Deny the word and every one of those givens remains—demanding precisely the synthesis the word names. To object that “Trinity” is post-biblical is rather like objecting that a doctor’s diagnosis is not written on the patient. The condition is what the word is about.

The Serious Objection: The Biblical-Unitarian Case

The challenge that genuinely deserves a steelman comes not from people who have never opened the New Testament but from those who know it intimately and read it differently. Contemporary biblical unitarianism—represented most ably by the analytic philosopher Dale Tuggy (PhD, Brown), longtime host of the trinities podcast—is not modalism, not Arianism, and not a denial that the New Testament is authoritative. It is the claim that the New Testament is itself unitarian: that the one God simply is the Father, and no one else is, and that the church later read a later doctrine back into texts that do not teach it.6

Stated at full strength, the case is formidable. The texts that most clearly say “the only true God” identify that God as the Father, not as a triune being: Jesus prays to the Father as “the only true God” and distinguishes himself from him (John 17:3); Paul writes that “there is one God, the Father…and one Lord, Jesus Christ”—one God, says Tuggy, is reserved for the Father, while Jesus gets the lesser title Lord (1 Corinthians 8:6). The New Testament constantly subordinates the Son: he is sent, he obeys, he does not know the day or hour (Mark 13:32), the Father is “greater than I” (John 14:28), and in the end the Son himself will be subjected to the Father (1 Corinthians 15:28). The Spirit, the unitarian adds, is regularly spoken of in impersonal terms—poured out, given by measure, the very “power” of God (Luke 1:35; 24:49). The clean, simple reading is that the Father alone is God, Jesus is his uniquely exalted human Messiah, and the Spirit is God’s active presence. The Trinity, Tuggy argues, is not what the apostles taught but a fourth-century construction—and one whose coherence (one being, three persons who are each the one God) remains, in his judgment, philosophically unproven. This is the strongest form of the case, and it should not be waved away.

The Reply—Conceding What Should Be Conceded

Several of the unitarian’s points must be granted outright. The persons are co-equal and co-eternal in being; only their order of acting differs.. Classical orthodoxy not only allows this but insists on it. The distinction the doctrine draws is between the eternal nature the persons share and the order in which they act in salvation—what theologians call the economy. “The Father is greater than I” is spoken by the Son who has, in Paul’s words, taken “the form of a servant,” having been “in the form of God” (Philippians 2:6–7); greater in position, within the incarnate mission, not greater in deity. To read functional subordination as ontological inequality is to commit the very error—Arianism—that the church rejected. It is worth conceding, too, that the doctrine was indeed clarified over centuries, and that no single verse states it whole.

But the unitarian reading purchases its simplicity by deleting data. Take John 17:3, the unitarian’s strongest verse. Five verses later, the same Jesus asks the Father to glorify him with the glory he had “before the world existed” (John 17:5)—glory that Isaiah says YHWH shares with no other (Isaiah 42:8), in the same Gospel that ends with Thomas saying “my Lord and my God.” John 17:3 excludes the idols, not the Son; it is monotheism against paganism, not the Father against Jesus. The same move answers 1 Corinthians 8:6: as Wright and Bauckham observe, Paul is not demoting Jesus by calling him “Lord” but including him in the Shema, for “Lord” (kyrios) is the Greek translation’s standard rendering of the divine name YHWH. Far from a lesser title, “one Lord” places Jesus inside Israel’s confession of the one God.

The deepest reply is methodological. Each rival to the Trinity survives only by suppressing a line of evidence. Unitarianism must explain away the deity texts and the worship of Jesus that Hurtado documents from the earliest period. Modalism must explain away the texts of distinction—the Son who prays to the Father is not praying to himself. Tritheism must explain away the relentless monotheism. The Trinity alone keeps every given. It is, to borrow the language of inference, the best explanation of the whole body of data rather than the tidiest explanation of a selected part. That is not a knockdown proof—and here honesty requires the next concession.

Where the Argument Reaches Its Limit

The New Testament does not hand us the doctrine of the Trinity in finished, technical form. It hands us the materials—the three givens—and the church spent three centuries, through real and sometimes bitter controversy, working out the formulation that holds them. The decisive terms (one ousia, three hypostaseis) are the product of that labor. Tuggy is right that this development happened; where Trinitarians disagree is in calling it a faithful drawing-out of what was already there rather than the invention of something new. Nicaea, on this reading, did not vote Jesus into godhood; it rejected the genuinely new idea—Arius’s created Son—by finding language for what congregations had been doing since before Pliny wrote his letter.

It must also be conceded that the coherence of the doctrine—how one being can be three persons without contradiction—is a live philosophical question, not a settled one. Tuggy and other able critics press it, and Trinitarian philosophers (from Brian Leftow’s “Latin” models to the “social” models of figures like Cornelius Plantinga Jr. and William Lane Craig) continue to debate the best account among themselves.7 What the centuries of attack have produced is not a demonstrated contradiction but a recognized mystery: a contradiction requires the same thing affirmed and denied in the same respect, and “one in essence, three in person” carefully avoids that—what God is is one, who God is is three. That the doctrine outruns our full comprehension is, for the Christian, unsurprising; a God we could fully diagram would not be the God of Israel.

So the scope of the argument should be stated plainly. The New Testament evidence does not prove the Nicene formula the way a theorem is proved. What it does is far stronger than the popular caricature allows: it shows that the three givens are each genuinely and pervasively taught, that they sit in tension, and that the Trinity is the synthesis that loses none of them while every rival loses one. The argument establishes that the doctrine is the most faithful reading of the data—not that no thoughtful person could read it otherwise.

Conclusion: The Shape of the Gospel

The fairest summary of the scholarship is this. There is now broad agreement, across confessional and skeptical lines alike, that a remarkably high view of Jesus—and a pattern of devotion offered to him as to God—appears at the very earliest layer of the Christian movement, not as a late Hellenistic accretion. On this much there is a broad, hard-won near-consensus—documented especially by Hurtado and reflected in the work of Wright and Fee—and it is what makes the older “Constantine invented it” story untenable. Bauckham’s sharper proposal—that the New Testament writers deliberately place Jesus within the divine identity—has been enormously influential but remains genuinely contested: James D. G. Dunn and James F. McGrath, among others, judge that “divine identity” reads later precision back into more developmental first-century language. So the high-devotion datum is firm; the divine-identity framing is a powerful but disputed interpretation of it. What also remains genuinely contested is the philosophical articulation—and there the conversation between Trinitarians and careful unitarians like Tuggy continues in good faith.

The doctrine of the Trinity, then, is best understood not as an abstraction bolted onto the gospel but as the gospel’s own shape. The God who is love did not become loving when he made the world; he is, eternally, Father loving Son in the Spirit. When Pliny’s Christians sang to Christ before dawn, they were not confused monotheists. They were the first witnesses to a truth the church would spend centuries learning to confess: that the one God of Israel is, and has always been, Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. The word came later. The worship came first—and the worship is where the doctrine was born.

Frequently asked questions

If the word Trinity isn't in the Bible, isn't the doctrine just made up later?

The word is post-biblical, but the absence of a label is not the absence of the thing. Words like monotheism, omnipotence, and incarnation aren't in Scripture either; summary terms name what a body of texts teaches. The New Testament supplies the data the word gathers: one God, with Father, Son, and Spirit each treated as God and genuinely distinct. Deny the word and every one of those givens remains.

Doesn't the New Testament show Jesus is lesser than the Father since he prays and obeys?

Classical orthodoxy grants that the Son is genuinely sent, obeys, and prays. The distinction is between the eternal nature the persons share and the order in which they act in salvation. When Jesus says the Father is greater, he speaks as one who took the form of a servant while being in the form of God. That is subordination in role within the mission, not inequality of deity, which would be the Arianism the church rejected.

Why can't Christians just say God is one person who appears as Father, Son, and Spirit?

That view, called modalism, cannot keep the New Testament's texts of distinction. The Son prays to the Father in Gethsemane, is sent by the Father, and promises to send another Advocate, the Spirit. One who prays to another, is sent by another, and sends another is not a single person wearing three masks. The persons genuinely relate to one another, so the doctrine holds one being in three distinct persons, not one person in three modes.
