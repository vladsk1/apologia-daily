Did Jesus Claim to Be God? The Historical Case from His Own Words and Deeds

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Short on time? Read the quick answer →

A paralyzed man is lowered through a torn-open roof, dust raining onto the heads of the crowd packed into a Galilean house. Everyone expects the healer to heal. Instead he looks at the man and says something nobody asked for: “Son, your sins are forgiven” (Mark 2:5). The room goes cold. The religious scholars sitting nearby do not say “how kind” or “how strange.” They think something far sharper: “Why does this man speak like that? He is blaspheming! Who can forgive sins but God alone?” (Mark 2:7).

That reaction is the doorway into one of the most contested questions in the study of Jesus. Not the question the later church answered at Nicaea—was Jesus God—but a prior, strictly historical one: did Jesus of Nazareth, in his own lifetime, understand and present himself as somehow standing in the place of God? It is worth being honest from the first sentence that serious historians disagree, and that the answer is more layered than a single proof-text.

Stating the Question Carefully

We must separate three things that often get blended. First, there is what the later church confessed—the full Trinitarian and incarnational doctrine of the creeds, hammered out over centuries. Second, there is the abstract logical argument sometimes called the “Lord, liar, or lunatic” trilemma—the claim that if Jesus said he was God, he was either right, lying, or deluded. That logic is treated in a companion essay (on Jesus’s character and the trilemma) and is not our concern here. Third—our actual subject—there is the historical question of Jesus’s own self-understanding: what did he claim about himself, as best we can reconstruct it from the earliest sources?

Here lies the real difficulty, and it must be named up front. The most explicit divine self-claims in the Gospels all cluster in John, the latest of the four to be written (commonly dated to the 90s AD). It is in John that Jesus says, “I and the Father are one” (John 10:30), “before Abraham was, I am” (John 8:58, echoing the divine name of Exodus 3:14), and “whoever has seen me has seen the Father” (John 14:9). These are breathtaking. But precisely because they appear late, and in a Gospel famous for its theological development, a critic can plausibly say they reflect the church’s mature faith placed on Jesus’s lips, rather than his own words. An argument that leans its whole weight on John has, at best, won a contested skirmish.

So the stronger move—the one this essay will make—is to build primarily on the implicit claims embedded in the earlier Synoptic material (Mark, and the traditions shared by Matthew and Luke). These are harder to dismiss as late invention, and they are arguably more revealing: a man who quietly acts as though he occupies God’s place may be claiming more than one who simply asserts a title.

The Case from the Earliest Layers

Return to that Galilean house. The scribes’ outrage is not a misunderstanding the Gospel writer wants us to correct; it is the point. In Second Temple Judaism, a person could declare that God had forgiven someone, or pronounce forgiveness within the Temple system on the Day of Atonement. But Jesus bypasses the Temple, the priesthood, and the sacrifices and pronounces forgiveness on his own authority, as if the offended party were standing there in his own skin.1 The scribes’ logic, the New Testament scholar will note, is theologically correct: sin is ultimately an offense against God, so only God can absolve it. Jesus does not deny their premise; he acts inside it.

This is the pattern across the earliest strata. In the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus repeatedly says, “You have heard that it was said… but I say to you” (Matthew 5:21–22, 27–28, and following). The Torah was, for a first-century Jew, the very word of God. A prophet would say, “Thus says the LORD”; a rabbi would cite earlier authorities. Jesus sets his own “but I say to you” over against the Mosaic law—not abolishing it (Matthew 5:17) but speaking as one with the standing to declare its true and final intent.2 He calls himself “lord of the Sabbath” (Mark 2:28), claiming authority over the day God himself sanctified at creation.

Most striking, Jesus makes a person’s eternal standing turn on their response to him: “Everyone who acknowledges me before men, I also will acknowledge before my Father who is in heaven, but whoever denies me before men, I also will deny before my Father who is in heaven” (Matthew 10:32–33). A prophet points away from himself to God. Jesus places himself at the hinge of the final judgment.

The historian N. T. Wright has drawn these threads together with a vivid thesis: Jesus behaved as a kind of “one-man Temple-substitute,” offering on his own authority the forgiveness and restored fellowship with God that the Temple existed to mediate.3 If that reading is right, Jesus was not merely teaching about God; he was acting out, in person, what Israel believed God did through the Temple. That is an implicit claim of staggering size, and it is woven into the earliest narrative fabric, not bolted on by John.

Then comes the trial. According to Mark—the earliest Gospel—the high priest asks directly, “Are you the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?” Jesus answers, “I am, and you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power, and coming with the clouds of heaven” (Mark 14:61–62). (Matthew’s parallel gives the more oblique “You have said so”; some judge that indirect form more original—but either way the decisive move is the Daniel 7 claim to come “with the clouds,” which both versions preserve.) The high priest tears his robes and cries blasphemy, and the council condemns him (Mark 14:63–64). Jesus is fusing two scriptural images: the enthroned figure of Psalm 110:1 (“sit at my right hand”) and the “one like a son of man” of Daniel 7:13–14, who comes on the clouds—a divine prerogative—and receives everlasting dominion and worship from all peoples. To claim to be that figure, seated beside God’s throne, was heard by the court not as messianic boasting but as something worse. The charge of blasphemy is the critics’ own testimony that they understood Jesus to be claiming far too much.

The Strongest Objection, Stated at Full Strength

The most formidable case against all of this has been pressed by Bart Ehrman, especially in How Jesus Became God (2014). His argument deserves to be heard at its best, because it is genuinely strong.

Ehrman contends that the historical Jesus understood himself as an apocalyptic prophet—announcing the imminent in-breaking of God’s kingdom—and perhaps as Israel’s messiah, a human king, but emphatically not as God.4 The high divine claims, Ehrman argues, are precisely the ones that fail the historian’s tests: they appear in our latest source (John), they grow more exalted the later the source, and they are absent from the earliest layers in the form of any plain “I am God” statement. On Ehrman’s reconstruction, belief in Jesus’s divinity ignited only after his death, when some followers became convinced he had been raised and exalted to heaven; the divinity is a post-Easter conviction read back onto his life.

Crucially, Ehrman and others press even on the trial scene. The phrase “son of man” need not be a divine title at all. Building on Géza Vermes’s influential argument in Jesus the Jew (1973), the Aramaic scholar Maurice Casey argued at length that the underlying idiom, bar (e)nāsh(ā), simply meant “a human being” or functioned as a modest way of saying “a man such as I,” carrying no built-in reference to Daniel 7.5 If Casey is right, then much of what sounds like a cosmic self-designation in English may be an ordinary Aramaic turn of phrase, and the Danielic enthronement reading is the later church’s overlay. To this we should add the cautious voice of James D. G. Dunn, no skeptic, who argued that even in the New Testament worship is characteristically offered to God through Jesus, and who warned against reading later doctrine too quickly into the earliest evidence.6

A Reply—and What It Can and Cannot Carry

The reply does not run through John. It runs through the implicit claims, and it has been developed most rigorously by Richard Bauckham and Larry Hurtado.

Bauckham’s influential (though itself debated) thesis is that the earliest Christology was already the highest Christology: the first Christians did not slowly promote a human teacher up a ladder toward divinity but included Jesus within the unique “divine identity” of the one God of Israel—the God who alone creates, rules, and is worshipped.7 Hurtado, working from a different angle, documented what he called an “explosion” of devotion to Jesus—prayer, worship, invocation—erupting within the very earliest years among Jewish monotheists who would have regarded worshipping a mere man as idolatry.8 Something about Jesus, in other words, broke the categories almost immediately.

That early eruption is, strictly, evidence about the first believers rather than about Jesus directly—but it is hard to explain as a sudden post-Easter novelty if Jesus had presented himself simply as one more prophet; it reads more naturally as the continuation of something already implicit in how he had acted. And it coheres with the implicit claims we have already seen: a man who forgives sins on his own say-so, who sets his word over the Torah, who makes himself the criterion of final judgment, and who applies the Daniel 7 throne-scene to himself at his trial is already behaving as one who belongs on God’s side of the line between Creator and creature. Even on the Son of Man question, Casey’s purely idiomatic reading is contested: critics such as Paul Owen and David Shepherd have argued that the specific emphatic form points to a particular figure, not a generic “anyone,” and the explicit citation of Daniel’s clouds-coming imagery in Mark 14:62 is difficult to drain of its enthronement meaning.9

But intellectual honesty requires conceding the limits, clearly. A case for Jesus’s self-claims that pretends John settles the historical question has overreached. Second, the genuinely strong ground—the implicit, behavioral claims in the Synoptics—is strong but not self-interpreting. Forgiving sins, claiming the Sabbath, invoking Daniel 7: each requires interpretation, and a determined skeptic can offer a lower-key reading of any single one (perhaps Jesus spoke for God as a uniquely authorized agent, not as God). The cumulative force comes from the pattern, and patterns persuade by weight rather than by proof.

And here the last word, for a moment, should go to the critic. Ehrman would say: a pattern of exalted authority is exactly what you would expect from a charismatic apocalyptic prophet convinced he was God’s final agent—no more divine self-claim is needed to explain it. That is a fair objection, and it cannot be waved away.

The reply is that the objection accounts for the authority but strains to account for its shape. An agent speaks for the king; he does not seat himself on the king’s throne, claim the king’s exclusive right to absolve, and make himself the standard by which the king will judge the world. When a man does all of that, “uniquely authorized agent” begins to bend under the load. The category that fits the data most economically is one that the earliest tradition reached for: he was claiming something that, on its terms, only God could rightly claim.

The Honest State of Scholarship

This remains a live debate, not a closed one. A substantial body of New Testament scholarship—Wright, Bauckham, Hurtado, and others working in the “early high Christology” stream—holds that the implicit divine claims reach back into the bedrock of the tradition. A serious and respected counter-stream—Ehrman most prominently, and on particular points the more cautious Dunn (who affirms the historic Christian faith but urges caution about how much the earliest evidence by itself establishes) and the late Casey—reads the earliest Jesus as prophet and would-be messiah, with full divinity arriving after Easter. Both camps are working with the same texts and the same critical tools; the disagreement is largely about how to weigh implicit evidence and how early the high claims can be pushed.

Conclusion: What This Does and Does Not Establish

What can be said with reasonable historical confidence is this: even setting John aside, the earliest layers of the tradition present a Jesus who assumed prerogatives—forgiving sins, overruling Torah, claiming the Sabbath, making salvation hinge on allegiance to him, taking the Danielic throne—that strain any merely-human category and that his own contemporaries heard as a claim to stand where only God stands. It is therefore reasonable to conclude that the historical Jesus claimed an authority and identity that cannot comfortably be reduced to “great teacher” or “prophet.”

What this does not establish is just as important. It does not prove the explicit Johannine sayings to be verbatim history. It does not, by itself, settle the debate against able critics who read the same evidence differently. And—most importantly—it does not prove the claim true. To show that Jesus claimed a divine identity is a historical conclusion; to ask whether that claim was warranted is a further question, one that turns on his character, his resurrection, and the kind of life he lived. This essay has tried only to clear the first ground honestly: the man from Nazareth does seem to have understood himself as far more than a man. What we make of that is where the real conversation begins.
