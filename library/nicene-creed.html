The Nicene Creed, Line by Line

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Short on time? Read the quick answer →

Every Sunday, in languages the original bishops never spoke and on continents they never saw, hundreds of millions of Christians stand and say the same words: “We believe in one God, the Father Almighty…” The creed they recite was hammered out across two councils—Nicaea in 325 and Constantinople in 381—and it remains, sixteen centuries later, the one confession Catholics, the Orthodox, and Protestants still hold in common. It is short enough to memorize and dense enough to study for a lifetime.1

This essay does something narrower than a history of the councils (for that, see our companion piece on whether Nicaea “invented” the Trinity). It walks the creed clause by clause and asks, of each line: what does this actually mean, and why is it worded exactly this way? Because almost every phrase was chosen with surgical care—often to close a door a specific heresy had propped open.

Why a Creed at All?

A creed (from the Latin credo, “I believe”) is a summary of the faith compact enough to confess together. The New Testament already contains creed-like fragments—“Jesus is Lord” (Romans 10:9), the terse hymn Paul quotes in 1 Corinthians 15:3–7. The Nicene Creed is not a new revelation added to Scripture; it is a faithful summary of Scripture, arranged around the three persons named at every baptism (Matthew 28:19): Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

What makes it feel technical is a handful of non-biblical words—most famously homoousios, “of one substance.” Critics ancient and modern have objected that the creed smuggles Greek philosophy into the gospel. But the technical vocabulary is a pastoral necessity, not a philosophical intrusion. The heretics used biblical words—“God,” “Lord,” “Son of God”—and emptied them of their meaning. When both sides can recite the same verse and mean opposite things, the church has to say plainly which reading it confesses. As C. S. Lewis put it, “Good philosophy must exist, if for no other reason, because bad philosophy needs to be answered.”2 The creed is biblical in substance and technical only in form.

“We Believe in One God, the Father Almighty”

The creed opens where Israel began: with one God. This was no abstraction in the fourth century but a battle line. Rome had a crowded pantheon, and Christians who refused to burn incense to the gods were branded atheists. Justin Martyr threw the charge back: “we confess that we are atheists, so far as gods of this sort are concerned, but not with respect to the most true God.”3 To say “one God” was to stand, at cost, against the whole religious order of the age.

“Almighty” translates the Greek pantokratōr, which means less “able to do anything” than “ruler of all”—the One who holds every created thing in his hand (Revelation 1:8). And “Father” carries two senses at once: God is Father of all he has made, and—eternally, before any creature existed—Father of the Son.4 That second sense matters, because the creed is not finished at “one God.” It does not stop where a Unitarian would. It goes on to confess the Son and the Spirit as that one God—which is why the very next word is and.

“And in One Lord Jesus Christ”—the Heart of the Creed

By far the longest section is devoted to the Son, because this is exactly where the fourth-century crisis fell. An Alexandrian presbyter named Arius had taught that the Son, though the highest of all creatures, was still a creature—that “there was when he was not.”5 The creed answers him phrase by phrase.

“The only-begotten Son of God, begotten of the Father before all worlds.” The word behind “only-begotten” is monogenēs—“only,” one of a kind. It does not mean the Son was manufactured at some point; “before all worlds” (literally, before the ages) places his generation outside of time altogether. The Son is not the first thing God made; he is eternally from the Father, as light is from a flame that was never without it.6

“Light of Light, very God of very God, begotten, not made.” This is the “X-from-X” formula, and each phrase is a scalpel. Light of Light: a ray shares the very nature of the light it streams from—not a lesser, borrowed brightness. Very God of very God (“true God from true God”) matters because the word “god” was slippery: Scripture itself calls angels and judges “gods” in a lower sense (Psalm 82:6). The creed insists the Son is God in the full and only real sense, not an exalted deputy. As the patristics scholar John Behr observes, “true God from true God” was precisely designed to exclude any intermediate, second-rank divinity between the Creator and creation.7

Note the careful grammar: God from God. This is not two gods, one sourced from another like a lamp lit off a lamp. It is the one God existing eternally as Father and Son—the Son deriving from the Father not in time or in rank but in the mystery of eternal relation, sharing wholly the one divine being.

The Word That Won: homoousios

Then comes the decisive phrase: “being of one substance with the Father”—in Greek, homoousios, “same essence.” This single word was the hinge of the whole controversy. To say the Son is homoousios with the Father is to say he is not like God or close to God but is, in his very being, everything the Father is except being the Father.

The word was contested even among the orthodox, and honesty requires saying why. It is not found in the Bible. It had troubling baggage—an earlier council at Antioch (c. 268) had rejected it in dealing with Paul of Samosata, and Gnostic writers had used it loosely. So the Arians attacked it as unbiblical novelty. Popular retellings love the image of the church split by a single letter: homoousios (“same”) against homoiousios (“similar”), one Greek iota deciding everything. That picture is vivid but simplified. “Similar in essence” was in fact the mid-century banner of a later, moderate party (the Homoiousians) trying to find middle ground after 325—not Arius’s own slogan; the strict Arians went further still, calling the Son unlike the Father.8 The great defender of the chosen word was Athanasius, who argued that if the Son is truly the Father’s image and radiance, then he must share the Father’s very nature, not merely resemble it. “Similar” would have left the door open to a lesser Christ; “same” slammed it shut.

“Begotten, not made.” Here is the whole dispute in three words. Everything in existence falls on one side or the other of a single line: it is either begotten (the eternal Son, of the Father’s own being) or made (creation, called from nothing). Arius put the Son on the creature side. The creed puts him, unambiguously, on the Creator side—“by whom all things were made.” There is no intermediate category and no intermediary being.

“For Us and for Our Salvation”—the Incarnation

Now the creed pivots from the eternal to the historical, and the pace quickens into a run of verbs: came down, was incarnate, was made man, was crucified, suffered, was buried, rose, ascended, will come again. If the Trinity is the skeleton of the creed, the Incarnation is its beating heart—and notice that it is anchored to us: “who for us men, and for our salvation.”

“Came down from heaven.” This is the language of poetry, not geography. The eternal Son did not travel a distance or leave a location; he cannot, since he fills all things. He “came down” by taking up our humanity while remaining fully God. Augustine captured the paradox by noting that the Son “came” to a world in which he was already wholly present.9 And Gregory of Nazianzus fenced the mystery against a dangerous misreading: in becoming man the Son did not shed or shrink his deity—“What He was He continued to be; what He was not He took to Himself.”10 This guards against the later idea that Christ “emptied himself” of divine attributes; the creed knows no half-God. He added humanity; he subtracted nothing.

“Incarnate by the Holy Ghost of the Virgin Mary, and was made man.” The Incarnation is the joint work of the Trinity—the Spirit’s overshadowing (Luke 1:35)—and it is fully human: born of a real mother, in a real body. The One who upholds the galaxies became a nursing infant.

“Crucified also for us under Pontius Pilate.” Of all the names available, the creed reaches for a mid-level Roman bureaucrat. Why? Because Pilate is a date stamp. The gospel is not a timeless myth but an event that happened at a nameable moment under a namable official (cf. 1 Timothy 6:13). “He suffered and was buried” presses the point further: the Son of God went all the way down—real pain, real death, a real grave—in complete solidarity with us.11

“The third day he rose again, according to the Scriptures… and ascended into heaven… and he shall come again, with glory… whose kingdom shall have no end.” That closing clause has a specific target. A bishop named Marcellus of Ancyra had read 1 Corinthians 15:24–28—where the Son “hands over the kingdom to God the Father”—to mean that the Son’s reign, even his distinct existence, would one day dissolve back into God. That is a modalist-leaning error, and the council blocked it with four words: his kingdom shall have no end (echoing Luke 1:33).12

“And in the Holy Ghost, the Lord and Giver of Life”

The article on the Spirit is where the creed of 381 most enlarged the creed of 325, which had said only, tersely, “And in the Holy Ghost.” In the intervening decades a new party had arisen—the Pneumatomachi, “Spirit-fighters” (traditionally called Macedonians)—who granted the Son’s deity but denied the Spirit’s, treating him as a created force or an exalted angel. Basil of Caesarea answered them in his treatise On the Holy Spirit (c. 375), and Gregory of Nazianzus pressed the point at the council itself.13

The creed’s reply is beautifully indirect. It never uses the disputed word homoousios of the Spirit; instead it confesses him by his works and his worship. He is “the Lord”—the same divine title given the Son. He is the “Giver of life,” a work only God does. And he “with the Father and the Son together is worshiped and glorified”—the decisive stroke. In Scripture, worship belongs to God alone (Exodus 20:3–5; Revelation 22:9). To say the Spirit is co-worshiped with the Father and Son is to confess his full deity as surely as any philosophical term could. “Who spake by the prophets” adds that this same Spirit is the author of Scripture—the God who inspired the book is the God the book reveals.

“And the Son”: the Filioque

Here we reach the creed’s most painful line—the one Western Christians add and Eastern Christians do not. The original 381 text says the Spirit “proceedeth from the Father.” Somewhere in the Latin West a phrase was added: “from the Father and the Son”—in Latin, filioque. It became one of the flashpoints of the schism between East and West in 1054, and Apologia Daily takes no side on it; here we only lay out the honest history.14

The facts are these. First, filioque was not in the creed the council of 381 approved; that is simply a matter of record. Second, it entered gradually through local Western usage—the regional Councils of Toledo in Spain are the usual landmarks—and only much later spread to Rome. Third, the East’s objection has two layers: a procedural one (no local church may amend an ecumenical creed on its own authority) and a theological one (they fear it blurs the Father’s unique role as the one source within the Godhead). The West replies that it means only to honor the deep unity of Son and Spirit, and to guard the Son’s full divinity—never to make two sources.

Two things are worth holding onto. One is that a serious bridge has long been on the table: the formula “from the Father through the Son,” defended in a Letter to Marinus attributed to Maximus the Confessor (its authenticity is debated), which many on both sides have found they can affirm.15 The other is that thoughtful voices in both traditions have judged the wording an insufficient reason for the churches to have broken communion—the Reformed theologian Francis Turretin, for one, held that the point, rightly understood, ought never to have occasioned schism, while on the Eastern side the very defenders of “through the Son” sought the same reconciliation.16 This is a genuine, still-unresolved intra-Christian question—and the shared core of the creed does not hinge on it.

The Final Articles

The creed closes with four short confessions. “One holy catholic and apostolic Church”—here “catholic” keeps its ancient meaning, “universal” (from kata holon, “according to the whole”), not the name of one communion; the church is one, set apart, spanning all places, and built on the apostles—their teaching, and, for some traditions, their succession.

“One baptism for the remission of sins” confesses the single, unrepeatable washing that marks entry into Christ. Christians read the mechanics of that line differently—some traditions hold that baptism itself conveys the grace it signifies, while many Protestants and Baptists recite the creed wholeheartedly while understanding baptism as the sign and seal of a forgiveness received by faith. The creed states the shared confession and leaves that dispute to be worked out elsewhere; it does not settle it.17

“We look for the resurrection of the dead, and the life of the world to come.” The creed ends where the gospel ends—not with the soul’s escape from the body, but with the body’s redemption: a new creation, and life without end. “Amen” is not a full stop but a signature—“so be it, and so we stake ourselves.”

What the Creed Does—and Does Not—Do

The Nicene Creed is not a proof. It does not, by itself, demonstrate that God is triune or that Jesus rose; it summarizes what the church, reading Scripture, confesses to be true, and the demonstrations live in the biblical and historical arguments the rest of this library takes up. Nor does it settle every later dispute—the two natures of Christ (Chalcedon, 451), the filioque, baptism, church order. It draws the boundary lines of the faith, not every line within them.

What it does is remarkable enough. It fixes, in words worn smooth by sixteen centuries of use, the irreducible center of Christianity: one God; the Son true God and true man, of one substance with the Father, crucified and risen for us; the Spirit, Lord and life-giver, worshiped with them. It is the widest circle Catholics, the Orthodox, and Protestants still stand inside together. To say it slowly, as an act of worship rather than a checklist, is to discover that nearly every phrase was bought at a price—and that the faith it guards is not an abstraction but a Person: the Word who remained what he was, and took up what he was not, for us and for our salvation.

Frequently asked questions

Is the Nicene Creed the same as the creed written in 325?

Not quite. The Council of Nicaea (325) produced a shorter creed ending in anathemas against Arianism. The version Christians recite today is the expanded form approved at the Council of Constantinople (381), which enlarged the article on the Holy Spirit and dropped the anathemas—so it is properly called the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed.

Why does the creed use non-biblical words like “of one substance”?

Because the heretics used biblical words and emptied them of meaning—calling Jesus “God” and “Son” while denying he was truly divine. When both sides quote the same verses and mean opposite things, the church has to state plainly which reading it confesses. The technical terms clarify Scripture rather than adding to it.

Do all Christians say the creed the same way?

Almost. The one longstanding difference is the filioque—the Western addition “and the Son” to the clause on the Spirit’s procession, which Eastern churches do not use. It remains a real, unresolved question between the traditions, but the creed’s shared core—one God; the Son true God and true man; the Spirit worshiped with the Father and Son—is confessed by Catholics, the Orthodox, and Protestants alike.
