Did the Universe Begin? The Kalam Cosmological Argument

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Free preview voice

A question that refuses to stay small

Ask a child where the world came from and you will get a chain of "and before that?" questions that does not naturally end. The adult version is no less stubborn. Either the universe has always existed, stretching back through an unbounded ocean of prior moments, or it had a first moment — a beginning before which there was, quite literally, nothing physical at all. There is no third option, and almost everything we believe about reality hangs on which it is. If the cosmos is a self-sufficient, eternal brute fact, then it is the deepest thing there is, and the religious instinct that something stands behind it is a sentimental error. But if the universe began — if space, time, matter, and energy all switched on together a finite time ago — then the most natural question in the world becomes unavoidable: what brought it into being? The Kalam cosmological argument is a disciplined attempt to take that question seriously without cheating, and to follow it wherever it leads.

The argument is ancient, but it is not a museum piece. It sits today at the intersection of medieval theology, contemporary analytic philosophy, and front-line cosmology, and it has drawn the sustained fire of some of the sharpest atheist philosophers alive. That alone makes it worth mastering carefully — not as a debating trick, but as an honest inquiry into the most basic feature of the world we find ourselves in.

The argument, stated with care

In its modern form the Kalam is a model of economy:

Premise 1. Whatever begins to exist has a cause of its existence.
Premise 2. The universe began to exist.
Conclusion. Therefore, the universe has a cause of its existence.

The logic is valid: if both premises are true, the conclusion follows with the force of arithmetic. Notice what the argument does not say. It does not claim that "everything has a cause" — a careless formulation that would invite the obvious retort, "then what caused the cause?" It restricts itself to things that begin to exist. That precision is not a dodge bolted on after the fact; it has been built into the argument from its origins.

The word "kalam" is Arabic for "speech" or "discourse," and it came to name the tradition of medieval Islamic theology that pressed this reasoning hardest. The Persian theologian al-Ghazali (1058–1111) gave the argument its classic shape in The Incoherence of the Philosophers, formulating it in a sentence that still sounds modern: "Every being which begins has a cause for its beginning; now the world is a being which begins; therefore, it possesses a cause for its beginning."1 Al-Ghazali was reacting against Muslim Aristotelians who, following the Greek master, held the universe to be eternal and beginningless.

But the deepest root runs earlier, to a Christian. In the sixth century, the Alexandrian philosopher John Philoponus turned Aristotle's own commitments against him. Aristotle had banned a completed, actually infinite quantity from the physical world; Philoponus, in works such as Against Aristotle, on the Eternity of the World (c. 530), argued that an eternal past would require exactly such a completed infinite of elapsed events — and so, by Aristotle's own lights, the world must have begun.2 This finite-past reasoning passed into Islamic kalam, then into Jewish and Christian scholasticism, and finally fell dormant for centuries. Its modern revival is owed almost single-handedly to the philosopher William Lane Craig, whose 1979 monograph and decades of subsequent work — culminating in a 100-page chapter with the physicist James Sinclair in The Blackwell Companion to Natural Theology (2009) — rebuilt the argument with the tools of analytic philosophy and contemporary physics.3

Premise 1: out of nothing, nothing comes

The first premise looks almost too obvious to defend, and that is its strength. The principle that being does not arise from non-being — ex nihilo nihil fit — is among the most secure intuitions we have. If things could simply pop into existence uncaused, out of nothing, it becomes inexplicable why we do not see it happening constantly and everywhere: why bicycles and Beethoven symphonies and herds of zebra do not flicker into being in midair. The fact that reality is orderly rather than a lottery of spontaneous somethings is itself evidence that coming-to-be requires a cause. Premise 1 is also confirmed relentlessly in experience and never once falsified — a track record that makes it more reasonable to affirm than to deny.

The most serious scientific challenge points to quantum mechanics. In the quantum vacuum, "virtual particles" appear and vanish in ways that are not deterministically caused; doesn't this show that things can begin without a cause? The reply is precise, not evasive. The quantum vacuum is emphatically not "nothing." It is a real physical entity — a sea of fluctuating energy, governed by the laws of quantum field theory, with a rich structure that determines exactly which particles can emerge and how often. A particle arising from the vacuum is no more an instance of something-from-nothing than a wave arising from the ocean. The objection trades on an equivocation: "nothing" in the physicist's casual usage means empty space with its quantum fields, while "nothing" in Premise 1 means non-being — no space, no fields, no laws, no anything. Against that latter, genuine nothing, the principle stands unscathed.

Premise 2: the case for a beginning

The controversial premise is the second. It is defended along two independent lines — one philosophical, one scientific — which is part of what makes the argument robust: even if one line failed entirely, the other would remain.

The philosophical arguments against an infinite past

The first philosophical argument holds that an actual infinite — an infinite collection existing all at once in reality — cannot exist, because it generates absurdities. The mathematician David Hilbert illustrated the trouble with a famous thought experiment. Imagine a hotel with infinitely many rooms, all occupied. A new guest arrives; the manager simply moves the guest in room 1 to room 2, room 2 to room 3, and so on forever, freeing room 1 — so a "full" hotel takes another guest. Worse, infinitely many new guests can be accommodated the same way. And if the guests in all the odd-numbered rooms check out, infinitely many have left, yet exactly as many remain as before.4 These are not mere curiosities; they are signs, the argument runs, that an actually infinite number of real things cannot be instantiated in the concrete world. If so, the series of past events cannot be actually infinite — so the past is finite, and the universe began.

The second philosophical argument grants, for the sake of debate, that an actual infinite could exist, and still finds a problem: you cannot form an actual infinite by adding one member after another. The past is not a static set; it is built up successively, one event at a time. But no matter how long you go on adding moments, you never complete an infinite collection — you can always add one more. To arrive at the present moment by traversing an infinite series of prior moments would be to complete the uncompletable, like counting down from infinity and finally reaching zero. Since we have arrived at today, the series we traversed to get here must have been finite. Critics such as Morriston reply that this picture quietly smuggles in a starting point from which to count: an infinite past has no first moment to set out from, so the image of "counting down from infinity" may beg the very question at issue — a reply the defender must answer rather than dismiss.

These arguments are contested, and a careful defender concedes that the actual-infinite premise is exactly where critics push hardest. The strongest reply is that Cantorian set theory shows the actual infinite to be perfectly coherent mathematically — Hilbert's Hotel is counterintuitive, not contradictory — so the defender's claim must be narrowed to this: an actual infinite cannot be concretely instantiated in the physical world. Whether that metaphysical claim holds, given the mathematical consistency of the infinite, is precisely the disputed hinge. The arguments are offered, then, not as knock-down demonstrations but as serious reasons, drawn from the nature of infinity and succession, to doubt that the past stretches back forever.

The scientific corroboration — stated honestly

Independently, modern cosmology points the same direction. The standard Big Bang model traces the expansion of the universe back toward a finite past boundary some 13.8 billion years ago. More striking is a result from 2003. The cosmologists Arvind Borde, Alan Guth, and Alexander Vilenkin proved a theorem — "Inflationary Spacetimes Are Not Past-Complete" — showing that any universe which has, on average, been expanding throughout its history cannot be extended infinitely into the past; its history must have a past boundary.5 Crucially, the theorem requires no assumptions about the energy content of the universe, which makes it far more general than earlier singularity theorems, and it applies even to most multiverse and eternal-inflation scenarios.

Here intellectual honesty is essential, because the argument is sometimes overstated. The Borde–Guth–Vilenkin theorem is a result about classical spacetime geometry. It establishes that the classical description breaks down at a past boundary; it does not, by itself, tell us what lies at that boundary — a genuine beginning, a quantum-gravity regime we do not yet understand, or something else. Vilenkin's own conclusions are strong but measured. In his book Many Worlds in One he writes that "with the proof now in place, cosmologists can no longer hide behind the possibility of a past-eternal universe. There is no escape: they have to face the problem of a cosmic beginning."6 In a later essay he is careful to say the answer to "Did the universe have a beginning?" is, "It probably did," adding that "we have no viable models of an eternal universe" — and, candidly, "a deep mystery remains."7 A 2012 paper with Audrey Mithani surveyed the three leading models proposed to avoid a beginning — eternal inflation, cyclic universes, and the "emergent" universe — and argued that none of them can in fact be past-eternal.8

And yet one of the theorem's own co-authors is not persuaded the universe began. Alan Guth has expressed sympathy for models — such as the Carroll–Chen proposal — on which the universe is past-eternal after all. The right conclusion, then, is sober: the science offers powerful corroboration that the universe as we know it had a beginning, not an airtight proof. This is precisely why the philosophical arguments matter independently — and why a careful case leans on convergence rather than on any single result.

A third consideration, the thermodynamic one, reinforces the picture. The second law of thermodynamics tells us that the universe's usable energy is steadily running down toward equilibrium — a uniform "heat death." But if the universe had already existed for an infinite time, it would presumably have reached that featureless equilibrium an infinite time ago. That it has not — that the cosmic clock is still winding down — suggests the clock was wound up a finite time ago. The details are genuinely disputed: in an expanding universe the maximum possible entropy rises over time, so some cosmologists, notably Sean Carroll, argue that equilibrium need never be reached. The thermodynamic consideration is therefore best treated as suggestive rather than decisive — but the low-entropy past we actually observe still sits awkwardly with a literally infinite history.

From a cause to a Creator

Suppose the argument succeeds: the universe has a cause. What can be said about that cause, working purely from the premises, before importing any religious commitment?

A great deal, it turns out. The cause brought all of physical reality into being — all space, all time, all matter and energy. Therefore the cause cannot itself be spatial, temporal, or material; it must be spaceless, timeless, and immaterial, transcending the very framework it produced. It must be unimaginably powerful, having brought the entire cosmos into existence. And it must be uncaused — for if it began to exist it would, by Premise 1, require a cause of its own, and we have simply moved the question back a step. The cause of the universe is itself beginningless.

One further inference, more tentative but powerful, points toward a personal cause — a mind rather than a mechanism. Two considerations converge. First, whatever is timeless, spaceless, and immaterial seems to fall into one of only two categories we can conceive: an abstract object (like a number or a set) or an unembodied mind. But abstract objects, by their nature, stand in no causal relations — the number seven does not do anything. By elimination, the cause is plausibly a mind. Second, there is the puzzle of how a timeless cause produces a temporal effect at all. If the cause were a changeless set of sufficient conditions — like water freezing the instant the temperature drops below zero — then wherever the cause existed, the effect would exist too, and a timeless cause would yield an equally timeless, beginningless universe. But the universe is not beginningless. The most natural explanation for how a changeless, eternal cause gives rise to an effect with a beginning is a free act of will — an agent who can bring about a new state of affairs without any prior determining change. This is agent causation, and it is the signature of a person.

These last steps are best understood as inference to the best explanation, not deductive proof. One can resist them. But taken together they make a personal Creator the most reasonable account — a transcendent mind that freely brought the universe into being.

The strongest objections, fairly stated

An argument is only as good as its performance against its ablest critics. Here are the most serious, each in its strongest form.

"What caused God?"

The most common objection charges special pleading: if everything needs a cause, then God does too, and stopping at God is an arbitrary exception. But this misreads the first premise. Kalam never claims that everything has a cause; it claims that everything which begins to exist has a cause. God, by definition, did not begin to exist. So He is not an unexplained exception to a universal rule — He simply falls outside the scope of a rule that was about beginnings all along. The question "what caused God?" is, as defenders like to put it, rather like asking what the bachelor's wife is doing this evening: it presupposes what the case has already ruled out.

Wes Morriston on the causal principle

A far more sophisticated challenge comes from the philosopher Wes Morriston, a fair-minded and persistent critic. In "Must the Beginning of the Universe Have a Personal Cause?" Morriston questions whether we really have a rational intuition supporting Premise 1 as applied to the beginning of the universe itself.9 Our confidence that things have causes, he argues, is built up from experience of events within time and space — one thing emerging from prior materials in an existing world. The absolute beginning of all space and time is utterly unlike that. So why assume the principle reaches that far? Morriston also presses on the move to a personal cause, arguing that the very idea of a timeless agent "timelessly willing" a temporal first moment is harder to make coherent than defenders admit. This is the objection worth taking most seriously: the defender must argue that the causal principle is grounded not merely in inductive habit but in a metaphysical insight about being and non-being that holds regardless of whether the "beginning" is inside time or of time.

Graham Oppy and the brute-fact alternative

The Australian philosopher Graham Oppy, perhaps the most formidable contemporary atheist philosopher of religion, mounts a different attack. In Arguing about Gods and elsewhere, Oppy contends that the naturalist is under no rational compulsion to accept Kalam's conclusion.10 He questions whether the causal principle has the warrant its defenders claim, and he argues that even if the universe had a first state, the naturalist may simply take that initial state as a brute fact — something that exists without further explanation — or appeal to an impersonal natural cause rather than a mind. On Oppy's view, theism and naturalism each ultimately rest on some unexplained starting point, so theism enjoys no decisive advantage. The defender's reply is that a personal cause is a better stopping point than a brute, unexplained physical state precisely because it offers an explanation — an agent's intention — rather than a refusal to explain; but Oppy's challenge keeps the debate genuinely open and forces the theist to defend, not assume, the superiority of the personal hypothesis.

Quantum cosmology and the no-boundary proposal

A scientific objection holds that quantum cosmology dissolves the beginning altogether. The most famous version is the Hartle–Hawking "no-boundary proposal," in which, near the earliest era, ordinary time is replaced by a fourth dimension of space, so that the universe has no sharp boundary or first moment — it is finite in the past but without an edge, "like the surface of the earth" with no place that is the "beginning." Stephen Hawking drew the theological moral himself in A Brief History of Time: "if the universe is really completely self-contained, having no boundary or edge, it would have neither beginning nor end: it would simply be. What place, then, for a creator?"11 The reply has several strands. The model is speculative and depends on the contested device of "imaginary time," whose physical interpretation is disputed even among physicists. More importantly, even on the no-boundary model the universe still has a finite past and is not eternal; it still begins to exist, in the relevant sense, even if there is no first instant in the way we ordinarily picture one. And smoothing the geometry of the earliest moment does not by itself supply a cause for why there is a finite cosmos at all rather than nothing.

"The cause needn't be the God of theism"

A final objection is simply true, and a careful defender should say so plainly. Even granting everything, the argument delivers a spaceless, timeless, immaterial, enormously powerful, and plausibly personal cause of the universe. That is a long way toward theism — but it is not yet the triune God of the Nicene Creed, nor even a morally good or worship-worthy being. Kalam, on its own, is consistent with deism. This is not a flaw; it is honesty about scope. No single argument of natural theology was ever meant to carry the whole weight. Kalam is one strand in a cumulative case, alongside arguments from fine-tuning, moral value, contingency, and ultimately the historical evidence for the resurrection. Its job is more modest and more foundational: to show that the universe is not a self-explaining brute fact, and that the door to a transcendent cause stands open.

The honest state of the debate

Where does the argument stand today? Among philosophers, it remains live and genuinely contested. Premise 1 is widely regarded as plausible, though critics like Morriston have raised real questions about its scope. Premise 2 is the battleground: the philosophical arguments against an actual infinite have able defenders and able critics, and the scientific evidence, while strongly suggestive of a beginning, is corroborative rather than conclusive — a point the more careful proponents readily grant. The conceptual step from cause to personal Creator is the most contested of all, and is best held as a reasoned inference rather than a proof. The Kalam has not ended the conversation; it has sharpened it, and forced naturalists to articulate exactly where they get off the train.

What Kalam does and does not establish

Stated without exaggeration, the Kalam cosmological argument makes a serious, defensible case that the universe began to exist and therefore has a transcendent cause — one that is spaceless, timeless, immaterial, beginningless, enormously powerful, and most plausibly a personal mind. It does not, by itself, prove the existence of the God of any particular religion, and it does not silence every objection; the best critics remain in the field, and an honest advocate names them rather than waving them away. What it accomplishes is to put the deepest question back on the table and to show that "the universe just exists, inexplicably" is not the only rational option — and perhaps not the most reasonable one. For the believer, it is not the whole house but a load-bearing wall: a reminder, offered "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15), that the heavens still declare something, and that the oldest question a child can ask may have the most consequential answer of all.
