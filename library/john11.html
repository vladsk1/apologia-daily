"In the Beginning Was the Word": John 1:1 and the Eternal Pre-existence of Christ

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Three Clauses That Refuse to Be Small

Most of the New Testament begins where you would expect a story about Jesus to begin: a manger, a baptism, a genealogy. The Gospel of John begins before all of that — before there was a beginning at all. "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."1 In a single breath, the Evangelist reaches back past creation, past time, and locates Jesus of Nazareth there. The claim is staggering, and the church has spent twenty centuries unpacking it. The Council of Nicaea (325) leaned on it; Arius and Athanasius fought over it; the translators of the New World Translation rendered it differently than nearly everyone else, and the dispute over a single missing Greek article still draws blood at doorsteps and in seminar rooms.

Why does it matter? Because if John 1:1 means what the historic church has taken it to mean, then the man who wept at Lazarus's tomb is not a creature, however exalted, but the eternal God in person. Everything downstream — atonement, worship, the Trinity — turns on whether the Word "was God." This essay defends the classical reading, explains the Greek for the non-specialist, and answers the strongest objections fairly, by name.

The Prologue and Its Architecture

John 1:1–18 is a self-contained overture, often called the Prologue. It moves in concentric arcs: from the Word's relation to God and creation (vv. 1–5), to John the Baptist's witness (vv. 6–8), to the Word's coming into the world and its rejection and reception (vv. 9–13), and finally to the climactic announcement, "the Word became flesh and dwelt among us" (v. 14).2 The structure matters because pre-existence is not asserted once and dropped; it is the load-bearing beam of the whole passage. The Word who "was" in the beginning (v. 1) is the one through whom "all things were made" (v. 3), and the same Word who "became flesh" (v. 14). Eternity, agency in creation, and incarnation are welded to a single subject.

Clause by clause

The opening verse has three clauses, and each carries a distinct freight. First: "In the beginning was the Word." The verb is the imperfect ēn ("was"), not the aorist egeneto ("came to be") that John uses of created things in verse 3 and of John the Baptist in verse 6. The Word does not come into being at the beginning; the Word already is when the beginning happens. This is the grammar of eternal pre-existence.

Second: "and the Word was with God" (pros ton theon). The preposition pros with the accusative suggests relationship and orientation — the Word is face-to-face with God, distinct from him. Whatever John means in the third clause, he does not mean that the Word simply is the Father. There are two who can be "with" one another.

Third, and most contested: "and the Word was God" — kai theos ēn ho logos. Here the Evangelist holds two truths in tension. The Word is distinct from God (clause two) and yet the Word is God (clause three). The orthodox reading does not collapse that tension; it confesses it.

The Greek of the Third Clause, for the Layperson

The whole modern debate lives in the word order and the article. In Greek, "the" (the definite article, ho) does a great deal of work. In clause three, theos ("God") has no article and stands before the verb: kai theos ēn ho logos. Word for word the order runs "and—God—was—the—Word," but because ho logos ("the Word") carries the article it is the subject, and the anarthrous theos is the predicate — so the sense is "and the Word was God."

In 1933, the grammarian Ernest Cadman Colwell observed a pattern: definite predicate nouns that precede the verb usually lack the article.3 "Colwell's rule" was soon misapplied in reverse — as if an anarthrous predicate before the verb must therefore be definite, proving theos means "the God." That overreaches; Colwell's rule does not run backward, and many scholars who love the orthodox conclusion have leaned on it too hard.

The more careful work was done by Philip B. Harner in a 1973 article in the Journal of Biblical Literature.4 Harner studied anarthrous predicate nouns preceding the verb and argued that their primary force is usually qualitative — they describe the nature of the subject. His paraphrase of John 1:1c is precise: "the logos… had the nature of theos."5 Harner judged that the qualitative force here is "so prominent that the noun cannot be regarded as definite." His own preferred sense is that the Word "had the same nature as God" — neither the bare "a god" (which would make the Word merely one divine being among others) nor a flat "the Word was God" read as identity with the Father, but a statement that the Word fully shares the divine nature while remaining personally distinct.6 Daniel B. Wallace, in his standard reference grammar, reaches the same verdict: in John 1:1c theos is "neither definite nor indefinite" but qualitative — the Word possesses the full nature of deity that the Father possesses, while remaining personally distinct from him.7

This is the elegant payoff. Had John written ho theos ēn ho logos (with the article on theos), he would have said the Word is identical to the Father — the heresy later called modalism. Had he written theos ēn ho logos intending the indefinite "a god," he would have made the Word a lesser divine being — a kind of polytheism. What John actually wrote threads the needle: the Word shares the very nature of God without being the person of the Father.

Where the "Word" Came From

John did not coin Logos in a vacuum. Two streams fed it. The Greek stream runs through Heraclitus and the Stoics, for whom the logos was the rational principle ordering the cosmos. The Jewish stream is deeper and, most scholars now judge, primary. In the Hebrew Scriptures, God's word creates ("by the word of the LORD the heavens were made," Psalm 33:6) and his Wisdom is personified as present at creation (Proverbs 8:22–31). Intertestamental Judaism developed this further: in the Wisdom of Solomon, Wisdom is "a breath of the power of God" (7:25), and the philosopher Philo of Alexandria spoke of the Logos as God's agent in creation and revelation — though for Philo the Logos remained a mediating figure, not God himself.8

John takes this rich vocabulary and does something no Jewish writer had done: he identifies the Word not merely as God's instrument but as God, and then says the Word "became flesh" — a particular man, in history. That is the hinge. The categories were Jewish; the claim was new.

Pre-existence Across the Gospel

The Prologue is not an isolated flourish. Pre-existence echoes through John. In 1:3, "all things were made through him, and without him was not any thing made that was made" — the Word is on the Creator's side of the line between Creator and creation. In 8:58, Jesus says, "Truly, truly, I say to you, before Abraham was, I am" (prin Abraam genesthai egō eimi), and his hearers pick up stones — a reaction most naturally explained by a claim that provoked a charge of blasphemy, and, on a common reading, an echo of the divine "I am" (Exodus 3:14, and more precisely the egō eimi of Isaiah 40–55).9 In 17:5, Jesus prays to be glorified "with the glory that I had with you before the world existed." The Prologue's grammar is confirmed by Jesus's own words in the body of the Gospel.

The Strongest Objections, Stated Fairly

1. The Jehovah's Witnesses and "the Word was a god"

The New World Translation renders John 1:1c, "and the Word was a god."10 This is not arbitrary, and Witnesses who defend it deserve a real hearing. Their argument: theos here lacks the article, whereas ton theon in clause two has it; Greek can use the article to mark definiteness; therefore the anarthrous theos should be read indefinitely — "a god" — preserving the distinction between the Father (ho theos) and the Word. They can point to John 1:18's "only-begotten" language and to a theology in which the Son is God's first and greatest creation. It is a coherent, textually motivated reading, and it is held by sincere people.

The mainstream grammatical reply is that the absence of the article does not by itself make a noun indefinite, and that the construction here points to quality, not indefiniteness. Harner's study showed that anarthrous predicate nouns before the verb are predominantly qualitative; on his analysis "a god" misreads the grammar.6 Bruce Metzger judged the rendering "a frightful mistranslation," noting that the absence of the article in this position does not make the predicate indefinite when the context does not require it.11 Wallace concurs that the qualitative force is most likely.7 One should concede a fair point to the Witnesses: the article-less theos does safeguard a distinction — the Word is not the Father. But the distinction John draws is between two persons sharing one divine nature, not between God and a demigod. It is also worth noting, gently, that the NWT inserts "a" before "god" but does not do so consistently in other anarthrous-theos texts, which is the inconsistency most critics press — though defenders reply that context, not a uniform rule, should govern each rendering.

2. The Arian / subordinationist reading

The fourth-century presbyter Arius held that the Son, though pre-existent and exalted above all creatures, was himself created — "there was when he was not." Modern subordinationists echo this: John 1:1 makes the Word divine in some derived sense, not the eternal God. The strongest form of the argument leans on John 14:28 ("the Father is greater than I") and on the genuinely subordinate language of the Son's mission throughout John.

The reply, classically, is that the Son's subordination in John is functionalFunctional, not ontological, subordinationIs sayingIn his incarnate mission the Son willingly submits to the Father's will — an economic/functional ordering of roles.Not sayingNot that the Son is lesser in being, made, or later than the Father (Arianism). He is homoousios — co-equal and co-eternal, sharing one divine nature. (in his role and incarnation), not ontological (in his nature). The verb ēn in 1:1 — eternal "was," not created "came to be" — rules out a beginning to the Word's existence, which is precisely what Arianism requires. Athanasius pressed this against Arius: a creature cannot be the agent through whom "all things were made" (1:3) without being made through himself.12 The Nicene fathers concluded that the Son is homoousios — of the same nature as the Father — and John 1:1, read with its imperfect verb and qualitative predicate, is one of the texts that drove them there.

3. Bart Ehrman: high Christology as a late development

The most formidable modern objection comes not from translation but from history. In How Jesus Became God (2014), Bart D. Ehrman argues that belief in Jesus's divinity evolved: the earliest Christians held an "exaltation" Christology (Jesus was a human exalted to divine status at the resurrection), which was progressively pushed back — to his baptism, then his birth, then eternity — until John, the latest Gospel, finally portrays a pre-existent, incarnate divine Word.13 On this account John 1:1 is the end of a long development, not the historical bedrock; the divine Logos is a theological achievement of the late first century, not a datum about Jesus himself.

Ehrman's thesis must be taken seriously; he is a serious historian, and the Gospels plainly differ in how explicitly they state Christ's deity. The honest concession is that the explicitness of John 1:1 has no exact parallel in Mark. But the claim that high Christology is late faces a powerful published rebuttal. Larry W. Hurtado, in Lord Jesus Christ (2003), documents that the worship of Jesus — devotion of a kind Jews reserved for God alone — erupts astonishingly early, within the first two decades, well before any Gospel.14 Richard Bauckham, in Jesus and the God of Israel (2008), argues that "the earliest Christology was already the highest Christology," locating Jesus within "the unique identity" of the God of Israel by way of pre-Pauline confessions and the Christ-hymn of Philippians 2.15 If Hurtado and Bauckham are right, John 1:1 does not invent a high Christology; it gives mature literary voice to one that was there from the start. Ehrman, in turn, replies that worship and exaltation are not the same as the metaphysical incarnation John teaches — and there the scholarly conversation genuinely continues.

The State of the Scholarship

On the grammar, there is a broad consensus that theos in 1:1c is qualitative and affirms the full deity of the Word; this is the position of Harner, Wallace, Metzger, and the standard reference grammars. The minority who defend "a god" are concentrated outside the academic mainstream, though their grammatical objection (that anarthrous nouns can be indefinite) is not nonsense and deserves the careful, not dismissive, answer given above. On the history, the field is more divided: the "early high Christology" school (Hurtado, Bauckham, Hengel) is strong but not unanimous, and critics such as Ehrman and, in different ways, James D. G. Dunn — who in Christology in the Making argued that full incarnational pre-existence is clearly present only in John — represent a real and respectable dissent.16 A reader should know that the dating and development of Christology is genuinely contested terrain, even where the meaning of John's own words is not.

What This Does and Doesn't Establish

John 1:1, carefully read, establishes that this author, writing this Gospel, confesses the Word as eternally pre-existent, personally distinct from the Father, and fully sharing the divine nature — and that he sees the man Jesus as that Word enfleshed. That is a great deal. It is enough to anchor the Nicene confession in the text and to answer both the "a god" and the Arian readings on grammatical and contextual grounds.

It does not, by itself, settle the historical question of how quickly the whole church arrived at this confession; that argument requires Paul's letters, the early hymns, and the worship evidence that Hurtado and Bauckham marshal. Nor is John 1:1 the entire New Testament case for Christ's deity — it is one strand, woven together with Colossians 1, Hebrews 1, Philippians 2, and Thomas's cry, "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28). What John 1:1 offers is the strand stated most plainly: in the beginning was the Word, and the Word was God, and the Word became flesh. The whole of Christian faith has found it worth a lifetime's study — offered, as Peter urged, with gentleness and respect to all who weigh it.

Frequently asked questions

Doesn't John 1:1 say the Word was 'a god' since the Greek has no 'the' before God?

Jehovah's Witnesses reasonably note that in the third clause theos lacks the article, unlike ton theon in clause two, and take this to mean 'a god.' But absence of the article does not by itself make a noun indefinite. Philip Harner's 1973 study showed such preverbal predicate nouns are usually qualitative, describing nature: the Word had the very nature of God, fully divine, while remaining personally distinct from the Father.

If the Word was 'with God,' how can the Word also be God without contradiction?

John holds two truths in tension rather than collapsing them. Clause two, 'the Word was with God' (pros ton theon), marks the Word as face-to-face with God and personally distinct. Clause three affirms the Word shares the divine nature. Had John written the article on theos he'd have taught modalism; had he meant 'a god,' polytheism. Instead the Word fully shares God's nature without being the person of the Father.

Isn't Jesus being called God just a late invention by the last Gospel writer?

Bart Ehrman argues high Christology evolved, with John's divine Word the end of a long development. That deserves serious engagement, and John 1:1's explicitness has no exact parallel in Mark. But Larry Hurtado and Richard Bauckham document that devotion to Jesus reserved for God alone erupts within the first two decades, before any Gospel. On their case, John gives mature voice to a high Christology present from the start.
