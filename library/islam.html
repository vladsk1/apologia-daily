Islam and Christianity: Can Both Be True?

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

More than half the human race professes either Islam or Christianity. The two faiths share a great deal—one Creator, prophets, angels, scripture, a coming judgment, a reverence for Jesus and his mother Mary that may surprise outsiders. A Muslim and a Christian can pray side by side for the poor and agree on more than they disagree. But beneath that overlap lies a hard logical fact: on the questions that matter most, the two cannot both be right. Christianity stands or falls on a crucified and risen Lord who is God incarnate. Islam denies all three claims—the cross, the resurrection, and the deity of Christ—while honoring the man at their center as a sinless prophet. Either the carpenter from Nazareth died on a Roman cross and rose, or he did not. Either he was God among us, or he was a creature. The law of non-contradiction is not a Western trick; it is a condition of any serious truth-claim. So the honest question is not whether the two religions can coexist—they manifestly do—but whether both can be true.

This essay will not try to cover every dispute. The companion Islam track already surveys ten of them—the Quran’s preservation, hadith reliability, the Paraclete, and more. Here we go deep on the three issues that bear the most weight, the ones on which the whole disagreement turns: the historical crucifixion, the charge that the earlier scriptures were corrupted, and whether Islam’s famous critique of the Trinity even hits the doctrine Christians actually hold. Throughout, the aim is the one Peter sets—to give a reason “with gentleness and respect” (1 Peter 3:15)—and to state the strongest Muslim case, by name, before any reply.

I. The Cross: The Best-Attested Fact and the Quran’s Denial

The Quran appears to deny the crucifixion in a single, famous passage. Speaking of the Jews who boasted of killing Jesus, it says: “And they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but [another] was made to resemble him to them” (Surah 4:157, Sahih International).1 On the dominant later reading, God rescued his prophet and a substitute was crucified in his place; Jesus was raised bodily to heaven (4:158). This is the standard Muslim position, and it puts Islam squarely against what is, by wide agreement, the single best-established fact about Jesus of Nazareth.

That is not a Christian boast; it is the verdict of secular historians. Bart Ehrman, the agnostic New Testament scholar, writes flatly that “the crucifixion of Jesus by the Romans is one of the most secure facts we have about his life,” and elsewhere that “one of the most certain facts of history is that Jesus was crucified on orders of the Roman prefect of Judea, Pontius Pilate.”2 The convergence of evidence is unusual for any event in antiquity. Four Gospels report it. Paul cites it inside a creed—“Christ died for our sins…was buried…was raised on the third day” (1 Corinthians 15:3–5)—that scholars across the spectrum date to within a handful of years of the event; even the atheist critic Gerd Lüdemann placed its core in the “first two years” after the crucifixion.3 Outside the church, the Roman historian Tacitus records that “Christus…suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus” (Annals 15.44).4 Josephus, in a passage most scholars regard as authentic at its core once Christian additions are stripped out, reports that Pilate “condemned him to the cross.”5 No serious historian of any faith treats the crucifixion as doubtful.

The criterion that makes it so secure is the one historians call embarrassment. A crucified messiah was, to a first-century Jew, a contradiction in terms—“cursed is everyone who is hanged on a tree” (Deuteronomy 21:23; Galatians 3:13). No movement hoping to win Jews would invent a humiliated, executed founder. The early church proclaimed it anyway, because it happened.

The strongest Muslim and academic response

Here the case must be steelmanned, not caricatured. The popular “substitution” theory—someone else (Judas, Simon of Cyrene, a look-alike) died in Jesus’s place—is historically hopeless and most thoughtful Muslims know it; it has no evidence and names no substitute. But there is a far more serious move, and it comes from inside the academy. The Notre Dame Islamicist Gabriel Said Reynolds argues that the Quran does not actually deny that Jesus died; read carefully, 4:157 denies only that the Jews killed him—a denial of their boast and their power, not of the historical event—and the Quran elsewhere (3:55; 19:33) speaks of Jesus dying.6 The late Mahmoud Ayoub, one of the most respected Muslim scholars of comparative theology, reached a parallel conclusion: the verse is a theological statement that human beings could not vanquish the Word of God, “not a denial of the death of Christ” as a historical fact.7 On this reading—a minority among Muslims but a real and learned one—there is no head-on collision with history at all.

So the critic gets the last word first: You have aimed your best evidence at a reading of the Quran that the Quran may not require. If Reynolds and Ayoub are right, Islam can grant the crucifixion and reinterpret 4:157, and your “decisive fact” decides nothing between us.

The reply is twofold, and it should concede what is true. First, this is a genuine and welcome convergence on history: where a Muslim accepts the crucifixion, one enormous obstacle between us simply dissolves, and that is worth celebrating. But second, the Reynolds–Ayoub reading is a minority position precisely because it cuts against the entire weight of classical Islamic exegesis. The great mufassirun—al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir—overwhelmingly read 4:157 as denying the crucifixion itself and narrate the substitution.8 So the move rescues the verse only by setting aside the mainstream tradition that has carried it for a millennium. And it relocates rather than removes the problem: if Jesus truly died on the cross, the Christian then asks why—and the New Testament’s answer, “for our sins,” is exactly the atoning, dying-and-rising Messiah that Islam exists to deny. The honest scope of this section, then: history establishes the crucifixion as fact; it does not by itself prove the resurrection or the atonement—those are further arguments. What it does is force a choice. Islam must either reinterpret its own tradition or contradict the best-attested event in the life of the man it venerates.

II. The Corruption Charge and the Quran’s Own Witness

Most Muslims, pressed on why the Bible contradicts the Quran, will answer that the Bible was corrupted—tahrif. The trouble is that the Quran itself speaks of the Torah (Tawrat) and the Gospel (Injil) with striking respect, and in the present tense. It calls itself “confirming” (musaddiq) the scriptures before it (Surah 3:3). It tells Christians of Muhammad’s own day to “judge by what Allah has revealed therein”—in the Gospel they then held (Surah 5:47).9 Most remarkably, it instructs Muhammad himself: “So if you are in doubt…about that which We have revealed to you, then ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you” (Surah 10:94).10 One does not send a prophet to consult a corrupted book for reassurance. This is the structure Christian apologist David Wood popularized as “the Islamic Dilemma”: if the earlier scriptures were already corrupt, the Quran endorsed and commanded obedience to a false book; if they were sound, they contradict the Quran. Either horn cuts.

The historical leg of the argument is the manuscript record, and here the dating does real work. Whatever Bible sat in seventh-century Christian and Jewish hands is, by the physical evidence, essentially the Bible we read. The Dead Sea Scrolls (copied c. 250 BC–AD 70) give us a Hebrew Isaiah a thousand years older than anything previously known and substantially identical to the medieval text. The great codices Vaticanus and Sinaiticus, from the fourth century, carry the same four Gospels three hundred years before Islam. There was no closed window in which the text could have been secretly rewritten after the Quran affirmed it: copies were already scattered across three continents in Greek, Latin, Syriac, and Coptic, in the hands of rival communities who could never have coordinated an identical forgery. The Christian point here is modest and must stay modest: the manuscripts establish preservation, not truth. They show we read what the authors wrote; whether what they wrote is true is a separate argument.

The strongest Muslim and academic response

The serious reply is not the crude “the Bible was rewritten” claim—which the manuscripts refute—but a more careful, historically informed one. The standard scholarly survey, Martin Whittingham’s A History of Muslim Views of the Bible, shows that the earliest Muslim writers did not generally hold that the biblical text was corrupted; the dominant early charge was tahrif al-ma’na—corruption of meaning, of interpretation—and the full-blown textual-corruption doctrine was systematized only later, above all by Ibn Hazm of Cordoba (d. 1064), four centuries after Muhammad.11 So a sophisticated Muslim can say: the Quran never taught that the Bible’s words were changed; it taught that Jews and Christians concealed and misread a text that remained intact (cf. Surah 2:42; 3:78). And it can add the supersession move: the Quran is muhaymin, “guardian” over the earlier books (Surah 5:48), confirming their authentic kernel while standing as the final criterion.

The critic’s last word: Your dilemma attacks a doctrine—wholesale textual corruption—that the Quran does not actually teach and that the best Muslim scholarship treats as a late development. When the Quran calls itself musaddiq (3:3), it confirms the prophetic mission and core monotheism of what came before, not the textual particulars of any later canon; and its commands to “consult” and “judge by” the earlier scriptures (10:94; 5:47) were spoken to a seventh-century audience about whatever authentic revelation then survived—not a blanket endorsement of today’s Bible. The Quran confirms the true revelation while correcting how its custodians read it. No contradiction, no trap.

This deserves a real concession. The blanket claim that “Muslims believe the Bible’s text was rewritten” is historically too crude; Whittingham is right that the early consensus was corruption of meaning, and the apologist who ignores this is not arguing in good faith. But notice where the corruption-of-meaning reading lands. If the text was preserved and only the interpretation twisted, then Christians still possess the genuine words of the Gospel—and those genuine words teach the deity, death, and resurrection of Christ as plainly as anything in them (John 1:1; 20:28; 1 Corinthians 15:3–4). The corruption-of-meaning answer therefore concedes the very thing that activates the second horn: an authentic text that contradicts the Quran. As for supersession—it explains why a Muslim need not become a Christian, but it does not explain why God would tell Muhammad to consult (10:94) and Christians to obey (5:47) scriptures present in the seventh century if those scriptures already taught what God meant to deny. And the manuscripts settle which text that was: the Gospels Christians held in Muhammad’s day—attested by codices three centuries older than Islam—are materially the Gospels we hold now, the ones that proclaim Christ crucified and risen. The “authentic revelation then surviving” just is that Gospel. The dilemma is narrowed and pressured by the best response. It is not cleanly escaped. Honest scope: this argument exposes a tension internal to Islam; it does not, by itself, prove Christianity. It clears the ground so the contents of the Gospel can be argued on their own.

III. Whose Trinity Does the Quran Reject?

The Quran’s rejection of the Trinity is unambiguous in tone: “Do not say, ‘Three’…Allah is but one God” (Surah 4:171); “They have certainly disbelieved who say, ‘Allah is the third of three’” (Surah 5:73). But the decisive question is which doctrine these verses describe. The clearest window is Surah 5:116, where on the Day of Judgment God asks Jesus: “Did you say to the people, ‘Take me and my mother as deities besides Allah’?”12 The triad in view is God, Jesus, and Mary—and Christians have never believed that. Mary is no member of the Trinity. No creed, council, or catechism has ever placed her there. The doctrine the Quran appears to condemn is the worship of three separate divine beings, one of them a woman—a tritheism that orthodox Christianity also condemns.

What the Nicene Creed actually confesses is not three gods but one God eternally existing in three persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—of one being (homoousios). “Three of three” (5:73) and a Father–Mary–Jesus family (5:116) are not that doctrine; they are a misdescription of it. So the Christian can say something disarming: I agree with the Quran here. Anyone who worships Mary as a goddess, or believes in three gods, is wrong—and so do I. The Quran’s polemic, on its face, lands on a heresy, not on Nicaea.

The strongest Muslim response

A thoughtful Muslim will not concede the point so easily, and the best reply has two parts. First, on the history: some Christians in seventh-century Arabia may in fact have venerated Mary in ways that blurred toward worship—there were sects (the so-called Collyridians are sometimes cited) who offered her cultic honors—so the Quran may be correcting a real distortion present on the ground, not a strawman.13 Second, and more sharply, the Muslim presses past the caricature to the doctrine itself: Even your refined Trinity—one being, three persons—still calls a man, Jesus, God; still divides the indivisible Creator; still compromises pure monotheism (tawhid). The Quran’s deeper objection is not to a Mary-triad but to any association (shirk) of a creature with the Creator—and that objection cuts against Nicaea exactly.

The critic’s last word, then, is not “you misread us” but: grant that 5:116 targets a folk distortion—the Quran’s monotheism still rules out the Incarnation in principle, so Christianity remains shirk however carefully you state it.

This is the real disagreement, and it should be named as such rather than papered over. The concession: the deepest Muslim objection is not the historical confusion in 5:116 but the principled claim that God cannot become incarnate without ceasing to be one. That is a serious metaphysical conviction, not a mistake. The reply is that the Trinity does not divide God into parts or add a fourth being; it claims that the one God’s inner life is not a bare, undifferentiated unity but an eternal communion—and that this God, without ceasing to be one, united human nature to himself in Christ. One may reject that as false; one may not honestly reject it as tritheism, which is what the Quranic verses describe. Here the apologist must label the contested hinge plainly: whether divine unity can be triune is a genuine philosophical dispute, argued by able people on both sides, not a knock-down proof either way. What the textual evidence does establish is narrower and still significant—the specific picture the Quran rejects (God, Mary, and Jesus as three deities) is not the Trinity the church confesses. The conversation has to move from the caricature to the real doctrine before it can even begin.

The State of the Question

Where does the live debate actually stand? On the crucifixion, the historical consensus is settled and not seriously contested; the open question is internal to Islam—whether the Reynolds–Ayoub reinterpretation, still a minority view, will gain ground against the classical exegesis. On the corruption charge, Islamic-studies scholarship (Whittingham, and specialists such as Ilkka Lindstedt) increasingly agrees that the Quran does not teach textual corruption—which, ironically, strengthens the Christian’s second horn even as it corrects the apologist’s first. On the Trinity, the one verse that explicitly names the triad (5:116) points to a Mary-triad rather than Nicene orthodoxy—though scholars debate how far 4:171 and 5:73 engage the doctrine proper—while the substantive question—whether monotheism can be triune—remains exactly where it has been for thirteen centuries: a real, respectful, unresolved philosophical disagreement. None of these is a debate Christians win by volume; all are debates better had slowly, with the texts open on the table.

Conclusion: What This Does and Does Not Establish

Set side by side, the two faiths cannot both be true at their centers, and pretending otherwise honors neither. But it is worth being precise about what the strongest arguments achieve. They do not prove Christianity true; that case rests finally on the resurrection, argued on its own ground. They do not show that Muslims cannot be sincere, intelligent, or morally serious—they manifestly are. And they do not let the Christian off the deeper hooks: the Muslim’s principled worry about the Incarnation is a real intellectual challenge that deserves a real answer.

What the arguments do establish is a set of hard pressures that a thoughtful Muslim must face. The crucifixion is as certain as ancient history gets, and Islam must either reinterpret 4:157 against its own tradition or deny the best-attested fact about Jesus. The Quran’s own posture toward the Torah and Gospel—confirming them, commanding obedience to them—sits in tension with the later need to treat them as unreliable. And Islam’s sharpest critique of the Trinity, as the Quran states it, misses the doctrine Christians actually hold. None of this is a cudgel. It is an invitation—to read the texts together, follow the evidence where it leads, and let the man both faiths revere ask his own question: “Who do you say that I am?” (Matthew 16:15).
