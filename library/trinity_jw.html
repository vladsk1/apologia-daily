Answering Jehovah’s Witnesses on the Trinity

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

It usually begins on a doorstep. Two people, neatly dressed, a magazine in hand, asking whether you have ever wondered why God allows suffering or what the Bible really teaches. Behind that gentle question stands one of the most carefully organized missionary movements on earth—the Jehovah’s Witnesses, the public face of the Watchtower Bible and Tract Society—and behind their question stands a conviction that the central confession of historic Christianity is a pagan corruption. The Trinity, they hold, is not in the Bible. Jesus is not God. To worship him as God is idolatry.1

It would be easy to meet that conviction with impatience. It deserves something better. The Witness at your door is, in most cases, sincere, biblically literate by the standards of the average churchgoer, and genuinely trying to honor the oneness of God. The apostle Peter’s instruction is exact: be ready to give a reason for your hope, “yet do it with gentleness and respect” (1 Peter 3:15). This essay tries to model that—to state the Watchtower case at its strongest, in its own words, and then to answer it on the merits, text by text, with the grammar and the context laid open. The aim is not to win an argument. It is to think carefully, together, about who the carpenter from Nazareth really is.

What the Watchtower Actually Teaches

Jehovah’s Witnesses are not vaguely “non-Trinitarian.” They hold a specific and structured Christology, set out most accessibly in the 1989 Watchtower brochure Should You Believe in the Trinity?2 Only the Father, whom they call by the recovered divine name Jehovah, is God in the full sense. The Son is God’s first and greatest creation—the one being Jehovah made directly, who then served as the agent through whom everything else was made. Before his earthly life this Son was, they teach, the archangel Michael; he became the man Jesus, died, and was raised as a spirit creature. The Holy Spirit is not a person at all but God’s impersonal “active force.”3

Historically this is a form of Arianism—the view, named for the fourth-century Alexandrian presbyter Arius, that the Son is the highest of creatures but a creature nonetheless, so that, in the slogan the Council of Nicaea condemned in 325, “there was when he was not.”4 Witnesses do not embrace the label, and there are differences in detail; but the structural claim is the same one Athanasius and the Nicene fathers contested. Against it the historic church confesses that the Son is homoousios—of the same nature as the Father—coequal, coeternal, and consubstantial: one God in three persons. The question between us is therefore precise. Not “is Jesus important?” but “is Jesus a creature or the Creator?”

The Witness Case, Stated at Full Strength

A fair hearing means giving the argument its best form before answering it. The Watchtower case rests on a small set of texts deployed with real skill, and a sincere Witness can run them faster than most Christians can reply.

The flagship is the New World Translation’s rendering of John 1:1: “the Word was a god.”5 The reasoning is grammatical, not arbitrary. In the third clause of John 1:1, the Greek word theos (“God”) lacks the definite article, whereas the second clause—“the Word was with God”—has it (ton theon). Greek can use the article to mark definiteness; so, the argument runs, the anarthrous theos in clause three should be read indefinitely, “a god,” preserving the distinction between the Father (ho theos) and the Word, who is divine in a lesser, derived sense. To the Witness, the standard translation simply flattens a distinction the Greek itself preserves.

The second pillar is Colossians 1:15, which calls Christ “the firstborn of all creation.” On the plain reading, the firstborn of a group belongs to that group: the firstborn of the flock is a sheep, the firstborn of Egypt is an Egyptian. So the firstborn of all creation is the first and chief created thing—exactly the Watchtower’s Christ. Revelation 3:14 is read as confirmation: Jesus is “the beginning of the creation by God” (NWT), where the Greek archē is taken to mean that he is the first thing God brought into being. Proverbs 8, where personified Wisdom says “the LORD created me as the beginning of his way” (8:22, in the Greek Septuagint), is read as a portrait of the pre-incarnate Christ, and so as further evidence that the Son was created.6

Third comes Jesus’s own statement in John 14:28: “the Father is greater than I.” To this the Witness adds the whole texture of Jesus’s dependence—he prays, he obeys, he is sent, he confesses he does not know the day or the hour (Mark 13:32), he calls the Father “my God” (John 20:17). How, the Witness asks, can someone who ranks himself below God, and is ignorant of what God knows, be God? The texts seem to hand us a subordinate being.

Finally there is the identification of Jesus with Michael the archangel, inferred from 1 Thessalonians 4:16 (the Lord descends “with an archangel’s voice”) and Revelation 12:7 (Michael leads the armies of heaven, as the returning Christ does). Knit together, these strands yield a coherent picture: an exalted, pre-existent, mighty Son who is nonetheless Jehovah’s creature and servant, not Jehovah himself. It is this picture—not a caricature of it—that a good answer must engage.

John 1:1 and the Grammar of “a god”

Begin where the debate is hottest, and concede at once what should be conceded: the absence of the article is a real feature of the text, and it is doing real work. The article-less theos does safeguard a distinction—the Word is not the person of the Father. John has just said the Word was “with” God; he is not about to collapse the two. A Witness who insists that clause three cannot mean “the Word is identical to the Father” is correct, and historic orthodoxy agrees: that error has its own ancient name, modalism.

The problem is the inference from “no article” to “therefore indefinite, therefore ‘a god.’” That inference does not hold in Greek. The 1933 study by Ernest Cadman Colwell is often invoked here—he observed that definite predicate nouns preceding the verb usually lack the article—but it is essential not to overstate it, as some apologists have.7 Colwell’s rule describes what happens to nouns already known to be definite; it does not run in reverse to prove that an anarthrous pre-verbal noun must be definite. Leaning on Colwell to prove “the Word was the God” is a genuine overreach, and a careful Witness will catch it.

The decisive work was done not by Colwell but by Philip B. Harner, in a 1973 article in the Journal of Biblical Literature.8 Harner examined anarthrous predicate nouns that precede the verb—exactly the construction in John 1:1c—and argued that their primary force is usually qualitative: they describe the nature of the subject rather than identifying or counting it. On his analysis the clause means that “the logos… had the nature of theos,” and he judged the qualitative force here “so prominent that the noun cannot be regarded as definite.”9 Daniel B. Wallace, in the standard intermediate grammar, reaches the same verdict: in John 1:1c theos is “neither definite nor indefinite” but qualitative—the Word possesses the full nature of deity that the Father possesses, while remaining personally distinct from him.10

This is why “a god” is the one reading the construction will not bear. An indefinite “a god” makes the Word one divine being among a possible plurality—polytheism—from the pen of an author whose entire framework is Jewish monotheism. Bruce Metzger, the dean of twentieth-century textual scholarship, put it bluntly in a 1953 study: the rendering, he wrote, is “a frightful mistranslation,” and “if the Jehovah’s Witnesses take this translation seriously, they are polytheists.”11 The honest conclusion is not that “a god” is one defensible option among several; it is that the qualitative reading, which affirms the Word’s full deity, is the majority judgment of the grammarians, and that even were one to retreat to a purely qualitative paraphrase—“the Word was fully divine in nature”—the created-angel Christology has already lost. A being who shares the very nature of God is not a creature.

One further point is worth making gently rather than as a gotcha. The same anarthrous-theos construction appears elsewhere in the very same chapter—at John 1:6, 1:12, and 1:13—and there the New World Translation renders it “God,” not “a god.”12 A single rule applied in one place and dropped in three is hard to defend as translation rather than theology. Witnesses fairly reply that context, not a mechanical rule, should govern each verse—which is exactly right, and exactly the point: the context of John 1:1, saturated in creation and monotheism, is what rules “a god” out.

“Firstborn of All Creation”: Rank, Not Sequence

Colossians 1:15 looks, at first glance, like the Watchtower’s strongest verse, and its plain-sense force should be granted: “firstborn of X” can mean the first member of the group X. But Greek prōtotokos, like the Hebrew bekhor behind it, carries a second and very common meaning—preeminence, the rank and inheritance rights of the firstborn heir—and which sense is in play is settled by context, not by the bare phrase.

Scripture itself supplies the controlling parallel. In Psalm 89:27 God says of David—who was the youngest, the last-born, of Jesse’s sons—“I will make him the firstborn, the highest of the kings of the earth.”13 Here “firstborn” cannot mean “born first”; it means supreme in rank. Likewise Israel, a nation that did not exist at the dawn of creation, is called God’s “firstborn” (Exodus 4:22). The word is a title of status. So “firstborn of all creation” can mean “heir and Lord over all creation”—and the question is which Paul intends.

Paul answers it himself in the next breath, and this is where the Watchtower reading breaks. Colossians 1:16–17 gives the reason for the title, opening with “for” (Greek hoti): “for by him all things were created, in heaven and on earth… all things were created through him and for him. And he is before all things, and in him all things hold together.”14 The firstborn is the one through whom, and for whom, all created things exist—and a being for whom all things were made, and who holds all things together, is on the Creator’s side of the line, not among the things created. That is why the firstborn is preeminent: he is the maker and heir of creation, not its first product.

The strain this puts on the Watchtower reading is visible in their own translation. To make Christ a creature while keeping “all things were created through him” coherent, the New World Translation inserts the word “other” four times in verses 16–20—“all other things”—so that Christ creates everything except himself.15 But “other” (Greek alla) is not in the Greek text at those points, as the Watchtower’s own Kingdom Interlinear shows in its word-for-word column. The inserted word is the tell: the unaltered text places Christ outside the category of created things, and a word had to be added to put him back in.

Revelation 3:14 and Proverbs 8: “Beginning” Reconsidered

Revelation 3:14 calls Christ “the beginning of God’s creation” (Greek hē archē tēs ktiseōs tou theou). The Watchtower reads archē as “the first created thing.” But archē ordinarily means “origin, source, ruler, first cause”—it is the root of English words like “architect” and “monarch.” Most translators and lexicographers take it actively here: Christ is the source or origin of creation, its active beginning, not its first item.16 This fits the rest of Revelation, where the same Christ is “the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end” (Revelation 22:13)—titles Isaiah reserves for Yahweh alone (Isaiah 44:6). One does not call a creature the Alpha.

Proverbs 8 is subtler, and here the honest answer is to resist over-reading in either direction. The chapter personifies divine Wisdom, who says she was “brought forth” (Hebrew) or “created” (Greek Septuagint, 8:22) before the world. The Arians of the fourth century leaned hard on the Septuagint’s “created me,” and the Witnesses follow them. But two cautions apply. First, the Hebrew verb in 8:22 (qanah) is disputed and is often rendered “possessed” or “acquired” rather than “created.” Second, and more important, Proverbs 8 is a poem personifying an attribute of God—his wisdom in ordering the world—and most modern interpreters, across the theological spectrum, doubt that it was intended as a literal description of a pre-incarnate person at all.17 To build a Christology on whether poetic Wisdom was “created” is to ask a wisdom poem to answer a metaphysical question it never poses. The New Testament nowhere cites Proverbs 8:22 to teach that the Son was created; that move is the reader’s, not the text’s.

“The Father Is Greater Than I”: The Strongest Objection

John 14:28 is the Watchtower’s best card, and it should be met at full strength rather than dodged. The strongest form is not merely the one verse; it is the cumulative weight of Jesus’s subordination throughout the Gospels—he prays, learns, obeys, suffers, dies, and confesses ignorance of the hour. A single proof-text can be parried; this is a pattern. As the analytic philosopher and critic of classical theism Dale Tuggy presses the point, the New Testament repeatedly distinguishes Jesus from “the one true God,” the Father, in ways that a strict identification struggles to absorb.18 The objection is serious and must be granted its force.

The classical reply turns on a distinction the Witnesses tend to collapse: between what is true of the Son ontologically (in his eternal nature) and what is true of him economically—in the role he freely took on in the incarnation. Historic Christianity has never claimed that the incarnate Son was, in his human life, functioning as the Father’s equal in glory and office. The opposite is the heart of the gospel: he “emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant” (Philippians 2:7) and was made “lower than the angels” for a time (Hebrews 2:9). When Jesus says “the Father is greater than I,” the word is one of position, not of kind: the sender is greater than the sent, the Father in unveiled glory greater than the Son veiled in flesh. Notably, Jesus does not say the Father is “better” than he is in nature (Greek kreittōn); he says “greater” (meizōn), the language of rank.

The reading is not imposed from outside; the same Gospel forbids the ontological interpretation. In John 5:18 Jesus’s opponents seek to kill him because he was “calling God his own Father, making himself equal with God”—and Jesus does not retract it. In John 10:30 he says “I and the Father are one,” and in John 17:5 he prays to be restored to “the glory that I had with you before the world existed.” A creature does not share the Father’s pre-creation glory. So the same John who records “the Father is greater” records “I and the Father are one”: the only reading that holds both is the incarnational one. To make “greater” mean “of a higher order of being,” the Witness must read it against the immediate context that John supplies. As for Mark 13:32, the orthodox tradition has long held that the Son’s not knowing the hour belongs to the genuine limits of his human consciousness—the same self-emptying—not to a deficiency in the divine nature he never ceased to possess. One may find that distinction subtle; it is not ad hoc, for it is the very logic of an incarnation.

Is Jesus Michael the Archangel?

The identification of Jesus with Michael is an inference, and the inference is weaker than it first appears. That the Lord descends “with an archangel’s voice” (1 Thessalonians 4:16) no more makes him an archangel than his descending in the same verse “with the trumpet of God” makes him a trumpet; the preposition signals accompaniment, not identity. And Revelation 12 shows Michael leading heaven’s armies, but so do many texts show angelic commanders; leadership in battle does not establish that two figures are one.

More decisively, Scripture itself draws a contrast that the identification cannot survive. In Jude 9, the archangel Michael, disputing with the devil over the body of Moses, “did not presume to pronounce a blasphemous judgment, but said, ‘The Lord rebuke you’”—Michael cannot rebuke Satan on his own authority; he must appeal to the Lord. Jesus, by contrast, rebukes Satan directly and on his own word (Matthew 4:10; Mark 8:33). Above all, the entire first chapter of Hebrews is a sustained argument that the Son is categorically higher than the angels: “to which of the angels did God ever say, ‘You are my Son’?” (Hebrews 1:5), and—quoting a text of worship—“Let all God’s angels worship him” (Hebrews 1:6).19 A chapter written to prove the Son is not an angel is difficult to reconcile with the claim that he is the chief one.

And this opens onto the deepest problem for the whole Watchtower system: worship. In Revelation 22:8–9 the apostle John falls down to worship an angel and is sharply refused: “You must not do that!… Worship God.”20 Faithful angels do not accept worship. Yet the risen Jesus accepts exactly that from Thomas, who says to him, “My Lord and my God!” (John 20:28)—a confession Jesus blesses rather than corrects, and which the New World Translation leaves unaltered. In Revelation 5 every creature in heaven and earth ascribes worship to the Lamb together with the One on the throne (5:13–14). If Jesus were a faithful angel, that worship was his to refuse, as his colleague refused it; he received it instead. The created-angel Christology cannot explain why the Son accepts in heaven what every other faithful creature refuses.

The State of the Question

It is worth being candid about where the dispute really lies. On the grammar of John 1:1, the field is not evenly divided: the qualitative reading affirming the Word’s full deity is the settled judgment of the standard reference grammars and of specialists like Harner, Wallace, and Metzger, and “a god” survives almost entirely outside the academic mainstream. That is not a reason to dismiss Witnesses—but it is an honest statement of the scholarly weather.

The genuinely live debate is elsewhere. Critics such as Dale Tuggy and, on historical grounds, Bart Ehrman argue that the New Testament’s own Christology is more diverse—and in places more subordinationist—than the later Nicene synthesis allows, and that the full doctrine of consubstantiality is a fourth-century development rather than a first-century datum.21 They are right that the doctrine’s formulation was refined over centuries, and right that the texts must be read without smuggling Nicaea into every verse. The Trinitarian reply, developed by scholars like Richard Bauckham and Larry Hurtado, is that the raw materials—the worship of Jesus, his inclusion within the divine identity, his role in creation—are present astonishingly early, well before any creed.22 That is a real and continuing scholarly conversation. But notice that it is a different conversation from the one at the door: even the harshest academic critics of Nicene orthodoxy do not defend “the Word was a god,” the partitive reading of Colossians 1, or the equation of Jesus with Michael. On those specific Watchtower exegeses, the answer is firm.

What This Does and Doesn’t Establish

These arguments establish something narrower than “the Trinity, proved”—and saying so plainly is part of honesty. The full doctrine of the Trinity is built from the whole of Scripture: the deity of the Son, the deity and personhood of the Spirit, the distinction of the three, and the oneness of God, held together. This essay has defended one load-bearing piece of that structure—that the Son is the uncreated Creator, not the first creature—against the specific texts the Watchtower marshals against it. It has not, here, made the separate case for the personhood of the Holy Spirit, which a complete answer to the Witnesses must also address.

What it has shown is that the Watchtower’s key prooftexts, read in their own contexts and even in the Witnesses’ own translation, point the other way. “A god” founders on the grammar and on John’s monotheism; “firstborn” means heir and is explained by “for by him all things were created”; “the Father is greater” is the language of the incarnation, not of a lesser nature; and the worship the Son receives is precisely what faithful angels refuse. None of this is a reason to feel superior to the people who knocked on the door. It is a reason to invite them, gently, to read the texts they love one chapter at a time—and to let Thomas’s unedited confession, “My Lord and my God,” ask its question. The conversation is not won by cleverness. It is won, if it is won at all, by truth offered in love.
