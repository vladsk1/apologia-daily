The Argument from Reason: Can a Mindless Universe Account for Thinking Minds?

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Every argument for naturalism is itself an act of reasoning. The materialist who says "all that exists is matter in motion" expects you to follow his premises to his conclusion — to see that the inference is valid, that the conclusion is warranted by the evidence. He is, in that moment, trusting his own mind to track truth. But here is the unsettling question at the heart of one of the oldest and most stubborn arguments in the philosophy of religion: on his own account of where his mind came from, is that trust justified?

This is the Argument from Reason. It does not begin with the heavens declaring God's glory or the fine-tuning of physical constants. It begins with the arguer himself, and asks whether the naturalist's story about the origin of human cognition can survive being applied to the very act of telling it.

The Argument Stated

The core claim can be put simply. Naturalism holds that reality is exhaustively physical, and that the human mind is the product of causes that are not themselves rational — the blind churning of physics and an evolutionary process that selected our ancestors for survival, not for truth. But genuine rational inference seems to require something more than this. When I conclude B because I see that A entails it, my believing B must be governed by the logical relationship between the propositions — by the ground, not merely pushed along by prior physical causes that know nothing of logic.

If our beliefs are simply the terminal links in chains of non-rational causation, the worry runs, then no belief is held because it is true or well-supported; each is held because the atoms did what atoms do. And that conclusion is self-undermining, because it applies to the belief "naturalism is true" as much as to any other. The naturalist saws off the branch he is sitting on.

A History of an Idea

The argument has distinguished ancestry. The British statesman and philosopher Arthur James Balfour gave it an early modern formulation in The Foundations of Belief (1895), arguing that naturalism is self-undermining because it can provide no grounds for trusting the very cognitive processes it presumes to be reliable; he pressed the point against Darwinism specifically, noting that natural selection optimizes faculties for survival, not truth.1

The most famous one-sentence version belongs to the geneticist J. B. S. Haldane — an atheist and Marxist, which makes the admission striking. In Possible Worlds and Other Essays (1927) he wrote: "If my mental processes are determined wholly by the motions of atoms in my brain, I have no reason for supposing that my beliefs are true. They may be sound chemically, but that does not make them sound logically. And hence I have no reason for supposing my brain to be composed of atoms."2 (Haldane himself later distanced himself from the argument; but the difficulty he had named outlived his second thoughts.)

It was C. S. Lewis who developed the point into a sustained argument. In Miracles (1947), in the chapter that became "The Cardinal Difficulty of Naturalism," Lewis pressed the distinction between two relations a belief can stand in. One belief can be the cause of another (being hit on the head causes me to believe I am surrounded by green fog); but reasoning requires that one belief be the logical ground of another. Knowledge, he argued, occurs only when the ground-consequent relation and the cause-effect relation coincide in the right way — when I believe a conclusion because I grasp that the premises entail it. If naturalism reduces all believing to mere event-causation, that coincidence is never secured, and the whole edifice of reasoning — including the reasoning that produced naturalism — is left without validity.3

Half a century later the philosopher Victor Reppert gave Lewis's intuition a rigorous reconstruction in C. S. Lewis's Dangerous Idea: In Defense of the Argument from Reason (2003), distinguishing several distinct "arguments from reason" — from intentionality, from truth, from mental causation, from the psychological relevance of logical laws — and defending each against contemporary objections.4

Plantinga's Rigorous Cousin

The argument's most technically developed form is Alvin Plantinga's Evolutionary Argument Against Naturalism (EAAN), first set out in chapter 12 of Warrant and Proper Function (1993) and refined in Where the Conflict Really Lies (2011).5 Plantinga's move is probabilistic rather than a claim of flat self-contradiction. Consider the conjunction of naturalism and unguided evolution (call it N&E), and ask: what is the probability that our cognitive faculties are reliable — that they generally produce true beliefs — given N&E?

Plantinga argues this probability is low or inscrutable. Natural selection rewards adaptive behavior; it does not directly "see" the content of beliefs. What selection cares about is that an organism behaves in survival-conducive ways, and — on a naturalist picture where beliefs are neurophysiological states — behavior is caused by the neurophysiological properties of those states, not by their semantic content. So the truth or falsity of the belief is, evolutionarily speaking, along for the ride.

His memorable illustration is Paul, a hominid who needs to avoid tigers. Paul might run from the tiger because he believes tigers are dangerous and running away is the way to escape. But he might equally run because he believes the tiger is a large, friendly cat he wants to pet — and that the best way to pet it is to run away from it. The behavior is identical; the beliefs are wildly false. Endless such combinations of false belief plus compensating false belief yield adaptive behavior. If content is invisible to selection, the chance that selection delivered mostly true content looks no better than a coin toss — and arguably worse.6

The conclusion is not "naturalism is false" but something subtler: the naturalist who accepts evolution acquires a defeater for the reliability of his faculties — and therefore a defeater for every belief those faculties produce, naturalism included. The position is, in Plantinga's diagnosis, not self-contradictory but self-defeating to hold.

The Strongest Objections

1. Anscombe at the Socratic Club

No discussion can be honest without the most famous episode in the argument's history. On 2 February 1948, the philosopher G. E. M. (Elizabeth) Anscombe — a brilliant Catholic logician and student of Wittgenstein — read a paper to the Oxford Socratic Club titled "A Reply to Mr C. S. Lewis's Argument that 'Naturalism' is Self-Refuting."7

Her central objection was a careful distinction Lewis had blurred. There is a difference between an irrational cause of a belief and a merely non-rational one. Giving a full causal history of how a belief arose does not, by itself, impugn its validity. As Anscombe put it, if a man has reasons, and they are good reasons, and they are genuinely his reasons for thinking something, then his thinking is rational — whatever further causal statements we might truly make about the neurons underneath.8 Naturalistic causation and rational justification might operate at different levels of description, both true at once, the way a sentence can be both a sequence of ink-marks and a valid argument. Lewis's word "valid," she noted, was being made to do too many jobs.

This critique had teeth, and Lewis felt it. He substantially revised the chapter for the 1960 Fontana edition, changing its title from "The Self-Contradiction of the Naturalist" to "The Cardinal Difficulty of Naturalism," and recasting his case in terms of the cause/ground distinction and the conditions under which reasoning can yield knowledge.9 The revision is widely regarded as an improvement.

It must be added, in fairness to the historical record, that a legend grew up around this exchange: that Lewis was so humiliated he abandoned apologetics for children's fiction. This is contested — indeed, largely debunked. Reppert, among others, has argued in detail that the "Lewis–Anscombe legend" outruns the evidence; Lewis continued to write apologetic and philosophical work, Anscombe herself thought Lewis took the criticism in good part and improved his argument, and the timeline does not support a crisis of faith.10 One should resist a good story that the facts do not bear.

2. True Beliefs Are Adaptive

The most common reply to both Lewis and Plantinga is disarmingly natural: surely true beliefs help you survive. An animal that correctly perceives the cliff edge, the predator, the ripe fruit, outbreeds the one that does not. Selection, the objection runs, would therefore tend to track truth, because acting on accurate beliefs is precisely what aids survival. This is the line taken by several contributors to Naturalism Defeated? (ed. James Beilby, 2002), the major collection of critical essays on the EAAN — including responses pressing that the connection between true belief and fitness is far tighter than Plantinga allows.11

3. Plantinga's Rejoinder

Plantinga's reply is the crux, and it is genuinely subtle. Grant that behavior is adaptive. The question is whether the content of the beliefs causing that behavior must be true. On a naturalist account, he argues, a belief causes behavior in virtue of its neurophysiological properties — its place in the physical causal web — not in virtue of what it means. The semantic content rides on top of the neurophysiology. And for any given adaptive behavior, indefinitely many belief-contents are compatible with it (recall Paul and his friendly tiger). So even granting that our behavior is well-tuned to the world, the probability that the contents attached to it are largely true remains low or inscrutable.6

The Honest State of the Scholarship

It would be a failure of the gentleness and respect that should mark this kind of argument (1 Peter 3:15) to pretend the matter is settled. It is not. The EAAN is contested, and many capable philosophers — theist and naturalist alike — reject it. Critics such as Branden Fitelson and Elliott Sober argued that Plantinga's probability claims rest on a flawed treatment of conditional probability and that the relevant probability is not low. Others contend that content and neurophysiology are not as detachable as Plantinga needs — that on the right theory of mind, semantic content just is causally efficacious. Plantinga has replied in turn, and the exchange continues without a knockout on either side.

Let the critic have the last objection plainly: if a fully naturalistic account of mental content can be given on which content is causally relevant and tied to the world, then the inference from "selection sees only behavior" to "content is probably false" fails, and the defeater dissolves. That is a real possibility, and a respectable research program in philosophy of mind aims at exactly it.

The theist's reply is not to deny the difficulty of the project but to point at its cost: the more one insists that semantic content is robustly real and genuinely causal, the harder it becomes to see how a purely physical, content-blind process could have produced and sustained it — which is, in a different key, the original worry returning. The argument is not refuted, but neither is it a proof. It is a live, unresolved problem.

What It Does — and Does Not — Establish

Honesty about scope is essential. Even at its most successful, the Argument from Reason is not a direct proof of God. It is a problem for naturalism. It seeks to show that the naturalist's picture of the mind is in tension with his confidence in his own reasoning — that he cannot, without strain, both explain reason away as a non-rational byproduct and rely on it to establish his worldview.

What it points toward, if it succeeds, is that reason is more plausibly grounded in a rational source than in a mindless one — that minds are better explained as deriving from a Mind than from the motions of atoms. That is a reason to prefer theism over naturalism on this particular score; it is not a demonstration of the God of Abraham, and it leaves untouched many other live options. It functions best as a defeater-style consideration and as one strand in a cumulative case, not as a freestanding pillar.

And yet, even chastened to its proper size, it retains its power to provoke. It asks the most committed skeptic to turn his skepticism on the instrument of skepticism itself. Haldane saw the difficulty and never fully escaped it. Whatever else it does, the Argument from Reason ensures that no one can dismiss the question of mind as cheap — because the dismissal, too, would have to be an act of reason, and reason is exactly what is in question.
