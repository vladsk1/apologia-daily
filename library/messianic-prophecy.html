The Witness of the Prophets: How the Hebrew Scriptures Converge on Jesus

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

An Argument as Old as the Church Itself

On the first Easter evening, two disconsolate disciples walked the road to Emmaus, and a stranger fell in beside them. "Was it not necessary," he asked, "that the Christ should suffer these things and enter into his glory?" Then, Luke tells us, "beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself."1 The risen Jesus did not first appeal to the empty tomb. He appealed to the prophets.

His followers never forgot it. When Peter stood before the Pentecost crowd, he reached for the Psalms (Acts 2:25–31). When Philip met the Ethiopian official puzzling over Isaiah 53, he "told him the good news about Jesus" from that very page (Acts 8:35). Paul "reasoned with them from the Scriptures" in synagogue after synagogue (Acts 17:2–3). The argument from prophecy is not a modern apologetic invention; it is the oldest Christian argument there is, and it deserves to be made well — which means made honestly.

This essay sets aside a popular but flawed version of the argument (the "statistical" case, treated below) and turns instead to the prophecies themselves: what they say, where the New Testament finds them fulfilled, and — crucially — which carry real evidential weight and which do not. A serious case is one that can survive its own footnotes.

How to Read Prophecy Honestly

The first thing a careful reader must grasp is that the New Testament writers use the word "fulfill" (Greek plēroō) in more than one sense, and not always to mean "a prediction came true." Sometimes it means exactly that — a future-tense forecast realized in a specific event. But often it means something closer to "filled full": an earlier pattern, person, or institution reaches its intended completion in Christ. This is what scholars call typology, and a related idea, sensus plenior — a "fuller sense" God intended in a text beyond what its human author consciously foresaw.

The classic example is Hosea 11:1, "Out of Egypt I called my son." In Hosea the verse is plainly about the past — Israel's exodus — and contains no future-tense verb at all. When Matthew applies it to the infant Jesus' return from Egypt (Matthew 2:15), he is not claiming Hosea predicted it. He is drawing an analogy: Jesus relives and succeeds in Israel's story.2 To call that a "fulfilled prediction" would be to misread Matthew himself.

This honesty matters enormously, because the strength of the prophetic argument is uneven, and pretending otherwise discredits the whole. So throughout what follows, prophecies are flagged: strong ones concern matters outside any individual's control or staging (birthplace, lineage, the manner and timing of an execution under Roman law); weaker or typological ones are real and meaningful but could in principle be arranged, or were not predictions in their original setting. The cumulative case rests chiefly on the strong ones.

A Catalogue of the Prophecies

Lineage and Birth

The expected deliverer is mapped genealogically with increasing precision. He is the "seed" of Abraham, through whom "all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:3; 22:18) — applied to Christ in Galatians 3:16. He comes from the tribe of Judah: "The scepter shall not depart from Judah... until tribute comes to him" (Genesis 49:10), echoed in Revelation 5:5. He is of the line of David, to whom God promised an everlasting throne (2 Samuel 7:12–16; Isaiah 9:7; Jeremiah 23:5), a claim the Gospels press hard (Matthew 1:1; Romans 1:3). Strength: lineage is outside an individual's control, and the New Testament's confident appeal to it — in a culture that took genealogical descent with great seriousness — is notable (though the two Gospel genealogies, Matthew 1 and Luke 3, differ in ways that are a genuine difficulty, not merely a matter of scholarly debate—the proposed harmonizations, such as Luke giving Mary’s line or a levirate-marriage explanation, are possible but none is decisive).

Micah names the town: "But you, O Bethlehem Ephrathah... from you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel" (Micah 5:2), fulfilled in Matthew 2:1–6. The Hebrew adds that the ruler's "origin is from of old, from ancient days" — a phrase debated between "ancient lineage" and something more.3 Strength: a specific, falsifiable birthplace; this is one of the strongest data points.

The most contested birth text is Isaiah 7:14: "the almah shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel." Here we must be candid. The Hebrew ‘almah means a young woman of marriageable age; it does not by itself specify virginity (the more precise word would be betulah). The Jewish translators of the Septuagint, however (Isaiah was rendered into Greek in the second century BC), translated it parthenos, "virgin" — and it is the Septuagint Matthew quotes (Matthew 1:23).4 Moreover, the verse's immediate context is a sign to King Ahaz about a child born in his own lifetime. Many scholars therefore read Isaiah 7:14 as having a near-term referent in Isaiah's day and a fuller messianic sense Matthew discerns in hindsight — a "dual fulfillment." This is a contested, largely typological case, not a knockdown prediction, and it should be presented as such.

Mission and Ministry

A forerunner prepares the way: "Behold, I send my messenger" (Malachi 3:1) and "a voice cries: In the wilderness prepare the way of the LORD" (Isaiah 40:3), both applied to John the Baptist (Mark 1:2–3). The light dawns in the north: "Galilee of the nations... the people who walked in darkness have seen a great light" (Isaiah 9:1–2), cited in Matthew 4:14–16. The Deliverer is a "prophet like Moses" (Deuteronomy 18:15; cf. Acts 3:22). His ministry brings healing — eyes of the blind opened, the lame leaping (Isaiah 35:5–6) — which Jesus invokes as his credential (Matthew 11:4–5). He teaches in parables (Psalm 78:2; Matthew 13:34–35), and zeal for God's house consumes him (Psalm 69:9; John 2:17). He enters Jerusalem "humble and mounted on a donkey" (Zechariah 9:9; Matthew 21:1–9) — though this is the clearest example of a stageable prophecy, one the Gospels themselves present Jesus as deliberately arranging. Strength: mixed. The forerunner and the geography are reasonably firm; healing and teaching-style are the kind of thing that could be shaped in the telling, so they carry less independent weight.

Rejection and Passion

Here the texts grow startling. The Suffering Servant of Isaiah 52:13–53:12 is "despised and rejected," "wounded for our transgressions," "like a lamb that is led to the slaughter" who "opened not his mouth," who "makes his soul an offering for guilt," and who is "with a rich man in his death." Isaiah 53:9 even pairs a criminal's death ("with the wicked") and a wealthy burial — answered in Jesus' crucifixion between two thieves and burial in Joseph of Arimathea's tomb (Matthew 27:57–60). The Servant's silence before accusers (Isaiah 53:7) meets Jesus' silence before Pilate (Matthew 27:12–14).

Psalm 22, sung centuries before crucifixion was a Roman practice, opens with the cry Jesus echoed from the cross — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Psalm 22:1; Matthew 27:46) — and goes on to describe mockery (v. 7–8), thirst and exhaustion (v. 14–15), and lots cast for clothing (v. 18; John 19:24). Then comes verse 16, one of the most disputed lines in the Hebrew Bible. The traditional Masoretic Text reads ka'ari — "like a lion" — yielding the awkward "like a lion my hands and my feet." But a fragment from Nahal Hever (5/6HevPs, 1st century AD) reads ka'aru, and the ancient Septuagint reads ōryxan, "they dug/pierced." The Septuagint (pre-Christian) and a disputed, faded 1st-century AD fragment together suggest a "pierced/dug" reading is at least old and not a Christian invention — though the Nahal Hever fragment is 1st-century AD rather than pre-Christian, and its reading is contested; scholars such as Brent Strawn defend the Masoretic "lion," and the fragment's ink is faded. This verse is genuinely contested; honesty requires saying so, and the broader Psalm does not depend on it. The point about the manner of Jesus' death and burial should therefore rest not on the disputed Psalm 22:16 but on the textually secure Isaiah 53:9—a criminal's death paired with a rich man's burial—which the manuscripts place well before the crucifixion.5

Other passion details: betrayal by an intimate, "my close friend... who ate my bread" (Psalm 41:9; John 13:18); thirty pieces of silver thrown "to the potter" (Zechariah 11:12–13), eerily matching Judas's betrayal price spent on a potter's field (Matthew 27:3–10 — which, candidly, attributes the quotation to Jeremiah, a long-noted difficulty usually explained by scroll-naming convention or a composite citation).6 No bone is broken (Psalm 34:20; cf. the Passover lamb of Exodus 12:46; John 19:33–36); his side is pierced (Zechariah 12:10, "they look on me, on him whom they have pierced"; John 19:34–37).

And there is Daniel 9:24–27, which foretells that "an anointed one shall be cut off" after a span of "sixty-two weeks" following a decree to restore Jerusalem — language Christians have read as timing the Messiah's death before the Second Temple's destruction (the text says the city and sanctuary will be destroyed afterward, which happened in AD 70). But the dating is heavily contested: which decree starts the clock (Cyrus c. 538 BC, or Artaxerxes in 458 or 445 BC?), and whether the "anointed one" is the Messiah, a high priest, or Onias III, are all live scholarly disputes. The passage is suggestive but should not be overclaimed.7

Vindication and Resurrection

The Scriptures do not end at the grave. "You will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" (Psalm 16:10) — which Peter (Acts 2:25–31) and Paul (Acts 13:35) argue David could not have meant of himself, since David died and decayed. The vindicated one is enthroned: "The LORD says to my Lord, Sit at my right hand" (Psalm 110:1), the Old Testament verse the New Testament quotes most often. And in Daniel 7:13–14, "one like a son of man" comes "with the clouds of heaven" and receives "an everlasting dominion" — the very title Jesus chose for himself.

The Honest Objections

1. The retrojection worry

The sharpest objection is this: the Gospel writers knew the Hebrew Scriptures intimately, so what looks like fulfillment may be the writers shaping their narratives to match the texts. This must be conceded for the "stageable" prophecies — a man could deliberately ride a donkey into Jerusalem (Zechariah 9:9), and an author could narrate a detail to echo a psalm.

The reply is to lean on the prophecies outside anyone's control or staging: where one is born, what tribe and lineage one belongs to, and the manner and timing of one's death under an occupying empire that alone held the power of capital crucifixion. A first-century Jew could not arrange to be born in Bethlehem of David's line, nor engineer a Roman execution by a method Psalm 22 seems to describe a thousand years early. And the texts are demonstrably pre-Christian: the Great Isaiah Scroll from Qumran (1QIsaᵃ) — containing Isaiah 7, 9, 35, 40, and all of chapter 53 — is dated by paleography and radiocarbon to roughly 125 BC, well over a century before Jesus.8 No one shaped Isaiah after the fact.

2. The "not originally messianic / out of context" objection

Jewish counter-missionary scholars — Rabbi Tovia Singer foremost among them — argue, fairly, that many of these texts were not predictions of an individual Messiah at all in their original setting. Isaiah 7:14 addresses Ahaz; Hosea 11:1 looks backward to the Exodus. Most pointedly, the "Servant" of Isaiah is in several nearby passages explicitly named as the nation Israel (Isaiah 41:8; 49:3), so Isaiah 53, on this reading, depicts suffering, faithful Israel among the nations — not a crucified Messiah.

This deserves a real and respectful answer. First, on Isaiah 53: within the song the Servant is "stricken for the transgression of my people" (53:8) — distinguished from the people, which fits awkwardly with a purely collective reading, though its defenders appeal to a righteous remnant suffering for the wider nation. Second, the messianic-individual interpretation is not a Christian imposition: both individual-messianic and collective readings are attested in ancient Judaism — the Targum on Isaiah 52:13 names "my servant the Messiah" (though it notably transfers the suffering away from the Servant) — and the collective-Israel reading became dominant only in later Jewish tradition, a shift many associate with Rashi in the medieval period.9 Christians should hold this point gently. The collective reading is sincere, ancient enough, and was sharpened in centuries when Jewish communities were suffering at Christian hands. The fair conclusion is that Isaiah 53's referent is genuinely contested, and that the Christian reading is a defensible — not the only conceivable — interpretation. The honest move is to let it strengthen the cumulative pattern, not to wield it as a club.

3. The statistical (Stoner) argument

Here is the reframe this essay was built around. In Science Speaks (developed across editions from the late 1950s), mathematician Peter Stoner argued that the odds of one man fulfilling just eight prophecies were one in 1017 — a figure he reached by taking a raw product of roughly 1028 and dividing by the estimated number of people who have ever lived; he put forty-eight prophecies at 1 in 10157. The image is vivid — Texas knee-deep in silver dollars, one of them marked — and it has circulated for eighty years.

It should be retired. The probabilities are not real measurements; they are numbers assigned by guesswork ("the chance of being born in Bethlehem is 1 in 280,000," and so on), then multiplied as if independent. A reviewer in The Quarterly Review of Biology charged Stoner with "using only these facts which bolster his hypothesis"; even Stoner's own grandson now concedes the book is of more historical than evangelistic value.10 The method is pseudo-mathematics: precise-looking figures resting on arbitrary inputs. The case for the prophecies does not rest on it, and never needed it. Convergence, not a manufactured probability, is the real argument.

What the Argument Does — and Does Not — Establish

So what is left when the weak links are removed and the contested texts are flagged as contested? A great deal, but stated precisely.

What the prophetic argument does establish is a striking cumulative convergence: a single first-century figure who fits a constellation of expectations — birthplace, lineage, a rejected and suffering deliverer, a death by piercing and a rich man's burial, a vindication beyond the grave — drawn from documents provably older than he was. The most evidentially weighty of these lie outside any human power to arrange. That convergence is not nothing; it is, in the proper sense, remarkable, and it is why the apostles preached it.

What the argument does not establish is a single, knock-down, mathematical proof. It is a cumulative, probabilistic case, the way a verdict is built from many testimonies rather than one. Some "fulfillments" are typological, not predictive. Several key texts — Isaiah 7:14, Psalm 22:16, the dating in Daniel 9, the referent of Isaiah 53 — are genuinely disputed among honest scholars, and a reader is entitled to know that before being asked to weigh them.

The prophets, then, do not hand us a proof to end argument; they hand us a portrait, sketched across a thousand years by many hands, that comes startlingly into focus on one face. Whether that focus is coincidence or providence is the question each reader must answer. But the case is strong enough to be worth answering — and honest enough, made this way, to be made "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15).
