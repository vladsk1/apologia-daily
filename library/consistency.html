Is the Bible Internally Consistent? Contradictions, Differences, and the Honest Case

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Open four newspapers the morning after a chaotic event—a stadium collapse, a sudden resignation—and you will find four accounts that do not match. One says the crowd was ten thousand; another, “thousands.” One leads with the mayor, another with a bystander. One reports a detail the others omit entirely. No serious reader concludes that the event never happened, or that the reporters are liars. We grasp, intuitively, that independent testimony to a real event almost never lines up perfectly—and that perfect alignment, far from reassuring us, would smell of collusion.

The Bible asks for something like that ordinary literary maturity, and rarely receives it. For two centuries skeptics have catalogued its discrepancies; for two centuries defenders have answered, sometimes with care and sometimes by force-fitting the pieces together with a hammer. This essay tries to do neither. It asks a hard question honestly: is the Bible internally consistent?—and it refuses to win the argument by pretending the difficult cases are easy. Some are not. A few remain genuinely debated. The goal is not to flatten Scripture into a single seamless voice, but to think clearly about what a contradiction actually is, how ancient writers actually composed, and what consistency could—and could not—establish even if it held.

Stating the Question Carefully

Two confusions doom most discussions before they begin. The first is to treat the Bible as one book. It is not; it is a library—some sixty-six documents written by perhaps forty authors over roughly fifteen centuries, in Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek, spanning law, chronicle, poetry, prophecy, proverb, letter, apocalypse, and ancient biography. To ask whether a love poem “contradicts” a genealogy is often a category mistake. Genre governs what counts as accuracy: a parable is not falsified by being fictional, and a psalm of despair does not contradict a psalm of praise any more than a blues song contradicts a hymn.

The second confusion is the more important one, and it deserves a name. A difference is not a contradiction. The law of non-contradiction states that the same proposition cannot be both true and false in the same sense at the same time. Two accounts contradict only if they cannot both be true—not merely if they differ, emphasize different things, or one mentions what the other omits. “There was an angel at the tomb” and “there were two angels at the tomb” look like a clash, but they are not formal contradictories: a report of one does not deny a second unless it says “only one.” Most alleged biblical contradictions are differences of selection, perspective, or paraphrase. Some, however, are genuinely harder, and we will not pretend otherwise.

It is also worth stating the scope at the outset, because it cuts both ways. Establishing that the Bible is internally consistent would not prove its contents true—an internally consistent novel is still a novel. And conceding a genuine discrepancy in a peripheral detail would not, by itself, sink the historical core of the texts; ancient historians routinely trust sources that disagree on minor points. Consistency is a question about coherence, not about truth or inspiration directly. Getting that straight keeps the conversation honest in both directions.

How the Argument Developed

The problem is old, and so are the serious answers. As early as the second century, the pagan critic Celsus and the Christian heretic Marcion both pressed the disunity of the texts—Marcion so far as to reject the Old Testament and most of the New. Against him, Irenaeus argued that one God stood behind both Testaments and the fourfold Gospel.1 Around AD 400, Augustine of Hippo wrote the first great systematic treatment, De Consensu Evangelistarum (“On the Agreement of the Evangelists”), examining the Gospels case by case to show that their differences need not be contradictions—already deploying arguments about order, selection, and paraphrase that modern scholars still use.2

The modern debate was born with the Enlightenment, as critics like Hermann Samuel Reimarus in the eighteenth century turned the discrepancies into a weapon against the historical Jesus. The twentieth century professionalized the question on both sides: form and redaction critics studied how the Evangelists shaped their material, while evangelical scholars such as Craig Blomberg and Darrell Bock mounted detailed historical defenses.3 For a general audience, the debate now has two recognizable poles: Bart Ehrman’s Jesus, Interrupted (2009), which urges readers to read the Gospels “horizontally” and confront their disagreements; and Michael Licona’s Why Are There Differences in the Gospels? (2017), which reads them against the conventions of ancient biography.4 The contemporary discussion lives largely in the space between those two books.

The Strongest Objection, Stated at Full Strength

Bart D. Ehrman is the most effective popular critic on this front, and his case deserves to be put at full power. His central move in Jesus, Interrupted is methodological: stop reading the Gospels “vertically”—each straight through, blending them in your mind into a single harmonized story—and read them “horizontally,” setting parallel accounts side by side and noticing where they conflict.5 Do this, Ehrman argues, and the discrepancies are not trivial misspellings; they reach into the narratives themselves.

He marshals concrete cases, and they are real. The day of the crucifixion. In Mark, the Last Supper is a Passover meal (Mark 14:12), so Jesus dies on the day of Passover; in John, Jesus dies on “the day of Preparation for the Passover” (John 19:14), as the lambs are being slaughtered—a full day earlier.6 The death of Judas. Matthew says Judas threw the silver into the temple and “went and hanged himself” (Matt 27:5); Acts says he “fell headlong” in a field he had bought, “and all his bowels gushed out” (Acts 1:18). The two even disagree on who bought the field and why it was called the Field of Blood.7 The two genealogies. Matthew and Luke trace Jesus’ line through David, but by different sons—Solomon in Matthew, Nathan in Luke—and they name a different father for Joseph: Jacob in Matthew (1:16), Heli in Luke (3:23).8 And Easter morning. Who went to the tomb, how many angels, whether they were sitting or standing, what the women did next—the four accounts differ at nearly every point.9

Ehrman’s deeper claim is that harmonizers cheat. To reconcile the accounts, he argues, you must invent a fifth Gospel that none of the four actually wrote—splicing details together into a composite story that flattens each author’s distinct theological voice. The differences, he insists, are not accidental noise; they are the result of each Evangelist shaping the tradition to make his own point, and pretending they all agree does violence to what each one was actually trying to say. That is a serious charge, and a fair one. It should not be dodged.

A Reply—by Genre, Perspective, and Ancient Convention

The reply has three layers, and it works best when it concedes Ehrman’s strongest point first: he is right that crude harmonization can distort the text, and right that the Evangelists were authors with theological aims, not stenographers. The question is whether that makes their differences contradictions—and here three considerations change the picture.

1. Ancient biography had its own rules

The most important advance in this debate in a generation comes from Michael Licona’s study of Plutarch. The Gospels belong to the genre of Greco-Roman bios (biography), and Plutarch—writing around the same era—left us several Lives that narrate the same events more than once, giving us a comparison case. Licona found about thirty episodes that Plutarch tells two or more times, then catalogued how the same author reports the same event differently: he compresses time, transfers a saying from one occasion to another, displaces events for thematic reasons, simplifies the number of participants, and paraphrases freely.10 These were not errors by the standards of the day; they were accepted compositional devices, taught in the rhetorical handbooks (the progymnasmata).11 When the Gospels do the very same things, then, we are not catching them in mistakes—we are watching first-century authors write the way first-century authors wrote. To demand modern courtroom precision of an ancient bios is an anachronism, like faulting a Rembrandt for not being a photograph.

This is a genuine reframing, not a dodge, but it must be labeled honestly: Licona’s thesis is contested, and not only by skeptics. Some inerrantist scholars, notably Norman Geisler before his death, sharply criticized Licona for conceding that the Evangelists altered details at all, fearing it surrenders too much.12 Ehrman, from the other side, would reply that calling a change a “compositional device” rather than a “contradiction” is a relabeling that does not dissolve the underlying tension. Licona’s framework explains why the differences exist and shows they were not regarded as falsehoods; it does not turn every hard case into a smooth one. It lowers the temperature; it does not end the debate.

2. Most differences are complementary, not contradictory

Apply the difference/contradiction distinction case by case and the great majority resolve, not by force but by reading carefully. The resurrection accounts are the textbook example. They differ on incidentals—the number of women named, one angel or two, the exact words—but converge on a striking common core: Jesus was buried, the tomb was found empty on the first day of the week, women were the first witnesses (an embarrassing detail no one would invent in that culture), and the risen Jesus appeared.13 “Mary went to the tomb” and “Mary and others went to the tomb” do not contradict; mentioning one angel does not deny a second. This is exactly the convergent-yet-varied pattern of independent witnesses—the pattern courts treat as a mark of authenticity rather than collusion, and the pattern Ehrman’s own “horizontal” method, ironically, brings into relief.

The two genealogies are best read as a real difference with more than one reasonable explanation rather than a flat contradiction. The oldest solution, going back to the African church father Julius Africanus in the third century, appeals to levirate marriage, by which Joseph could be the legal son of one man and the biological son of another.14 The most common modern proposal holds that Matthew gives Joseph’s line while Luke gives Mary’s. Honesty requires admitting that neither solution is certain, and that the texts do not tell us which (if either) is right—this is a place where the harmonization is plausible but not provable. What can be said is that the two genealogies are not formal contradictories: a line traced legally and a line traced biologically can both be true of the same man.

3. The hardest cases, conceded as hard

Two of Ehrman’s cases resist easy resolution, and integrity means saying so plainly.

The death of Judas admits a standard harmonization—Judas hanged himself (Matthew), the body later fell and burst (Acts), and the “purchase” in Acts is loosely attributed to Judas because it was bought with his returned money.15 That sequence is possible and not contrived; people who object that a hanged body cannot fall underestimate decomposition and time. But the accounts were plainly written for different purposes and never meant to be read against each other, and the harmonization, while reasonable, is an inference we supply, not something either text states. It removes the charge of strict contradiction; it does not pretend the two passages are saying the same thing.

The day of the crucifixion is the most discussed of all. The strongest harmonizations are substantive, not hand-waving: the phrase “day of Preparation” (Greek paraskeuě) is the ordinary word for Friday, the day of preparation for the Sabbath, so John may simply mean “the Friday of Passover week”; and there is solid evidence that different Jewish groups followed different calendars, so a Passover celebrated on different days is historically possible.16 Yet many careful scholars—including some who fully affirm the Gospels’ reliability—judge that John has, for profound theological reasons, presented Jesus as the true Passover Lamb dying as the lambs were slain (John 1:29; 19:36), and that this shapes his chronology. Licona himself treats it as a likely instance of an ancient author’s compositional freedom rather than a tidy harmony.17 This is, frankly, a case where the data underdetermine the answer. The most defensible position is not “there is obviously no difference” but “there is a real difference, there are serious explanations of it, and on either reading the central claim—that Jesus was crucified under Pilate at Passover—stands untouched.”

The Critic’s Last Word—and the Reply

Ehrman should get the final objection before we answer. He would say: you have conceded the game. Once you admit that John shaped his chronology theologically, that the genealogies cannot both be straightforward records, that Judas’s death needs an unstated splice—you have granted that the Gospels are not simply telling us “what happened,” but reshaping events to make points. Calling that “genre” rather than “contradiction” is a sophisticated way of saving the texts from their own discrepancies; the honest word for an author who moves an event for effect is that he has gotten the event wrong.18 That is a real and pointed challenge.

The reply turns on what “wrong” means. Accuracy is genre-relative. A documentary and a docudrama can portray the same event with different levels of literal precision, and we do not call the docudrama a pack of lies; we judge it by its own conventions. The decisive empirical point is Licona’s Plutarch comparison case: when we watch a single, respected ancient author narrate the same event two ways, he does precisely what the Gospels do—which means these features are the fingerprints of the genre, not of falsehood or of late legend. Ehrman is right that the Evangelists are authors with aims; he is wrong to assume that a first-century author exercising the ordinary liberties of his craft is thereby “getting it wrong” by his own culture’s lights. And his most rhetorically effective examples cut the other way: the resurrection accounts diverge on the surface and converge on the core, which is exactly what truthful independent testimony looks like. The genuinely harder divergence here is the location of the appearances—Matthew and Mark point the disciples to Galilee, while Luke keeps the appearances in and around Jerusalem—and that tension should be named, not glossed; the usual harmonizations (appearances in both regions over the forty days) are plausible but not airtight. The honest verdict is not that every difference dissolves, but that the residue of genuinely hard cases is small, peripheral, and powerless to overturn the central narrative they all share.

The Honest State of Scholarship

Where does the live debate actually stand? On the wider claim—that Scripture’s many books cohere into one developing storyline—there is broad, cross-confessional agreement that a real unity exists, however one explains it. On the narrower question of Gospel discrepancies, the field is genuinely divided, and the division is honest. Critics like Ehrman hold that some differences are true contradictions that no harmonization repairs without special pleading. Defenders like Blomberg and Licona hold that, read in their ancient context, the differences are overwhelmingly the expected variation of independent and genre-bound testimony. A growing center—including many who affirm the Gospels’ substantial reliability—accepts that the Evangelists used ancient compositional conventions, which means conceding more literary freedom than older harmonizers allowed while denying that this amounts to error. That middle position has itself drawn fire from the strict-inerrancy wing, which shows the debate is live inside the church as well as across its borders. What essentially no working scholar holds is the popular extreme on either end: that the Bible is a hopeless tangle of contradictions, or that it contains no real differences requiring thought. The truth, as usual, is more interesting than the slogans.

Conclusion: What Consistency Does and Does Not Establish

So—is the Bible internally consistent? With the difference/contradiction distinction in hand, the genre of each text respected, and the conventions of ancient writing taken seriously, the answer is: substantially yes, with a handful of genuinely hard cases that remain debated and that honesty forbids us to pretend away. The differences among the Gospels are, overwhelmingly, the differences of real witnesses to real events, not the seams of a clumsy forgery—and on the central claims the whole tradition is built on, the accounts converge.

But the scope must be stated as plainly as the conclusion. Demonstrating consistency removes a defeater; it does not, by itself, prove the contents true. An internally coherent account can still be mistaken, and clearing away the charge of contradiction only earns the texts a fair hearing—it does not win the case. Whether what the authors wrote is true turns on other arguments: whether the text has been faithfully preserved, whether the accounts show the unplanned interlocking of genuine eyewitness reports, and how the canon that carries this story came to be. Consistency is the threshold those arguments must cross to be heard. It is worth crossing honestly—conceding the hard cases, refusing the hammer, and trusting that a faith confident of its sources can afford to look its own difficulties full in the face.

Frequently asked questions

Are there contradictions in the Bible that Christians can't explain?

Some differences are genuinely hard, and this essay refuses to pretend otherwise. A key distinction helps: two accounts contradict only if they cannot both be true, not merely if they differ or one omits what another includes. Most alleged contradictions are differences of selection or perspective. A residue of harder cases remains debated, but they are peripheral and don't overturn the central narrative the accounts share.

Why do the Gospels tell the resurrection story so differently?

But they converge on a striking common core: Jesus was buried, the tomb was found empty on the first day of the week, women were the first witnesses, and the risen Jesus appeared.
