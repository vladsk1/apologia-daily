Was Jesus a Muslim? The Christian Response

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Short on time? Read the quick answer →

It is a claim a Christian increasingly meets—in a campus conversation, a comment thread, a kind question from a Muslim coworker: Jesus was a Muslim. The claim is not meant as an insult. For most Muslims it is the opposite—an act of reverence. Islam holds Jesus (Isa) in high honor: virgin-born, the Messiah, a worker of miracles, “a word from God,” one of the greatest of the prophets. To call him a Muslim is, on Muslim lips, to enroll him in the noble line of those who bowed wholly to the one God. So the first thing a Christian owes the claim is not a rebuttal but a hearing. There is real love for Jesus behind it, and Peter’s charge to answer “with gentleness and respect” (1 Peter 3:15) applies before the first word of disagreement.

And yet the claim is, in the end, mistaken—not because Muslims honor Jesus too much, but because the word muslim is being asked to carry two different meanings at once. This essay puts the Muslim case in its strongest form, then offers a Christian response that turns, finally, on the most delicate point of all: that the Jesus of the Gospels really did submit to his Father—and that his submission, rightly understood, is not the mark of a prophet but the self-humbling of God the Son.

The Muslim Case, Stated Fairly

The argument is simpler and stronger than its critics often allow. The Arabic word islam means submission—specifically, submission to God; a muslim is one who submits. In this root sense the Qur’an applies the word freely across sacred history. Abraham, who long predates the religion, is called “neither a Jew nor a Christian, but…one inclining toward truth, a Muslim [submitting to Allah]” (Surah 3:67, Sahih International).1 Jesus’s own disciples declare it of themselves: “We have believed in Allah and testify that we are Muslims [submitting to Him]” (Surah 3:52); and again, “bear witness that indeed we are Muslims [in submission to Allah]” (Surah 5:111, Sahih International).2 On this reading, every true prophet preached the same message—islam, submission to the one God—and Jesus preached it too.

The Qur’anic Jesus himself is the model submitter. From the cradle he says, “Indeed, I am the servant of Allah” (Surah 19:30); the Qur’an insists the Messiah would never “disdain to be a servant of Allah” (Surah 4:172); and twice he proclaims pure monotheism: “Indeed, Allah is my Lord and your Lord, so worship Him” (Surah 19:36; cf. 3:51, Sahih International).3 A servant of God who calls all people to worship God alone—that, says the Muslim, is precisely a muslim.

Now comes the strongest part: the New Testament itself seems to agree. In Gethsemane Jesus “fell on his face and prayed…‘not as I will, but as you will’” (Matthew 26:39); “not my will, but yours, be done” (Luke 22:42). He says, “I can do nothing on my own…I seek not my own will but the will of him who sent me” (John 5:30). He proclaims the oneness of God in the very words of the Jewish Shema: “Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one” (Mark 12:29). He prayed, fasted, and bowed his body to the ground in worship. A man who prostrated himself before God and surrendered his will to God’s—was he not, in the truest sense, a submitter? The Muslim case is not a word-trick; it is a sincere reading of texts both communities revere. It deserves a careful answer, not a dismissive one.

The First Problem: Two Words Wearing One Coat

The answer begins by noticing that the argument quietly slides between two senses of a single word. There is islam with a lowercase i—the generic posture of submission to God, which any faithful Jew, Christian, or theist might be said to share. And there is Islam with a capital I—the specific historical religion founded in seventh-century Arabia, defined by its creed (the shahada: “there is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God”) and by the Qur’an revealed to Muhammad. To prove Jesus was a “submitter” (lowercase) and conclude he was a “Muslim” (uppercase) is to trade on the ambiguity. The premise is about a disposition; the conclusion is about a religion. They are not the same thing, and the inference from one to the other is the logical fallacy of equivocation.4

The point is not merely verbal; it is chronological. The defining content of the religion of Islam did not exist in the first century. There was no shahada, because there was no Muhammad to confess; no Qur’an, because it would not be recited for six hundred years; no five daily prayers toward Mecca, no fast of Ramadan, no pilgrimage of hajj. A first-century figure cannot practise a seventh-century religion. Now, a thoughtful Muslim will answer that this misses the real claim: Islam holds that there is one true religion of all the prophets—Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus alike submitted to the one God (Surah 42:13)—so calling Jesus a “muslim” is not anachronism but Islam’s own theology. That is a fair and serious framing, and it rightly moves the question off rituals. But it also relocates the test: if Jesus is a “muslim” in this perennial sense, it can only be because he held what every true submitter must hold—strict tawhid, the absolute oneness of God, associating no partner with him. And whether he did is not settled by the word at all; it turns entirely on what Jesus actually taught and claimed about himself—which is where the case is decided.

The Second Problem: The Only Jesus History Knows Was a Jew

Strip away the equivocation and ask the historical question directly: what religion did Jesus of Nazareth actually practice? The answer is not in serious dispute among historians of any faith or none. Jesus was a first-century Palestinian Jew. He was circumcised on the eighth day, kept Passover, taught in synagogues, debated Torah with Pharisees, wore the fringes of a devout Jew, and worshipped at the Jerusalem Temple. When he summarized the faith, he quoted the Shema (Mark 12:29) and Leviticus. The eminent Jewish scholar Geza Vermes built a career on situating Jesus firmly within Galilean Judaism; indeed, the historical Jesus’s thorough rootedness in first-century Judaism is one of the few points held right across the scholarly spectrum, from confessional historians to skeptics.5 He observed no Islamic law for the simple reason that none yet existed. The Jesus accessible to history is the Jewish Messiah of the Gospels—not a proto-Muslim, and not a Muslim.

The Third Problem: He Claimed What No Submitter Could

Here the difficulty for the Muslim reading deepens. A muslim, in the Islamic sense, is bound by tawhid—the absolute oneness of God—and the one unforgivable sin is shirk, associating any creature with the Creator. A genuine prophet, on Islamic terms, could never accept worship or claim equality with God; that would be the gravest blasphemy. Yet this is exactly what the Jesus of the Gospels does.

He forgave sins on his own authority. To the paralytic he says, “Son, your sins are forgiven”—and the scribes react with the very logic a Muslim would use: “Why does this man speak like that? He is blaspheming! Who can forgive sins but God alone?” (Mark 2:5–7). Jesus does not correct their theology; he claims the prerogative they reserved for God, and proves it by healing the man (Mark 2:10–12).6 He accepted worship without rebuke—from his disciples in the boat (Matthew 14:33), from the women at the tomb (Matthew 28:9), and from Thomas, who calls him “My Lord and my God!” and is commended rather than corrected (John 20:28–29).7 He applied to himself the divine name of Exodus: “before Abraham was, I am” (John 8:58), and his hearers picked up stones, understanding the claim exactly. He said, “I and the Father are one” (John 10:30), spoke of the glory he shared with the Father “before the world existed” (John 17:5), and taught that all should “honor the Son, just as they honor the Father” (John 5:23).8

On Islamic terms, every one of these is shirk—the unthinkable sin, impossible for any true prophet, let alone the sinless Messiah the Qur’an describes. So the Gospels present a Jesus who is not merely a non-Muslim but, by Islam’s own standard, the opposite of one. The figure who accepts worship and claims oneness with God is the very thing islam exists to forbid.

The Dilemma

This sharpens into a clean dilemma. Either the Gospels are historically reliable about Jesus, or they are not. If they are, then Jesus made and accepted claims to deity—and so was emphatically not a Muslim. If they are not reliable, then we have no first-century portrait of Jesus at all, and no basis for any claim about him—including the claim that he was a Muslim. For the Qur’an offers no independent line of access to the first century. It was recited in Arabia some six hundred years after Jesus, with no chain of historical testimony connecting it to the events. If the Gospels cannot tell us what Jesus believed, the Qur’an certainly cannot. Either way, “Jesus was a Muslim” fails.9

One historical detail seals the point. The crucifixion of Jesus under Pontius Pilate is among the most securely established facts of ancient history—the agnostic scholar Bart Ehrman calls it “one of the most certain facts of history”—attested in the early creed Paul cites in 1 Corinthians 15:3–4 (which critical scholars trace to within a few years of the event), in all four Gospels, and outside the church by Tacitus and Josephus.10 The Qur’an, on its dominant reading, denies it: “they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him” (Surah 4:157).11 Here the Qur’an’s denial collides with some of the firmest evidence we have from antiquity. The same documents that are our only access to Jesus also record his death and his divine claims—and we cannot keep the former while discarding the latter.

“But He Submitted to the Father”—Answered Without Heresy

Now to the strongest and most delicate part of the Muslim case, the one a Christian must answer with great care: Jesus did pray, fast, and submit his will to the Father. He did say, “the Father is greater than I” (John 14:28). If he is God, how could he submit to God at all? Does not his submission prove he was a creature—a submitter, a muslim—after all?

The Christian answer is not to deny the submission; it is to understand who is submitting and why. The historic, orthodox confession is that the one person Jesus Christ exists in two natures, fully God and fully man, united without confusion in a single person—the doctrine defined at the Council of Chalcedon in AD 451.12 The eternal Son did not stop being God when he became man; rather, as Paul puts it, “though he was in the form of God, [he] did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men…he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death” (Philippians 2:6–8).13 The servant is not a creature who climbed up to God; he is God who stooped down to serve.

So when Jesus prays in Gethsemane, fasts in the wilderness, and surrenders his will, he is exercising the genuine, complete humanity he freely took on—and acting out the role he voluntarily assumed within God’s plan of salvation: the obedient Son, the suffering Servant. This is what theologians call functional or economic submission—submission within the saving work (the “economy”) of redemption—not a submission of being. “The Father is greater than I” speaks to the Son’s humble station in the incarnation, not to any inferiority in his divine nature; as Athanasius and the Nicene fathers insisted against Arius, the Son is “of one substance with the Father,” co-equal and co-eternal.14 The Trinity is one God in three co-equal, co-eternal persons; the incarnate Son’s obedience is the freely chosen self-humbling of a divine equal, not the deference of a lesser to a greater.

This is the decisive difference from the Muslim reading. A muslim submits because he is a creature and owes his Creator everything—submission is his only possible posture. The Son submits though he is the Creator, freely taking the servant’s place he was never obliged to take. One submission is the necessity of a creature; the other is the gift of God. To call Jesus’s submission “the obedience of a muslim prophet” is to mistake the condescension of God for the duty of a man. His submission does not diminish his deity; it displays it—for only a love that is fully God could stoop so low.

What the Comparison Establishes—and What It Does Not

Let the conclusion be honest about its reach. This essay does not prove the deity of Christ from the Qur’an; that case stands on the Gospels and is argued on its own ground. It does not assume the Muslim is arguing in bad faith—the love for Jesus behind the claim is real, and the generic sense of islam as “submission” is a genuine and beautiful idea. What the comparison does establish is narrower and firmer: that “Jesus was a Muslim” trades on two meanings of one word; that the only Jesus history knows was a first-century Jew, not a practitioner of a religion that did not yet exist; and that the Jesus of the Gospels claimed and accepted exactly what Islam most forbids—making him, by Islam’s own measure, the opposite of a muslim. A Muslim may of course reject the Gospel portrait; but then he has set aside the only first-century evidence there is, and the claim that Jesus was a Muslim loses its last support along with everything else.

There is common ground worth treasuring: Muslims and Christians are both monotheists, both honor the virgin birth, both call Jesus the Messiah and a “word” from God, both await his return — even as they mean profoundly different things by the one God. That shared reverence is the right place to begin—not to win an argument, but to ask a better question. The Gospels show us someone who did far more than submit to God: he forgave sins as God, accepted worship as God, and laid down his life as God in the flesh. The real Jesus is not merely a submitter to the Lord; he is the Lord who invites our submission—and who offers what no prophet ever could, not a law to obey but himself, crucified and risen, for us. “Come to me,” he says, “all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest” (Matthew 11:28). That is an invitation no submitter could issue—and the deepest reason he could never have been merely a Muslim.

Frequently asked questions

Was Jesus a Muslim, since he submitted to God?

For most Muslims this is an act of reverence, and the love behind it is real. The Arabic islam means submission, and Jesus did pray, fast, and surrender his will. But the argument slides between two senses of one word: the generic posture of submission any theist shares, and the historical religion founded in seventh-century Arabia. Concluding from the disposition to membership in that religion is the fallacy of equivocation.

If Jesus is God, why did he pray to the Father and say the Father is greater?

The answer is not to deny the submission but to understand who submits and why. The one person Jesus Christ is fully God and fully man. The eternal Son did not stop being God when he became man; he humbled himself, taking the form of a servant. His obedience is functional, within the work of redemption, not an inferiority of nature. A creature submits from necessity; the Son submits freely, as a gift.

What religion did the historical Jesus actually practice?

Historians of every faith and none agree Jesus was a first-century Palestinian Jew. He was circumcised, kept Passover, taught in synagogues, debated Torah, and quoted the Shema. Scholars like Geza Vermes situate him firmly in Galilean Judaism, a point held across the scholarly spectrum. He observed no Islamic law for the simple reason that none yet existed. The only Jesus history knows is the Jewish Messiah of the Gospels.
