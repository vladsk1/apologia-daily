The Islamic Dilemma: The Quran, the Bible, and a Two-Horned Trap

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Imagine a Muslim and a Christian sitting together over tea, the Qur'an open between them. The Christian does not begin with the Gospel of John or the empty tomb. He begins, instead, with a question drawn entirely from the Muslim’s own book. He turns to Surah 10:94, where God addresses the Prophet himself: “So if you are in doubt, [O Muhammad], about that which We have revealed to you, then ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you.”1 And he asks, gently: if that earlier Scripture had already been corrupted, why would God send His own prophet to it for reassurance?

That single question opens onto one of the most discussed arguments in modern Christian–Muslim conversation. Christian apologist David Wood, of Acts 17 Apologetics, popularized it under the name “the Islamic Dilemma,” and the Christian debater Christopher Frost has since given it a book-length treatment with a foreword by Wood.2 Its force does not come from pitting one scripture against another by brute assertion. It comes from inside the Qur'an—from the remarkable, repeated, almost tender way the Qur'an speaks of the Torah and the Gospel. The aim of this essay is to state that argument carefully, to handle the Islamic doctrine of tahrif (alteration) with the precision it deserves rather than the caricature it usually receives, and to give the strongest Muslim replies at full strength before weighing them. The goal throughout is the one set by 1 Peter 3:15: an honest question pressed “with gentleness and respect,” not a trap sprung in triumph.

What the Quran Says About the Earlier Scriptures

The first thing to notice is the Qur'an’s own self-description in relation to the books that came before it. Its favorite word for this relationship is musaddiq—“confirming.” The Qur'an presents itself not as the abrogator of the Torah and the Gospel but as their confirmer, “confirming what was before it” (3:3), revealed “confirming that which preceded it of the Scripture” (5:48), and the same posture appears in 2:97, in 35:31, and in roughly a dozen places besides.3 One recent study counts no fewer than twelve occurrences of this confirming language and argues that tasdiq (confirmation), not naskh (abrogation), is the Qur'an’s own preferred category for its relationship to the prior revelation.4

The fifth surah, al-Ma'idah, presses this further—and crucially, into the present tense. Of the Gospel it says: “And We gave him [Jesus] the Gospel, in which was guidance and light” (5:46); and then, addressing the Christians of Muhammad’s own day, “let the People of the Gospel judge by what Allah has revealed therein” (5:47).5 Of the Torah it speaks the same way: “in which is the judgement of Allah” (5:43–44), a living authority the Jews are expected to consult. And in 5:68 the demand is blunt: “Say, ‘O People of the Scripture, you are [standing] on nothing until you uphold [the law of] the Torah, the Gospel, and what has been revealed to you from your Lord.’”6 These are not statements about a vanished ancient text. They are commands to seventh-century Jews and Christians to obey the books in their hands.

Two further threads tighten the knot. First, the Qur'an insists that God’s words are inviolable: “None can alter His words” (6:115), a refrain echoed in 18:27 and 10:64.7 If the Torah and Gospel are God’s words—as 5:46 and 3:3 plainly say—then on the Qur'an’s own logic they cannot have been textually overthrown. Second, this is not only a Qur'anic posture; the hadith corpus reinforces it. In a narration in Sahih al-Bukhari, Muhammad calls for the Torah to settle a case of adultery, and when a teacher tries to hide the verse of stoning with his hand, the verse is uncovered and read aloud—Muhammad then judges by it.8 In Sunan Abi Dawud, the Torah is brought, Muhammad places it on a cushion and says, “I believed in thee and in Him Who revealed thee.”9 That is not how a man treats a book he believes to be a forgery.

The Two Horns

From this material the argument builds a dilemma—a choice between two options, both of which are said to count against Islam. We can state it formally. Premise 1: the Qur'an affirms the Torah and Gospel as God’s revelation and treats the scriptures present in the seventh century as authoritative. Premise 2: the Bible of the seventh century is, by the manuscript evidence, essentially the Bible we possess today. Premise 3: that Bible contradicts the Qur'an on central claims. The conclusion is that the Muslim faces a fork with no comfortable exit.

Horn 1—the Bible is corrupted. If the present Bible is false, then the Qur'an affirmed, confirmed, and commanded obedience to corrupted books, and sent its own prophet to consult them. A God who points seekers to a falsified text as “guidance and light” is either mistaken or misleading—and neither fits the God the Qur'an proclaims.

Horn 2—the Bible is reliable. If it stands as a true witness, then it contradicts the Qur'an precisely where the Gospel is most load-bearing. The Bible affirms the deity of Christ (“the Word was God,” John 1:1; Thomas’s “My Lord and my God,” John 20:28), which the Qur'an denies (4:171; 5:72–73). The Bible makes the crucifixion the hinge of salvation (1 Corinthians 15:3–4), which the Qur'an denies: “they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but [another] was made to resemble him to them” (4:157).10 And the Bible rests everything on the bodily resurrection (1 Corinthians 15:17). If the Bible is trustworthy where it contradicts the Qur'an, then by the law of non-contradiction both cannot be God’s unaltered word.

The pincer is the claim that there is no third option: a text is either reliable or it is not. Reliable, and it refutes the Qur'an; corrupted, and the Qur'an endorsed a corrupt book. That is the argument in its strongest, cleanest form. Now it must be tested—and the testing belongs mostly to the Muslim replies, which are better than the popular version of the dilemma usually admits.

Handling Tahrif Precisely

Everything turns on what Muslims actually mean when they say the Bible is “corrupted,” and here the apologetic literature often gets the history wrong. The Arabic is tahrif, and the tradition has long distinguished two senses. Tahrif al-ma'na is corruption of meaning—the charge that Jews and Christians misread, misinterpret, or conceal the true sense of a text whose wording remains intact. Tahrif al-nass (or al-lafz) is corruption of the text itself—the charge that the very words were rewritten.11 These are very different accusations, and conflating them is the single most common mistake in this debate.

What the Qur'an explicitly charges is narrower than later polemic assumes. It accuses some among the People of the Scripture of concealing what they know (2:42, 2:159, 3:71), of those who “alter the Scripture with their tongues” (3:78), and of twisting words “from their [proper] usages” (4:46).12 Notably, several leading classical commentators—al-Tabari (d. 923) and Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (d. 1209) among them—leaned toward reading these as corruption of meaning, not wholesale textual rewriting; al-Razi reasoned that a book transmitted by so vast a community could not plausibly have been altered word-for-word without detection.13 This matters enormously, because if the dominant Qur'anic and early-Islamic charge is misinterpretation of a basically intact text, then the Muslim still holds the genuine words—which lands him back on Horn 2.

But honesty cuts the other way too, and the apologist who claims that “no Muslim before Ibn Hazm ever alleged textual corruption” has overstated the case. The most authoritative recent survey—Martin Whittingham’s A History of Muslim Views of the Bible: The First Four Centuries (2021)—argues precisely against a tidy chronology in which textual-corruption doctrine is a late invention. Whittingham finds Muslims advancing charges of the Bible’s textual corruption by the ninth century, and probably as early as the eighth, and he explicitly challenges the neat dichotomy between tahrif al-ma'na and tahrif al-nass on which popular apologetics often leans.14 What is true is that the systematic, full-throated case for textual corruption—the view that the Bible as a book is a fabrication—was developed and popularized by Ibn Hazm of Cordoba (d. 1064), and that it hardened into the default Muslim position only later.15 So the careful statement is this: the Qur'an itself does not teach wholesale textual corruption and overwhelmingly affirms the earlier scriptures; the dominant early reading favored corruption of meaning; but textual-corruption charges are older than the “Ibn Hazm invented it” slogan allows. Anyone using this argument should make that concession up front; it costs nothing and earns credibility.

The Strongest Muslim Replies, Stated at Full Strength

Reply 1: The Qur'an affirms the original Torah and Gospel, not today’s Bible

This is the most common response, and in its best form it is not naive. The Muslim grants that God gave a true Tawrat to Moses and a true Injil to Jesus—and indeed that the Qur'an honors them—but holds that the Injil was a single revealed book given to Jesus, now lost, and not to be identified with the four canonical Gospels written about Jesus by Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. On this reading, the Qur'an confirms a revelation that no longer survives intact; what Christians carry today is a later, human production. The confirmation is real but its object is not the modern New Testament.

This deserves a serious answer, not a brush-off. The reply has two pressures on it. First, the Qur'an’s commands are addressed to scriptures physically present in the seventh century: it tells the People of the Gospel to “judge by what Allah has revealed therein” (5:47) and sends Muhammad himself to “those who have been reading the Scripture before you” (10:94). One cannot judge by, or consult, a book that has vanished. Whatever the Injil originally was, the Qur'an treats the Christians of its own day as possessing a usable, authoritative Gospel—and the Gospel those Christians possessed was the four we have. Second, the “lost original” must be a book for which there is no manuscript, no quotation in any Father, and no historical trace whatsoever—while the Gospels the seventh-century church actually read are attested by the fourth-century codices Vaticanus and Sinaiticus and by fragments centuries older still.16 The honest concession is that one can hold the “lost Injil” view; it is just that it requires positing a book that left no footprint, while denying the evidential standing of the one that did.

Islam’s own earliest tradition presses from an unexpected direction here. In the account of the first revelation preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari, Khadija takes the shaken Muhammad to her cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal, described as a man who “during the Pre-Islamic Period became a Christian and used to write the writing with Hebrew letters” and who “would write from the Gospel in Hebrew as much as Allah wished him to write.”17 One need not overstate what this shows. It does not tell us which Gospel Waraqah copied, or how much of it, or settle the language (some narrations add Arabic). But it does place a real, written Gospel in the hands of a Christian inside Muhammad’s own family, at the very hour Islam dates its beginning—and it presents that Gospel-reading Christian as the very witness who reassures Muhammad about his first revelation. Islam’s foundational narrative assumes a Gospel one could still read, copy, and trust; it does not describe a lost book.

There is a more careful escape from the strict either/or, and it deserves to be put at full strength. A thoughtful Muslim need not say the Qur'an endorses the whole canonical Bible. He can say the Qur'an affirms the authentic prior revelation—the true Torah and Gospel as God gave them—while standing as muhaymin, criterion and guardian, over a present text that contains that revelation in a partially distorted form. On this view 5:47 commands the People of the Gospel to judge by the true revelation preserved within their book, not to ratify every line of it. That is a real distinction, and it does genuinely blunt the strict, all-or-nothing version of the dilemma; a Christian should grant as much. But it relocates the difficulty rather than removing it, and a narrower point survives intact: in 10:94 the Qur'an tells Muhammad himself—“if you are in doubt about that which We have revealed to you, then ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you”—to seek his own reassurance from the scripture in the hands of seventh-century believers. A “criterion” that must send the recipient of revelation to consult a partly corrupted book for confirmation is harder to absorb: one does not steady one’s doubts against a witness one regards as unreliable. The criterion model softens the broad either/or; it strains hardest exactly at 10:94.

Reply 2: The Qur'an supersedes (abrogates) the earlier scriptures

This is the more sophisticated reply, and the thrust of much academic pushback on Wood’s argument. The Qur'an, on this view, is the final and governing revelation. Surah 5:48 calls it not only “confirming” the prior Scripture but also muhaymin—rendered “a criterion over it” or “guardian” over it.18 So the Qur'an endorses what was authentic in the Torah and Gospel while standing as the final arbiter, correcting and superseding what diverges from it. Affirming the earlier revelation in principle, the Muslim says, does not commit him to every detail of the present canonical Bible; the Qur'an guards the truth and supersedes the rest.

This is the best response on offer, and it should be granted its real strength: it explains why a Muslim need not become a Jew or a Christian, and it has a genuine foothold in the word muhaymin. But two pressures remain. The first is historical, and it comes from the Muslim side of the scholarship: Whittingham observes that early Muslim writers did not generally treat the previous scriptures as abrogated, and the dedicated study in Religions argues that the Qur'an never presents itself as an “abrogator” of the Bible at all, only as a “confirmer”—so the supersession framework may be a later theological overlay rather than the Qur'an’s own category.19 The second pressure is logical: even granting the Qur'an the role of guardian, 5:47 still issues a present-tense command to the People of the Gospel to judge by their Gospel, and 10:94 still sends Muhammad to consult it. Supersession explains why the Qur'an gets the last word; it does not explain why God would affirm, confirm, and command obedience to books He knew to be corrupted. The supersession reply, in other words, narrows the dilemma and relocates it—but does not dissolve it.

Reply 3: Tahrif means corruption of meaning, not text

Many thoughtful Muslims—and, as we saw, major classical commentators—hold that the Qur'an charges Jews and Christians with misinterpreting and concealing their scriptures, not with rewriting them. The contemporary scholar Yasir Qadhi has been associated with reviving this older “corruption of meaning” position as the more defensible one. It has the virtue of fitting the Qur'anic vocabulary—concealing, distorting with the tongue—and of cohering with the manuscript reality that the biblical text demonstrably did not change.

The difficulty is that this reply, taken seriously, largely concedes the heart of the argument. If only the meaning was twisted while the text remained intact, then Christians today still possess the genuine words of the Torah and the Gospel. And the genuine words—“the Word was God,” “Christ died for our sins”—are exactly the words the Qur'an contradicts. So the “meaning, not text” position delivers the Muslim safely onto Horn 2. This is why the most consistent defenders of Islam against the dilemma tend, in the end, to need some version of textual corruption after all—which is precisely the claim the early tradition and the manuscript evidence make hardest to sustain.

Horn 1 Under the Manuscript Evidence

Horn 1 stands or falls on a historical question: had the biblical text already changed by Muhammad’s time, or could it have changed afterward? Both escape routes are closed by the manuscripts. For the Hebrew Scriptures, the Dead Sea Scrolls—copied roughly between 250 BC and AD 70—include a virtually complete scroll of Isaiah that matches the medieval Masoretic text with striking fidelity across a thousand-year gap.20 For the New Testament, the great fourth-century codices Vaticanus and Sinaiticus preserve the same four Gospels Christians read in the seventh century and read now.21 The Bible in the hands of Muhammad’s contemporaries was, in substance, our Bible.

And there was no later window for a secret rewrite. By the seventh century the biblical text existed in thousands of copies, in Hebrew, Greek, Latin, Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, and more, scattered across three continents among rival communities who shared neither motive nor means to coordinate an identical forgery. Remarkably, the classical Muslim commentator al-Razi reasoned to the same conclusion centuries ago: a text transmitted by so vast a multitude could not have been uniformly altered without the alteration being exposed.22 Horn 1 cannot be escaped by saying “it was corrupted later,” because the manuscript record shows the text did not change.

Beyond the Dilemma: A Second Internal Tension

The Accountability Argument

A related tension, developed at length by Frost, does not depend on the two horns at all; it arises from a single command and a single prohibition standing side by side in the Qur'an. On one hand, the Qur'an commands the People of the Gospel to obey their book: “let the People of the Gospel judge by what Allah has revealed therein” (5:47), warning that those who fail to are “defiantly disobedient.” And it grants that Christians have the capacity to do so—that among them are those who “recite it with its true recital” (2:121) and who “believe in Allah and what was revealed to you and what was revealed to them” (3:199).23 On the other hand, actually to obey the Gospel is to confess Jesus as Lord and Son of God—which the Qur'an calls shirk, associating a partner with God, the one sin it says God “does not forgive” (4:48; 4:116).24 Command and prohibition appear to pull in opposite directions: obey the Gospel and fall into shirk; refuse it and stand among the disobedient.

The Muslim reply is strong and must be heard in full. The shirk, a thoughtful Muslim will say, is precisely what the Qur'an holds the Gospel was corrupted into. The command in 5:47 is to obey the true, uncorrupted Gospel—which, on the Islamic account, taught Jesus as a prophet and never as God. So there is no bind at all: obeying the authentic Gospel would lead a person toward Islam, not into shirk. Granting its premise—that the true Gospel was corrupted away from the canonical one—the reply is internally consistent, and it would relieve the tension. Stated that fully, it is the strongest form of the response.

But notice what the reply leans on. It works only if the Gospel in Christian hands teaches something other than what the canonical Gospels actually say—the very “corrupted Gospel” premise the rest of this essay has already pressured. The Gospel the seventh-century People of the Gospel possessed, and were told to judge by, was the canonical one, and 10:94 sends even Muhammad to consult it. So the second tension is not removed but relocated—onto the same question the dilemma already raises. Bounded honestly, the point is modest and no more: if the Gospel Christians actually hold is substantially the one the Qur'an affirms, then command and prohibition do strain against each other, and the reply that relieves the strain rests on the same corruption claim the manuscript record makes hardest to sustain. This is an internal tension the strongest reply narrows; it is not, on its own, a disproof of Islam, and it is not offered as one.

The Honest State of the Debate

Where, then, does this leave a fair-minded reader? It is worth being candid about both the argument’s strength and its limits. The argument is strongest as an exposure of an internal tension within the Islamic position: the Qur'an’s pervasive, present-tense affirmation of scriptures it then contradicts, combined with its insistence that God’s words cannot be altered, sits uneasily with the later Islamic need to treat those scriptures as unreliable. That tension is real, and the best Muslim replies—supersession above all—narrow it without cleanly removing it.

The argument is weakest when overstated, and two honest limits should be marked. First, the claim that the Qur'an “nowhere even hints” at textual corruption is too strong; a handful of verses (2:75, 2:79, 3:78) were read by some later exegetes as gesturing toward tampering, even if the mainstream early reading was misinterpretation. The most-pressed of them, 2:79—“So woe to those who write the Scripture with their own hands, then say, ‘This is from Allah,’ in order to exchange it for a small price”—repays a close look, since it is the verse Muslims most often cite for textual corruption. In context it will not bear that weight. It sits in a passage (2:75–85) addressed to the Jews of Muhammad’s Medina about a specific deceit; it never names the Torah or the Gospel as the thing written, and it reads most naturally as condemning a particular fraudulent practice—passing off forged writings for money—rather than announcing the wholesale corruption of the biblical text. A further clue points the same way: the very next verse has these same people declare, “Never will the Fire touch us, except for [a few] numbered days” (2:80), which closely echoes a rabbinic tradition that the wicked are punished in Gehinnom only for a bounded term—classically twelve months (Mishnah, Eduyot 2:10).25 That is a recognizably Jewish claim of Muhammad’s own day, not a statement about Scripture at all. So 2:79 is better read as rebuking a local fraud than as teaching that the Bible as a book was rewritten. The defensible claim is that the Qur'an does not teach textual corruption and overwhelmingly affirms the earlier books—not that no verse could ever be enlisted. Second, the history is messier than the slogans: Whittingham’s work shows textual-corruption charges arose earlier than the “Ibn Hazm invented it” story implies, so the argument should lean on the Qur'an’s own confirming language rather than on a clean dichotomy the specialists have complicated.26 A real Muslim critic—and Whittingham is no apologist for the dilemma—would press exactly this point: the tradition is more flexible, and the categories more porous, than the two-horned framing suggests. The candid reply is that this complicates the historical packaging of the argument without removing the core tension the Qur'an’s own verses generate.

Conclusion: What the Dilemma Does and Does Not Establish

The Islamic Dilemma does something real and something limited. What it does is press a genuine internal question on the Islamic account of revelation, drawn not from Christian premises but from the Qur'an’s own words and the Prophet’s own practice. The Qur'an calls the Torah and Gospel God’s revelation, light, and guidance; commands their present readers to live by them; declares God’s words unalterable; and is honored by a Prophet who placed the Torah on a cushion and professed belief in it. Reconciling all of that with the later conviction that those very books are unreliable is harder than it first appears—and the strongest reply, supersession, mitigates rather than dissolves the strain.

Notice what none of the three replies reaches. Each one loosens the broad, all-or-nothing framing: the Qur'an need not ratify every line of the canonical Bible, and a Muslim can appeal to its role as muhaymin—guardian and criterion—to explain why he remains a Muslim. Yet every reply leaves the same residue untouched, and it sits at Surah 10:94, where God tells the Prophet himself, in his own hour of doubt, to “ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you.” A guardian criterion does not steady its own doubts against a witness it regards as unreliable; a superseding revelation does not send its recipient back, for reassurance, to a book it treats as partly distorted; and “corruption of meaning” leaves that book’s words—the very words the Qur'an contradicts—intact in the reader’s hands. Each reply narrows the dilemma; none removes the strain at its core. That is the honest verdict: not that a Muslim has been cornered by a debater’s trick, but that the tension is generated by the Qur'an’s own verses, and it does not dissolve.

This is why the dilemma is worth pressing—and worth pressing gently. It is not, and is not meant to be, a proof of the Resurrection; that Jesus is the incarnate Son who died and rose rests on the historical evidence taken up elsewhere in this library, beginning with the early creed of 1 Corinthians 15:3–7. What the dilemma does is clear the ground: it invites a friend to look again at his own book—and, having looked, to look with the same honesty at ours. “Always be prepared to give an answer,” Peter wrote, “with gentleness and respect.” The dilemma is at its best when it serves that aim: an open question asked in friendship, not a checkmate declared in triumph.

Frequently asked questions

What is the two-horned dilemma about the Quran and the Bible?

The Quran repeatedly affirms the Torah and Gospel as God's revelation and tells their seventh-century readers to obey them. So a Muslim faces a fork: if the Bible was already corrupted, the Quran endorsed a false book and sent Muhammad to consult it; if it was reliable, it contradicts the Quran on Christ's deity, crucifixion, and resurrection. Neither horn offers a comfortable exit.

Why does it matter that the Quran tells Muhammad to ask people who read earlier scripture?

Surah 10:94 tells Muhammad, if he doubts, to ask those who have been reading the Scripture before him. That command is spoken to a book physically present in the seventh century. One cannot consult a vanished text for reassurance. Even the sophisticated Muslim reply that the Quran is a guardian over an authentic core strains hardest here, since it still directs the recipient of revelation to a scripture he is asked to treat cautiously.

Couldn't the Quran only be affirming the original Torah and Gospel, not today's Bible?

This is a serious reply, and a Christian should grant it real strength. But the Quran's commands are addressed to scriptures physically present in the seventh century, telling the People of the Gospel to judge by them. The Gospels those Christians held were the four we have, attested by fourth-century codices. A vanished original leaves no manuscript, quotation, or historical trace, while the Bible that did survive is materially our Bible.
