The Virgin Birth and Isaiah 7:14: What the Gospels Claim and What History Can Say

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Every December, in churches across the world, a line is spoken that ought to stop us short: conceived by the Holy Spirit, born of the Virgin Mary. It is one of the few biographical claims that made it into the creeds at all. The Apostles' and Nicene Creeds skip from birth to death in a single clause about Pontius Pilate, yet they pause to insist on how Jesus was conceived. That deliberateness invites a question worth asking honestly: what exactly do the Gospels claim about the virginal conception, why did the early church think it mattered, and what can the historian responsibly say about it?

This essay makes a modest case. The virgin birth is a creedal affirmation resting on the testimony of two Gospels. It is not a public event open to investigation the way the crucifixion is. It is held by faith, on the evidence of Matthew and Luke, with reasons—not demonstrated like a theorem. Saying that plainly is not a retreat; it is the only honest place to begin.

The claim, stated carefully

The doctrine is precise, and precision matters because so many objections aim at a blurrier target. The claim is the virginal conception: that Mary conceived Jesus while a virgin, without a human father, by the creative power of God's Spirit. It is not a claim about Mary's perpetual virginity, nor (in the New Testament texts themselves) about a painless or miraculous delivery, nor about the immaculate conception of Mary herself—later developments that should not be confused with the original assertion.1

Two narratives carry it. In Matthew 1:18–25, Mary is "found to be with child from the Holy Spirit" before she and Joseph "came together," and an angel reassures Joseph in a dream. In Luke 1:26–38, the angel Gabriel tells Mary she will conceive, and she objects, "How will this be, since I am a virgin?"—to which the answer is, "The Holy Spirit will come upon you" (Luke 1:34–35, ESV). The two accounts are widely regarded by scholars as literarily independent: they share almost no wording, sequence their events differently, and cannot easily be derived one from the other.2 Yet across that independence they converge on a striking cluster of agreements—virginal conception, the name Jesus given by angelic command, birth at Bethlehem, descent from David, and the role of the Holy Spirit. Independent witnesses agreeing on a core is the kind of thing historians take seriously, even when they dispute the details.

The case in depth

The strongest positive consideration is precisely that convergence-amid-independence. If Matthew and Luke had collaborated, their agreements would prove little. If they had simply copied a common source, we would expect verbal overlap. Instead we find two writers, working from different angles—Matthew largely through Joseph's experience, Luke largely through Mary's—who nonetheless land on the same astonishing core. Raymond Brown, whose The Birth of the Messiah remains the landmark critical study, treats the virginal conception as one of the few genuine points of agreement between the two infancy traditions.3

A second consideration is the criterion of embarrassment. A pregnancy before the couple "came together" carried, in first-century Judaism, the unavoidable shadow of illegitimacy. Matthew's narrative shows Joseph "resolved to divorce her quietly" (Matthew 1:19)—a detail no early Christian eager to defend Jesus' honor would invent gratuitously. Hostile traditions did in fact seize on the opening: by the second century, charges that Jesus was born of an irregular union circulated, preserved in the pagan critic Celsus and echoed in later rabbinic material.4 A church fabricating a flattering origin story would have had easier options than one that handed its opponents this weapon. The awkwardness is itself a fragment of evidence—not that the conception was virginal, but that something about Jesus' origin was, from very early, in need of explanation.

Third, the doctrine is early. It is not a medieval accretion. Ignatius of Antioch, writing around 107 AD, already lists "the virginity of Mary and her giving birth" among the "mysteries to be cried aloud."5 Within roughly a generation of the Gospels, the virginal conception is simply assumed, not argued for—suggesting it was received tradition rather than fresh invention.

Isaiah 7:14: handle with care

Matthew alone ties the conception to a text: "All this took place to fulfill what the Lord had spoken by the prophet: 'Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call his name Immanuel'" (Matthew 1:22–23), quoting Isaiah 7:14. This is where the most serious objection lives, and it deserves to be stated at full strength. (The full textual treatment belongs to the companion essay on messianic prophecy; here I summarize.)

In its original setting, Isaiah 7 is a word to King Ahaz of Judah around 734 BC, besieged by a coalition of Syria and Israel. The sign of a child to be born is, on its face, a sign to Ahaz—a near-term assurance that the threatening kings would soon be gone. The Hebrew word Isaiah uses is 'almah, which means a young woman of marriageable age. It does not strictly mean "virgin"; Hebrew has a more specific word for that, betulah. An 'almah was typically presumed unmarried and so presumably a virgin, but the term's focus is youth, not sexual status.6 So the honest concession is this: read in Hebrew, in context, Isaiah 7:14 is not a transparent prediction of a virgin birth seven centuries later. A skeptic who says "Isaiah did not predict a virgin birth" has a real point, and a Christian who pretends otherwise is not being truthful.

What Matthew quotes, however, is not the Hebrew but the Septuagint—the pre-Christian Greek translation made by Jews, which rendered 'almah with the Greek parthenos, "virgin."7 Matthew did not engineer that rendering; he inherited it. So the fair description is layered: Jewish translators, well before Christianity, chose parthenos; Matthew, reading his Scriptures in Greek and convinced that Jesus fulfilled them, saw in that wording a fit with what he believed had happened to Mary. The best account of his logic is not prediction-and-proof but dual fulfillment: a prophecy that had a real meaning for Ahaz's own day and a fuller resonance that Matthew, looking back through the resurrection, found realized in Christ. On this reading the conviction came first—the church already believed Jesus was virginally conceived—and the text was seen, in hindsight, to illumine it. Isaiah 7:14 is best treated as a window, not a proof. As a standalone argument for the virgin birth, it does not work, and the case should not be hung on it.

The strongest objections

The translation objection. Stated at its best (and it has able Jewish and critical defenders): Matthew built a doctrine on a Greek mistranslation; the Hebrew never spoke of a virgin; the whole edifice is a linguistic accident. I concede the core of this. Isaiah's 'almah is not betulah, and the verse is not a slam-dunk prediction. The reply is narrower than apologists sometimes wish: the virgin birth does not derive from Isaiah 7:14. The earliest tradition rests on the events Matthew and Luke report; Isaiah is cited to interpret, not to generate, the claim. Strip the verse away entirely and Luke—who never quotes it—still reports a virginal conception. So the objection wounds a bad argument, not the doctrine itself.

The pagan-parallels objection. Critics in the history-of-religions tradition long argued that the virgin birth is borrowed mythology—Mithras, Dionysus, the divine paternity of Augustus, and others. This must be taken seriously, not waved away. The reply turns on a disanalogy that holds up well. The pagan tales, where they involve miraculous birth at all, are overwhelmingly stories of divine impregnation: a god, in some bodily or quasi-bodily form, unites with a mortal woman, who then is no longer a virgin. Dionysus is the offspring of Zeus and the mortal Semele. The Augustus legend has the god Apollo as a physical agent. Mithras, in the Roman cult, is born from a rock, with no woman in the scene at all.8 None of these is a virginal conception—a woman conceiving with no consort, divine or human, by the non-sexual creative act of God's Spirit. The Jewish, fiercely monotheistic setting makes a borrowed divine-consort myth especially unlikely; the last thing a Palestinian Jewish community would import is a god siring children on women. The candid scholarly verdict, even among many non-confessional historians, is that the alleged parallels are too loose and too late to ground a derivation. That said, this is a negative argument—it shows the borrowing thesis is weak, not that the conception occurred.

The argument from silence. Here the objection bites hardest. Our earliest Christian writer, Paul, never mentions the virgin birth; he says Jesus was "born of woman, born under the law" (Galatians 4:4) and "descended from David according to the flesh" (Romans 1:3)—phrases that neither affirm nor require a virginal conception. Mark, the earliest Gospel, has no infancy narrative at all. John, the latest, opens with a cosmic prologue and never mentions Mary's virginity. So the doctrine is attested in only two of the New Testament's voices, and in the two that wrote infancy narratives at all. The legendary-development thesis—that the virginal conception grew up in the decades between Paul and Matthew/Luke—is a serious historical hypothesis, and Raymond Brown lays it out with notable fairness, concluding that the historicity of the virginal conception cannot be settled by historical-critical methods alone.9 The honest concession: attestation is limited and arguably late, and silence in Paul and Mark is a real datum, not a trick. The measured reply is twofold. First, silence is not denial—Paul wrote occasional letters to churches, not biographies, and had no obvious occasion to discuss Jesus' conception; Mark begins his story at the Jordan as a matter of literary design. Second, the silence cuts less far than it seems precisely because the two writers who did tell the birth story did so independently: their agreement on the virginal conception pushes the tradition back behind both Gospels into an earlier, shared stream rather than a single source one of them invented. The silence is a real datum, and an honest reader will feel its weight—but silence is not denial, and two independent witnesses to a private family matter push the tradition earlier, not later. What the argument from silence cannot do is convert an early, independently attested conviction into a late invention.

The internal-tension objection. Matthew and Luke do not merely differ; in places they pull against each other. Their genealogies of Jesus diverge sharply—Matthew traces Joseph's line through David's son Solomon to a father named Jacob; Luke runs through David's son Nathan to a father named Heli.10 Their itineraries differ: Matthew has a flight to Egypt; Luke has a return to Nazareth with no Egypt at all. Proposed harmonizations exist—that Luke records Mary's line, or that levirate marriage explains the two fathers—but they are reconstructions, not statements the texts themselves make, and a fair reader should label them contested. What the tensions do not touch, however, is the shared core. Two accounts that disagree on route and lineage yet agree on virginal conception, Bethlehem, and Davidic descent look less like one fabricated story than like two independent memories of the same extraordinary claim, each elaborated in its own way.

The honest state of scholarship

Conservative scholars (e.g., the contributors gathered in studies defending the doctrine's historicity) hold that the convergence and the embarrassment criterion make a virginal conception the best explanation of the data. Critical scholars such as Brown regard the question as historically undecidable and theologically held; others, more skeptical still, judge it a relatively late confessional development. Among Jewish interpreters, the reading of Isaiah 7:14 as a near-term sign to Ahaz, with 'almah meaning simply "young woman," is the mainstream and longstanding view, and Christians owe that reading respect rather than caricature. No purely historical method can adjudicate a private, miraculous event attested by two sources a generation or two after the fact. That is the real limit of the evidence, and it should be stated without flinching.

What it does and does not establish

So what can be said? The virgin birth is not a public, multiply-corroborated event like the crucifixion under Pilate. It is the testimony of two Gospels, early and independent, agreeing on a core that ran against the church's social interest. Those features give it real evidential weight; they do not amount to proof. Isaiah 7:14 is best read as dual fulfillment—a word to Ahaz that Matthew saw illumined in Christ—and not as a predictive demonstration. The pagan-parallel charge fails on closer inspection; the silence of Paul, Mark, and John is a genuine weight on the other side of the scale.

In the end this is a confession held by faith, on the testimony of two witnesses, with reasons—and that is exactly how the creeds present it. The early church did not think the virginal conception proved Jesus' divinity; rather, having come to believe he was God-with-us, they saw in the manner of his coming a fitting sign that the new thing God did in Christ was, from the very first cell, God's own initiative and not merely ours. "Behold, the virgin shall conceive... and they shall call his name Immanuel"—God with us. Whether one receives that as history, the reader will have to weigh; but it should be weighed as what it is, neither oversold as airtight proof nor dismissed as borrowed myth.

Frequently asked questions

Did Isaiah 7:14 really predict a virgin birth, or is that a mistranslation?

The essay concedes the skeptic has a real point. In Hebrew, Isaiah uses almah, a young woman of marriageable age, not the specific word for virgin, betulah; in context it's a near-term sign to King Ahaz around 734 BC. Read that way it isn't a transparent prediction of a virgin birth centuries later. The pre-Christian Greek Septuagint rendered almah as parthenos, virgin, and Matthew inherited that. So Isaiah is best treated as a window illumining the claim, not a standalone proof.

Can historians actually prove Jesus was born of a virgin?

No, and the essay makes only a modest case. The virginal conception isn't a public, multiply-corroborated event like the crucifixion under Pilate; it's the testimony of two Gospels, held by faith with reasons, not demonstrated like a theorem. Its evidential weight comes from Matthew and Luke agreeing on the core while writing independently, and from the embarrassment of a pregnancy that risked charges of illegitimacy. Those features give real weight but do not amount to proof.

Doesn't Paul's silence about the virgin birth show the story was invented later?

The essay calls this the objection that bites hardest and concedes silence in Paul and Mark is a genuine datum. The measured reply is twofold: silence is not denial, since Paul wrote occasional letters with no obvious occasion to discuss Jesus' conception, and Mark begins at the Jordan by design. And the silence cuts less far because Matthew and Luke told the birth story independently, pushing the tradition back behind both into an earlier shared stream, though that stream need not reach all the way to the event itself.
