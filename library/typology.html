Christ in All the Scriptures: How the Old Testament Foreshadows Jesus Through Typology

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

A Boy Carrying the Wood Up the Mountain

Long before there was a cross, there was a hillside in the land of Moriah, and a boy climbing it with a load of firewood on his back. His father walked beside him carrying the fire and the knife. "My father," the boy said, "where is the lamb for a burnt offering?" And Abraham answered, "God will provide for himself the lamb" (Genesis 22:7–8). Any Jewish reader has always felt the weight of that scene. But a Christian reading it after Good Friday feels something more: a beloved, only son, carrying the wood of his own sacrifice up a hill outside Jerusalem, while a father says God will provide the lamb himself. The story was not a coded prediction. It was Abraham's true history. And yet it rhymes.

This essay is about that rhyme. It is the companion to our essay on messianic prophecy, which handles the Old Testament's forward-looking predictions. Here we take up something different and, in some ways, richer: typology — the way the persons, events, and institutions of the Hebrew Scriptures form patterns that come to a point in Jesus of Nazareth. This is not prophecy, and treating it as prophecy misunderstands it. It is not a probability trick, and it proves nothing by odds. It is, done honestly, one of the most beautiful and unifying features of the Christian Scriptures — and it has its own distinct logic, its own warrant, and its own honest limits.

What Typology Is — and What It Is Not

A type (from the Greek typos, "pattern" or "stamp") is a real person, event, or institution in the Old Testament that God so shaped within Israel's history that it foreshadows a greater reality — the antitype — fulfilled in Christ.1 Three things distinguish typology from its neighbors. First, unlike allegory, typology honors the historical reality of the type: Isaac really lived, the Passover lamb was really slain, the temple really stood. The pattern does not dissolve the history into a hidden meaning; it rests on the history. Second, unlike prediction, a type is usually recognized retrospectively — read backward, in the light of the resurrection, not decoded in advance. Third, unlike coincidence, a genuine type involves both correspondence and escalation: the antitype is not merely similar to the type but greater than it. Isaac is spared; the Son is not. The Passover lamb covers one household for one night; the Lamb of God takes away the sin of the world.

Crucially, this is not a reading Christians imposed on an unwilling text from the outside. The New Testament authors do it themselves, and they hand us the vocabulary. Paul calls the wilderness generation's experiences typoi — "these things took place as examples for us" — and says "they were written down for our instruction" (1 Corinthians 10:6, 11). He calls Adam "a type [typos] of the one who was to come" (Romans 5:14). The letter to the Hebrews describes the tabernacle as "a copy and shadow [skia] of the heavenly things" (Hebrews 8:5) and the whole law as having "a shadow of the good things to come instead of the true form of these realities" (Hebrews 10:1). Paul writes that the festivals and food laws were "a shadow of the things to come, but the substance belongs to Christ" (Colossians 2:16–17). Peter even coins the mirror-word, calling baptism the antitypon of Noah's flood (1 Peter 3:21).2 And Jesus frames the whole enterprise: on the Emmaus road, "beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself" (Luke 24:27; cf. 24:44; John 5:46). The Christian is not smuggling Jesus into the Old Testament. The Christian is following a reading the New Testament performs on nearly every page.

The honest danger: parallelomania

But here we must be candid, because the method can be abused, and often has been. In 1961 the Jewish scholar Samuel Sandmel, in his presidential address to the Society of Biblical Literature, named the disease "parallelomania": "that extravagance among scholars which first overdoes the supposed similarity in passages and then proceeds to describe source and derivation as if implying literary connection flowing in an inevitable or predetermined direction."3 The warning is exactly right, and Christians should own it. If you go looking for Jesus in every scarlet thread and every piece of wood, you will "find" him everywhere — which means you have found him nowhere in particular, because a method that confirms everything confirms nothing.

So responsible typology submits to controls. Following scholars such as Leonhard Goppelt, whose 1939 study Typos remains the standard treatment, and Richard Davidson, who examined the New Testament's own typos vocabulary, three tests separate a real type from a pious coincidence.4 (1) Is the link drawn by the New Testament itself, or is it the interpreter's ingenuity? The strongest types — Adam, the Passover lamb, the bronze serpent, Melchizedek, the tabernacle — are ones the apostles explicitly name. (2) Is there genuine correspondence plus escalation, so that Christ is manifestly the greater reality, or is it mere surface resemblance? (3) Is the pattern organic to the one unfolding story of redemption, or is it a stray detail wrenched from context? We should concede plainly that many cherished typological readings — Rahab's scarlet cord, the two coins of the Good Samaritan — are later devotional extensions, edifying perhaps, but not apostolic. This essay flags the difference, and leans on the types the New Testament itself certifies.

Persons Who Prefigure the Person

Adam, the last Adam

Paul builds an entire theology on one man mirroring another. "As in Adam all die, so also in Christ shall all be made alive" (1 Corinthians 15:22). Where the first Adam's disobedience brought condemnation to all, the "last Adam" — the "second man" from heaven — brings life-giving righteousness (Romans 5:12–21; 1 Corinthians 15:45). Here the escalation is total: the type is the man who failed the garden; the antitype is the man who obeyed in the wilderness and the greater garden of Gethsemane. This is not resemblance for its own sake; it is the architecture of the gospel.

Isaac and the mountain

Return to Genesis 22. The correspondences are not manufactured — they are in the text: the "only" and beloved son (22:2), the wood carried by the son himself (22:6), the three-day journey to a mountain in the region where Jerusalem's temple would later stand (22:2, 4; cf. 2 Chronicles 3:1), the substitute ram caught in the thicket (22:13), and the naming of the place "The LORD will provide" (22:14). The writer to the Hebrews reads it as a resurrection story in miniature: Abraham "considered that God was able even to raise him from the dead, from which, figuratively speaking [Greek en parabolē], he did receive him back" (Hebrews 11:19).5 Note the escalation, and note the mercy: where the knife was stayed over Isaac, it was not stayed over Christ — because this time the Father himself supplied the Lamb.

Joseph, betrayed and enthroned

Joseph is never called a type of Christ in so many words, so we hold this one at the level of theme rather than certified type — but the shape of his story is unmistakable. The beloved son is rejected and sold by his own brothers for pieces of silver, condemned though innocent, and then raised from the pit of prison to the right hand of a Gentile king — from which position he becomes the savior of the very brothers who betrayed him. And his own summary is one of the most Christ-shaped sentences in the Old Testament: "As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to bring it about that many people should be kept alive" (Genesis 50:20). That is the cross in a single line: human betrayal and divine salvation running through the same event.

Melchizedek, priest and king

A mysterious figure meets Abraham in Genesis 14 — Melchizedek, "king of Salem" and "priest of God Most High," who blesses Abraham and receives his tithe. Psalm 110:4 then swears that the coming king will be "a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek," and Hebrews 7 makes him a type of Christ: a priest who is also a king, whose priesthood does not depend on descent from Levi. Hebrews says he was "without father or mother or genealogy, having neither beginning of days nor end of life"Melchizedek as a typeIs sayingGenesis records no lineage, birth, or death for Melchizedek, and that deliberate silence of the record makes him a fitting portrait of a priest whose office never ends.Not sayingThat Melchizedek was an eternal or divine being — nor that the Son’s eternal generation derives from him. The type is only a shadow; the eternal Son casts it, and is not diminished by it. (Hebrews 7:3). This line must be handled with care, because it is easily misread. Hebrews is not making a metaphysical claim that Melchizedek was an eternal being, still less identifying the Son's eternal generation with him. The author is reasoning from the silence of the record: Genesis, which meticulously supplies the genealogy and death of every other patriarch, gives Melchizedek none — and that literary silence makes him a fitting portrait of a priest whose office never ends.6 The type is the shadow; the eternal Son casts it, and is not diminished by it.

David, the anointed and unrecognized king

David completes a pattern the others begin — and here, as with Joseph, we are in the realm of theme rather than a type the New Testament explicitly names. The shepherd from Bethlehem is secretly anointed by Samuel while another still sat the throne (1 Samuel 16), and then endures years of rejection, exile, and pursuit before he actually reigns. The interval between anointing and enthronement — the true king already chosen but not yet acknowledged, hunted rather than crowned — has long been read by Christians as echoing the shape of the Messiah's first and second comings. The Davidic kingship, by contrast, the New Testament does apply directly to Jesus: it is no accident that "Son of David" became the crowd's title for him (Matthew 21:9; cf. Luke 1:32–33).

Events and Institutions That Point Beyond Themselves

Typology is not only about persons. Israel's great rescue, its worship, and its architecture all lean forward.

The Passover and the exodus

Paul states it flatly: "Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed" (1 Corinthians 5:7). The correspondences are dense and deliberate in the Gospels: an unblemished lamb (Exodus 12:5), blood applied so that judgment passes over (Exodus 12:13), and the striking rule that "you shall not break any of its bones" (Exodus 12:46) — which John explicitly invokes when the soldiers, finding Jesus already dead, do not break his legs: "these things took place that the Scripture might be fulfilled: 'Not one of his bones will be broken'" (John 19:36).7 Behind the lamb stands the whole exodus: a people enslaved, redeemed by blood, and led out of bondage into freedom — the master-pattern the New Testament uses for redemption itself.

The bronze serpent, the manna, the rock

Jesus reads his own crucifixion out of a strange episode in Numbers: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in him may have eternal life" (John 3:14–15; cf. Numbers 21:4–9). Those bitten by the serpents were healed by looking, in faith, at the thing lifted up. He reads himself out of the manna, too: "I am the bread of life... the bread that comes down from heaven" (John 6:35, 48–51; cf. Exodus 16). And Paul reads him out of the rock that gave water in the desert: "they drank from the spiritual Rock that followed them, and the Rock was Christ" (1 Corinthians 10:4; cf. Exodus 17). In each case the sustaining gift in the wilderness turns out to be a shadow of the One who sustains eternally.

The tabernacle, the temple, and the torn veil

The deepest institutional type is the dwelling of God with his people. The tabernacle and temple were where heaven and earth met, where a holy God made his presence available behind a veil. John says the reality has come in person: "the Word became flesh and dwelt among us" — the Greek eskēnōsen literally means "pitched his tent," "tabernacled" among us (John 1:14). Jesus is the true meeting place of God and humanity. Hebrews works this out at length: the earthly holy places are "copies of the true things," and Christ entered "into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God on our behalf" (Hebrews 9:24). And at the moment he died, "the curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom" (Matthew 27:51) — the barrier removed, so that we now have "confidence to enter the holy places by the blood of Jesus, by the new and living way that he opened for us through the curtain, that is, through his flesh" (Hebrews 10:19–20).

The Day of Atonement and the sacrificial system

On the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16), the high priest entered the Most Holy Place with blood, and two goats were used: one slain, and one — the scapegoat — sent away bearing the people's sins into the wilderness. Hebrews reads the entire apparatus as a shadow whose repetition was itself the point: "in these sacrifices there is a reminder of sins every year. For it is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins" (Hebrews 10:3–4). The escalation is precisely in the word repeated versus once: the type had to be renewed every year because it never finished the job; the antitype "has appeared once for all at the end of the ages to put away sin by the sacrifice of himself" (Hebrews 9:26). Even Noah's ark joins the pattern — salvation carried safely through judgment, which Peter links to baptism and to Christ (1 Peter 3:20–21).

The Three Offices That Converge

One theme deserves its own heading, because it unifies much of the above. In Israel, three roles were set apart by anointing with oil: the prophet who spoke God's word, the priest who mediated God's presence, and the king who ruled God's people. They were deliberately kept separate — when King Uzziah tried to seize the priest's role, he was struck with leprosy (2 Chronicles 26:16–21). Yet each office pointed beyond its bearer: no prophet spoke the final word, no priest offered the final sacrifice, no king reigned forever. The very title "Messiah" (Hebrew mashiach, Greek christos) means "anointed one" — and in Jesus the three anointed offices that Israel held apart converge in one person: the Prophet greater than Moses (Acts 3:22), the Priest after the order of Melchizedek (Hebrews 7), and the King of David's everlasting line (Luke 1:32–33). What Israel could only distribute among many, the antitype holds together in one. Theologians have long called this the munus triplex, the threefold office of Christ.

A word of caution about Wisdom

Some writers add the personified Wisdom of Proverbs 8 to this list, since the New Testament calls Christ "the wisdom of God" (1 Corinthians 1:24) and says that in him "are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3). This theme is legitimate only when handled with real care. Proverbs 8 is a poetic personification of an attribute of God, and its line "The LORD possessed me at the beginning of his work" (Proverbs 8:22) has been misused since Arius to argue that the Son is a created being. It cannot bear that weight, and we do not rest anything on it. The Son is not a creature and had no beginning; he is "true God from true God, begotten, not made" (Nicene Creed). Christ is the wisdom of God because the eternal Son is God — not because a literary personification proves it. We note the theme; we refuse the Arian misreading.

The Strongest Objection — Stated Fairly

The most serious objection is Sandmel's, sharpened: this method can prove anything, and a method that can prove anything proves nothing. Give a clever reader any two texts and a devotional motive, the critic says, and patterns will appear — the human mind is a pattern-finding machine that sees faces in clouds. Worse, the objection continues, typology looks like a takeover: the Hebrew Scriptures had their own meaning for the community that wrote and treasured them, and Christians have read their conclusion back into another people's book, effectively telling Jewish readers that the plain sense of their Scriptures was never the real point. This is not a trivial worry, and it should be voiced in its full force, not a watered-down version. It is felt keenly, and honestly, by Jewish scholars and by secular critics of Christian reading alike.

Three replies, conceding what should be conceded. First, we grant the observation and deny the inference. Yes, patterns can be over-read; that is exactly why the controls above exist, and why this essay repeatedly flags which readings are apostolic and which are later devotion. And we should concede the sharpest edge of the point: pointing to the apostles' own example (control 1) carries weight chiefly for someone who already grants their authority, so that control mostly disciplines our selection rather than refuting the skeptic outright. The independent work — telling a disciplined correspondence from an arbitrary resemblance — is done by the other two controls, escalation and organic fit within a single unfolding story, which can be weighed on their own terms. The abuse of a method does not discredit its disciplined use — historians, doctors, and detectives all reason by pattern and correspondence, and the answer to sloppy pattern-reading is careful pattern-reading, not none. Second, the original meaning is real and is not erased. Christian reading at its best affirms what scholars call sensus plenior, a "fuller sense" God intended in the text that its human author need not have consciously foreseen — a fuller sense that completes the plain meaning rather than deleting it.8 Isaac's binding really happened and really taught Israel about faith and substitution before any Christian read it; the exodus really rescued Israel before it became the pattern of a greater rescue. We are not claiming Moses secretly foresaw Calvary. We are claiming that the God who authored both the history and its completion built the correspondence in. Third, this is a family reading, not a hostile one. The earliest people to read the Hebrew Scriptures this way were Jewish — Jesus, Peter, Paul, the author of Hebrews. The New Testament's typology is an argument conducted within Israel's Scriptures about their destination, offered, as Paul insisted, "with great sorrow and unceasing anguish" for his own people (Romans 9:2–3), never with contempt. Charity here is not softness; it is accuracy. The Jewish reading of these texts in their own context is real and serious, and Christians dishonor their own case when they caricature it.

The State of the Question

Among biblical scholars, typology has undergone a striking rehabilitation. For much of the twentieth century, under the influence of purely historical-critical method, typology was dismissed as pre-critical fancy. But the recovery of "biblical theology" — the study of Scripture's own inner-textual connections — has restored it to respectability. Goppelt's Typos, translated into English in 1982, and major studies by Richard Davidson, G. K. Beale, and Benjamin Gladd have argued that the New Testament's typological reading is not arbitrary but follows discernible, describable patterns rooted in the text.9 The current debate is largely a family one within Christian scholarship: it concerns how far typology extends (the "maximalists," who find Christ densely woven through the Old Testament, versus "minimalists," who restrict it to the cases the New Testament names), and whether the human authors had any awareness of the fuller sense or whether it is entirely retrospective.10 What is not seriously disputed is that the New Testament authors themselves read this way, deliberately and pervasively. The question is how far to follow them, not whether they did it.

What This Does — and Does Not — Establish

Be clear about the genre of this argument, because misusing it does real harm. Typology is not a probability proof, and no honest advocate should dress it in the borrowed clothes of statistics. It does not, by itself, compel a skeptic from unbelief to faith. Unlike a fulfilled prediction — which can in principle be verified by a neutral observer comparing a dated text to a later event — a type is recognized in hindsight, in the light of the resurrection. If you do not yet have reason to trust that Jesus rose, the patterns will, quite understandably, look like patterns one could have drawn anywhere. That is why the resurrection, not typology, is where the case for Christ begins.

What typology does do is different and, in its place, immense. For those who already have reason to trust the resurrection — from the early creed of 1 Corinthians 15, the empty tomb, the transformation of the disciples — typology supplies something no single prediction can: coherence. It shows that the same authorial hand that ended the story wrote its beginning; that a library composed over a millennium by dozens of writers who never met turns out to have a single center. It is the difference between a fulfilled forecast and a finished symphony — where a theme sounded quietly in the opening bars returns, transfigured, in the finale, and you realize it was going there all along. Isaac's question — "where is the lamb?" — waited two thousand years for John the Baptist to point and say, "Behold, the Lamb of God" (John 1:29). The type does not prove the answer. But once you have the answer, the type shows you how long, and how lovingly, it was prepared. And that is why the risen Christ, with the whole of Scripture at his disposal on the Emmaus road, chose to open it — "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15) — and show two heartbroken disciples himself.

Frequently asked questions

Aren't you just reading Jesus into everything? Couldn't you find patterns anywhere?

This is the strongest objection, and it has a name: parallelomania, coined by the Jewish scholar Samuel Sandmel in 1961. We grant it real force. A method that can find Jesus everywhere has found him nowhere in particular. That is exactly why responsible typology uses controls: the strongest types are ones the New Testament authors themselves draw (Adam, the Passover lamb, Melchizedek, the tabernacle); a genuine type shows correspondence plus escalation, not mere resemblance; and it belongs to the one unfolding story of redemption. We flag which readings are apostolic and which are later devotion, and lean only on the former.

Isn't this just the same as prophecy?

No, and the difference matters. A prophecy is a forward-looking prediction that can, in principle, be checked by comparing a dated text to a later event. A type is a real person, event, or institution recognized as a foreshadowing in hindsight, read backward in the light of the resurrection. Prophecy says "this will happen"; typology says "this pattern finds its completion here." Typology is not a probability argument and proves nothing by odds. It supplies coherence for those who already have reason to trust the resurrection, showing that one authorial hand shaped the whole story.

Does the Christian reading erase the original Jewish meaning of these texts?

It should not, and at its best it does not. The original meaning is real: Isaac's binding taught Israel about faith and substitution, and the exodus really rescued Israel, long before any Christian read them. Christians affirm a fuller sense (sensus plenior) that God intended and that completes the plain meaning rather than deleting it. We are not claiming Moses foresaw Calvary. The earliest people to read this way were themselves Jewish believers, and Paul made the case with anguish for his own people, never contempt. Charity here is accuracy: the Jewish reading in its own context is serious and deserves an honest hearing.
