“We Will Guard It”: The Preservation Promise and the Lost Verses

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Walk into almost any conversation about why Muslims trust the Qur’an, and one verse will surface quickly. It is short, confident, and printed on posters in Islamic centers around the world: “Indeed, it is We who sent down the Qur’an and indeed, We will be its guardian.”1 This is Surah al-Hijr 15:9, and for a great many Muslims it is the headline guarantee that the book they hold has reached them flawless—not a letter added, not a letter removed—preserved by God himself in a way no other scripture has been. It is offered, in particular, as the decisive contrast with the Bible: where Christians (it is said) lost the original wording, God kept the Qur’an whole.

That is a serious claim, and it deserves a serious and respectful hearing. But here is the tension this essay exists to examine, and it is a tension that arises not from Christian polemic but from Islam’s own most trusted sources. The same hadith tradition that carefully transmits Surah 15:9 also transmits the second caliph ’Umar, standing on the Prophet’s own pulpit, testifying that a “verse of stoning” was once recited as Qur’an and is now nowhere in the book. It transmits ’A’isha reporting a “verse of suckling” that was Qur’an at the Prophet’s death and is absent today. It records the caliph ’Uthman burning every variant copy to enforce one text. The question, then, is not whether Muslims are dishonest—they are transmitting their own scriptures faithfully—but whether the popular, maximal reading of 15:9 can survive contact with the hadith that sit right beside it.

Stating the Question Carefully

Precision matters here more than almost anywhere, because it is easy to overstate this case into something false. The argument is not that the Qur’an is “full of holes,” or that its text is wildly unstable, or that whole doctrines have vanished. The consonantal Arabic text that Muslims read today is, by the standards of ancient documents, remarkably stable; that is a separate question, treated in the companion essay on the Qur’an’s preservation. Nor is this the same as the question of abrogation in the abstract, which has its own dedicated treatment.

The claim here is narrower and, precisely because it is narrow, harder to escape. It is this: the strong form of the preservation claim—“perfectly guarded, not one word ever lost”—requires heavy qualification on Islam’s own reports. The argument can be put formally. Premise 1: the popular reading of 15:9 asserts that every word of the revelation has been divinely protected from loss. Premise 2: Islam’s own most authoritative sources report that material once recited as Qur’an is no longer in the Qur’an. Conclusion: therefore the strong reading of 15:9 cannot be maintained without redefining what “preservation” means—and the redefinition costs the verse its force as a knockdown contrast with the Bible. The premises do the work, and a Muslim can resist the conclusion; the rest of this essay tries to weigh whether the resistance succeeds.

The Promise: Surah 15:9 and Its Reception

The Arabic of 15:9 speaks of al-dhikr, “the Reminder,” which the mainstream tradition takes to mean the Qur’an, and of God being its hafiz, its guardian or keeper. The plain sense is a divine commitment to protect the revelation. What turns this verse into the centerpiece of modern preservation apologetics is the maximal gloss placed on it in popular teaching—the routine assertion that the Qur’an “has remained exactly as it was revealed, with not a word or letter added or removed.” That is the bar Muslims themselves frequently set, and it is an extremely high one. A bar set that high can be cleared only if nothing was ever lost. So the relevant test is simple: does the tradition report any loss of recited revelation? It does—and the reports are not fringe.

The Strongest Case: ’Umar and the Verse of Stoning

The single weightiest report concerns the “verse of stoning” (ayat al-rajm). It is preserved in both Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim—the two collections Sunni Islam ranks as the most reliable books after the Qur’an itself. In Sahih Muslim, the caliph ’Umar ibn al-Khattab sits on the pulpit and declares that God sent Muhammad with the truth and revealed the Book to him, “and the verse of stoning was included in what was sent down to him. We recited it, retained it in our memory and understood it.” He recounts that the Prophet carried out stoning for adultery and that the community did so after him. Then comes his stated worry: that with the passage of time someone will say, “We do not find the verse of stoning in the Book of Allah,” and so go astray by abandoning a duty God revealed.2 The Bukhari version carries the same substance.3 In a widely cited form of the report, ’Umar adds that were it not that people would accuse him of adding to the Book of God, he would have written the stoning verse into the text with his own hand.4

The force of this is hard to overstate, and it owes nothing to any Christian source. By the testimony of Islam’s second caliph—a Companion of the highest rank, speaking publicly, recorded in the two Sahihs—there was once a recited verse of the Qur’an that is simply not in the Qur’an today. Whatever one concludes, the maximal claim “not one word ever removed” has met a direct internal counter-witness.

The Verse of Suckling—and the Sheep

A second strand concerns the “verse of suckling.” In Sahih Muslim, ’A’isha, the Prophet’s wife, reports: “It had been revealed in the Holy Qur’an that ten clear sucklings make the marriage unlawful, then it was abrogated (and substituted) by five sucklings, and Allah’s Messenger died and these words were among what was recited in the Qur’an.”5 Read carefully, this report has the “five sucklings” wording still being recited as Qur’an at the moment of the Prophet’s death—and it is not in today’s text.

A more dramatic version appears in Sunan Ibn Majah. There ’A’isha says that the verse of stoning and the verse of suckling an adult ten times were revealed and written on a sheet kept under her bed, and that when the Prophet died and the household was distracted by grief, “a tame sheep came in and ate it.”6 Honesty requires flagging at once that this last detail is contested even within Islam: the chain of the Ibn Majah report runs through Muhammad ibn Ishaq narrating with the ambiguous ’an, and mainstream hadith scholars—the fatwa site IslamQA among them—judge the sheep detail weak (da’if) and reject it as an anomaly, noting that the stronger parallel through Imam Malik (as in Muslim and the Muwatta’) does not mention the animal at all.7 The responsible apologist does not lean on the sheep. The point that survives the weak link is the one already established by the Sahih material: that recited revelation on the subjects of stoning and suckling existed and is no longer present.

A Surah Once Far Longer

A third strand is broader still. Several reports indicate that Surah al-Ahzab (chapter 33), which today contains 73 verses, was once dramatically longer. The fifteenth-century scholar al-Suyuti, in his classic manual of Qur’anic sciences al-Itqan fi ’ulum al-Qur’an, transmits reports that al-Ahzab was once recited with around 200 verses, and that ’A’isha and the Companion Ubayy ibn Ka’b held it had been as long as—or longer than—Surah al-Baqara, which runs to 286 verses.8 Here, too, charity and care are owed: the authenticity of these particular narrations is genuinely disputed among Muslim scholars. Some, such as Ibn Hazm and the modern hadith specialist al-Albani, regard a key chain as sound; others, such as al-Busiri and al-Haythami, judge the relevant isnads weak.9 Because the grading is contested, this strand should be presented as reported, not asserted—but it is part of the cumulative internal picture, and it comes from Muslim scholarship, not from outside it.

The Mechanism Offered: Naskh al-Tilawa

None of this caught Muslim scholars by surprise. The classical tradition long ago developed a category precisely to house these reports: naskh al-tilawa, the abrogation of recitation. The doctrine distinguishes several kinds of abrogation, and the relevant one holds that God could withdraw the wording of a verse from the recited text while—in the stoning case—leaving its ruling in force. On this account the stoning verse was not lost; it was deliberately removed by the same God who revealed it, an act of divine editing entirely within his prerogative. This is not an improvised dodge. It is a settled position in the standard works on Qur’anic sciences, and it is the answer a thoughtful Muslim will give. The argument cannot proceed honestly until that answer is stated at full strength.

The Mainstream Muslim Reply, at Its Best

Set out properly, the defense has real coherence, and it runs along three lines.

First, on 15:9 itself: the verse, the defender argues, was always about the Qur’an as the finally collected and divinely intended book—the mushaf God meant his community to possess—not a guarantee that no word would ever be withdrawn during the period of living revelation. Revelation was dynamic while the Prophet lived; God gave, abrogated, and replaced. The promise to guard “the Reminder” attaches to the completed product, which has indeed been kept with extraordinary fidelity ever since. So 15:9 and the lost-verse reports are not even talking about the same moment.

Second, on the nature of the loss: there was, strictly, no loss at all. Loss implies failure, accident, an outcome God did not will. But naskh al-tilawa describes the opposite: an intentional divine removal. A poem from which the author deletes a stanza has not been corrupted; it has been edited by the one with the right to edit it. That ’Umar and ’A’isha could report the withdrawn material is, on this reading, evidence of the system working transparently, not of a leak in it.

Third, on the status of the reports: the strongest of them describe pre-canonical material—revelation in flux during and immediately after the Prophet’s lifetime, before the text was closed under Abu Bakr and standardized under ’Uthman. They therefore describe the process by which the canon was settled, not gaps in the settled canon. The community that received these very hadith as authoritative never concluded that its Qur’an was deficient; it concluded that abrogation had done its work. And the famous sheep detail, the defender adds rightly, is weak and should be dropped from the discussion entirely.

This is a genuinely strong position. It is internally consistent, it is anchored in classical scholarship, and it dissolves the crude version of the objection (“your book is corrupted”) completely. Any assessment that ignores it is not worth reading.

Weighing the Reply

So does the defense hold? The honest verdict is layered: it succeeds against the strong charge and fails against the modest one—and the modest one is all the argument ever needed.

Begin with what must be conceded. The abrogation framework genuinely blocks the accusation of corruption. If naskh al-tilawa is accepted, then nothing was lost through human failure; the present text is exactly what God intended to remain. A Christian who insists, against this, that the Qur’an is “tampered with” has not understood the position he is criticizing. That concession should be made freely and early.

But notice the price of the rescue. The maximal claim that draws Muslims to 15:9 in the first place—“not a single word has ever been added or removed”—is now plainly false on Islam’s own terms. Words were removed; the tradition simply says God removed them on purpose. “Perfect preservation” has quietly become “preservation of whatever God chose to keep,” which is a different and far weaker proposition. It is compatible with recited verses being present at the Prophet’s death and absent a generation later. The verse on the poster and the verse in the seminar are no longer the same verse.

And here the critic deserves the sharpest form of the second-order objection, before any reply. A skeptic of the maximal claim—set against the backdrop of the wider academic study of the Qur’an’s text and variant readings10—will press the point further: how does one tell principled divine withdrawal from ordinary human loss? Both produce the identical observable outcome: a verse once recited, now gone. “God meant to remove it” is unfalsifiable; it is exactly what one would say to protect a doctrine of preservation from any conceivable counter-evidence. The “sheep” report, whatever its weakness, at least describes loss in the plain sense—accident, distraction, a chewed sheet—and the very fact that the tradition reaches for an animal to explain a gap suggests the early community did not always experience these absences as serene acts of divine editing.

That objection lands a real blow, and it should not be brushed aside. The considered reply, though, is to grant its force while marking its limit. The Christian apologist is not, in fact, obliged to prove that the losses were human accidents rather than divine acts. That stronger thesis is unfalsifiable in both directions and is not what the argument needs. The argument needs only the modest conclusion, and the modest conclusion is secure: on Islam’s own most trusted sources, material once recited as Qur’an is no longer in the Qur’an, and therefore the popular “not one letter ever changed” reading of 15:9 cannot stand without redefinition. Whether the redefinition is theologically respectable (it may well be) is a separate question from whether it leaves the verse usable as a trump card against the Bible (it does not). Once “preservation” means “God kept what he meant to keep,” it has become close to what thoughtful Christians have always said about Scripture—and it can no longer do the polemical work of marking the Qur’an off as categorically different.

The ’Uthmanic Standardization

One further datum sharpens the picture rather than carrying the argument by itself. Sahih al-Bukhari 4987 records that roughly two decades after the Prophet, the Companion Hudhayfa was alarmed that Muslims in Syria and Iraq were reciting the Qur’an differently enough that he feared they would “differ about the Book as the Jews and the Christians did before them.” The caliph ’Uthman responded by commissioning one authoritative text and then ordering that “all the other Qur’anic materials, whether written in fragmentary manuscripts or whole copies, be burnt.”11 One must be fair about what this does and does not show. A Muslim will reasonably reply that standardization is an act of stewardship—eliminating scribal and dialectal divergences to safeguard a single text—and that is a coherent reading. It is not, by itself, proof of lost content. But it does sit awkwardly beside the maximal slogan: a text that God had already kept letter-perfect and uniform across the whole community would not have generated divergences alarming enough to require a centralized text and a bonfire. The burning is, at minimum, a fingerprint of human editorial effort at the heart of the canon’s formation—effort that the strong reading of 15:9 has trouble accommodating.

The Honest State of the Question

Where does this leave matters among people who study it carefully? The factual core is essentially uncontested. Sunni scholarship has never denied the stoning-verse report; it is in the two Sahihs, and the classical doctrine of naskh al-tilawa exists precisely to accommodate it. The live disagreement is interpretive, and it runs along a spectrum. At one end, traditional scholars affirm abrogation of recitation as a settled reality and feel no tension with 15:9, which they read as a promise over the completed canon. At the other, some modern Muslim thinkers are uneasy with naskh al-tilawa altogether, precisely because it concedes that recited revelation was withdrawn, and prefer to minimize or reinterpret the reports. Western academic scholars—from the skeptical (Wansbrough) to the more text-focused (Nasser, and the broader Corpus Coranicum project in Berlin)—treat these traditions as windows onto a messier, more human process of canonization than the devotional account allows. What no serious party holds is the bumper-sticker claim that the reports are Christian fabrications; they are Islam’s own sahih material, and that is the whole point.

Conclusion: What This Does and Does Not Establish

This argument establishes something real and something limited, and the discipline is in keeping them apart. It does not establish that the Qur’an is corrupted, unreliable as a text, or riddled with loss; the abrogation framework is a coherent answer to that charge, and the Arabic consonantal text is genuinely stable. A Christian who claims more than this has overreached and should retract.

What it does establish is narrower and, on its own ground, secure: the maximal, popular reading of Surah 15:9—“perfectly guarded, not one word ever lost”—cannot be maintained against Islam’s own most trusted reports without redefining “preservation” into something far more modest. And once it is redefined to “God kept what he chose to keep,” it stops being the clean, decisive contrast with the Bible that it is so often made to be. That is worth saying gently, because it touches something Muslims hold dear, and because the goal is never to win a point but to free a conversation. The deepest Christian confidence has never rested on a claim of miraculous letter-perfect transmission at all—manuscripts establish preservation, not truth—but on the historical reality of a man who died and, his followers testified, rose again. To that the lost verses, the kept verses, and the burned copies are all, finally, pointing the same direction: toward the question of where one’s hope is actually grounded.
