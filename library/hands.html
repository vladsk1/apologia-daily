Five Fingers Pointing to One Identity: The HANDS Framework and the Deity of Christ

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Five Fingers Pointing to One Identity

Somewhere around the year 55, a Jewish follower of Jesus named Paul dictated a letter to a congregation in Philippi. He was a monotheist to the marrow — raised on the Shema, "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is one" (Deut. 6:4). And yet he wrote that one day "at the name of Jesus every knee should bow… and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord" (Phil. 2:10–11). Any Jew in Philippi who knew his Scriptures would have heard the echo instantly. Those words belong to God alone: "To me every knee shall bow, every tongue shall swear allegiance" (Isa. 45:23) — spoken by YHWH in the very chapter where he declares, "I am the LORD, and there is no other" (Isa. 45:5).1 Paul did not flinch. He took language from the most jealously exclusive claim in the Hebrew Bible and applied it to a crucified Galilean.

That is the puzzle the New Testament keeps setting before us. How did the earliest followers of Jesus — Jews who would die before bowing to an idol — come to honor a man as they honored God, and believe they were still being faithful to the one God? The most useful map of the biblical evidence is a memory device coined by Robert M. Bowman Jr. and J. Ed Komoszewski in their 2007 book Putting Jesus in His Place: The Case for the Deity of Christ: the acronym HANDS.2

The Framework and Its Origin

Bowman and Komoszewski argue that the New Testament presents Jesus as sharing five things that belong to God alone — and they arrange them as the five fingers of a hand: Honors (Jesus shares the honors due to God), Attributes (he shares the attributes of God), Names (he shares the names of God), Deeds (he shares in the deeds only God does), and Seat (he shares the seat of God's throne).2

The acronym is theirs, but the underlying logic belongs to a broader movement in New Testament scholarship sometimes called "divine identity" Christology. Its leading exponent is the British scholar Richard Bauckham, whose 1996 Didsbury Lectures were published as God Crucified: Monotheism and Christology in the New Testament (Paternoster, 1998) and later expanded into Jesus and the God of Israel (Eerdmans, 2008).3 Bauckham's thesis is that Second Temple Jews defined their one God not chiefly by abstract attributes but by identity — by who God is and what marks him out: he alone created all things, he alone rules all things, he alone bears the divine name, he alone is worshiped. The New Testament writers, Bauckham argues, deliberately include Jesus within that unique identity. The historian Larry Hurtado reached a complementary conclusion from a different angle in his Lord Jesus Christ: Devotion to Jesus in Earliest Christianity (Eerdmans, 2003): a "binitarian" pattern of worship — devotion offered to Jesus alongside God — erupted astonishingly early, within the first couple of decades, and among Jews.4 HANDS is, in effect, a layperson's organization of the same evidence these scholars marshal.

H — Honors

In John 5:23 Jesus says the Father has given all judgment to the Son "that all may honor the Son, just as they honor the Father." The standard of honor is not like God's but identical to it. More striking is worship. In Revelation 5:13–14 every creature sings "to him who sits on the throne and to the Lamb" the same blessing and honor, and the elders fall down and worship — even though the same book twice has an angel refuse worship with the command "Worship God!" (Rev. 19:10; 22:9). The Lamb receives what the angel forbids.

A — Attributes

The New Testament ascribes to Jesus attributes that belong to God by nature. He is eternal: "In the beginning was the Word… and the Word was God" (John 1:1). He is the agent and upholder of all things: "in him all things hold together" (Col. 1:17); he "upholds the universe by the word of his power" (Heb. 1:3). John even reports that Jesus "knew all people and… knew what was in man" (John 2:24–25). These are not, taken singly, knock-down proofs — but cumulatively they describe one who stands on the Creator's side of the Creator–creature line.

N — Names

Three threads converge here. First, the title Kyrios ("Lord"). The Greek Old Testament used Kyrios to render the divine name YHWH, and New Testament writers repeatedly take such YHWH texts and apply them to Jesus (Phil. 2:10–11 with Isa. 45:23; Rom. 10:13 with Joel 2:32). Second, "I am." In John 8:58 Jesus says, "Before Abraham was, I am" — and his hearers pick up stones, the response to blasphemy. Third, the outright title "God": Thomas's confession "My Lord and my God!" (John 20:28), which Jesus accepts; and Titus 2:13, "our great God and Savior Jesus Christ."

D — Deeds

Jesus does what only God does. He creates: "All things were made through him" (John 1:3); "by him all things were created" (Col. 1:16). He gives life and judges: "the Son gives life to whom he will… the Father… has given all judgment to the Son" (John 5:21–22) — and the Old Testament insists God alone gives life and judges the earth.

S — Seat

The most quoted Old Testament verse in the New Testament is Psalm 110:1: "The LORD says to my Lord, 'Sit at my right hand.'" To sit on God's throne is to share God's rule. Revelation pushes furthest: there is one throne "of God and of the Lamb" (Rev. 22:1, 3) — a single seat, shared.

The Strongest Objections

A responsible case must let its critics speak at full strength. Three objections deserve a hearing.

1. The agency reading (Jehovah's Witnesses and biblical Unitarians)

The most serious challenge comes from those who affirm the Bible but deny that Jesus is God by nature — Jehovah's Witnesses and biblical Unitarians among them. Their key tool is the Jewish principle of agency, the shaliach: "a man's agent is as the man himself."5 An ambassador is honored, obeyed, and received as though he were the king — without being the king. On this reading, every HANDS line is the dignity of a supreme agent. Jesus is honored (John 5:23) precisely because the Father gave him judgment; he does God's deeds because God works through him; he sits at God's right hand as the exalted Messiah of Psalm 110, the man at God's side, not God himself. They note John 8:58 is rendered "I have been" in their New World Translation, claiming a statement of pre-existence rather than the divine name.6 The agent does the sender's work and bears the sender's authority; that is what agency means. This is the steelman, and it is genuinely strong: much New Testament language about Jesus' being "given," "sent," and "appointed" fits it naturally.

2. The developmental thesis (Bart Ehrman)

Bart Ehrman, in How Jesus Became God: The Exaltation of a Jewish Preacher from Galilee (HarperOne, 2014), argues that the historical Jesus did not claim to be God and was not worshiped as God in his lifetime. Christology developed: an early "exaltation" model (God adopted the man Jesus and raised him to heavenly status) gave way to a later "incarnation" model (a pre-existent divine being came down), reaching its height in the latest Gospel, John, and culminating only at Nicaea in 325.7 On this view the loftiest HANDS texts — "I am," Thomas's confession, the shared throne — are the church's later theology read back onto Jesus, not history.

3. The functional-versus-ontological objection

A more academic worry, rooted in Oscar Cullmann's influential The Christology of the New Testament (1959), is that the New Testament speaks functionally — about what Jesus does and the role he plays in salvation — not ontologically, about his eternal nature.8 To say Jesus exercises divine functions, the objection runs, is not yet to say he is divine in essence; the metaphysics of Nicaea is a later (if legitimate) development. The respected scholar James D. G. Dunn pressed a version of this in Christology in the Making (1980), arguing that Paul's Wisdom language need not imply Christ's personal pre-existence, and that full pre-existence appears clearly only in John.9

Reply

Take the agency reading first. It explains a great deal — and the proponents are right that the New Testament really does use the language of sending and giving. But agency has a ceiling, and the HANDS evidence crashes through it. No shaliach in Jewish thought ever received worship; agency conferred authority, never adoration. That is exactly why the angel in Revelation — himself God's messenger — refuses the worship the Lamb accepts. An agent represents the sender; he does not share the sender's throne (Rev. 22:1), bear the sender's exclusive name (Phil. 2 with Isa. 45), or do the sender's unshareable work of creating all things (John 1:3; Col. 1:16). Bauckham's point is decisive here: a creature, however exalted, stands on the created side of the line; the New Testament places Jesus on the Creator's side. As for John 8:58, even on the milder "I have been" rendering, the reaction — an immediate attempt to stone him (John 8:59) — is hard to square with a tame statement of pre-existence; and where John does state the ground of such an attempt, it is the charge that "you, being a man, make yourself God" (John 10:33).

Against Ehrman, the strongest evidence is the one thing his late-development thesis cannot easily accommodate: the early material. Philippians 2 is widely regarded as a pre-Pauline hymn — older than the letter, within twenty-odd years of the crucifixion — and it already applies an exclusive-worship text of YHWH to Jesus. The Aramaic prayer Marana tha, "Our Lord, come!" (1 Cor. 16:22), is a fossil of devotion in the original language of the Palestinian church. Hurtado's whole argument is that the highest devotion is early, not late — and Bauckham insists the inclusion of Jesus in the unique divine identity occurs across the New Testament writings, not just John.3 The development was in articulation, not in invention.

On functional versus ontological: the distinction is real and worth keeping. The New Testament's idiom is largely functional, and Nicaea's precise vocabulary of "essence" is indeed later. The honest concession is that one cannot read Chalcedon's metaphysics straight off the page of Mark. But — and here the framework's cumulative logic matters — the functions the New Testament assigns Jesus are functions Jewish monotheism reserved to God's being. Creating the world, ruling from the one throne, receiving worship, bearing the Name: in Second Temple Judaism these were not job descriptions a creature could be hired for. They were identity markers. To do what only God does, as God's equal recipient of worship, is already to be implicated in who God is. The function presupposes the identity.

What the Scholarship Honestly Shows

It would be triumphalist to claim the matter is closed. The "divine identity" reading associated with Bauckham and Hurtado has become something like the majority view among specialists, though Dunn himself continued to press a more cautious line to the end (see his Did the First Christians Worship Jesus?, 2010).9 But it is contested. A capable critic such as Dunn maintained that some Pauline texts are read too maximally, and a minority of scholars still favor a more developmental or adoptionist account of the earliest period. Individual proof-texts remain debated: whether Titus 2:13 and Romans 9:5 call Jesus "God" turns on Greek grammar; whether Philippians 2 quotes YHWH specifically, or "Lord" more loosely, is disputed.1 No single HANDS finger, pressed alone, will compel a determined skeptic.

That is precisely why the framework is cumulative and convergent. The case does not rest on one verse or one line. It rests on the way five independent strands — honors, attributes, names, deeds, seat — all point in the same direction across documents written by different authors in different decades. One ambiguous text proves little; five converging lines, each drawing on the exclusive prerogatives of Israel's God, are far harder to explain as anything but the deliberate inclusion of Jesus within the divine identity.

What It Does and Doesn't Establish

The HANDS framework is not a single mathematical proof of the Trinity, and it should not be sold as one. It does not settle by itself every question of metaphysics that the fourth-century councils would later define; nor can it force assent from someone committed to a unitarian reading of every passage. What it does establish is narrower and, in its way, more powerful: that the New Testament — read in its own first-century Jewish context — presents Jesus of Nazareth as sharing the honors, attributes, names, deeds, and throne of the one God of Israel, and does so early, pervasively, and on purpose. The doctrine of Christ's deity is not a Hellenistic accretion bolted on at Nicaea. It is the riddle the New Testament itself sets, and to which Thomas, falling at the feet of the risen Jesus, gave the only adequate answer: "My Lord and my God."
