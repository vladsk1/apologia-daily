Answering Mormonism on the Godhead

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

On a warm spring afternoon in 1844, at a general conference in Nauvoo, Illinois, Joseph Smith stood before a vast crowd—perhaps twenty thousand people—and preached a funeral sermon for a man named King Follett. Within three months Smith himself would be dead, killed by a mob at Carthage Jail. But the sermon endured, because in it he said something no Christian had ever quite said before. “God himself was once as we are now,” he declared, “and is an exalted man, and sits enthroned in yonder heavens.”1 And then, to the living: “you have got to learn how to be Gods yourselves…the same as all Gods have done before you.”2

It is hard to overstate how much of the difference between Latter-day Saint faith and historic Christianity is compressed into those two lines. They are not said in anger or in jest. They express, beautifully and earnestly, a vision of God and of human destiny that millions of devout people have found worth ordering their lives around. And they are, by the measure of the Nicene Creed and—this essay will argue—of the Bible itself, a different doctrine of God altogether. The purpose here is to state that difference accurately, weigh it fairly, and answer it with the gentleness and respect that the apostle Peter required of anyone who would give a reason for their hope (1 Peter 3:15).

Stating the Question Carefully

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints uses the word Godhead, and it confesses the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost. To an outsider that can sound Trinitarian. It is not—and Latter-day Saints are the first to say so. The question of this essay is therefore not whether Mormons are sincere, or moral, or Christ-centered; many plainly are. The question is narrower and sharper: is God one being or several? Everything else follows from how that is answered.

Two further clarifications keep the conversation honest. First, the dispute is genuinely theological, not merely verbal: both sides agree they disagree, and the LDS Church openly distinguishes its teaching from the creeds. Second, charity requires that we let each side speak in its own words and reckon with its own best texts. So we will begin where fairness demands—with what the Latter-day Saints actually teach, drawn from their own canon and their own official sources.

What the Latter-day Saints Actually Believe

The contemporary, official statement is admirably clear. The Church’s own Gospel Topics entry on the Godhead describes God the Father, His Son Jesus Christ, and the Holy Ghost as “three separate beings” who are “one in purpose and doctrine.” The Father and the Son “have tangible bodies of flesh and bones, and the Holy Ghost is a personage of spirit.”3 This is not a fringe reading; it is catechesis.

The teaching has deep roots in the LDS scriptural canon. Doctrine and Covenants 130:22, recorded from Joseph Smith in 1843, states: “The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man’s; the Son also; but the Holy Ghost has not a body of flesh and bones, but is a personage of Spirit.”4 Where historic Christianity confesses (with John 4:24) that “God is spirit,” LDS revelation locates the Father in a glorified physical body.

Two further doctrines complete the picture, and they are the most distinctive of all. The first is what Latter-day Saints call eternal progression or exaltation—the conviction that God was once a man who progressed to godhood, and that the faithful may follow the same path. Its most famous summary is the couplet composed by Lorenzo Snow, later the fifth president of the Church, around 1840: “As man now is, God once was: As God now is, man may be.”5 (The couplet is also widely quoted with “may become”; Snow’s recorded wording is “may be.”) This is the same teaching Joseph Smith pressed in the King Follett discourse. The second is a corollary: a genuine plurality of gods. The Book of Abraham, part of the LDS canon, narrates creation in the plural—“the Gods organized and formed the heavens and the earth” (Abraham 4:1).6 Critics of the LDS view describe the result, in technical terms, as a form of social tritheism tending toward henotheism: three divine persons who are numerically three beings, set within a wider order of gods, with worship directed to the Father. This is a critic’s characterization, and LDS thinkers contest it—many prefer to speak of one Godhead united in purpose and will, and resist the “tritheism” and “henotheism” labels as importing categories foreign to their own self-understanding.

It is worth pausing to honor what is attractive here. The LDS vision dignifies the human person with an almost vertiginous destiny, and it takes the language of being “partakers of the divine nature” (2 Peter 1:4) with great seriousness. The disagreement that follows is not a failure to find this moving. It is a disagreement about whether it is true.

The Historic Doctrine: One God in Three Persons

Against this stands the Nicene confession, shared by Catholics, the Eastern Orthodox, and the historic Protestant traditions alike: there is one God—numerically one divine being, one undivided essence—who exists eternally as three coequal, coeternal persons: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. The persons are really distinct (the Father is not the Son), but they are not three beings; they share, wholly and without division, the one being of God. As the philosopher Dale Tuggy notes in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, this insistence that the three are “the same God” without being identical to one another is precisely what makes the doctrine philosophically demanding—and what distinguishes it from any form of tritheism.7

Two features of this God are load-bearing for the argument with Mormonism. He is numerically one; and he is immutable and eternally God—he did not become God, and there was never a God before him. The LDS Godhead, by contrast, affirms three beings and a God who progressed to deity. The two pictures cannot both be right.

The Case from Scripture: Strict Monotheism

The decisive biblical material is Isaiah’s great monotheism oracles. They do not merely assert that Israel should worship one God; they make a sweeping claim about reality. “Before me no god was formed, nor shall there be any after me” (Isaiah 43:10). “I am the first and I am the last; besides me there is no god” (44:6). And, most pointedly: “Is there a God besides me? There is no Rock; I know not any” (44:8).8

Read against the LDS doctrines, the force is hard to miss. The phrase “before me no god was formed” uses a verb of origination and excludes precisely a God who existed prior to Yahweh—the predecessor-deity that eternal progression requires. “Nor shall there be any after me” excludes the exalted gods that exaltation promises. And “I know not any” functions as the strongest possible denial—Yahweh disclaiming knowledge of any rival—rather than, on its face, the report of a literal divine inventory. The decisive weight sits in the surrounding language: the origination verb “formed,” which rules out a God prior to Yahweh; Isaiah 44:24, where Yahweh “stretched out the heavens alone”—not in concert with a council of separate deities; and the address to “all the ends of the earth” (45:22), which extends the claim past Israel to reality as such. To this the New Testament adds the immutability and spirituality of God: “I the LORD do not change” (Malachi 3:6); “from everlasting to everlasting you are God” (Psalm 90:2); “God is not man” (Numbers 23:19); “God is spirit” (John 4:24).

The Strongest LDS Replies, Stated at Full Strength

A charitable treatment must let the best Latter-day Saint reading of these texts stand at its full height before answering. There are three serious moves, and the first two are stronger than critics often admit.

First steelman: “Ye are gods”—the Bible itself teaches deification

Psalm 82:6 says, “I said, you are gods, sons of the Most High, all of you.” And in John 10:34–36, Jesus himself quotes that very verse to defend his own claim to deity. The LDS reader presses: deification is not a Mormon invention smuggled into Scripture; it is in Scripture, on the lips of Christ. The God-and-gods language the creeds find scandalous is biblical. Moreover, the early church fathers spoke this way too. Athanasius wrote that the Word “became man that we might be made god”—theosis, the deification of the believer, is ancient orthodoxy, not heresy. On this reading, Mormonism has recovered a truth the later creeds obscured.

Second steelman: the texts show three beings

The strongest LDS case is not really exegesis of single words; it is the cumulative force of the narrative scenes. At Jesus’ baptism, the Son stands in the water, the Spirit descends as a dove, and the Father speaks from heaven (Matthew 3:16–17)—three, in three places, at once. In Gethsemane the Son prays to the Father, his will distinct from the Father’s (Luke 22:42). Stephen, dying, sees “the Son of Man standing at the right hand of God” (Acts 7:55–56)—two figures, side by side. The Latter-day Saint says, with real plausibility: I am simply reading what the text shows. It is the creeds that import a Greek metaphysics of “one substance” that the biblical writers never used. Mormons keep the scenes; Trinitarians read Athens.

Third steelman: Isaiah is anti-idol rhetoric, not a metaphysical census

Finally, the LDS reader argues that Isaiah’s context is polemic against Babylonian idols. “There is no god besides me” means there is no rival worthy of Israel’s worship—no carved Marduk that can save—not a metaphysical headcount ruling out exalted beings on other worlds. The same idiom appears where the sense is plainly comparative: Babylon says in her heart, “I am, and there is no one besides me” (Isaiah 47:8), without claiming to be the only city on earth. To wield Isaiah against eternal progression, the critic is reading a worship-polemic as if it were systematic theology.

The Considered Reply

Each of these deserves a real answer, and the first requires a concession.

On Psalm 82 and theosis. The Creator/creature line is never crossed: we are made partakers, not partners of the same species., and historic Christianity has its own rich doctrine of theosis. The fathers were not embarrassed by 2 Peter 1:4. But the patristic and the LDS doctrines share a word and almost nothing else. Athanasius, in the very treatise where he speaks of our becoming “god,” insists relentlessly on the absolute divide between the uncreated Creator and everything created.9 The believer is deified by grace, as a glorified creature who never crosses into being God by nature; we are made “partakers,” not partners of the same species. Crucially, no church father ever taught that God the Father was once a man who progressed to deity—that is the claim with no patristic parallel, and it is the heart of the King Follett doctrine. As for Psalm 82 itself, read to its end it is the worst possible proof-text: the “gods”—whether corrupt human judges or rebel heavenly powers—are condemned to “die like men” (82:7). It is a demotion scene. And Jesus’ use of it in John 10 is an a fortiori argument about the elasticity of a word, deployed while he claims something categorically higher (“I and the Father are one,” 10:30)—which is why his hearers still reached for stones.

On the narrative scenes. This is the objection to take most seriously, and the honest reply is that the scenes prove distinction, which the Trinity affirms every bit as firmly as Mormonism does. The baptism and Gethsemane refute modalism—the idea that Father, Son, and Spirit are mere masks—not Trinitarianism. What the scenes cannot do, by themselves, is settle the question of essence: three persons acting distinctly is equally compatible with three beings and with three persons of one being. To decide between those, one must read the scenes together with the statements—“the LORD is one” (Deuteronomy 6:4); “the Word was God” (John 1:1); the single “name” (singular) of Father, Son, and Holy Spirit at baptism (Matthew 28:19). The Trinity is the reading that keeps both the distinction and the oneness; tritheism keeps the scenes and drops the oneness texts. John 17:21–22 is sometimes pressed here—Jesus prays that believers may “be one” as he and the Father are one—but the comparison must be completed carefully, or it backfires. The oneness believers are invited into is participation by grace: creatures drawn into communion, not absorbed into the divine essence. The Father-Son oneness in the same Gospel is categorically different in kind: the Son shares in creation (“all things were made through him,” John 1:3), speaks the divine “I AM” (John 8:58), and receives the worship due to God alone (John 5:23; 20:28). Believer-unity is participation in the life of God; the Son’s unity is the very deity that life participates in. So 17:21 does not flatten the Father and the Son into the same category as glorified believers—and that is precisely why it gives no purchase to a reading on which exaltation makes the faithful gods of the same kind.

On Isaiah as rhetoric. The polemical setting is real—concede it. But the language is engineered to outrun the loophole. Isaiah does not say “no god for you”; he says none was formed, a verb about origins, ruling out a deity prior to Yahweh. He has Yahweh claim ignorance of any other god—“I know not any”—which is an odd thing to say if the point were only that idols are useless. And he addresses the claim to “all the ends of the earth” (45:22), not to Israel alone. The Babylon parallel (47:8) actually tells against the objection: Isaiah deliberately puts Yahweh’s “there is none besides me” and Babylon’s arrogant echo of it side by side—and the whole point is that what is blasphemous self-deception in Babylon’s mouth is sober truth in Yahweh’s. The text is built to exclude every conceivable rival, in every direction of time.

A Surprising Witness: The Book of Mormon Itself

Here the conversation can take an unexpectedly gracious turn, and it is worth handling with care rather than as a gotcha. The earliest layer of LDS scripture—the Book of Mormon, published in 1830—teaches what looks like classical monotheism. Amulek is asked plainly, “Is there more than one God?” and answers, “No” (Alma 11:28–29). The Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are called “one Eternal God” (Alma 11:44). Other passages speak of “the Eternal God” who is “unchangeable from all eternity to all eternity” (Moroni 8:18; cf. Mosiah 3:5).10

Latter-day Saint scholars reply, reasonably, that “one God” in these texts can be read as oneness of purpose, and that the Book of Mormon’s Trinity-sounding language reflects an early stage of revelation later clarified. That is a fair internal reading and should be granted as one possible harmonization. But the tension is real and is felt within the tradition: the doctrines of a God who was once a man and of a plurality of gods are products of Joseph Smith’s later Nauvoo period (1843–44), not of the 1830 text. The point to make—gently, and as an invitation rather than a trap—is this: a Latter-day Saint who finds Isaiah’s monotheism foreign need only open Alma 11 to find the same God confessed in their own first book. The question is not creed versus prophet; it is the later sermon versus the earlier scripture.

The Deepest Issue: Did God Become God?

Beneath the proof-texts lies a question of pure reason that the contingency and cosmological arguments sharpen. If God was once a man who progressed to deity, then he was exalted by some prior god, who was exalted by a prior god, in an infinite regress—and LDS theology indeed envisions such a chain, set within eternally existing matter and law that God organizes rather than creates from nothing. The difficulty is not aesthetic. Such a chain explains nothing: the system of exaltation, the matter, and the laws all simply exist, unexplained, with every god a member of the system and none its ground. The Latter-day Saint may fairly retort that the Christian, too, posits one eternal, uncaused being and calls it mysterious—so why is one brute fact respectable and an eternal chain not?

The reply is that the two are not symmetrical. A self-existent, necessary being is not one more contingent item in a series; it is the terminus that any explanation of contingent things requires—the conclusion that the cosmological arguments reach on independent grounds. An infinite regress of dependent gods is a series of items each of which needs explaining, with the explanation forever deferred. And here Scripture and reason converge on the same verdict: “before me no god was formed” is exactly the denial that God is a member of any such chain. A God who became God is, by the Bible’s own definition—and by the logic of ultimate explanation—not God at all.

The Honest State of the Question

Several things should be said plainly. The descriptive claims are not in dispute: LDS theology really does teach three separate divine beings, a Father with a physical body, eternal progression, and a plurality of gods—this is the Church’s own self-description, not a hostile caricature, and recent LDS scholarship (for instance in the Church’s 2014 essay “Becoming Like God”) reaffirms it while nuancing the more speculative edges.11 What is contested is the exegesis. Serious Latter-day Saint thinkers offer genuine readings of Psalm 82, John 17, and the narrative scenes, and the “social” family of Trinitarian models in mainstream theology shows that the line between robust distinction of persons and tritheism is itself philosophically delicate—a point Tuggy and others press against Trinitarians as well.7 The honest verdict is not that the LDS position is absurd, but that it cannot be squared with Isaiah’s monotheism or with the immutability of God without straining both—and that, on its own deepest commitment, a God who progressed cannot be the uncreated ground of all things.

Conclusion: What This Does and Does Not Establish

This argument establishes a real and important conclusion: the LDS doctrine of the Godhead is not a variant within the Trinity debate but a departure from the biblical monotheism the Trinity was formulated to protect. Three separate beings “one in purpose” is not the one undivided God of Isaiah; a Father of flesh and bone is not the God who is spirit; a God who was once a man is not the God who is “from everlasting to everlasting.” On the central question—is God one being or several, and has he always been God—Scripture and the historic creeds answer one way, and the King Follett discourse answers another.

What the argument does not establish is anything about the sincerity, the morality, or the love for Christ of the Latter-day Saints who hold it—much of which puts many of us to shame. It does not by itself prove the Trinity true; that case rests on its own scriptural and rational supports. And it does not settle every disputed verse; the exegesis of Psalm 82 and John 17 remains a live conversation. What it does is locate the real fault line precisely, so that the conversation can be the right one: not a quarrel about words, but a shared search for the living God—conducted, as it must be, with gentleness and respect, and with the hope that the God who is one will make himself known.
