The Minimal Facts Argument: Building the Resurrection Case on Ground Even Sceptics Concede

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

An Argument Designed to Survive Disagreement

Most arguments for the resurrection of Jesus run into the same wall on the first move. The case begins by quoting the Gospels, and the sceptic answers that the Gospels are precisely what is in dispute — late, biased, theologically motivated documents written by believers for believers. The conversation stalls before it starts. The achievement of the minimal facts approach, developed over four decades by the philosopher and historian Gary Habermas and pressed into its fullest scholarly form by his student Michael Licona, is to refuse that stalemate. Instead of asking the sceptic to grant the reliability of the New Testament, it asks only for a short list of facts that the sceptic almost certainly already grants — and then argues that the resurrection is the best explanation of those.1

This essay is the overview of the resurrection case as a whole. It does not re-argue each fact in depth — the empty tomb, the early creed, Paul's conversion, the appearances, the transformation of the disciples, and the conversion of James each have their own dedicated treatment in this library. The aim here is to understand the method: why minimal facts, what the two criteria are, how the inference to the best explanation works across the whole set, and — crucially — where serious critics say the method strains. The strength of the argument is also the source of its sharpest objections, and both deserve a careful hearing.

The Two Criteria

A "minimal fact," in Habermas's sense, is not simply a fact he finds persuasive. It is a fact that satisfies two deliberately demanding tests. First, it must be strongly evidenced — typically by multiple independent lines of testimony. Second, and this is the distinctive move, it must be accepted by the great majority of scholars who study the subject, including the sceptical ones — atheist, agnostic, Jewish, and liberal scholars, not only evangelicals.2 The second criterion is doing the rhetorical work. It means that whatever survives the filter is common ground; the believer and the unbeliever can argue about its explanation without first having to settle the inspiration or even the general reliability of Scripture.

Habermas's claim to know what the consensus is rests on an unusual project. Since the mid-1970s he has compiled a running bibliography of academic writing on the resurrection in English, French, and German, tracking how scholars come down on roughly 140 sub-questions. That bibliography now contains on the order of several thousand sources.3 From it he distils the facts that command near-universal assent. The list is usually given as four to six items; in its standard form it runs: Jesus died by crucifixion; his disciples sincerely believed he rose and appeared to them; the persecutor Paul was converted by what he took to be an appearance of the risen Jesus; the sceptic James, Jesus' own brother, was likewise converted; and — added with an explicit flag that it is more contested — the tomb was found empty.4

Why crucifixion is the bedrock

The first fact illustrates the method's discipline. That Jesus died by Roman crucifixion is attested by all four Gospels, by Paul, and — outside the Christian sources entirely — by the Jewish historian Josephus and the Roman historian Tacitus, who called the new movement a "pernicious superstition" and had no motive to flatter it.5 This is why even John Dominic Crossan, co-founder of the Jesus Seminar and one of the most sceptical figures in the field, can write that Jesus' execution "is as sure as anything historical can ever be."6 Habermas does not cite Crossan because Crossan is friendly; he cites him because Crossan is hostile, and the concession therefore counts. That is the engine of the whole approach — letting the opposition supply the premises.

From Facts to Resurrection: The Inference

The minimal facts argument is not a deduction. It is an inference to the best explanation, the same form of reasoning a historian or a detective uses: among the live hypotheses, which one accounts for the whole body of data with the most explanatory scope and power, the least strain, and the fewest unsupported assumptions? Licona's 2010 monograph, The Resurrection of Jesus: A New Historiographical Approach, makes this explicit, borrowing the criteria for adjudicating hypotheses from the philosophy of history — explanatory scope, explanatory power, plausibility, degree of being ad hoc, and illumination — and applying them as a working historian would to any contested event.7

The argument's force is cumulative. No single fact, taken alone, forces the resurrection. An empty tomb by itself admits of theft or relocation. Visionary experiences by themselves admit of hallucination. What the naturalistic alternatives struggle with is the conjunction. The strongest form of the visionary objection is not the private individual hallucination but the well-documented phenomenon of shared bereavement and religious visions — the kind Bart Ehrman presses, citing collective experiences such as Marian apparitions, where many people sincerely report seeing a figure together. That deserves a direct answer. Two features resist it: first, the appearances include people with no grief and no prior expectation — Paul, a hostile outsider actively persecuting the movement, and James, a sceptic who during Jesus' lifetime thought his brother was out of his mind8 — whereas bereavement visions arise among those already devoted to and longing for the one they have lost; and second, no such apparition tradition has ever produced a community that concluded a particular person had been bodily raised and then proved willing to suffer and die proclaiming it. The stolen-body hypothesis might explain an empty tomb, but it cannot explain why the disciples would suffer, and in documented cases die, for what they would then know to be a fraud, nor why Paul, attacking the movement from the outside, would convert. The legend hypothesis founders on chronology: the resurrection was being preached in Jerusalem within weeks, and the creed Paul "received" and "delivered" in 1 Corinthians 15:3–7 can be dated to within a few years of the crucifixion — far too early for legend to have done its slow work.

The argument, then, is that each rival explanation can cover one or two facts but breaks on the others, while a single hypothesis — that Jesus actually rose — covers all of them at once. Licona's conclusion is stated with care: "Jesus' resurrection from the dead is the best historical explanation of the relevant historical bedrock."9 Note the wording. He claims the best explanation, reached by ordinary historical method, not a mathematical proof.

The Strongest Objections

The minimal facts approach is popular, and popularity has made it a target. The serious objections do not dispute that Jesus was crucified or that the disciples believed they saw him. They attack the method — and an honest survey has to let them land.

Dale Allison: probability is in the eye of the beholder

The most formidable critic here is not a village atheist but Dale Allison, one of the most respected New Testament scholars alive, whose 2005 Resurrecting Jesus and 2021 The Resurrection of Jesus: Apologetics, Polemics, History treat the evidence with more care than most apologists do — and who remains, by his own account, genuinely undecided. Allison grants much of the data. His objection cuts deeper than any single fact: he argues that the inference from the facts to the resurrection cannot be made independently of the worldview the investigator brings to it. "Probability," he writes, "is in the eye of the beholder."10 Whether the resurrection is the best explanation depends in part on one's prior estimate of whether such a thing can happen at all — and that prior is not itself settled by the historical data.

This is the strongest objection in the literature, and it should be conceded as far as it goes. The minimal facts do not, by themselves, compel assent from someone whose background convictions assign a vanishingly small prior to any miracle — the point David Hume pressed in 1748 and which Allison restates with modern rigour.11 The honest reply is not to deny this but to reframe what the argument claims. It does not claim to coerce the committed naturalist. It claims that, if one is open to the possibility that God might raise the dead, the historical evidence then makes the resurrection the most reasonable explanation of an otherwise baffling set of facts. The argument removes the historical excuse for unbelief; it cannot remove the metaphysical one, and it should not pretend to. Allison himself, notably, judges the resurrection appearances and the empty tomb to be better evidenced than most sceptics allow — he simply will not take the last step on historical grounds alone. That is a position to respect, not to caricature.

Robert Cavin, Richard Carrier, and the "consensus" problem

A second objection targets the second criterion directly. When Habermas says a fact is "accepted by the majority of scholars," critics such as the philosopher Robert Greg Cavin and the historian Richard Carrier ask: which scholars, counted how?12 Habermas's bibliography is not a randomised survey; it is a compilation of those who have chosen to publish on the resurrection — a population that may over-represent the religiously committed. A widely circulated statistical critique by members of the Global Center for Religious Research argued that the underlying dataset is opaque and that the consensus claims, especially the figure that some three-quarters of scholars accept the empty tomb, cannot be independently verified from what Habermas has published.13

This is a fair methodological warning and the figures should be handled with the corresponding caution. The empty-tomb percentage in particular — drawn from Habermas's 2005 article in the Journal for the Study of the Historical Jesus — is his own tally of the literature he has read, not a poll, and critics are right that it cannot be audited.14 Two things, however, blunt the force of the objection. First, the strongest minimal facts do not depend on a contested headcount at all: that Jesus was crucified and that the disciples sincerely believed they had seen him alive are conceded by named sceptics — Crossan, Lüdemann, Ehrman — on the merits of the evidence, quite apart from any survey. Second, the empty tomb is precisely the fact Habermas flags as more disputed, and a careful presentation of the argument should treat it as the soft point in the chain rather than as a settled premise. The case is strongest when it leans on the facts the critics themselves grant, and weakest when it inflates "majority" into "virtually all."

The "minimal" facts may not be minimal enough

A subtler worry, pressed from within friendly scholarship as well as from outside, is that some items on the list smuggle in more than the consensus actually concedes. "The disciples believed they saw the risen Jesus" is nearly universally granted; "they saw group appearances," as the creed reports, is granted by fewer; and "the tomb was empty" by fewer still. The honest version of the argument keeps these tiers distinct, building first on the genuinely common ground and only then arguing — separately and openly — for the more contested items. Where presenters blur the tiers, treating every fact as equally bulletproof, they hand the critic an easy and deserved rebuttal. The method's integrity depends on its restraint.

The State of the Scholarship

Where does the live debate actually stand? On the foundational data, there is genuine and broad agreement: across the spectrum, scholars accept that Jesus was crucified and that his followers had experiences they sincerely interpreted as encounters with the risen Christ. That much is not seriously contested. What is contested is everything downstream — the cause of those experiences, the historicity of the empty tomb, and above all whether history can license the supernatural conclusion at all.

The naturalistic alternatives remain on the table and are defended by serious people. Gerd Lüdemann argued for visions rooted in guilt and grief; Bart Ehrman appeals to well-documented bereavement visions; Allison holds the question open. None of these scholars thinks the disciples were lying, and the apologist should not either — the experiences were real to those who had them. The dispute is about their source. The minimal facts argument contends that no naturalistic mechanism yet proposed accounts for the whole set — the crucifixion, the early and group experiences, the conversion of a persecutor and a sceptic, and the empty tomb — without straining badly somewhere. Its critics contend that the resurrection hypothesis strains worse, because it requires a miracle. That, in the end, is the real fault line, and it is a philosophical one as much as a historical one.

What the Argument Does and Doesn't Establish

The minimal facts argument is best understood as a carefully bounded instrument. It establishes that a small set of facts about the death and aftermath of Jesus is firmly grounded and broadly conceded, and that the most natural explanation of those facts together — for anyone not antecedently committed to ruling miracles out — is that Jesus rose from the dead. It does this without asking the sceptic to grant biblical inspiration, biblical inerrancy, or even the general reliability of the Gospels. That is a genuine and unusual achievement, and it is why the approach has reshaped how the resurrection is argued in the last forty years.

What it does not do is force the conclusion on someone whose worldview assigns no room to the act of God. It does not prove the resurrection in the way a theorem is proved; it makes it the best explanation, which is the most any historical argument about any event can claim. And it depends, at its contested edges — the empty tomb, the precise scope of the consensus — on premises that the honest advocate must label as more disputed than the rest. Habermas and Licona, at their best, say exactly this. The argument's power is that it begins where even the critic stands; its limit is that the last step, from "best historical explanation" to "and therefore God acted," is one the evidence invites but cannot compel. As the apostle whose own conversion is one of the minimal facts would put it, the case is to be made "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15) — confident in how far the evidence reaches, and candid about where argument ends and trust begins.

Frequently asked questions

Did the disciples just hallucinate that they saw Jesus alive?

The strongest version appeals to shared bereavement visions, which deserves an answer. But two features resist it. Some who reported appearances had no grief or expectation, like Paul, a hostile persecutor, and James, a former skeptic. And no apparition tradition has ever produced a community convinced a person was bodily raised, then willing to suffer and die proclaiming it.

Weren't the Gospels written decades later, so isn't the resurrection just a legend?

The legend theory founders on chronology. The resurrection was being preached in Jerusalem within weeks, and the creed Paul says he received and delivered in 1 Corinthians 15:3-7 can be dated to within a few years of the crucifixion, far too early for legend to do its slow work. The case also starts from facts even skeptics like Crossan concede on the evidence.

Do most scholars really accept the empty tomb?

Handle that claim with caution. The empty-tomb figure is Habermas's own tally of the literature he has read, not an audited poll, and critics rightly note it can't be independently verified. This essay flags the empty tomb as the more disputed, softer point in the chain. The case is strongest leaning on facts critics themselves grant, like the crucifixion and the disciples' sincere experiences.
