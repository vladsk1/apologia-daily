Daniel's Seventy Weeks: A Prophecy on the Edge of Proof

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

The Most Dangerous Verse to Calculate

Sir Isaac Newton, who spent more ink on prophecy than on optics, regarded the book of Daniel as the one by which the other prophecies are to be tried — the key to the rest. Three centuries later a different kind of investigator, Sir Robert Anderson, head of the Criminal Investigation Department at Scotland Yard, sat down with an almanac and a pencil and tried to convict history of fulfilling it. He counted days. The number he arrived at — 173,880 of them — has been recited from a thousand pulpits as if it were a fingerprint left at the scene.1

It is one of the most thrilling claims in all of apologetics, and one of the most contested. Handled carelessly, it becomes a parlor trick: a chain of conveniently chosen dates that "proves" Jesus to the decimal. Handled honestly, Daniel 9 is something stranger and, in the end, more interesting — a text that even its critics date no later than the second century BC, and which genuinely seems to expect an Anointed One within the Second Temple era, and to expect him to be killed. The task of this essay is to walk the line between overclaiming and dismissing, and to leave you knowing exactly where the evidence is strong and exactly where it is not.

The Prophecy, Stated Carefully

The angel Gabriel comes to Daniel as he prays over Jeremiah's seventy years of exile, and answers with a far larger seventy:

"Seventy weeks are decreed about your people and your holy city, to finish the transgression, to put an end to sin, and to atone for iniquity, to bring in everlasting righteousness, to seal both vision and prophet, and to anoint a most holy place. Know therefore and understand that from the going out of the word to restore and build Jerusalem to the coming of an anointed one, a prince, there shall be seven weeks. Then for sixty-two weeks it shall be built again… And after the sixty-two weeks, an anointed one shall be cut off and shall have nothing. And the people of the prince who is to come shall destroy the city and the sanctuary…" (Daniel 9:24–26, ESV)

The Hebrew word translated "weeks" is shabuʿim — literally "sevens." Almost everyone, Jewish and Christian, conservative and critical, reads these as sevens of years: seventy times seven equals 490 years.2 The structure is 7 + 62 + 1 weeks (49 + 434 + 7 years). Everything in the debate turns on four questions hidden inside that sentence.

Four Questions That Decide Everything

1. When does the clock start?

"From the going out of the word to restore and build Jerusalem." Which word? Scripture records more than one Persian decree, and the candidates land decades apart:

  • Cyrus's decree, c. 538 BC (Ezra 1) — but it authorizes the temple, not the city.
  • Artaxerxes I's decree to Ezra, his seventh year, 458/457 BC (Ezra 7).
  • Artaxerxes I's authorization to Nehemiah, his twentieth year, 445/444 BC (Nehemiah 2) — the one that explicitly rebuilds the walls and gates.

Artaxerxes I's regnal years are reasonably fixed by Persian and Babylonian records: his accession was 465/464 BC, making his seventh year 458/457 and his twentieth year 445/444.3

2. Who is the Anointed One?

"An anointed one" — mashiach, the word that becomes "Messiah." Hebrew has no capital M; the term simply means "anointed," and in the Old Testament it can name a king, a priest, or even the pagan Cyrus (Isaiah 45:1). So Daniel 9 does not say "the Messiah" in the later technical sense. It says an anointed one comes, and then an anointed one is "cut off" — a verb (karat) used elsewhere for violent death and judicial execution. The whole interpretive war is about who these figures are.

3. Are the years 360 days long?

Here is where the famous calculation lives. Anderson and, later, the Dallas Theological Seminary scholar Harold Hoehner argued that biblical "prophetic years" run 360 days, inferring this from Revelation, where 42 months, 1,260 days, and "time, times, and half a time" (3½ years) are equated — implying a 30-day month and a 360-day year.4

4. What happens in the seventieth week?

Verse 26 places the "cutting off" and the city's destruction after the 69 weeks. Verse 27 describes a figure who "confirms a covenant with many for one week" and in its middle stops sacrifice and brings "abomination" and "desolation." Whether the seventieth week follows immediately or is separated by a long gap is the great fault line of Christian eschatology — and one this essay will deliberately not adjudicate.

The Apologetic Argument: A Date with the Triumphal Entry

Assemble the conservative answers and the argument becomes vivid. Start at Artaxerxes' decree to Nehemiah; treat the 69 weeks (7 + 62 = 483 years) as the span "to the coming of an anointed one"; use 360-day years; and you land on Jesus.

Anderson did the arithmetic first. From March 14, 445 BC to April 6, AD 32 — the date he assigned to the Triumphal Entry — he counted 173,880 days, which is exactly 483 × 360. The Messiah, he argued, was presented to Jerusalem as king on the very day the 69 weeks expired, and "cut off" days later.5

Hoehner refined it, correcting Anderson's astronomy and shifting to a 444 BC start and an AD 33 crucifixion. From March 5, 444 BC he counted the same 173,880 days to March 30, AD 33 — on his reckoning the day of the Triumphal Entry, with the crucifixion that same week.6 The structural reading underneath the math is shared far beyond dispensationalism: the venerable view, held by Calvin, E. W. Hengstenberg, and Edward J. Young alike, is simply that the 69 weeks run from a Persian decree to the public ministry and death of Christ in the first century AD — no 360-day gymnastics required, the rough order of magnitude being the point.

The qualitative force is real. Daniel 9 expects an anointed figure, ties his coming to a Persian rebuilding decree, places his violent death before the destruction of the city and temple, and dates the whole to within the Second Temple period. Jerusalem and its temple were destroyed, in AD 70, by "the people of the prince" — the Romans. For a text composed no later than the second century BC, that is a striking convergence even before anyone counts a single day.

The Critical Reading, Steelmanned

Now the hard part. The majority of academic Old Testament scholars do not read Daniel 9 as pointing to Jesus at all, and their case deserves to be stated at full strength, not waved away.

The foundation is the date of the book. The critical consensus — represented authoritatively by John J. Collins's Daniel in the Hermeneia series, the standard scholarly commentary — holds that Daniel reached its final form around 165 BC, during the persecution of the Jews by the Seleucid king Antiochus IV Epiphanes.7 The evidence is the book's own focus: Daniel 11 narrates the wars of the Hellenistic kingdoms with such accuracy that critics read it as history written after the fact (vaticinium ex eventu), and notably stumbles only when it reaches Antiochus's death — exactly where a second-century author writing in real time would lose the thread.

On this reading the seventy weeks are not a stopwatch but a theological structure: ten jubilees of years (490 = 70 × 7, and 49 × 10), a symbolic span answering Jeremiah's seventy years. And the figures resolve to the Maccabean crisis:

  • The "anointed one" who comes after the first seven weeks (Daniel 9:25) is the high priest Joshua or the anointed leadership of the restoration.
  • The "anointed one cut off" of verse 26 is Onias III, the legitimate Zadokite high priest deposed and then murdered around 171 BC.
  • The "prince who is to come" and the desolator of verse 27 is Antiochus IV, who halted the temple sacrifices and erected the "abomination of desolation" — a pagan altar — from 167 to 164 BC, the "half a week" of suspended worship.8

Two pieces of evidence inside the text cut hard for this view. First is the Hebrew punctuation. The Masoretes placed a major dividing accent (the atnach) after "seven weeks" in verse 25, which reads as: "from the word… to an anointed one, a prince, seven weeks; and for sixty-two weeks it shall be built." On that reading the anointed one arrives after only 49 years — the era of the return, not the time of Jesus — and the 7 and 62 are two separate periods. Modern critical translations such as the NRSV follow the Masoretic accent; the King James and NIV traditions join the 7 and 62 into 69 to reach the first century, following the earlier Greek versions (Septuagint, Theodotion) and the Vulgate, which did not separate them.9 This is a genuinely contested philological point, and the messianic reading depends on the disputed side of it.

Second is the chronology problem — and here the critic, Collins, gets the floor. Whatever decree you choose, ordinary years do not yield 49 years (the first seven weeks) to any plausible "anointed one," and 49 + 434 years of ordinary reckoning overshoots the Maccabean period by roughly a century. The author's numbers, Collins argues, are simply inexact — schematic figures that never balanced against a real calendar, which is exactly what we should expect from apocalyptic literature trading in symbolic completeness rather than chronometry. The 70 weeks, on this view, are theology in the shape of a number.

Where the Apologist Must Concede

Three concessions are owed, and intellectual honesty requires making them plainly.

First, the 360-day "prophetic year" is contested even among conservatives, and to outside critics it looks like special pleading. There is no statement in Daniel that its years are 360 days; the inference is imported from Revelation, and the suspicion is hard to shake that the 360-day figure was selected precisely because 365-day years do not produce a clean landing on Jesus. Critics have also noted technical slips in the Anderson–Hoehner calculations — for instance, that the chosen start and end points do not fall on the same day of the week, which they must if a whole number of seven-year units has elapsed, and that Hoehner's Hebrew-calendar conversion of the crucifixion date has been disputed.10 When an argument's persuasive force depends on hitting an exact day, being off by a day or two is not a rounding error; it is the difference between a fingerprint and a smudge.

Second, the "decree" question is not cleanly settled. The text's "word to restore and build Jerusalem" most naturally evokes the rebuilding of the city, which favors Nehemiah's 444 commission — but Daniel 9:25 may equally refer to the divine word through Jeremiah, not any Persian edict at all, which is the critical default.

Third, and most fundamentally: the messianic reading and the Maccabean reading are not symmetrical guesses. The Maccabean reading fits the book's own preoccupations and requires no special arithmetic; the messianic reading requires a particular decree, a particular punctuation, and (for the day-precise version) a particular year-length. That is a lot of dials to set before the picture resolves.

The Reply

And yet the critical case is not airtight, and the apologist has real ground to stand on — provided he claims the right amount.

On dating: the Maccabean thesis is the majority view but not a proof. Fragments of Daniel were found at Qumran, and the book was treated as authoritative scripture there soon after its supposed 165 BC composition; conservatives argue this presses an uncomfortably short window for a freshly written work to gain such status, though critics judge the gap sufficient (the earliest fragments are dated paleographically to around 125 BC). Conservative scholars press the dating on other grounds—the short Qumran-reception window and disputed linguistic arguments—while granting that the detailed accuracy of Daniel 11 down to Antiochus IV is the critic’s strongest evidence, on any view of whether predictive prophecy is possible.

On the chronology: that the numbers are "inexact" cuts both ways. If the author of a Maccabean Daniel was writing about events in his own lifetime, his inability to make 490 years reach cleanly from any starting point to 164 BC is itself a little awkward — he, of all people, knew when Antiochus desecrated the temple. The looseness Collins identifies is real, but it equally undermines any tidy this-equals-that Maccabean fulfillment.

And on the larger meaning, there is a suggestive point from ancient Jewish interpretation — though it must be stated carefully, lest it be recruited past what it supports. The rabbinic chronicle Seder Olam Rabbah (compiled c. AD 160 and edited later) measured 490 years from the first temple's fall to the second's, mapping Daniel's seventy weeks onto the lifespan of the Second Temple.11 The rabbis read those weeks non-messianically, and on a famously compressed Persian chronology; this is no Jewish endorsement of the Christian reading. The narrow point it does establish is only this: Jewish tradition itself tied Daniel 9's clock to the end of the Second Temple era. Josephus, an eyewitness to AD 70, likewise treats Daniel as a prophet whose visions were most naturally read as pointing to the Roman desolation of the sanctuary.12 Neither source reads Daniel 9 as Christians do — but both locate its horizon at the end of the Second Temple era, which is precisely the era of Jesus. The framework that an Anointed One would come, and be cut off, around that time was in the air, in Jewish soil, in the first century.

What It Establishes — and What It Doesn't

Strip away the overclaiming and a sober verdict remains.

Daniel 9 does not deliver a mathematical demonstration of Jesus' messiahship. The day-precise calculations of Anderson and Hoehner are too dependent on contestable choices — the decree, the punctuation, the 360-day year, the exact crucifixion date — to function as proof, and presenting them as a knockdown "countdown to Christ" invites the charge of rigging the dials, a charge that sometimes sticks. The honest apologist retires the stopwatch.

But Daniel 9 does establish something quieter and harder to dismiss. A text that even its critics date to no later than the second century BC announces that within the era of the rebuilt Jerusalem an anointed one would come and be "cut off," and that the city and sanctuary would then be destroyed. Whether you read it through Onias III or through Jesus, the shape is fixed in advance: an anointed death, then ruin. And the city and its temple did fall, to "the people of the prince," in AD 70. Even ancient Jewish sources tied the prophecy's clock to that catastrophe. The Christian claim is not that Daniel 9 calculates Jesus to the day, but that Jesus answers its shape with uncanny fit — arriving in the Second Temple era, anointed, and cut off, before the sanctuary's end.

That is a suggestive strand, not a proof. It is the kind of evidence that rewards the person already weighing the whole cloth of the case for Christ, and frustrates the person demanding a single decisive thread. Newton was wrong to make it the stone all other prophecies are tried by; it cannot bear that weight. But he was not wrong to be fascinated. Daniel 9 remains one of the places where the Old Testament seems to lean forward, into a century it should not have been able to see, and to whisper — not shout — a name.
