Is Jesus God in the New Testament? The Whole Witness, Surveyed

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Short on time? Read the quick answer →

In a locked room a week after the crucifixion, a hard-headed fisherman who had refused to believe his friends' reports put his hand toward the wounds of a man he had watched die. He did not say, "So it is really you." He said something no devout Jew said to anyone but the LORD of Israel: "My Lord and my God."1 John, who built his entire Gospel around the conviction that there is one God, lets the line stand without correction. It is the climax of the book.

That confession is one thread. The question this essay asks is what happens when you pull on all of them at once. Pick up any individual verse and a determined reader can propose another reading. But the New Testament is not a single verse; it is a library of some twenty-seven documents, written by perhaps nine or ten authors across roughly half a century, in two languages, to scattered communities who never coordinated their theology. The striking thing is what they converge on. This is the wide-angle essay — the survey that ties the threads together. The close work on individual texts lives in the companion pieces, and I will point you to them as we go rather than repeat their detail.

The thesis, stated carefully

Here is the claim, and its limits. The New Testament writers do not hand us the later vocabulary of "one essence in three persons" or "two natures in one person." Those are fourth- and fifth-century formulations — the church's attempt to systematize, against rival readings, what it found already in these texts. What the New Testament does, repeatedly and across independent authors, is something the British scholar Richard Bauckham has called including Jesus within the unique identity of the one God of Israel.2 Jesus is placed on the Creator's side of the line that Jewish monotheism drew between God and everything else: he shares God's throne, God's acts of creation, God's eternal name, and — most tellingly — God's worship.

So the thesis is not "the New Testament teaches the Nicene Creed in so many words." It is the more modest and more defensible claim that the raw data the creeds later organized is already here, distributed across the whole canon, and that the developed doctrine is the faithful articulation of it rather than a later imposition upon it.

The spread of the evidence

Begin with John, where the case is most explicit. The Gospel opens by reaching behind Genesis: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."3 The grammar of that last clause is genuinely contested — Jehovah's Witnesses render it "a god," and the focused treatment of why the standard translation holds lives in the companion essay on John 1:1. The same Gospel has Jesus take the divine self-designation of Exodus and Isaiah onto his own lips — "before Abraham was, I am"4 — and closes, as we saw, with Thomas. Whatever one makes of any single line, John's frame is unmistakable: the Word who was God became flesh and was confessed as God.

Paul, writing decades earlier, is often said to be quieter on this. He is not. In Colossians he calls Christ "the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of all creation," the one "by him all things were created," and then says flatly that "in him the whole fullness of deity dwells bodily."5 In Philippians he quotes what most scholars regard as a pre-Pauline hymn — material older than the letter itself — describing one who was "in the form of God" and did not count "equality with God" a thing to be grasped, and who is finally given "the name that is above every name," at which every knee shall bow.6 That last phrase is lifted from Isaiah 45, where the LORD swears that to him alone every knee shall bow — and Paul applies it to Jesus. The hymn has its own deep-dive: see the Philippians 2 hymn. Two further Pauline texts — Romans 9:5 and Titus 2:13 — appear to call Jesus "God" directly, though both turn on Greek grammar and punctuation, and I will concede below that they are disputed.

Hebrews is, if anything, bolder. Its first chapter strings together Old Testament passages and aims them at the Son. God says to the Son, "Your throne, O God, is forever and ever."7 Then the author quotes Psalm 102 — a psalm addressed to the LORD, the unchanging Creator — and applies it to the Son: "You, Lord, laid the foundation of the earth in the beginning, and the heavens are the work of your hands... but you are the same, and your years will have no end."8 A text about Yahweh's eternity and creative work is, without apology, read as words to Christ.

Revelation completes the circle. The risen Christ tells John, "Fear not, I am the first and the last,"9 and at the book's end, "I am the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end."10 "The first and the last" is precisely how the LORD identifies himself in Isaiah — "I am the first and I am the last; besides me there is no god."11 The title God reserves for himself, the exalted Jesus claims.

The evidence that isn't a title

Names and texts can be argued. Harder to argue away is what the earliest Christians did. Here the work of Larry Hurtado is central. In his major study Lord Jesus Christ, Hurtado documents a pattern of devotional practice — what he calls a "binitarian" shape of worship — that emerged "amazingly early," within the first years of the movement, among Jewish believers who would have regarded worshiping any creature as blasphemy.12 They sang to Christ, baptized into his name, called on his name, broke bread in his presence, and prayed to him. And they did this while insisting, with the Shema, that God is one.

Consider three strands. First, Jesus receives worship — the prostration and devotion due God alone — and accepts it, where in the same literature angels twice refuse it (Revelation 19:10; 22:9). Second, Jesus forgives sins, prompting the objection, "Who can forgive sins but God alone?"13 — an objection the narrative does not deny but answers by demonstration. Third, and perhaps most telling, the earliest Christians prayed to Jesus. Paul preserves an Aramaic prayer untranslated into his Greek-speaking churches: Marana tha, "Our Lord, come!"14 That the invocation survives in Aramaic is the point. It carries us back behind Paul's Greek to the Aramaic-speaking Judean church — to the very first community, calling on the risen Jesus as the Lord who will come. The highest devotion is not a late Hellenistic overlay. It is bedrock.

For the framework that organizes all this — divine titles, divine attributes, divine throne, divine works, divine worship, and the divine name — see the companion essays on the HANDS framework and the Titles of Jesus.

The strongest objections, fairly stated

Two communities deserve to be heard at their best. Jehovah's Witnesses and biblical Unitarians read this same evidence within an "agency" framework deeply rooted in the Hebrew Bible. There, an emissary can bear the sender's name and authority — the angel of the LORD speaks as God, Moses is "made God to Pharaoh" (Exodus 7:1) — without being the LORD. On this reading, Jesus is God's supreme agent, given the divine name and even a kind of worship as the one who perfectly represents God, while remaining a distinct and subordinate being. John 1:1c, they argue, should read "the Word was a god" or "was divine"; the worship Jesus receives is the relative homage due an exalted vice-regent; and the loftiest texts describe a created firstborn raised to glory, not the Creator. The agency motif it appeals to is genuinely present in the Hebrew Bible; the question is whether it can bear the weight this reading puts on it.

On the disputed proof-texts, candor is required. Titus 2:13 ("our great God and Savior Jesus Christ") and 2 Peter 1:1 depend on the Granville Sharp rule — the grammatical principle that a single article governing two singular personal nouns joined by kai points to one person. Daniel Wallace's detailed study argues the rule holds without exception in native Koine when its conditions are properly met, which would make these verses call Jesus God directly.15 But the rule's application is debated, and a reader is right to note that translators have not always agreed. Romans 9:5 is frankly a matter of where one places a comma the original lacked: it can be read as calling Christ "God over all, blessed forever," or as breaking into a separate doxology to the Father. The case for Christ's deity does not stand or fall on these verses, and I will not pretend the grammar is uncontested. It is contested.

The most formidable academic challenge is Bart Ehrman's developmental thesis in How Jesus Became God. Ehrman argues that the historical Jesus did not claim divinity, that his earliest followers held an "exaltation" Christology — God raised the man Jesus and adopted him to divine status — and that a full "incarnation" Christology developed only later, reaching its peak in John, the latest Gospel.16 On this view, deity is the endpoint of a decades-long evolution, not the starting point. Ehrman writes for a wide audience and the trajectory he sketches is, on its face, plausible.

The reply — and the concession

The reply, drawn principally from Hurtado and Bauckham, is that the timeline does not cooperate with a slow evolution. The most explosive evidence is also the earliest. Marana tha is Aramaic, pre-Pauline, rooted in the Jerusalem church of the 30s. The Philippians hymn is widely regarded as pre-Pauline, and the Colossians "fullness of deity" passage is what many scholars regard as inherited material — meaning the conviction predates the letters that contain it. (The pre-Pauline status of the Colossians hymn, Colossians 1:15–20, is more contested than that of Philippians 2, and the authorship of Colossians is itself disputed.) The pattern of worship Hurtado documents is in place within a decade or two of the crucifixion, not a century after. Bauckham's contribution sharpens the philosophical point: the early Christians did not slowly climb a ladder from low to high; they expressed the highest possible Christology — inclusion in the divine identity — using the very resources of Jewish monotheism, and they did so from the start.17 Bauckham's "divine identity" framework has been enormously influential, but it is itself contested: scholars such as James Dunn and (from a different angle) critics like James F. McGrath have questioned how sharply the boundary of divine identity can be drawn, and even Larry Hurtado, broadly an ally, preferred to frame the data in terms of devotional practice rather than Bauckham's identity categories. John is not the invention of a new doctrine but the most explicit articulation of an old one. The development is in the clarity, not in the substance.

Yet Ehrman is owed a real concession, and it is the same concession the thesis required at the outset. The New Testament gives us the data, the worship, the texts, the tensions; it does not give us the conciliar grammar that resolved them. Christians who claim otherwise overreach. What the development thesis gets wrong is not that doctrine developed — of course it did — but the assumption that the deity of Christ was a late arrival rather than the thing the developing doctrine was struggling to state.

What this does, and does not, establish

Honesty about scope is itself part of the case. This essay establishes one strand of a cumulative argument: that the New Testament, read across its full breadth, presents Jesus as sharing the identity of the one God of Israel and as the rightful recipient of God's worship — and that this presentation is early, widespread, and woven into the church's earliest practice, not bolted on at the end. The convergence of independent authors on the same astonishing inclusion is hard to explain as coincidence or gradual drift.

It does not, by itself, establish the developed creed. It does not settle the disputed grammar of Romans 9:5 or prove the Trinity from a proof-text. And it does not stand alone: the textual case here interlocks with the historical case for the resurrection, the witness of the earliest post-apostolic church, and the philosophical coherence of trinitarian monotheism — each treated elsewhere in this library. What it offers is the foundation those other lines build on. When Thomas reached toward the wounds and said "My Lord and my God," he was not inventing a category. He was naming, in four words, what the whole New Testament had been saying all along.

Frequently asked questions

Is Jesus actually called God in the New Testament, or is that a later invention?

The claim is not that the New Testament states the Nicene Creed word for word; that vocabulary is later. It is that independent authors, writing across half a century in two languages, repeatedly include Jesus within the unique identity of the one God of Israel. He shares God's throne, creation, eternal name, and worship. Thomas's My Lord and my God, John's Word who was God, Hebrews' Your throne O God, and Revelation's first and last all converge on the same astonishing inclusion.

Couldn't Jesus just be God's supreme agent, given the divine name without being God?

That agency reading, pressed by Jehovah's Witnesses and biblical Unitarians, is not frivolous; in the Hebrew Bible an emissary can bear the sender's name and authority. But the hardest evidence is not titles but practice. The earliest Christians worshiped Jesus, sang to him, baptized into his name, and prayed to him, where angels in the same literature twice refuse worship. The Aramaic prayer Marana tha, our Lord come, carries this devotion back to the first Judean church, not a later drift.

Didn't the belief that Jesus was God evolve slowly, as Bart Ehrman argues?

Ehrman's developmental thesis is the most formidable challenge, and it is owed a real concession: the systematic doctrine, the Trinity and Chalcedon, is unquestionably later. But the timeline resists slow evolution because the earliest evidence is the most explosive. Marana tha is pre-Pauline, rooted in the Jerusalem church of the 30s; the Philippians hymn is widely regarded as pre-Pauline. The high devotion is bedrock, not a late accretion. The development is in the clarity, not in the substance.
