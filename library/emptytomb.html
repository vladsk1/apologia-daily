The Empty Tomb: How Strong Is the Case That the Grave Was Found Empty?

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

A claim that could be checked

Of all the events the first Christians proclaimed, the empty tomb is the one that, in principle, anyone in Jerusalem could have falsified by a short walk. The appearances were experiences; you either had one or you did not. But a body either lies in a grave or it does not. When the apostles began preaching in the very city where Jesus had been executed weeks earlier — insisting that God had raised him — they were making a claim with a physical address attached. If a tomb known to the authorities still held a corpse, the simplest refutation in the history of religion was available: open it.

This essay is about that grave alone. It is not about whether the disciples saw the risen Jesus, nor about Paul's testimony, nor about the wider "minimal facts" method — those arguments are treated elsewhere in this library. Here the single question is narrower and prior: did the tomb of Jesus, on the third day, in fact stand empty? That the disciples believed it did is not in dispute. Whether the belief was rooted in a real, discoverable fact — and how a careful historian should weigh the evidence — is the matter at hand. The honest answer is that the empty tomb is well attested and accepted by a substantial body of scholars, that it is more contested than the appearances, and that the strongest sceptical alternative deserves to be stated in full before it is answered.

The fact and its supports

The empty tomb is not a free-floating assertion. It is lodged inside a cluster of details — the burial, the man who performed it, the location, the witnesses — each of which can be examined on its own. Five lines of evidence are usually advanced. None is decisive alone; their force is cumulative.

1. The burial by Joseph of Arimathea

The empty-tomb tradition is anchored to a named, identifiable burial. According to all four Gospels, Jesus was interred not in a common grave but in the rock tomb of Joseph of Arimathea, a member of the Sanhedrin — the very council that had handed Jesus over.1 This detail carries weight precisely because it is awkward. Early Christian writing is, on the whole, sharply hostile to the Jewish leadership that secured Jesus' death; for a pious member of that body to step forward and do right by Jesus cuts against the grain. The Catholic scholar Raymond Brown, who was no apologist for the miraculous, judged the burial by Joseph "very probable," reasoning that a Christian fabrication of a Sanhedrist who honours Jesus is "almost inexplicable" given that hostility.2 A named tomb in a named owner's possession also means the claim was, in the language of historians, falsifiable: there was a specific place to point to.

2. Enemy attestation: the counter-story

One of the strongest strands is that Jesus' opponents did not deny the tomb was empty — they offered a rival explanation for it. Matthew reports that the chief priests bribed the guard to say, "His disciples came by night and stole him away while we were asleep," and adds that "this story has been spread among the Jews to this day."3 Whatever one makes of the guard narrative itself (which is unique to Matthew and which critics reasonably question), the telling datum is the polemic: the earliest recoverable Jewish response to the resurrection preaching was not "the body is still there" but "the body was taken." To bother explaining why a tomb is empty is most naturally read as conceding that it was empty. It should be granted, in fairness, that this counter-story is securely attested only from Matthew's period onward, so the datum is strong but not contemporaneous with the events themselves.

This counter-story has an unusually long paper trail. Around 150, Justin Martyr records that Jewish authorities were sending emissaries to say the disciples "stole him by night from the tomb"; around 200, Tertullian in North Africa knows the same charge.4 A polemic that the opponents kept alive for over a century is hard to reconcile with the idea that there was simply no empty tomb to argue about.

3. The women witnesses and the criterion of embarrassment

In every Gospel the discovery of the empty tomb is credited first to women — in the lead, Mary Magdalene.5 In first-century Judaea this was not a flattering choice. Josephus, codifying the conventional attitude, writes: "Let not the testimony of women be admitted, on account of the levity and boldness of their sex."6 A movement free to compose its founding story for maximum credibility would naturally have put male disciples at the tomb. That the tradition instead makes women the first and primary witnesses — and shows the male apostles disbelieving their report as "an idle tale" (Luke 24:11) — is the kind of counter-productive detail the criterion of embarrassment marks as probably historical. The New Testament historian Richard Bauckham argues that the women are named so consistently across the sources precisely because they were known, public eyewitnesses whose testimony the tradition could not quietly drop.7

4. The Jerusalem factor

The resurrection was first proclaimed in Jerusalem, not in distant Galilee a generation later. This matters because the tomb was local and accessible, and the authorities had every motive and every means to end the movement by producing the corpse. As the philosopher William Lane Craig has pressed the point, a resurrection proclamation in the same city, within weeks, would have been impossible to sustain if the body lay where everyone could find it.8 The argument is not airtight — a body weeks dead might be hard to identify, and we cannot prove the authorities tried and failed — but the silence about any such refutation, in a context where one was so obviously wanted, is suggestive.

5. Multiple attestation and Mark's early source

The empty tomb is reported in Mark and, independently, in the special material of Matthew, Luke, and John, and the burial is presupposed in the pre-Pauline creed of 1 Corinthians 15 ("he was buried, … he was raised").9 Many scholars judge that Mark drew his passion-and-burial account from an earlier connected source, pushing the tradition back well before the Gospel's composition.10 Multiple independent lines reporting the same outcome — an empty grave — raise its probability above what any single witness could establish.

What the evidence does and does not show

It is essential to state the limit plainly. None of this proves a resurrection. An empty tomb, by itself, is consistent with theft, relocation, confusion, or causes we cannot now recover; the leap from "the grave was empty" to "God raised him" depends on the appearances and on the failure of natural alternatives, argued separately. The empty tomb is a necessary feature of the bodily-resurrection claim and a serious historical datum in its own right — not, on its own, a demonstration of the miracle. Conversely, even firm establishment of the tomb leaves untouched the question that the rest of the case must answer: why was it empty?

The sceptical case, in its strongest form

John Dominic Crossan: there was no honourable tomb at all

The most formidable challenge does not quibble about who first reached the tomb; it denies that there was a known tomb to reach. John Dominic Crossan, the most prominent voice of the Jesus Seminar on this question, argues that the burial narratives are theological fiction. Rome crucified to terrify, and routinely left the dead on the cross or in shallow graves as carrion for scavenging dogs and birds; a condemned provincial like Jesus, Crossan contends, would most likely have met the same fate, his remains never recovered.11 On this reading Mark "created that burial by Joseph of Arimathea," and the empty-tomb story is "neither an early historical event nor a late legendary narrative but a deliberate Markan creation."12 If Crossan is right, the entire structure above collapses: there is no tomb to be found empty, and the enemy polemic is simply a Christian literary device.

This is a serious argument from a serious scholar, and it must be conceded what is true in it. Roman crucifixion was indeed a horror designed to deny the victim honourable burial, and non-burial was common. Bart Ehrman, on independent grounds, has likewise argued that the burial tradition is doubtful and that Romans typically left crucifixion victims unburied — so the sceptical case here is not Crossan's alone.13

Yet the case has real countervailing weight — and because it turns on the burial rather than on the tomb itself, it is answered in full in the companion essay on the burial of Jesus. In brief: Jewish law required burial of the executed before nightfall, and Josephus says the Jews took down and buried even the crucified before sunset;14 the crucified man Yehohanan is direct material proof that some victims in first-century Jerusalem were given proper burial;15 and the very embarrassment of naming Joseph of Arimathea and the women tells against pure invention. Crossan's thesis is coherent and cannot be dismissed, but it requires treating as fiction precisely the details that bear the marks of awkward memory. The dispute over the burial is genuinely live, and it is the hinge on which the strongest version of the sceptical case turns.

The stolen-body and related theories

If one grants the tomb but seeks a natural emptying, the classic candidate is theft — the very charge the ancient polemic preferred. The difficulty is motive and consequence: the disciples who would have to be the thieves then went on to suffer and, in several cases, die for proclaiming a resurrection they would have known to be staged. Sincere people die for false beliefs all the time; what is psychologically strained is a coordinated group dying for what they personally know to be a fraud. (This essay does not rest the point on the disciples' deaths, treated elsewhere; it notes only that simple theft must carry that burden.) Variants — the women erred to the wrong tomb, the authorities quietly relocated the body — each founder on the same rock: had the body existed anywhere recoverable, producing it would have been the obvious and devastating reply, and no source records anyone doing so. These are not knock-down refutations; they are reasons the natural theories have not commanded a consensus.

Where the scholarship stands

It is sometimes claimed that "about 75 percent" of scholars accept the empty tomb. That figure comes from Gary Habermas's ongoing bibliographic survey of resurrection literature since 1975, and it should be cited with care: it is a count of published positions assembled by one researcher, not a randomised poll, critics have questioned how the sample was drawn, and Habermas himself has leaned on it less in recent work.16 What can be said responsibly is more modest and still substantial: the empty tomb is affirmed by a wide range of mainstream scholars — among them Brown, Wright, and the agnostic classicist Michael Grant, who concluded that "if we apply the same sort of criteria that we would apply to any other ancient literary sources, the evidence is firm and plausible enough to necessitate the conclusion that the tomb was indeed found empty."17 It is also genuinely disputed, with Crossan, Ehrman, and others on the other side. The empty tomb is better described as a strong, well-defended position than as a settled fact — and noticeably less secure than the near-consensus that the disciples had experiences they took to be appearances.

Conclusion

The case for the empty tomb is cumulative and circumstantial, in the way most ancient history is. A named burial by an awkward benefactor; an enemy polemic that explains the emptiness rather than denying it, traceable across a century; women as the embarrassing first witnesses; a proclamation launched in the one city where the body could most easily have been produced; and several independent strands of tradition converging on the same outcome — together these make the empty tomb more probable than not, and well worth defending. They do not, by themselves, prove the resurrection, and the most serious sceptical reply — that there was no honourable tomb to begin with — remains a live and respectable position that the evidence answers but does not annihilate. The verdict a careful inquirer can reach is therefore confident but bounded: the grave was, in all probability, found empty; what emptied it is the next and larger question. That conclusion is best held the way Peter urged any such case be made — "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15), sure of what the evidence shows and candid about where it stops.

Frequently asked questions

Does the empty tomb prove Jesus rose from the dead?

No, and the essay states this limit plainly. An empty tomb by itself is consistent with theft, relocation, or confusion; the leap to 'God raised him' depends on the appearances and the failure of natural alternatives, argued separately. The empty tomb is a necessary feature of the bodily-resurrection claim and a serious historical datum, but on its own it only raises the next question: why was the grave empty?

Couldn't the disciples have just stolen the body from the tomb?

Theft was the very charge the earliest Jewish polemic preferred, which itself suggests the tomb was empty. The difficulty is motive and consequence: the disciples who would be the thieves then suffered, and in several cases died, proclaiming a resurrection they would have known was staged. Sincere people die for false beliefs, but a coordinated group dying for what they personally know to be fraud is psychologically strained. And no source records anyone producing the body.

What if Jesus was never buried in a tomb at all?

This is the strongest skeptical case, argued by John Dominic Crossan: Rome often left crucifixion victims unburied, so perhaps there was no known tomb to find empty. The essay concedes this is serious and cannot be dismissed. But there is countervailing weight: Jewish law required burial before nightfall, Josephus says the crucified were buried before sunset, the crucified man Yehohanan shows some victims were properly buried, and naming Joseph of Arimathea bears the marks of awkward memory.
