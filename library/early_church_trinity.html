The Early Church and the Trinity

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Sometime around the year 112, a worried Roman governor sat down to write to his emperor for advice. Pliny the Younger had been executing Christians in the province of Bithynia—on the Black Sea coast of what is now Turkey—and he wanted to be sure he was doing it correctly. To gather evidence he had interrogated suspects, two female deacons among them, “under torture.” What he learned about this stubborn sect he reported almost as a curiosity. Their habit, he told Trajan, was to gather before dawn on a fixed day and “to recite a hymn antiphonally to Christ, as to a god.”1

That single line, scribbled by a hostile pagan administrator more than two centuries before the Council of Nicaea, is the quiet undoing of one of the most popular myths about Christianity ever made: that the deity of Christ and the doctrine of the Trinity were invented in the fourth century—voted into existence by bishops at the command of the emperor Constantine. The line gained mass currency through Dan Brown’s 2003 novel The Da Vinci Code, whose characters announce that “until that moment in history, Jesus was viewed by his followers as a mortal prophet… a great and powerful man, but a man nonetheless,” and that his divinity was established at Nicaea by “a relatively close vote.”2 It is repeated in pubs and comment threads to this day. And it is, on the plain historical record, false.

But there is a serious version of the developmental thesis—argued by careful scholars, not novelists—and it deserves to be met at full strength. This essay does both: it sets out what the pre-Nicene church actually believed and prayed, distinguishes the real story of doctrinal development from the myth of invention, and then squares up to the strongest critic in the room.

Stating the Question Carefully

Precision matters here, because two different claims are easily blurred. The first is the popular one: Nicaea invented the belief that Jesus is divine. The second is a scholarly one: the precise conceptual vocabulary of Trinitarian doctrine—one essence in three persons, the Son “of one substance” with the Father—developed over time, and was hammered into final form in the fourth century. The first claim is refuted by the evidence. The second is, in large part, simply true—and no informed Christian need deny it.

The thesis this essay defends is therefore narrower and more honest than triumphalism allows. It is that the early church worshipped and confessed Father, Son, and Holy Spirit from the earliest period we can document; that this devotion long predates Constantine; and that the councils of Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381) did not create that faith but clarified its language under pressure from a genuinely new denial. Development of terminology is not invention of belief. The praying came first; the definitions came to defend the praying.

The Pre-Nicene Church at Worship

The most important evidence is not a creed but a practice. Before there were technical terms, there was a pattern of devotion in which Jesus was reverenced alongside God the Father in ways Jews reserved for God alone. The late Larry Hurtado of Edinburgh—the leading historian of early Christian worship—called this a “binitarian” (later he preferred “dyadic”) shape of devotion, and argued that it emerged not after centuries of drift but within the first two decades of the movement, among monotheistic Jews who would have regarded worshipping a creature as blasphemy.3 This is what Hurtado meant by calling earliest Christianity a “mutation” within Jewish monotheism: Jesus was sung to, prayed to, baptized into, and invoked.

The fingerprints of that worship are embedded in the New Testament itself, often in material that predates the documents quoting it. Paul preserves the Aramaic prayer marana tha—“our Lord, come!” (1 Corinthians 16:22)—a petition addressed to Jesus that must have arisen in the first Aramaic-speaking churches of Judea. The hymn of Philippians 2:6–11, which most scholars regard as pre-Pauline tradition Paul is citing, applies to Jesus the words of Isaiah 45:23, where every knee bows to Yahweh. These are not fourth-century inventions; they are the oldest strata we possess.

The same pattern continues seamlessly into the generation after the apostles. Ignatius, bishop of Antioch, wrote seven letters around 107–110 while being marched under guard to his execution in Rome. He calls Jesus “our God” without apology or argument—in the opening greeting of his letter to the Ephesians he addresses a church “united… by the will of the Father and of Jesus Christ our God.”4 A man writing on his way to be eaten by beasts is not polishing an archive for posterity; he is recording the settled speech of his churches. And then there is Pliny—a Roman official with no stake in Christian theology—reporting that the worship of Christ “as to a god” was the defining habit of the sect. (Some scholars, noting Pliny’s Latin quasi deo, debate the precise force of “as to”; the classicist A. N. Sherwin-White argued the word here simply means “as,” not “as if.”)5

Three further structures of pre-Nicene faith confirm the threefold shape. Baptism was administered “in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit”—the formula of Matthew 28:19, already prescribed as standard practice in the Didache, a church manual most date to the late first or early second century.6 The Rule of Faith—the summary of belief converts confessed before baptism—ran in a threefold, Father-Son-Spirit pattern in Irenaeus and Tertullian well before any council, the seed from which the later creeds grew.7 And doxologies praised the three together: the account of Polycarp’s martyrdom (c. 155–160) closes with glory given to God “through whom, with the Holy Spirit, be glory… with Jesus Christ.”8 The grammar of worship was Trinitarian long before the metaphysics was defined.

The Ante-Nicene Fathers: Real Development, Honestly Told

None of this means the early Fathers spoke with the polished precision of the later creeds. They did not, and pretending otherwise does the case no favors. The pre-Nicene writers were reaching for language adequate to an experience that outran their vocabulary, and their formulations are uneven.

Justin Martyr, writing around 150–160, defends the worship of the Logos as God yet can also describe him as “another God” alongside the Father and as serving the Father’s will—language that sounds, to modern ears, subordinationist.9 Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180), combating Gnosticism, gave the church its enduring image of the Son and the Spirit as the “two hands” of the Father, insisting the one God always acts through Word and Wisdom.10 Origen of Alexandria (c. 185–253), the most powerful mind of the era, made a decisive contribution by teaching the eternal generation of the Son—the Son is begotten of the Father not at some point in time but eternally, so that there never was a time when the Father was without his Word—yet Origen too used language that ranks the Son below the Father.11

The Latin contribution came from Tertullian of Carthage. Around 210, in a treatise against a teacher named Praxeas who had collapsed the distinctions between Father and Son (the error later called modalism), Tertullian forged the vocabulary the West would use forever after. He appears to be the first Christian writer to use the Latin trinitas—“Trinity”—and he described the three as tres personae, una substantia: three persons, one substance.12 A full century before Nicaea, the formula that ordinary Christians still recite was already on the page.

So the honest picture is neither a pristine creed delivered intact from the apostles nor a fourth-century fabrication. It is a living tradition that knew what it worshipped—the Father, and the Son who is fully God and not a creature, and the Spirit—while still working out how to say it without lapsing into either modalism (one God wearing three masks) or tritheism (three gods). That is development. It is exactly what we should expect of a community thinking under pressure across generations.

What Nicaea Actually Did—and the Real Vote

Into this tradition, around 318–320, a presbyter of Alexandria named Arius introduced something his own bishop regarded as new and alarming. Arius taught that the Son, though exalted above all creation, was a creature—made by the Father out of nothing before the ages. His slogan was stark: “there was when he was not.”13 The Son, on this view, was not eternal and not of the same divine being as the Father. It was precisely the line the whole prior tradition—Origen’s eternal generation, the worship Ignatius and Pliny attest—had never crossed.

When Constantine, newly master of the whole empire and anxious for its unity, summoned the bishops to Nicaea in 325, the council's task was not to decide whether Jesus was divine; that was assumed by both sides. It was to decide whether the Son was created. To exclude Arius’s teaching, the council adopted into its creed the word homoousios—“of the same substance” (or essence) as the Father. The term was not biblical, and it was controversial even among the orthodox, since it had a checkered history in earlier disputes. But it had one decisive virtue: it was the one word Arius’s party could not sign while still meaning that the Son was a creature.

And the vote? Of roughly 250 to 318 bishops present (the sources vary), all but two of those present subscribed; only two Libyan bishops, Theonas and Secundus, refused outright and were exiled with Arius—though several who signed would soon waver in the turbulent decades that followed.14 Dan Brown’s “relatively close vote” is the reverse of the truth: the margin was overwhelming. Nor were these men Constantine’s creatures. Many of the bishops at Nicaea bore the scars and missing eyes of the Great Persecution under Diocletian, which had ended barely a decade earlier—hardly a docile assembly inclined to vote on imperial command. The doctrine they affirmed was the one their churches had been praying.

It is equally important to say what Nicaea did not do. It did not produce the creed most Christians recite today; the fuller “Nicene Creed,” with its developed clause on the Holy Spirit, comes from the Council of Constantinople in 381, which confessed the Spirit as “the Lord, the giver of life… who with the Father and the Son together is worshipped and glorified.”15 And Nicaea did not settle the matter. As the historian Lewis Ayres has stressed, homoousios was so far from being an instant slogan that for nearly twenty years after the council scarcely anyone—Athanasius included—even mentioned it; the decades after 325 were a chaos of competing parties, not a tidy triumph.16 The full Trinitarian synthesis was the achievement of the next two generations, above all the Cappadocian Fathers—Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Gregory of Nyssa—who clarified the distinction between the one ousia (essence) and the three hypostaseis (persons). Development, here, is undeniable. The point is what was being developed: the language for a faith already held, not the faith itself.

The Strongest Objection, Stated at Full Strength

The serious challenge does not come from Dan Brown. It comes from Bart D. Ehrman, the agnostic New Testament scholar whose How Jesus Became God (2014) gives the developmental thesis its most formidable popular statement. Ehrman explicitly rejects the Constantine myth—he knows the devotion is early. His claim is subtler and harder to answer.

Ehrman argues that the historical Jesus did not claim to be God and was not worshipped as God in his lifetime. After the crucifixion, some of his followers had visionary experiences they took to be appearances of the risen Jesus, and concluded that God had exalted him to heaven. From there, Christology “evolved”: Christians progressively pushed the moment of Jesus’s divine status earlier and earlier—from the resurrection, back to his baptism, back to his conception, and finally (in John) back before creation itself.17 Crucially, Ehrman insists that the ancient line between human and divine was porous, populated by a spectrum of semi-divine beings; so calling Jesus “a god” in the second century need not mean what Nicaea later meant by it. On this reading, the high Christology of Ignatius and the worship Pliny reports are real—but they are way-stations on a road, not the destination, and the full-strength claim that the Son is the one God, co-equal and co-eternal, is a later and contestable theological construction.

This is the objection at its best, and parts of it must be conceded. Ehrman is right that there was real development; right that early language is uneven; right that “god” carried a wider range in antiquity than in modern monotheism; and right, against the apologists who overstate the case, that one cannot simply read the Council of Constantinople off the lips of a Galilean carpenter. The history is messier than a slogan.

A Reply—and What It Can and Cannot Carry

Yet the developmental model strains against the very evidence Ehrman concedes. The decisive difficulty is one that scholars such as Hurtado and Richard Bauckham have pressed hard: the highest claims sit in the earliest strata. If devotion to Jesus had evolved slowly upward from a merely human prophet, we should find low Christology at the bottom and high Christology only at the top. Instead, the worship of Jesus—the marana tha prayer, the Philippians hymn applying an Isaiah text about Yahweh to Christ—is already there in the oldest material we can reach, within a couple of decades of the crucifixion, among Aramaic-speaking Jewish monotheists. Bauckham argues that the New Testament writers deliberately include Jesus within the unique “divine identity” of the God of Israel—creator and ruler of all—not as one more exalted being on a crowded spectrum.18 The data, in other words, run in the wrong direction for a smooth upward evolution.

To Ehrman’s point about the porous human-divine boundary, the reply is that the relevant boundary in Jewish monotheism was not porous at all: the line between the one Creator and everything created was absolute, and worship belonged to the Creator alone. That is exactly why Hurtado calls the worship of Jesus a startling “mutation” rather than an unremarkable promotion. The earliest Christians did not slot Jesus into a familiar pagan category of demigods; they did the unthinkable thing of giving a recently crucified man the devotion their Scriptures reserved for Yahweh—and they did it almost at once.

But the critic should get the last word before the reply closes, because the strongest form of his case is not yet exhausted. Ehrman can grant everything above and still press: worship and full conciliar dogma are not the same thing. Granting that the earliest Christians revered Jesus extraordinarily, the specific metaphysical claim—one identical divine essence in three co-equal persons—is a fourth-century philosophical settlement that the New Testament neither states nor requires. You have shown early high devotion; you have not shown the Nicene Creed. This is a fair point, and it draws the honest boundary of the argument.

The reply is to grant the distinction and deny that it rescues the “invention” thesis. Yes, the conceptual apparatus of ousia and hypostasis is later than the New Testament; the Cappadocians’ precision is a genuine theological achievement, not a verse one can cite. But the doctrine those terms protect—that the Son is fully God and not a creature, worshipped with the Father, and that the one God is somehow Father, Son, and Spirit—is the very thing the earliest worship already presupposed. The councils did not add deity to a human Jesus; they supplied a grammar that could hold together the threeness and the oneness the church had been confessing all along, and could rule out the two ditches—Arius’s created Son on one side, the modalists’ collapse of the persons on the other. The development is real; what it developed was not the worship but the words. And notably, Ehrman’s opponents on this point are not only confessional apologists but mainstream historians of religion: Hurtado was no defender of orthodoxy by trade, simply a historian who concluded the devotion was breathtakingly early.

The Honest State of the Scholarship

Where does the live debate actually stand? On the popular claim, there is no debate among historians: no serious scholar of any persuasion holds that Constantine or Nicaea invented Jesus’s divinity. That belief is documented in pagan and Christian sources alike for two centuries before the council, and the “close vote” is a fiction.

The genuine scholarly disagreement runs along a finer seam. The “early high Christology” school—Hurtado, Bauckham, and others—holds that Jesus was included in the divine identity essentially from the start. Ehrman and those influenced by older “history of religions” approaches hold to a more evolutionary model, even while conceding how early the devotion appears. Both sides agree the worship is early; they disagree over how to interpret it and how far it reaches toward later dogma. On the fourth century itself, the work of Ayres and the late R. P. C. Hanson has demolished the old caricature of a tidy fight between “Nicene” and “Arian” blocs, revealing instead a confusing field of overlapping theologies that took decades to resolve.19 This is the honest texture of the history—and it is far more interesting than either the apologist’s slogan or the skeptic’s.

Conclusion: What This Does and Does Not Establish

The historical record establishes, with as much confidence as the sources allow, that the early church worshipped and confessed Father, Son, and Holy Spirit from its earliest documented life—in its prayers, its hymns, its baptisms, and its rules of faith—long before any emperor convened a council. It establishes that Arianism, not Trinitarian faith, was the innovation Nicaea confronted, and that the council’s near-unanimous verdict was the defense of an inheritance, not the manufacture of a novelty. The popular claim that Nicaea invented the Trinity, or voted Jesus into godhood, simply cannot survive contact with the evidence.

It is equally important to mark what this does not establish. It does not show that the doctrine sprang fully formed from the apostles’ lips; the technical language of one essence in three persons was a real development, refined across the fourth century and beyond. It does not, by itself, prove the doctrine true—that the triune God exists is a further question, argued from Scripture, reason, and the claims and resurrection of Jesus himself. What the history removes is a specific and very common objection: the charge that Christians worship a god assembled by a committee under political duress. They do not. They worship the one their forebears were already singing to before dawn, “as to a god,” while the empire was still trying to make them stop. Whether that worship is rightly placed is the conversation that begins where this one ends.
