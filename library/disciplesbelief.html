Why Did the Disciples Believe Jesus Rose? The Origin of Resurrection Faith

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

A Question Almost Everyone Concedes

Begin with the surprising area of agreement. On the question of whether the earliest disciples of Jesus genuinely, sincerely believed that they had seen him alive after his crucifixion, the answer given by believing and unbelieving historians alike is the same: yes. Bart Ehrman, perhaps the best-known skeptical New Testament scholar writing in English, puts it plainly: some of Jesus' followers came to believe he was raised from the dead, and they had visions of him afterward.1 Gerd Lüdemann, an avowed atheist, grants in his own study that the appearances to Peter and the Twelve can be regarded as historical.2 The sincerity of the founding belief is not, in serious scholarship, a contested point.

That concession reframes the whole debate. The interesting historical question is no longer whether the disciples believed, but how that belief came to exist at all. A group of demoralized, frightened first-century Jews, whose leader had just suffered the most degrading death their world could inflict, somehow emerged within weeks convinced — and convinced enough to die for it — that he had been bodily raised. This essay is about the origin of that conviction. Its claim is narrow and, I think, strong: every naturalistic account on offer struggles to explain how this particular belief, with its particular and unprecedented shape, arose in this particular world. The resurrection is offered not as a thing that cannot be questioned, but as the explanation that does the most work with the fewest strains.

The Argument Stated

The argument can be set out in five steps.

(1) The earliest disciples came, very soon after the crucifixion, to a sincere and unshakable belief that Jesus had been bodily raised from the dead — a datum granted across the scholarly spectrum. (2) The specific content of that belief — an individual, bodily, mid-history resurrection of a crucified man — had no ready template in the Jewish world that produced it; nothing in the mainstream framework had a slot for this combination, and it was, in N. T. Wright's phrase, a startling mutation within Jewish hope. (3) A crucified Messiah was, in that world, very nearly a contradiction in terms, since crucifixion signaled God's curse. (4) The disciples' subsequent willingness to suffer and die shows the belief was sincerely held, not fabricated. (5) Therefore the belief's origin demands an adequate cause, and the disciples' own explanation — that they had encountered the risen Jesus — accounts for the data better than its naturalistic rivals.

The conclusion is comparative, not a proof. It says the resurrection is the best available explanation of the origin of the belief, while frankly granting that a determined naturalist can always prefer an unknown cause to a miraculous one. What the argument denies is that the naturalistic options now on the table do the explanatory job cleanly.

A Belief No One in That World Was Poised to Invent

The strongest and most distinctive plank of this argument is the Jewish background, and here the indispensable work is N. T. Wright's 800-page study The Resurrection of the Son of God.3 Wright's method is to map, in exhaustive detail, what "resurrection" meant across the ancient Mediterranean. His findings are not controversial in their broad strokes. For pagans, the door of death did not reopen; a return to embodied life was precisely what everyone knew did not happen. For Jews who affirmed resurrection — the Pharisees, much of the populace, against the Sadducees who denied it — the hope had a fixed and specific shape. It was bodily, not a flight of the soul to heaven. It was corporate: all the righteous dead would be raised together. And it was future: it belonged to the end of history, the great day when God set the world right.4

Nothing in that framework had room for what the disciples began proclaiming. They did not say that Jesus' soul was in heaven, or that he had been exalted as a spirit, or that they expected to see him again at the last day. They said that one man had been raised bodily, in the middle of ongoing history, while the rest of the dead slept on and the world rolled forward unredeemed. As Wright puts it, no first-century Jew expected a single individual to rise ahead of everyone else; the idea simply was not on the conceptual map.5 This is why Wright catalogues a set of striking modifications — he counts roughly seven — that Jewish resurrection belief underwent in the hands of the first Christians: resurrection moved from the margin to the center of their hope; the vague spectrum of Jewish views collapsed into one definite expectation; and, decisively, what was meant to be a single end-of-history event was split in two, with one man already raised and everyone else still waiting.6

The force of the point is about origins. People embellish and adapt the categories they already possess; they very rarely invent, out of whole cloth, a combination for which their mainstream framework had no ready template — and then stake their lives on it. Had the disciples been reaching for a comforting story after a beloved teacher's death, the materials lay ready to hand. They could have said Jesus had been "assumed" into heaven like Enoch or Elijah, or "exalted" to God's right hand (a thoroughly Jewish idea), or that he would return at the general resurrection. Each of these would have been intelligible, even respectable. Instead they said the one thing for which their world's mainstream hope had no ready slot — an individual, bodily, mid-history raising. The most economical explanation of why they described something unprecedented is that something unprecedented is what they were trying to describe.

The scandal of a crucified Messiah

The novelty runs deeper still, because the man in question had been crucified. To grasp how that landed on first-century Jewish ears, one must hold together two facts. First, "Messiah" meant a triumphant, anointed deliverer — a king who would defeat Israel's enemies, not be executed by them. A defeated, dead messiah was, by definition, a failed one; the movement should have died with him, as other messianic movements did when their leaders were killed. Second, the manner of death made matters worse. The Torah declares, "a hanged man is cursed by God" (Deuteronomy 21:23), a text that Second Temple Jews connected to crucifixion and that Paul himself applies directly to the cross.7 A crucified messiah was therefore not merely a disappointment; he was, on the face of it, a man God had publicly cursed.

This is exactly the offense Paul names when he calls the message of "Christ crucified" a "stumbling block to Jews and folly to Gentiles" (1 Corinthians 1:23).8 No one looking to launch a credible movement in that culture would choose a crucified figure as the centerpiece. The belief that Jesus was the risen Lord had to overcome the most natural reading of his death — that the cross had falsified his claims. Something had to be powerful enough to turn the curse of Calvary into the climax of God's plan. The disciples' answer was that God had reversed the verdict by raising him. Strip that away, and the rise of devotion to a crucified man becomes very hard to motivate.

Sincerity Sealed in Blood

The fourth premise concerns not the content of the belief but its seriousness. The disciples did not merely hold their conviction privately; they proclaimed it publicly, in Jerusalem, under threat, and many of them paid for it. Here care is needed, because the popular claim that "all twelve apostles were martyred" outruns the evidence. As the Christian apologist Sean McDowell has shown in the most thorough study of the question, only a few apostolic martyrdoms are strongly attested — Peter, Paul, James the brother of Jesus, and James the brother of John are the firmest — while others are probable or merely traditional.9 The argument does not need all twelve. It needs what the evidence in fact supplies: that the leaders of the movement faced real, lethal danger and did not recant.

The logic is often stated too crudely, so let it be stated carefully. Many people die for beliefs that are false; sincerity does not make a belief true. What martyrdom does establish is sincerity — and that is the relevant point here, because the disciples were uniquely positioned to know whether they were lying. A modern martyr dies for a conviction received on trust from others; the original disciples claimed to be eyewitnesses of the very thing in question. People rarely, and as a rule do not, knowingly die for what they themselves have fabricated, with nothing to gain. The willingness of the core disciples to suffer therefore closes off the crudest naturalistic option — deliberate fraud — and confirms that the belief whose origin we are trying to explain was genuinely, deeply held.

The Strongest Objections, Answered

Ehrman and Lüdemann: it was visions all along

The leading naturalistic explanation is not fraud but vision. Ehrman argues that a few followers — he thinks the firm cases are Peter, Paul, and Mary Magdalene — had visionary experiences of the sort grief is known to produce, "bereavement visions" in which the grieving vividly perceive a lost loved one; these were sincerely taken as appearances and grew, in the retelling, into the tradition of mass encounters.10 Lüdemann presses a similar line, tracing Peter's experience to guilt over his denial and Paul's to a repressed inner conflict.11 These are substantial proposals, and the honest first move is to concede ground: bereavement experiences are real and common, and the bare report "they saw him" is, by itself, compatible with a non-veridical vision.

The difficulty is that the vision hypothesis, taken alone, explains the wrong thing. It can perhaps explain that some disciples had experiences they interpreted as seeing Jesus. It does not explain why they interpreted those experiences as a bodily resurrection — the one category their tradition lacked. A first-century Jew who saw a vivid apparition of a dead friend had a ready and orthodox vocabulary for it: he had seen the man's spirit, or an angel, or a sign that the righteous one was safe with God awaiting the last day. Visions, however powerful, point naturally toward exaltation and heavenly vindication, not toward the claim that the general resurrection had begun in one man ahead of schedule. Wright's point bites precisely here: the disciples' belief is not what a vision would, in that world, have generated.12 The vision theory must therefore borrow what it cannot supply — an explanation of the empty tomb, which many scholars accept as historical, and of why bodily resurrection language was used at all.

Festinger: cognitive dissonance and the rationalizing group

The most sophisticated objection appeals to social psychology. In the classic 1956 study When Prophecy Fails, Leon Festinger and his colleagues documented a UFO sect whose apocalyptic prediction failed — and who, rather than disband, reinterpreted the failure and proselytized harder than before.13 From this Festinger drew his influential theory of cognitive dissonance: when reality contradicts a deeply held, publicly committed belief, people often resolve the tension not by abandoning the belief but by re-rationalizing it. Skeptics including Lüdemann have suggested that the disciples did something similar — that the trauma of the crucifixion, far from killing their faith, drove them to reconstruct it in a more invulnerable form, with "resurrection" as the rationalization.14

This deserves a careful, two-part reply, and the decisive part does not depend on any single study. First and most importantly, dissonance theory predicts the wrong outcome. The mechanism it describes is the preservation and rationalization of an existing belief in the teeth of disconfirmation. But the disciples did not preserve a prior expectation; they had no expectation of a dying-and-rising Messiah to preserve. They were not clinging to a prediction that Jesus would rise — the Gospels portray them as blindsided, despairing, and slow to believe even the first reports. Dissonance reduction can explain a group doubling down on what it already hoped for. It is poorly suited to explain a group inventing a brand-new category — a crucified and cursed Messiah, raised bodily ahead of the last day — contrary to its entire tradition, to describe a man it had given up for lost. The theory accounts for continuity under stress; what needs explaining here is radical discontinuity.

Second, and corroborating this, the analogy is weaker on its own terms than it looks — and recent scholarship has weakened it further. A 2025 study by Thomas Kelly, drawing on newly unsealed archival material, argues that Festinger's celebrated case was misreported: the Seekers had in fact proselytized before the prophecy failed and largely abandoned their belief after it, the opposite of the book's famous claim.15 The empirical foundation of the dissonance-spike model is, at the least, far shakier than its popularity suggests; one should not lean a historical reconstruction on it as if it were settled science. That is the kind of limit this argument is bound to flag: a contested theory cannot quietly become a decisive premise.

Oppy and the methodological naturalist

A more philosophically careful objection comes from naturalists such as Graham Oppy, who would grant much of the above and still decline the conclusion. Their point is principled: a resurrection is a miracle, and a miracle is so improbable on naturalistic priors that almost any natural explanation — even an unknown one — will be more probable than a divine intervention. We do not yet have the full psychology and sociology of the early movement; absence of a tidy natural mechanism is not presence of a supernatural one.16 This is the strongest form of the objection because it does not deny the data; it disputes how to weigh them.

The fair reply concedes the structure of the point and contests the weighting. Yes, if one assigns a vanishingly small prior to God's acting in history, no quantity of evidence will move the needle; the disagreement then is not really about the disciples but about whether such a God might exist at all — a question argued on other grounds. But for anyone who holds that prior open, the comparative case stands: the resurrection explains the origin, the shape, the timing, and the cost of the belief in a single stroke, where the naturalistic alternatives each leave a conspicuous remainder — the empty tomb, the bodily language, the unprecedented category, the conversion of those with no grief to project. To prefer an as-yet-unspecified natural cause is a permissible move; it is not, on the table as it stands, the better explanation.

The State of the Scholarship

It is worth being exact about where the consensus lies and where it stops. That the disciples sincerely believed they had encountered the risen Jesus, and believed it early, is held very widely, by skeptics included; Ehrman and Lüdemann are witnesses against interest on that score. Gary Habermas's long-running bibliographic survey reports that a strong majority of scholars writing on the subject since 1975 grant the disciples' sincere experiences — though that figure is Habermas's own compilation of the literature, not a polled census, and the methodology has fair critics.17 Wright's mutations argument is widely respected and widely cited, but it is not unopposed: critics question whether Second Temple Jewish belief was really as uniform as the schema needs, and whether the "novelty" can bear the full inferential weight Wright places on it. The interpretation of the data — vision, dissonance, or genuine encounter — remains exactly where the live debate is joined, and honesty requires saying that no historical method can settle, as such, whether a miracle occurred.

What the Argument Does and Does Not Show

This argument does not, by itself, prove that Jesus rose from the dead. It establishes something more modest and more secure: that the origin of the disciples' belief is a real historical puzzle, that the belief had a shape no one in their world was positioned to invent, and that the naturalistic explanations currently on offer — fraud, bereavement vision, cognitive dissonance — each leave a significant part of the data unexplained. It works best not in isolation but as one strand in a cable, alongside the early creed of 1 Corinthians 15, the empty tomb, and the conversion of skeptics like James and Paul, which are treated in their own essays. The resurrection is presented here as the hypothesis that best accounts for how frightened, scattered followers of a crucified and apparently cursed teacher became, within weeks, fearless witnesses to a category their tradition did not contain — and were willing to die rather than take it back.

That is a genuine argument, not a knock-down demonstration, and it is offered in the spirit the New Testament itself commends: to give a reason for hope "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15), confident in what the evidence shows and candid about where it ends. The reader whose background convictions rule out God's acting will not be moved by history alone — but that is a dispute about whether God exists and acts, not about the disciples. The question that started this essay still stands, and it is not a small one: where, in a world with no room for it, did this transformed conviction come from?

Frequently asked questions

Couldn't the disciples' belief that Jesus rose just be grief hallucinations, like Ehrman argues?

Bereavement visions are real, and the bare report "they saw him" is compatible with them. But a vision explains the wrong thing. A first-century Jew who saw an apparition of a dead friend had ready categories: a spirit, an angel, exaltation to God. Visions point toward heavenly vindication, not toward the claim that the general resurrection had begun bodily in one man ahead of schedule, the one category their tradition lacked.

Doesn't the fact that the disciples died for their faith prove Jesus rose?

No, and the essay states this carefully. Many people die for beliefs that are false; sincerity doesn't make a belief true. What martyrdom establishes is that the disciples genuinely held their conviction and weren't lying, since people don't knowingly die for what they've fabricated. It closes off the fraud theory and confirms the belief was deeply held; the truth of what they believed must be argued on other grounds.

Why couldn't the disciples' resurrection belief just be cognitive dissonance after Jesus died?

Dissonance theory describes a group preserving an existing belief despite disconfirmation. But the disciples had no expectation of a dying-and-rising Messiah to preserve; the Gospels show them blindsided and despairing. The theory explains continuity under stress, not the invention of a brand-new category contrary to their whole tradition. A 2025 study also argues Festinger's famous case was misreported, weakening the model further.
