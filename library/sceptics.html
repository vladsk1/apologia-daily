The Conversion of the Sceptics: James, the Brother of Jesus, as Hostile Witness

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

The brother who did not believe

Imagine you grew up in the same small house as a man who is now being called the Son of God. You knew him as a child. You watched him at chores and at meals; you saw him irritated and tired; you went to weddings with him. And when he left home to preach, you concluded — along with the rest of the family — that he had lost his mind. The Gospel of Mark records the verdict bluntly: his relatives "went out to seize him, for they were saying, 'He is out of his mind'" (Mark 3:21).1 The Gospel of John is blunter still: "For not even his brothers believed in him" (John 7:5).2

That man is James, the brother of Jesus. And the historically arresting fact is what happened next. Within a few years of the crucifixion, this same sceptic is not merely a believer but the recognized leader of the mother church in Jerusalem — the man Paul calls a "pillar," the figure who presides over the Jerusalem council, the brother whose ruling the apostles defer to (Galatians 2:9; Acts 15:13-21).3 And around the year 62, according to the Jewish historian Josephus, James was executed by stoning on the order of the high priest Ananus.4 A man who once thought his brother deranged ended his life as a martyr for the claim that this brother had risen from the dead.

What changed him? That is the question this essay presses. A separate essay in this library is devoted to the conversion of Paul, the persecutor (see The Conversion of Paul); here Paul appears only as the second of two hostile witnesses. The depth belongs to James, because the logic that makes both conversions evidentially weighty rests most cleanly on him.

The logic of the hostile witness

In both courtroom practice and historical method, not all testimony is created equal. A witness who confirms what he wants to be true, or what his community expects, is easy to discount; he may be reporting his hopes. But a witness who testifies against his own prior convictions, against his own interests, against everything he had reason to want — that witness carries a special weight. He is, in the legal idiom, a hostile witness, and his agreement is hard to explain away as wishful thinking.

This is closely related to what New Testament historians call the criterion of embarrassment: a tradition awkward for the people who preserved it is unlikely to have been invented by them. That the founder's own family thought him mad, and that one of the movement's chief leaders had to be won over from outright unbelief, is not the kind of detail a community fabricates to flatter itself. It is the kind of detail that survives because it is true.

Two men anchor the resurrection's hostile-witness case. Paul began as an active persecutor, "violently persecuting the church of God and trying to destroy it" (Galatians 1:13).5 James began as a domestic sceptic. The first had every ideological reason to disbelieve; the second had every familiar, this-is-just-my-brother reason. Gary Habermas and Michael Licona, in their "minimal facts" presentation, list the conversion of James and the conversion of Paul among the small set of data conceded by the great majority of critical scholars, precisely because each is multiply rooted and cuts against the grain.6 The argument is cumulative: no single feature is decisive, but together they make the naturalistic alternatives strain.

What we actually know about James

The case for James rests on a convergence of independent sources, which is what gives it traction.

His unbelief during the ministry is attested in two strands — Mark 3:21 (with the naming of the brothers, including James, in Mark 6:3) and John 7:5 — that scholars regard as independent. This passes the criterion of embarrassment with ease: the early church revered James, so a story of his earlier scepticism is the last thing it would have manufactured.

His place among the named recipients of a resurrection appearance is fixed by the oldest piece of Christian tradition we possess. The creed Paul recites in 1 Corinthians 15 — a formula most scholars date to within a few years of the crucifixion (see The Early Creed of 1 Corinthians 15) — states: "Then he appeared to James, then to all the apostles" (1 Corinthians 15:7).7 This is not a late legend about James; it is an early, pre-Pauline confession that singles him out by name.

His leadership of the Jerusalem church is independently confirmed by Paul's own letters and by Acts. Paul says that on his first post-conversion visit to Jerusalem, around AD 35-36, he "saw none of the other apostles except James the Lord's brother" (Galatians 1:19), and that James, Cephas, and John were "reputed to be pillars" (Galatians 2:9). Acts shows James presiding at the Jerusalem council and issuing its decision (Acts 15:13-21). The Australian scholar John Painter, whose Just James is the standard monograph on the figure, traces how James moved from outside the circle of believers to its very center — a trajectory that itself demands explanation.8

His martyrdom is recorded by a non-Christian source. Josephus, in Antiquities 20.200, narrates how the high priest Ananus, exploiting a gap between Roman governors around AD 62, "assembled the sanhedrin of judges, and brought before them the brother of Jesus, who was called Christ, whose name was James, and some others... he delivered them to be stoned."9 The overwhelming majority of scholars — including sceptics — regard this passage as authentic, in part because Josephus mentions James only incidentally, to explain why Ananus was deposed, with no Christian agenda to serve.10 A second-century Christian source, Hegesippus (preserved in Eusebius's Ecclesiastical History 2.23), adds a more elaborate martyrdom — James thrown from the temple parapet and then clubbed — and gives him the title "the Just." That account is more legendary in its details and should be cited with care, but the bare fact of a violent death it shares with Josephus.11

Put the strands together and a shape emerges that no single source could fabricate: a sceptical brother, named in the earliest creed as having seen the risen Jesus, who rose to lead the Jerusalem church and died for the conviction.

The transformation that needs explaining

The bare facts are not seriously in dispute. The interpretive question is what could turn a man who knew Jesus intimately, and disbelieved, into a leader willing to die. Here the precision matters, because James is in one respect a harder case than any other witness.

The most psychologically respectable naturalistic explanation of the resurrection appearances is the bereavement-vision or grief-hallucination hypothesis: grieving people who loved someone deeply, and who long for that person's return, sometimes vividly perceive the deceased as present. This is a real, documented phenomenon, and we should concede it plainly. But it requires a specific profile — love, longing, the ache to see the lost one again. James does not fit it. He is not on record as a devoted follower yearning for his brother's return; he is on record as having thought his brother out of his mind. A bereaved disciple primed by grief is one thing. A sceptical sibling, who had three decades of ordinary family knowledge weighing against any temptation to deify Jesus, is quite another.

The naturalistic explanations, taken seriously

Grief and longing

Could James, despite his earlier scepticism, have been so shaken by the trauma of his brother's public execution that he hallucinated him alive? The hypothesis is not absurd; trauma and guilt can disturb perception. But it cuts against the grain of what we know. Crucifixion was not merely a death but a shameful, covenant-cursed death (Deuteronomy 21:23; cf. Galatians 3:13). For a first-century Jew, a crucified messianic claimant was a refuted messianic claimant. Nothing about the manner of Jesus's death would have inclined a sceptical brother toward sudden faith; it would have confirmed his doubts. A grief vision must explain not just an experience but a complete reversal of conviction in someone who had no prior wish to believe.

Family loyalty and dynastic ambition

A sharper objection runs the other way: perhaps James converted not from grief but from opportunity. His brother's movement survived the crucifixion; James, as the eldest surviving brother, stepped in to lead it — a kind of dynastic succession, with the resurrection claim as the family business. Some sceptical and mythicist writers have floated versions of this "dynastic succession" reading, in which James simply inherited the family business.

The reply turns on timing and cost. Leadership of a persecuted sect of a crucified criminal in early-30s Jerusalem was not a prize; it was a liability. There was no power, wealth, or honor to inherit — only the social shame of being kin to an executed blasphemer and, eventually, a death sentence. Dynastic ambition explains why a man might lead a winning movement, not why a sceptic would join a losing and dangerous one and then refuse to recant under the threat of stoning. And the dynastic reading must still account for the early creed's specific claim — not that James inherited a role, but that the risen Jesus "appeared to James." The succession theory explains the leadership while leaving the conversion, which is the thing to be explained, untouched.

Gradual persuasion

A third option is the least dramatic: perhaps James was won over slowly — by the disciples' preaching, by reflection on the scriptures, by the moral force of the early community — and the "appearance" is a later, stylized way of marking a gradual change of mind. This is the most serious of the naturalistic readings, because it does not require any anomalous experience at all.

Two considerations weigh against it. First, the early creed of 1 Corinthians 15:7 does not describe a process of persuasion; it names a discrete appearance, and it is among the earliest tradition we have, leaving little room for a slow legend to form. Second, gradual persuasion does not easily produce martyrs. People are talked into opinions and talked out of them; the willingness to be stoned rather than recant is the mark of a conviction the holder regards as anchored in something he himself encountered, not merely argued into. The gradualist reading can explain a change of view; it strains to explain a death.

What the argument does — and does not — establish

Intellectual honesty requires drawing the line carefully, and critics are right to insist on it.

First, the sources for James are real but uneven. The Gospel data on his unbelief and the Pauline data on his leadership are early and strong. The Josephan notice of his death is solid. But the vivid Hegesippus martyrdom is later and partly legendary, and even the Josephus passage, while accepted by most, has been questioned by a minority who suspect the words "who was called Christ" of being a later gloss.12 The case for James should lean on the convergence, not on any single embellished narrative.

Second, and more fundamentally, this argument does not by itself prove the resurrection. What it establishes is that a sceptic became a martyr-leader, and that he and the earliest community attributed the change to an appearance of the risen Jesus. That James was sincerely and radically transformed is well supported. That the cause of his transformation was a literal resurrection is a further inference — the best explanation, the argument contends, of a conversion that grief, ambition, and slow persuasion all fail to capture. But it is an inference, defended alongside the empty tomb, the disciples' experiences, and the conversion of Paul, not a proof standing alone.

The honest claim is therefore modest and strong at once: the conversion of James is a fact the naturalistic theories handle poorly, and the cumulative case is the better for it.

The state of the question

That James existed, was Jesus's brother, was not a follower during the ministry, led the Jerusalem church, and died a violent death is close to consensus across the scholarly spectrum, from confessional historians like Richard Bauckham — who has written extensively on James and the relatives of Jesus — to sceptics who grant the same facts while disputing their cause.13 N. T. Wright treats the conversion of James, alongside Paul's, as part of the data any historian of Christian origins must explain, not a pious flourish.14 The live debate is not over whether James converted but over why — and there the naturalistic options remain on the table, defended by able critics, even as their proponents must concede how poorly James fits the grief profile their best theory relies on.

Paul belongs in the same conversation as the second hostile witness: the persecutor who became an apostle and martyr, whose own letters preserve his testimony in the first person. His case is developed in its own essay (The Conversion of Paul). Together the two form a pincer: the outside enemy and the inside sceptic, converging on the same claim from opposite starting points, each immune to the explanation that fits the other.

Conclusion

The conversion of James does not, on its own, prove that Jesus rose. It does something narrower and harder to dismiss: it places at the very center of the early church a man whose prior unbelief is embarrassingly well-attested, names him in the oldest creed as a witness to the risen Christ, and follows him to a martyr's death. The naturalistic explanations — grief, dynastic loyalty, gradual persuasion — each illuminate part of the picture and each leave the heart of it dark, because each was designed for a different kind of witness than a sceptical brother who had no wish to believe and every reason not to. That a man who once called his brother mad would die rather than deny he had seen him risen is exactly the sort of fact that gives the resurrection case its grip on serious historians.

None of this is to sneer at the doubter; James himself was one, and the church that revered him preserved the record of his doubt. The argument is offered, as Peter urged, "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15)15 — confident in what the evidence shows, candid about where it stops, and aware that the most powerful witness here is a man who started exactly where the honest sceptic stands today.
