The Early Creed of 1 Corinthians 15: How Close to the Cross Did the Resurrection Belief Begin?

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Free preview voice

A Tradition Older Than the New Testament

Sometime around the year 55, in a letter to a quarrelsome church in Corinth, the apostle Paul paused to remind his readers of something he insists he did not invent. "For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received," he writes, "that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures, and that he appeared to Cephas, then to the twelve" (1 Corinthians 15:3-5).1 What follows is a list of further appearances — to more than five hundred at once, to James, to all the apostles, and last of all to Paul himself.

The arresting thing is not the content alone but the framing. Paul is quoting. He uses the technical language of rabbinic tradition — receiving and delivering a fixed body of teaching — and the careful structure, the unusual vocabulary, and the foreign-sounding name "Cephas" all signal that he is reciting a formula older than the letter that contains it. If that is right, then buried inside a mid-50s epistle is a fossil from the first years of the Christian movement: a summary of the gospel that the earliest believers were already confessing before Paul ever picked up a pen. This essay examines why the great majority of scholars — including avowed skeptics — accept that the creed is early, what that early date establishes, and, just as importantly, what it does not.

The Marks of a Borrowed Formula

That 1 Corinthians 15:3-5 (with the list extending through verse 7) is a pre-Pauline creed is, as the critical commentator Hans Conzelmann put it in his standard commentary on 1 Corinthians, broadly recognized in the scholarship.2 Several converging features support this.

First, the introductory verbs. Paul says he "delivered" (Greek paredōka) what he "received" (parelabon) — the precise idiom used in Judaism for the transmission of authoritative tradition, the equivalent of the rabbinic masar and qibbel.3 Paul is not narrating his own experience here; he is handing on something handed to him.

Second, the language is in part not Paul's own. The phrase "for our sins," the verb "he has been raised" in a particular perfect form, the expression "the twelve," and "according to the Scriptures" cluster non-Pauline vocabulary into a few lines. Above all, the creed names Peter by his Aramaic name, Kēphas (Cephas), rather than the Greek Petros Paul more often uses.4

Third, the structure is liturgical. The lines fall into balanced clauses, each introduced by the Greek hoti ("that"), in a fourfold rhythm — died, was buried, was raised, appeared — that reads as a confession to be memorized rather than prose to be composed on the fly.5

Fourth, several scholars detect an Aramaic substratum beneath the Greek. Joachim Jeremias, who called this passage "the earliest tradition of all," argued that its Semitic flavor and the Aramaic "Cephas" point back to the Aramaic-speaking church in Jerusalem.6 Here a note of caution is in order: the claim of an Aramaic original is contested. Some scholars, including those who fully accept the formula's antiquity, think it was composed in Greek among Greek-speaking Jewish Christians, and that "Cephas" alone does not prove an Aramaic Vorlage. The pre-Pauline character of the formula does not stand or fall on the Aramaic question; it rests on the cumulative weight of the receiving/delivering language, the non-Pauline diction, and the creedal structure.

The Dating: Counting Backward From the Cross

If the creed is older than 1 Corinthians, how much older? The argument runs through Paul's own biography. Paul says that "after three years" he went up to Jerusalem "to get acquainted with" Cephas, staying fifteen days, and that he also saw James the Lord's brother (Galatians 1:18-19). The verb he uses, historēsai, occurs only here in the New Testament and connotes inquiry — getting to know a person and, plausibly, his account.7

Now the chronology. Most historians place the crucifixion around AD 30-33 and Paul's conversion within a year or three of it, around AD 33-35. His Jerusalem visit, "after three years," falls around AD 35-36 — and the two men he names there, Cephas and James, are precisely two of the named recipients of appearances in the creed.8 Whether Paul first received the formula at his conversion (from the Damascus or Antioch community) or had it confirmed in that face-to-face fortnight in Jerusalem, the tradition itself must predate his receipt of it. That pushes its origin into the first half of the 30s — within roughly two to five years of the crucifixion, and arguably less.

This is not a conclusion driven by apologetic motive; it is held across the spectrum. The atheist New Testament scholar Gerd Lüdemann judged that "the elements in the tradition are to be dated to the first two years after the crucifixion of Jesus... not later than three years," placing the formation of the appearance traditions in the years 30-33.9 Even the skeptic's skeptic concedes the floor is early.

What the Creed Establishes

The payoff is significant. The standard skeptical move against the resurrection has long been to treat it as legend — a story that grew by accretion over decades, the original facts blurred by time and distance until a dying-and-rising god emerged. The creed cuts against that. It shows that the four-part claim — Christ died, was buried, was raised, and appeared — was not a late development but the bedrock confession of the movement from its earliest traceable moment, recited by the very people (Peter, James, "the twelve") who claimed to be its witnesses. As the historian and bishop N. T. Wright argues, this primitive tradition of the mid-30s reaches behind all our written Gospels to the foundation of the church's faith.10

The window is too narrow for legendary embellishment to have done its slow work, and the named, living witnesses functioned as a check. Within a few years of the cross, the earliest community was already proclaiming that Jesus had died, been buried, and been seen alive.

What the Creed Does Not Establish

Here intellectual honesty demands a hard line. The early creed establishes early, sincere, eyewitness-anchored belief in Jesus' death, burial, and post-mortem appearances. It does not, by itself, prove the resurrection. That the first disciples were convinced they had seen the risen Jesus — and convinced very soon — is a historical datum the evidence supports strongly. That their conviction was true is a further inference, argued on separate grounds (the inadequacy of alternative explanations, the empty tomb, the transformation of the disciples, the conversion of skeptics like James and Paul). The creed is the indispensable first premise of the resurrection case, not the whole of it. To claim more is to overplay the hand — and serious critics are quick, and right, to notice when apologists do.

The Strongest Objections

Bart Ehrman: early belief, but only visions

The most prominent skeptical voice here, Bart Ehrman, grants a great deal. He affirms the historical core that the disciples came to believe they had seen Jesus alive, and he himself stresses that for Paul the resurrection was bodily, not merely spiritual.11 But Ehrman's explanation is naturalistic. In How Jesus Became God, he argues that "it was visions, and nothing else, that led the first disciples to believe" — perhaps as few as a handful of followers (Peter, Paul, Mary, probably James) had visionary experiences akin to the well-documented "bereavement visions" in which the grieving perceive a lost loved one, and these were retold and embellished until the tradition spoke of mass appearances.12

This is a serious argument and deserves a fair hearing. Bereavement visions are real and common; Ehrman cites the finding that a substantial fraction of the bereaved experience some sense of the deceased's presence. One should concede the point: the bare report "they saw him" is, on its face, compatible with hallucination.

The reply turns on the details the vision hypothesis must explain away. Hallucinations are, as a rule, private and individual; the creed reports group appearances ("the twelve," "more than five hundred"), and collective hallucinations of a single, shared, detailed percept are not an established clinical phenomenon. The hypothesis must also account for the conversion of James, a skeptic during Jesus' ministry, and of Paul, an active persecutor with no grief-driven longing to see Jesus restored — neither fits the bereavement profile. And it must explain the empty tomb, which many scholars publishing on the question accept as historical (though it is more contested than the appearances), and which a purely visionary belief leaves unaddressed.13 Ehrman's account is coherent; whether it is the best explanation of the full set of facts is exactly where the larger argument is joined.

Gerd Lüdemann: the same date, a psychological cause

Lüdemann presses the vision reading harder and from inside the early date he himself defends. He concedes — indeed insists — that Peter and the disciples had real experiences in which Jesus "appeared" to them, and that these go back to the earliest period. But he interprets Peter's experience as a vision triggered by guilt over his denial, and Paul's as the resolution of a repressed inner conflict.14

The philosopher William Lane Craig, in a detailed critical assessment, argues that Lüdemann's reconstruction smuggles in a speculative psychoanalysis of figures we cannot put on the couch, and that the "guilt complex" and "Christ complex" he posits are read into the texts rather than out of them.15 The honest concession is that Lüdemann is right about the date and right that the disciples had experiences they took to be appearances; the dispute is entirely over their cause, and there the psychological mechanism is conjecture, not evidence.

Richard Carrier: a revelation, not a man

From the mythicist fringe, Richard Carrier offers the most radical reading. In On the Historicity of Jesus, he accepts that the creed is early but argues that its "appearances" were revelatory visions of a celestial being who was never on earth; the verb "appeared" (ōphthē), he notes, is the language of revelation, and the creed mentions no one seeing Jesus before his death.16

Fairness requires stating that Carrier is a credentialed ancient historian who argues his case at length. But fairness also requires stating its standing: mythicism occupies the far margins of the field. Bart Ehrman — no friend of orthodoxy — wrote an entire book, Did Jesus Exist?, concluding that "virtually every scholar of antiquity, of biblical studies, of classics, and of Christian origins... agrees that Jesus existed," and that mythicism has "no support among critical scholars."17 The textual reply is direct: the same Paul who recites the creed also names Jesus' brother James as a man he met (Galatians 1:19) and speaks of Jesus as "born of woman, born under the law" (Galatians 4:4). A reading that turns these into celestial allegory strains the plain sense of the letters. The creed's ōphthē is the standard Septuagint verb for divine appearances, true; but it is also used in the Gospels for ordinary seeing, and Paul plainly distinguishes the appearances to others from his own "last of all" experience.

The State of the Scholarship

The early date of the creed is about as close to a consensus as ancient history offers. On the broader resurrection data, Gary Habermas has compiled a bibliographic survey of several thousand academic sources on the resurrection written since 1975, reporting that a strong majority grant the disciples' early, sincere experiences.18 This figure should be cited carefully: it is Habermas's own running compilation of the published literature, not a randomized poll of scholars, and critics have questioned how the sample was assembled.19 What is not seriously disputed is the narrower point this essay defends: that the creed is genuinely early. On that, Jeremias, Conzelmann, Wright, Lüdemann, and Ehrman — believers and atheists alike — agree.

Conclusion

The creed of 1 Corinthians 15 is rightly called the flagship of the resurrection case, but its value lies in knowing exactly what cargo it carries. It establishes, with a confidence rare in the study of antiquity, that within a few years of the crucifixion the earliest Christians — including named men who claimed to have seen the risen Jesus — were already confessing his death, burial, resurrection, and appearances. It forecloses the easy theory that the resurrection was a legend grown ripe over generations. It does not, on its own, prove that Jesus rose; it proves that the belief is early and the witnesses sincere, and it hands the deeper question — what best explains their experiences and the empty tomb — to the wider argument. That is a great deal for four lines of borrowed Greek to carry, and it is enough to make the creed the right place for any serious inquiry to begin. As Peter himself is remembered to have urged, the case is to be made "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15) — confident in what the evidence shows, candid about where it ends.
