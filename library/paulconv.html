Why Did Paul Convert to Christianity? His Conversion and the Early Creed

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Short on time? Read the quick answer →

The witness who had everything to lose

Most testimony to the resurrection comes from people who wanted it to be true. The women at the tomb had followed Jesus; the eleven had left their nets for him; the grieving had every reason, a skeptic will say, to see what they longed to see. That is precisely what makes one witness different. Before he was the Apostle Paul, he was Saul of Tarsus, and he did not love Jesus, did not follow him, did not grieve him, and did not expect him. He hunted his followers. He was, by his own unembarrassed admission, trying to destroy the movement he would die defending.1

This is the testimony that resists the easy explanations. You cannot call it grief-driven wish-fulfillment in a man who was not grieving. You cannot call it the fevered hope of a disciple in a man who thought the whole thing a blasphemy worth killing for. Whatever happened on the Damascus road, it turned the church's most capable enemy into its most relentless missionary — and it did so early enough that Paul could carry the resurrection claim back to the very men who said they had seen it, and check. This essay is about that man and that check. The four-line creed he quotes in 1 Corinthians 15 is treated in depth in its own companion essay;2 here the creed is the supporting witness, and Paul is the case.

The argument, stated with care

The reasoning from Paul to the resurrection is an inference to the best explanation, and it runs in a chain:

1. Saul of Tarsus was a sincere, capable persecutor of the early church — a hostile witness with no prior allegiance to Jesus.
2. He underwent a sudden, total transformation that he attributed to an encounter with the risen Jesus.
3. He paid for that claim with hardship and, by strong early tradition, finally with his life, and he had independent means to verify it — meeting the named witnesses in Jerusalem within a few years of the crucifixion.
4. The ordinary naturalistic engines of religious conviction — grief, prior hope, social pressure, gradual legend — do not fit his case.
Therefore Paul's transformation is best explained by his sincerely having experienced what he claimed, and his testimony anchors the resurrection appearances to the earliest moment of the movement.

Notice the modest shape of the conclusion. It does not, by itself, prove that Jesus rose bodily from the dead; an honest critic can grant that Paul was sincere and still ask whether he was mistaken. What the argument establishes is that the resurrection belief was held, from the start, by a man uniquely insulated from the usual psychological pressures — and that fact narrows the field of explanations dramatically. Where the argument ends, and where the harder inference to a real resurrection begins, is something we will mark plainly.

Saul the persecutor: the hostile witness

The most important fact about Paul is the one he never tried to hide. Writing to the Galatians, he reminds them of "my former life in Judaism, how I persecuted the church of God violently and tried to destroy it. And I was advancing in Judaism beyond many of my own age among my people, so extremely zealous was I for the traditions of my fathers" (Galatians 1:13-14).3 To the Philippians he lists his credentials with a kind of bitter pride: "circumcised on the eighth day, of the people of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin, a Hebrew of Hebrews; as to the law, a Pharisee; as to zeal, a persecutor of the church" (Philippians 3:5-6).4

This is not a hostile source describing Paul; it is Paul describing himself, in letters whose authenticity even the most skeptical critics accept. The German historian Martin Hengel, in his study The Pre-Christian Paul, insisted that "knowledge of Saul the Jew is a precondition of understanding Paul the Christian," and reconstructed a man of serious Pharisaic training and zealous conviction — exactly the sort of educated insider who understood what the Christian claim meant and rejected it as intolerable.5 Saul was not a confused bystander. He grasped the claim that a crucified man was Israel's Messiah, and he found it a scandal — for a hanged man was under God's curse (Deuteronomy 21:23), and a crucified Messiah was a contradiction in terms to a first-century Pharisee.

This is the heart of the hostile-witness argument. Consider what Paul did not have:

No grief. Paul had never been a disciple. He had nothing to mourn, no relationship to recover, no beloved teacher whose loss the mind might soften with a comforting vision. The bereavement-vision mechanism that skeptics invoke for the grieving disciples has no purchase on a man who wanted the dead man's followers in chains.

No prior expectation. Far from hoping for Jesus' vindication, Saul's entire theological framework ruled it out. He was not primed to "see" a risen Messiah; everything he believed told him a crucified pretender stayed dead and disgraced. As N. T. Wright stresses, the resurrection of Jesus forced Paul to rethink from the ground up everything he had previously believed about God, Messiah, and the end of history.6

No social incentive. Conversion cost Saul everything an ambitious Pharisee valued — status, security, the approval of his teachers, his very identity. He traded the persecutor's authority for the convert's danger, and was soon being lowered in a basket through a hole in the Damascus wall to escape the very kind of arrest he had once carried out (2 Corinthians 11:32-33).7

A movement's enemies do not usually defect to it, at ruinous personal cost, on the strength of nothing. Something happened.

The transformation and its own explanation

Paul tells us what he thinks happened, and he tells us tersely. He places his own experience at the end of the resurrection witness list — "last of all, as to one untimely born, he appeared also to me" (1 Corinthians 15:8) — using the same verb, ōphthē ("he appeared"), used of the appearances to Peter, the Twelve, and the rest.8 Elsewhere he describes it as God having "revealed his Son to me" (Galatians 1:16) and asks, pointedly, "Have I not seen Jesus our Lord?" (1 Corinthians 9:1).9

Two features of his account deserve weight. First, Paul never speaks of his experience as a private inward awakening but as a seeing — and, crucially, he groups it with the others' experiences as the same kind of event, even while marking it as last and abnormal. Second, the transformation was not gradual. He did not study his way into the church over years; by his own testimony and the independent narrative of Acts, the persecutor became the proclaimer almost at once. Whatever its cause, the cause was sudden, external in seeming, and powerful enough to overturn a life.

The verification trips: Paul went and checked

Here the argument gains a feature most ancient testimony lacks: the witness himself describes going to cross-examine the other witnesses. In Galatians 1, written to defend his independence, Paul lays out a timeline he has every reason to report accurately, because his opponents could have contradicted it. After his conversion, he says, he did not immediately consult the Jerusalem leaders; he went to Arabia, returned to Damascus, and only "after three years" went up to Jerusalem "to visit Cephas and remained with him fifteen days" — adding, "But I saw none of the other apostles except James the Lord's brother" (Galatians 1:18-19).10

The verb Paul chooses — historēsai, the only time it appears in the New Testament — carries the sense of inquiry, of getting to know a person and his account, not merely paying a social call.11 For fifteen days Paul had Peter to himself. It strains credulity to suppose the subject of the resurrection — the thing both men staked their lives on — never came up. And the man he names alongside Peter, James the Lord's brother, is precisely one of the named recipients of an appearance in the creed Paul quotes (1 Corinthians 15:7).

Fourteen years later, Paul made a second, more public verification trip. He went up to Jerusalem again, this time laying his gospel "before those who seemed influential... in order to make sure I was not running or had not run in vain" (Galatians 2:1-2). The "pillars" — James, Cephas, and John — "added nothing to me," recognized the grace given him, and "gave the right hand of fellowship" (Galatians 2:6-9).12 Whatever later friction there was at Antioch (Galatians 2:11-14), on the core gospel Paul and the original eyewitnesses were agreed. This matters enormously: the man who had seen the risen Jesus on the Damascus road and the men who claimed to have seen him in Jerusalem compared accounts, twice, and did not contradict each other.

The skeptic's natural worry — that the appearance traditions are independent inventions that grew up separately and were harmonized late — founders here. Paul reports direct, early, personal contact with the principal witnesses. He explicitly tells the Corinthians that the message is shared: "Whether then it was I or they, so we preach and so you believed" (1 Corinthians 15:11).13

Anchoring the creed's early date

This is where Paul's biography and the famous creed lock together. The creed of 1 Corinthians 15:3-7 is, by wide scholarly agreement, a formula Paul "received" and "delivered" — pre-Pauline tradition he is passing on, not composing.14 The question is only how early. Paul's own chronology supplies the answer. If the crucifixion fell around AD 30-33 and Paul's conversion within a few years of it, then his receipt of the tradition — at his conversion, or confirmed in that fifteen-day Jerusalem visit "after three years" — pushes the formula's origin into the first half of the 30s, within roughly two to five years of the cross.

It is worth stressing how little of this depends on Christian presupposition. The atheist New Testament scholar Gerd Lüdemann judged that "the elements in the tradition are to be dated to the first two years after the crucifixion of Jesus... not later than three years."15 Bart Ehrman, equally no friend of the resurrection, grants that the creed is early and that Paul received it from those before him.16 The full case for this dating, and what it does and does not prove, is the burden of the companion essay on the creed; the point here is simply that Paul is the reason we can date it. His autobiography in Galatians is the chronological backbone. Remove Paul, and the creed floats free of any fixed date; with Paul, it is nailed to the foundation of the movement.

The strongest objection: it was only a vision

The most serious naturalistic reply does not deny that Paul had an experience or that he was sincere. It reinterprets the experience. Paul, the objection runs, had a subjective vision — a powerful inner episode, perhaps psychologically generated — which he, in good faith, took for an encounter with the risen Lord. No corpse need have left any tomb.

Gerd Lüdemann presses this case from inside the early date he himself defends. He grants that Paul had a real, transformative "appearance" experience that goes back to the earliest period, but interprets it as the resolution of a repressed inner conflict — a "Christ complex" in which Saul's unconscious attraction to the gospel he was violently resisting finally broke through.17 Ehrman, more cautiously, locates Paul's experience in the broad category of visionary experiences that historians can document while staying agnostic about their cause.18 One should concede what is true here: the bare report "Paul saw Jesus" is, on its face, logically compatible with a vision rather than a bodily appearance. The argument cannot be settled by the word ōphthē alone.

But the vision hypothesis carries burdens that grow heavier the closer one looks. First, Lüdemann's "Christ complex" is, as the philosopher William Lane Craig argues in a detailed assessment, a speculative psychoanalysis of a man we cannot put on the couch — read into the texts rather than out of them, and resting on the assumption that Saul was secretly drawn to what he was in fact destroying.19 The texts give no hint of inner turmoil; they give us a man advancing confidently in his zeal.

Second, the vision reading does most of its work for the grieving disciples and almost none for Paul. Bereavement and longing are the usual psychological fuel for such experiences, and Paul had neither. A theory that explains the disciples' visions by their grief cannot, without strain, be transferred to the one witness who was not grieving.

Third — and this is the honest limit on both sides — Paul's case does not stand alone. He himself anchors his experience to a shared tradition of bodily appearances and to named witnesses he had personally interviewed. The vision hypothesis, to do its work, must explain not only Paul but the empty tomb, the group appearances, and the conversion of James, the skeptical brother who likewise had no grief-driven longing for a risen Messiah.20 James is treated in his own essay on the conversion of skeptics; here it is enough to note that Paul is not an isolated anomaly to be explained away but one of two hostile witnesses, converging. A theory that must independently invent a different psychological accident for each non-grieving convert is buying its way out of the evidence rather than explaining it.

The state of the scholarship

On the facts that drive this argument, there is remarkable agreement across the spectrum. That Saul of Tarsus was a real persecutor who became an apostle, that he wrote the letters in which he says so, that his conversion was early and sincere, and that he met Peter and James in Jerusalem — none of this is seriously contested. Critical scholars and confessing Christians alike treat Galatians 1-2 as bedrock autobiography, and Lüdemann and Ehrman build their own naturalistic reconstructions on the same data the apologist uses.

The live dispute, honestly stated, is not over what happened to Paul but over how to explain it. Did he encounter a risen person, or undergo a vivid subjective experience he sincerely misread? That question cannot be closed by Paul's testimony in isolation; it is decided, if at all, by the cumulative case — whether a bodily resurrection or some combination of naturalistic accidents better explains the whole pattern of the empty tomb, the multiple appearances, and the two hostile-witness conversions together. Gary Habermas's long-running survey of the academic literature finds broad agreement that the disciples and Paul had early, sincere experiences they took to be appearances of the risen Jesus; what that survey does not show, and does not claim to show, is consensus that the experiences were veridical.21 The data are agreed; the inference is where the real debate lives.

What Paul's conversion establishes — and what it doesn't

Paul's conversion does a specific and powerful job. It plants the resurrection belief, at the earliest traceable moment, in the mind of a witness who had every motive to reject it and none to invent it. It removes from the table the tidiest naturalistic explanations — grief, wish-fulfillment, prior expectation, slow legendary growth — at least for this witness. And through Paul's own movements between Damascus and Jerusalem, it anchors the early creed to a fixed date and ties the appearance traditions back to the named men who claimed to have seen the Lord.

What it does not do is prove, by itself, that Jesus rose. A determined skeptic can still say Paul was sincere and mistaken — that a subjective experience, however inexplicable in its trigger, fell short of a real encounter with a living man. That remaining gap is closed, if it is closed, only by the convergence of the evidence: the empty tomb, the range and independence of the appearances, and the second hostile conversion of James, each treated in its own essay. Paul is not the whole argument. He is its most stubborn single witness — the enemy who changed sides and then went, in person, to check the story with the men who started it.

That is why his testimony has weighed on serious inquirers for two thousand years, believer and skeptic alike. And it is why the case is best made, as the apostle himself urged, "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15) — confident in what the evidence shows about the man, and candid about where the evidence stops and faith's further step begins.

Frequently asked questions

Why does Paul's conversion count as evidence if he could just have been sincerely mistaken?

That concession is honest: an honest critic can grant Paul was sincere and still ask whether he was mistaken; the argument is an inference to the best explanation, not a proof of bodily resurrection. What it does establish is that the resurrection belief was held, from the start, by a hostile witness insulated from grief, prior hope, and social incentive—a fact that dramatically narrows the field of naturalistic explanations.

Wasn't Paul's Damascus road experience just a subjective vision, not a real encounter?

This is the most serious reply, and 'Paul saw Jesus' is logically compatible with a vision. Gerd Ludemann interprets it as a repressed 'Christ complex' breaking through. But as William Lane Craig argues, that is speculative psychoanalysis of a man we cannot examine, read into texts that show him advancing confidently in his zeal. Bereavement and longing fuel such experiences, and Paul, the persecutor, had neither.

How can we trust that Paul's message matched what the original disciples claimed?

Paul describes going to cross-examine the other witnesses. In Galatians 1 he reports staying fifteen days with Peter—using historesai, a verb of inquiry—and meeting James, both named recipients of appearances in the creed. Fourteen years later he laid his gospel before the 'pillars,' who 'added nothing.' The man who saw Jesus on the Damascus road and the men who saw him in Jerusalem compared accounts, twice, and did not contradict each other.
