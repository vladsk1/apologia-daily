The Titles of Jesus: What the New Testament's Names for Christ Reveal About Who He Claimed to Be

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

On a spring night in Jerusalem, a prisoner stood before the high priest. The case against him was collapsing; the witnesses contradicted one another. Then the high priest put a question that cut to the heart of the matter: "Are you the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?" The prisoner answered, "I am, and you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power, and coming with the clouds of heaven" (Mark 14:61–62). The high priest tore his robes and cried blasphemy.1 Whatever else that scene tells us, it tells us this: in the first century, the names a man claimed for himself could be a matter of life and death. The titles of Jesus are not decorative honorifics added by later piety. They are load-bearing. To understand who the New Testament says Jesus is, we have to understand what these titles meant to the people who first heard them.

The Question, Stated Carefully

The claim under examination is not that any single title, by itself, proves the deity of Christ. That would be a clumsy argument, and the honest scholar abandons it quickly. Several of Jesus' most important titles are, in their native setting, thoroughly human — royal, but human. The real claim is cumulative: that when the major christological titles are read in their first-century Jewish context, and read together, they place Jesus in roles, and finally under a name, that reach into the unique identity of the God of Israel. We will examine each title on its own terms, give the strongest objections their full weight, and then ask what the evidence does — and does not — establish.

Son of Man: The Misleading Title

"Son of Man" is the strangest of the titles, and Jesus' favorite self-designation — it appears roughly eighty times in the Gospels, almost always on Jesus' own lips, and almost never as a confession by others. To modern ears it sounds like a statement of humility: "son of man" simply means "a human being," as in Ezekiel, where God repeatedly addresses the prophet as "son of man" (Hebrew ben adam). And that ordinary sense is real. But the decisive background lies elsewhere — in a vision in Daniel 7.

There the prophet sees "one like a son of man, coming with the clouds of heaven." This figure approaches the Ancient of Days and receives something staggering: "to him was given dominion and glory and a kingdom, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him; his dominion is an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away" (Daniel 7:13–14).2 This is no mere mortal. He comes on the clouds — imagery the Hebrew Bible associates with God's own appearing (Psalm 104:3; Isaiah 19:1). He receives universal, everlasting dominion, and the service of all nations. In the Aramaic of Daniel, the verb translated "serve" (pelach) is one used elsewhere in the book for religious service rendered to God.3

This is the background Jesus invokes at his trial. By fusing Daniel 7:13 ("coming with the clouds") with Psalm 110:1 ("seated at the right hand"), Jesus claims not merely to be the Messiah but to be the heavenly figure enthroned beside God. Exactly why this counted as blasphemy is itself debated — Darrell Bock argues it was the perceived arrogation of a divine prerogative, taking a seat enthroned in God's presence, rather than the bare messianic claim — but blasphemy was the charge that stuck.4 "Son of Man," far from being the humble title, may be the most exalted of all.

The Casey Objection

Here the strongest challenge must be heard. The late Maurice Casey of Nottingham, one of the finest Aramaists of his generation, argued that the whole "Son of Man" edifice rests on a mistranslation. In Aramaic, he contended, the phrase bar (e)nash(a) was simply a generic idiom — a roundabout way of saying "a man," or "someone," sometimes used by a speaker to refer obliquely to himself. On this reading, when Jesus said "the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head," he was using ordinary Aramaic for "a man like me has nowhere to lay his head." The titular, apocalyptic "Son of Man" is then a creation of the Greek-speaking church, which read a generic Aramaic idiom as a fixed title and tied it to Daniel.5 Casey's view, building on Geza Vermes, is learned and not lightly dismissed.

Yet it has met serious resistance precisely on the linguistic ground Casey claimed. Paul Owen and David Shepherd, surveying the Aramaic evidence, argued that the emphatic form bar enasha does not behave as Casey's general-idiom thesis requires, and that Casey's appeal to a "stable" Aramaic ranged uncritically over texts spanning a thousand years.6 Casey replied vigorously in print, defending his readings.7 The dispute is genuine and not fully settled. But two points tell against the deflationary reading even if Casey's idiom existed. First, the Gospels overwhelmingly use the doubly definite Greek form, ho huios tou anthrōpou — "the Son of the Man" — an oddly specific construction for a generic "someone." Second, several sayings tell against the fully deflationary reading: no ordinary man "comes on the clouds" to judge the nations (Mark 13:26). Casey himself assigns this Danielic clouds-saying to a secondary layer rather than to Jesus, so the point is not decisive against him; but for those who judge such sayings to be early, the most economical explanation is that Jesus deliberately took up Daniel's enigmatic figure as a veiled self-designation — veiled enough to invite reflection, pointed enough to convict him at his trial.

Messiah, Son of God, Son of David: The Royal Titles

Here honesty requires a concession that some apologists resist. "Messiah" (Hebrew mashiach, Greek Christos, "anointed one") did not, by itself, mean "divine." It meant God's anointed king — the heir of David who would restore Israel. The background lies in God's promise to David: "I will be to him a father, and he shall be to me a son" (2 Samuel 7:14), and in the royal coronation Psalm: "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" (Psalm 2:7). In this idiom, "Son of God" was a royal title. The Israelite king was God's adopted son; the nation itself is called God's son (Exodus 4:22). The Dead Sea Scrolls confirm that "Son of God" language could attach to an expected messianic figure without, in itself, asserting deity.8

"Son of David" carries the same freight: it names Jesus as the legitimate royal heir, the king of the line of David. None of these titles, taken in isolation, settles the question of divinity. A thoughtful first-century Jew could call a man "Messiah," "Son of David," even "Son of God" in the royal sense, while affirming the strictest monotheism. We must let the contested claim be labeled as contested: these titles are royal and human before they are anything more.

And yet the New Testament does not leave them where it found them. The Gospel writers press the royal sonship toward a unique, ontological sonship — Jesus is not merely a son but the Son, the "only begotten" (John 3:16), who knows the Father as no creature does (Matthew 11:27). The royal title becomes a vessel filled with more than royalty. The argument here is not that "Son of God" proves deity, but that the New Testament authors expand the title beyond its original banks.

Lord (Kyrios): The Most Loaded Word

If one title bears the heaviest weight, it is Kyrios, "Lord." On the surface it is ordinary — a term of respect, like "sir" or "master." But it had become something far more in Greek-speaking Judaism. In the Septuagint, the Greek Old Testament read across the synagogues of the Diaspora, the divine name YHWH was regularly rendered Kyrios. "Lord" had become the spoken stand-in for the unspeakable Name.

This is what makes the New Testament's use of Kyrios for Jesus so startling. Paul writes to the Romans: "if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord… you will be saved… For everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved" (Romans 10:9–13). That last line quotes Joel 2:32, where "the Lord" upon whose name one calls is YHWH himself. Paul takes a text about calling on YHWH and applies it, without apology, to calling on Jesus.9 Even more remarkable is 1 Corinthians 8:6, where Paul appears to take the Shema — Israel's bedrock confession, "the Lord our God, the Lord is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4) — and split it around two figures: "for us there is one God, the Father… and one Lord, Jesus Christ, through whom are all things." Richard Bauckham has argued influentially — though the reading is itself contested, not settled — that Paul is not adding Jesus alongside the one God as a second deity, but including Jesus within the unique identity of the one God of Israel.10

The Bousset Objection

The classic challenge to this evidence is now over a century old, but it remains the most formidable. In Kyrios Christos (1913), the German scholar Wilhelm Bousset argued that worship of Jesus as "Lord" did not arise among the first Aramaic-speaking disciples in Palestine. It developed later, he held, in the Hellenistic Gentile churches — at places like Antioch — where Greek-speaking converts, surrounded by the "lords" and divinized rulers of pagan cult, gradually elevated Jesus to a cultic "Lord."11 On this thesis, the high Christology of Kyrios is a secondary Greek import, not the faith of Jesus' first followers.

The decisive reply came from Larry Hurtado, whose Lord Jesus Christ (2003) set out to replace Bousset's book as the standard treatment.12 Hurtado's counter-evidence is a single Aramaic word. In 1 Corinthians 16:22, writing in Greek to a Greek city around AD 53–55, Paul preserves an untranslated Aramaic prayer: Marana tha — "Our Lord, come!" The word will not behave as Bousset's theory needs. An Aramaic invocation of Jesus as "Lord" (Mar), addressed to him in prayer, could only have originated among the earliest Aramaic-speaking believers in Judea — the very community Bousset claimed had no such devotion.13 The prayer reaches back behind Paul, behind Antioch, into the first Aramaic-speaking church. Devotion to Jesus as Lord is not late and not Greek. It is primitive and Jewish.

Logos, Last Adam, and the Divine "I AM"

Three further titles round out the picture. John opens his Gospel with the Logos, the "Word" who "was with God, and the Word was God," and who "became flesh and dwelt among us" (John 1:1, 14) — language drawing on both the creative word of Genesis and Jewish reflection on God's Wisdom. Paul calls Jesus the "last Adam" (1 Corinthians 15:45), the head of a renewed humanity, undoing in obedience what the first Adam undid in disobedience. And in John, Jesus speaks the absolute "I am" (egō eimi): "before Abraham was, I am" (John 8:58) — an echo of God's self-naming to Moses (Exodus 3:14) so provocative that his hearers picked up stones. These confirm the trajectory the other titles trace.

The Honest State of the Scholarship

Where does this leave us? Several debates are real and ongoing. The exact force of "Son of Man" in Aramaic remains contested among specialists; Casey's view, though a minority position, is not refuted to everyone's satisfaction. The dating and interpretation of every title can be argued. It is also true — and must be stated plainly — that titles develop: communities reflect, and meanings deepen over decades. No responsible scholar denies that the church's understanding grew. The question is whether that growth was the invention of something foreign to Jesus and his first followers, or the unfolding of something already present in seed. On the central matter — whether high devotion to Jesus was early or late — the scholarly tide has largely turned against Bousset and toward Hurtado, Bauckham, and Martin Hengel, who concluded that more happened christologically in the first twenty years after the crucifixion than in the next seven centuries of doctrinal history.14

Conclusion: What the Titles Do and Do Not Establish

The titles of Jesus do not give us a single knockdown proof of his deity, and we should not pretend otherwise. "Messiah" and "Son of David" are royal and human; "Son of God," in its first sense, is the language of Israel's kings. An argument that leaned on any one title in isolation would deserve to fail. What the titles establish is something subtler and, in the end, weightier: a cumulative pattern. Read together and in context, they show the first Christians — Jewish monotheists who would die before bowing to an idol — taking the heavenly figure of Daniel, the worship-language of Joel, the very confession of the Shema, and the unspeakable Name itself, and applying all of it to a crucified Galilean. They did this early, in Aramaic, in prayer. The titles do not merely decorate Jesus. They locate him inside what Israel reserved for her God. That is not the whole case for who Jesus is. But it is a remarkable place for the evidence to begin.

Frequently asked questions

Doesn't 'Son of Man' just mean Jesus was calling himself an ordinary human being?

That ordinary sense is real; in Ezekiel God repeatedly addresses the prophet as 'son of man,' simply meaning a human. But the essay argues the decisive background is Daniel 7, where 'one like a son of man' comes on the clouds and receives everlasting dominion and the service of all nations. At his trial Jesus fused Daniel 7 with Psalm 110, claiming to be that enthroned heavenly figure, and the charge that stuck was blasphemy.

Isn't 'Son of Man' really a mistranslation of a common Aramaic phrase for 'a man'?

The essay honestly presents this challenge from Maurice Casey, building on Geza Vermes, that the Aramaic bar enasha was a generic idiom for 'someone,' turned into a fixed title by the Greek church. It is learned and not fully settled. But Owen and Shepherd pushed back on linguistic grounds, the Gospels use an oddly specific doubly definite Greek form, and sayings about coming on the clouds fit no ordinary man, so the deflationary reading faces real resistance.

If 'Messiah' and 'Son of God' were just royal titles, how do they point to Jesus being divine?

The essay concedes plainly that these titles are royal and human first: 'Messiah' meant God's anointed king, and 'Son of God' was applied to Israel's kings, even confirmed by the Dead Sea Scrolls as attaching to a messianic figure without asserting deity. No single title in isolation settles divinity. The argument is cumulative: read together and in context, especially with 'Lord' quoting YHWH texts, the titles locate Jesus inside what Israel reserved for her God.
