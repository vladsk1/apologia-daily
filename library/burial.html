Was Jesus Given an Honourable Burial? Joseph of Arimathea and the Empty Tomb

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

The fact that has to come first

Before there can be an empty tomb, there has to be a tomb. This is so obvious it is easy to walk past, but it is the hinge on which a great deal turns. The dramatic claim at the centre of Christianity is not merely that the disciples saw something, nor merely that they came to believe Jesus was alive; it is that a particular body, laid in a particular grave on a Friday evening, was gone on Sunday morning. Strip away the grave and the claim has no anchor. The whole evidential weight of the empty tomb — that it was a known, locatable, checkable place — depends on a prior fact: that Jesus was buried somewhere identifiable, by someone identifiable. That prior fact is the burial.

It would be tempting to treat the burial as a piece of stage furniture, a transitional sentence between the cross and the resurrection. It is not. Among the events of the passion, the burial of Jesus by Joseph of Arimathea is, in the judgment of a strikingly broad range of critical historians, one of the most securely established things we know about him. This essay sets out why — and then meets head-on the most serious scholarly challenge to it, the claim that crucified men in Roman Judaea were not buried at all but left for the carrion birds or thrown into a pit. What happened on the cross is treated elsewhere in this library; what happened to the empty tomb is its own argument, picked up in the companion essay on the empty tomb. Here the question is narrower and prior: was Jesus given a real, known burial — and how do we know?

The argument, stated carefully

The case can be set out as a short chain:

Premise 1. The burial of Jesus by Joseph of Arimathea is attested in the earliest stratum of Christian tradition and across multiple independent sources.
Premise 2. Joseph of Arimathea, a named member of the Sanhedrin, is the kind of detail early Christians would not have invented.
Premise 3. Jewish law and well-attested first-century practice made the burial of an executed man in Jerusalem not only possible but expected.
Conclusion. Jesus was buried in a known, identifiable tomb — which is precisely what makes the later claim of an empty tomb a verifiable historical assertion rather than an unfalsifiable rumour.

Note what the conclusion does and does not say. It establishes a location and a person, not a miracle. The burial is the foundation; the resurrection is the building. One can grant every word of the argument below and still ask, separately, what became of the body. That is exactly as it should be.

Premise 1: the burial is early and multiply attested

The earliest reference to Jesus' burial is not in any Gospel. It sits in the pre-Pauline creed that Paul quotes in 1 Corinthians 15, a formula most scholars date to within a few years of the crucifixion: "that Christ died for our sins… that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day… and that he appeared" (1 Corinthians 15:3-5).1 Burial is one of the four load-bearing clauses of the oldest Christian confession we possess. (The case for that early date is made in full in the companion essay on the early creed.) The creed does not name Joseph or describe a tomb, but it establishes that "he was buried" was bedrock from the start — not a later embellishment but part of the original proclamation, set there to underline the reality of the death it followed.

The narrative detail comes in the Gospels, and here the attestation is unusually strong. All four Gospels record the burial by Joseph of Arimathea (Mark 15:42-47; Matthew 27:57-60; Luke 23:50-54; John 19:38-42).2 Mark's account is widely regarded as resting on an early, pre-Markan passion source — a connected narrative of Jesus' final days that circulated before Mark wove it into his Gospel. The German scholar Rudolf Pesch argued for an especially early passion tradition behind Mark; while his specific dating is debated, the broader point that Mark drew on a pre-existing passion narrative is widely held.3 John's burial account shows little sign of literary dependence on the Synoptics, which means the burial is not a single story copied three times but a memory surfacing in independent channels. Multiple independent attestation, layered on top of the creedal reference, is about as good as the sources of ancient history get.

It is the convergence that carries the weight, not any one line of it. The creed gives antiquity; Mark gives an early connected narrative; the four-Gospel spread gives independence. Take any single strand away and the others still stand.

Premise 2: nobody invents a Sanhedrist hero

The most powerful consideration in favour of the burial is the identity of the man who performs it. Joseph is introduced not as a disciple from Galilee but as "a respected member of the council" (Mark 15:43) — that is, of the Sanhedrin, the very body the Gospels portray as having handed Jesus over to Pilate.4 This is the criterion of embarrassment working at full strength. The early Christian movement had every motive to cast the Jewish leadership as the villains of the passion; the polemic against "the chief priests and the elders" runs through the tradition. Inventing a pious Sanhedrist who does the right thing by Jesus cuts directly against that grain. It complicates the clean story of official rejection by conceding that at least one member of the condemning council acted honourably.

The Catholic scholar Raymond Brown, whose two-volume The Death of the Messiah remains the most exhaustive study of the passion narratives and who was no apologist, put the logic plainly: a Christian fictional creation of a Jewish Sanhedrist who does what is right is, given the hostility of early Christian writing toward the Jewish authorities, almost inexplicable.5 The point is not that a forger could not have invented Joseph but that he had no incentive to, and strong incentive not to. Embarrassing details are remembered because they happened, not because they help.

Two corroborating features sharpen this. First, Joseph is named and placed — "Joseph of Arimathea," tied to a specific town. Ancient invented characters tend to be vague and generic; concrete identification is the texture of memory, and a named council member is a dangerous thing to fabricate before an audience that could, in principle, ask after him. Second, the Gospels make women the witnesses to where Jesus was laid (Mark 15:47). In a culture where Josephus could write that the testimony of women was not to be admitted on account of "the levity and boldness of their sex,"6 putting women at the tomb is the last thing a fabricator polishing his case would do. Both details point the same way.

Honesty requires a concession here. The criterion of embarrassment is a probabilistic tool, not a proof, and it has critics. Some scholars note that what embarrasses one community may not have embarrassed another, and that Luke and John soften Joseph into a secret disciple, which could mark a developing legend. That is a fair caution. But the trajectory runs the wrong way for the sceptic: the earliest form (Mark) gives us the most awkward version — a Sanhedrist, full stop — and the later Gospels work to relieve the embarrassment, not create it. That is the signature of a hard fact being managed, not a convenient fiction being born.

Premise 3: a burial was expected, not exceptional

This brings us to the decisive objection, and it deserves to be stated in its strongest form before it is answered.

The strongest objection: Crossan and the unburied dead

John Dominic Crossan, co-founder of the Jesus Seminar and one of the most influential critical voices on the historical Jesus, argues that the burial story is essentially fiction — "hope and prayer," not history. On his reading, Roman crucifixion was designed precisely to deny its victims burial: the body was left on the cross as a deterrent, exposed to scavenging dogs and birds, and what little remained was tossed into a shallow common grave.7 The horror of denied burial was part of the punishment. If Crossan is right, there was no tomb, no Joseph, and therefore nothing for an "empty tomb" to be empty of. The entire edifice collapses at the foundation. Roman literary sources do, in fact, describe crucified bodies left for the birds, and Crossan can cite them.

This is the serious version of the objection, and it cannot be waved away. Crucifixion was meant to humiliate, and in much of the empire the denial of burial was indeed routine. The honest reply is not to deny the general Roman practice but to ask whether first-century Judaea was an exception — and the evidence that it was is considerable.

The reply: Jewish law, Roman concession, and a nail in a heel bone

Three lines of evidence converge against the "eaten by dogs" reconstruction in the specific case of Jerusalem.

First, Jewish law made same-day burial of the executed a religious imperative. Deuteronomy commands that the body of a hanged criminal "shall not remain all night on the tree, but you shall bury him the same day… you shall not defile your land" (Deuteronomy 21:22-23).8 This was not a dead letter. Josephus, describing the Jewish reverence for burial, states that the Jews are so careful about these rites that even those condemned to crucifixion are taken down and buried before sunset.9 What Josephus establishes is the strength of the Jewish burial norm — the religious expectation that the executed be interred the same day — not a claim that Rome invariably complied; his statement is evidence of the practice the occupying power had reason to accommodate, especially at festival time, rather than proof that it always did. The philosopher Philo of Alexandria records that on festival occasions crucified bodies could be taken down and given to relatives for burial — a clemency he mentions precisely because a brutal prefect had violated it, which itself shows the norm could be honoured or breached.10 Together they attest that the practice the Gospels presuppose was a genuine and weighty feature of the Jewish world that the authorities had strong reason to respect.

Second, the political situation made a Roman concession plausible. Burial of the executed required the governor's permission, and the Gospels say Joseph asked Pilate for it (Mark 15:43-45). This fits what we know of Roman administration in Judaea, where, especially around Passover with the city swollen and tense, governors had reason to avoid gratuitously offending Jewish religious sensibilities. Roman law itself, as the jurist Ulpian preserved it, allowed that the bodies of the executed were normally to be granted to any who sought them for burial.11 A request from a wealthy, respectable councilman, on the eve of a Sabbath and a festival, is precisely the kind of request a prudent prefect would grant.

Third — and this is the hard archaeological datum — we possess physical proof that at least one crucified man in first-century Jerusalem was given a proper Jewish burial. In 1968, excavators at Giv'at ha-Mivtar on the northern edge of Jerusalem found an ossuary inscribed "Yehohanan," containing a heel bone with an iron crucifixion nail still driven through it; the nail had bent against a knot in the wood and could not be removed, so it went into the grave with him.12 Here is a man crucified under Roman rule in the very city and roughly the very period of Jesus — and his bones were collected by his family, placed in an ossuary, and laid in a rock-cut tomb in the normal Jewish way. The single most concrete piece of evidence we have shows the opposite of Crossan's general rule operating in the particular place that matters.

Crossan's reconstruction describes the empire at large; it does not describe Jerusalem under the constraints of Jewish law, Roman pragmatism, and the festival calendar. The Gospels' burial is not historically anomalous. It is what the convergence of law, politics, and archaeology would lead us to expect.

A more careful sceptic: McCane and the shameful burial

It would be too easy to leave the impression that every critic denies the burial. The most interesting position is more subtle. The archaeologist and historian Byron McCane accepts that Jesus was buried but argues that it was a dishonourable burial — not the loving interment by a devoted follower the Gospels imply, but the hasty, shameful disposal in a criminal's tomb that Jewish law required for the executed, without mourning rites.13 On this reading the Gospels have softened a disgrace into a kindness.

What is striking is that McCane's challenge actually strengthens the core claim of this essay. He is disputing the manner of the burial, not its reality. Indeed, a shameful burial is, if anything, a more securely historical datum, because it is even less likely to be invented: a movement proclaiming its Lord's vindication would not gratuitously fabricate his being dumped in a criminals' plot. Whether Joseph acted out of full discipleship (as John says) or out of pious duty to keep the land undefiled (as the bare logic of Deuteronomy would suggest), the load-bearing fact survives intact: Jesus was placed in a specific, known tomb. And a known tomb — honourable or shameful — is all the empty-tomb argument requires.

Why the burial is the foundation

With the three premises in place, the structural payoff comes into view. The burial does at least three jobs for the larger resurrection case, and each depends on the body's resting place being publicly known.

First, it certifies the death. A body was not released for burial until the executioners were satisfied their work was done. The burial is, in effect, the Roman and Jewish authorities' public sign-off that Jesus was dead — which is why the much later "swoon" theory, on which Jesus merely fainted, founders on the burial as much as on the medical facts.

Second, it fixes the location. A tomb provided by a specific, named, traceable councilman means the resting place of the body was not a secret. That is what converts the resurrection claim from a private vision into a public, checkable assertion. If the tomb was known, then the question "is it empty?" had a determinate answer that interested parties — the authorities, the disciples, curious residents of a small city — could in principle go and settle.

Third, it sets the evidentiary stage. The earliest Jewish counter-explanation reported in the tradition is not "there was no tomb" or "you have the wrong grave" but "his disciples came and stole the body" (Matthew 28:13).14 Whatever one makes of that polemic, it presupposes a known, empty tomb whose emptiness both sides took for granted and were arguing about. A vague or anonymous disposal could generate no such dispute. The burial is what gives the later argument something concrete to be about.

The state of the question

Where does the scholarship actually stand? On the bare fact of the burial, the consensus is broad and crosses confessional lines. Raymond Brown, in his magisterial two-volume The Death of the Messiah, judged the burial by Joseph of Arimathea historical and defended it at length.15 The archaeologist Jodi Magness, surveying the physical evidence in Stone and Dung, Oil and Spit, concludes that the Gospel accounts of Jesus' burial are largely consistent with the archaeological and literary evidence for Jewish burial practice of the period — while making the sober point that Joseph most plausibly provided his own tomb as an emergency measure before the Sabbath, rather than a tomb reserved for the wealthy being lavished on a condemned man.16

The live dissent is real but narrower than it first appears. It runs along two fault lines. Crossan and a minority deny any burial at all; the weight of Jewish-law evidence, the Josephus and Philo testimony, and the Yehohanan remains tell heavily against them, and most critical scholars have not followed. McCane and others grant the burial but contest its honour; that debate, as we saw, leaves the foundational fact standing. Two claims should be kept distinct here. That Jesus was buried is not seriously contested by the mainstream of the field; on that bare fact, sceptics and believers largely agree. That the tomb was known and locatable — the further step the empty-tomb argument needs — is, in my judgment, better supported than its denials, but it is the genuinely debated point, and it should be defended as such rather than smuggled in under the consensus that covers the burial itself.

What the argument does and does not establish

Clarity about scope is owed to the reader. The argument from the burial establishes that Jesus was really dead and was really laid in an identifiable tomb by an identifiable man. It does not, by itself, establish that the tomb was later found empty — that is a further claim, resting on its own evidence and answered in its own place. Still less does it establish the resurrection, which is an inference drawn from the empty tomb, the appearances, and the transformation of the witnesses taken together. The burial is the first stone of the arch, not the keystone.

But it is genuinely the first stone, and a sceptic who hopes to dissolve the whole resurrection case by denying the tomb at the outset has, on the present evidence, chosen the worst possible place to make a stand. The convergence of an early creed, multiple independent narratives, the un-inventable figure of a Sanhedrist named Joseph, the explicit testimony of Jewish law and of Josephus and Philo, and a literal nail in a literal heel bone from first-century Jerusalem makes the burial one of the most solid facts the historian of Jesus has to work with. It is worth holding, as Peter is remembered to have urged, "with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15) — confident in what the evidence shows, honest about where the next question begins.

Frequently asked questions

Weren't crucified people just left on the cross for animals, so Jesus was never really buried?

That was the general Roman practice, and John Dominic Crossan builds his case on it. But first-century Judaea was an exception. Deuteronomy required same-day burial of the executed, Josephus says even crucified men were taken down before sunset, and a crucified man named Yehohanan was found buried in a Jerusalem ossuary with the nail still in his heel. Jewish law, Roman pragmatism at Passover, and archaeology all point to burial.

Why do scholars think the Joseph of Arimathea burial story wasn't just invented by Christians?

Joseph is introduced as a member of the Sanhedrin, the very body the Gospels blame for handing Jesus over. Early Christians had every reason to villainize that council, not to invent a pious member who honours Jesus. Raymond Brown, no apologist, called such a fictional creation almost inexplicable. The later Gospels soften the awkwardness rather than create it, the signature of a hard fact being managed.

Does proving Jesus was buried prove the resurrection?

No, and the essay is careful about this. The burial establishes a location and a person, not a miracle. It certifies Jesus was really dead, fixes a known tomb, and gives the later empty-tomb dispute something concrete to argue about. You can grant every point and still ask, separately, what became of the body. The burial is the first stone of the arch, not the keystone.
