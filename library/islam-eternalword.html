The Eternal Word Dilemma: Created or Uncreated?

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

In the year 833, the dying caliph al-Ma'mun—son of Harun al-Rashid, patron of the great translation movement that had poured Aristotle and Galen into Arabic—sent a letter from the Byzantine frontier ordering his governor in Baghdad to put the empire's scholars to a test. They were to be summoned, one by one, and asked a single theological question. Those who answered wrongly could lose their posts, their freedom, and, in the years that followed, very nearly their lives. The question was not about God's existence, nor His unity, nor His law. It was this: Is the Quran created, or uncreated?1

It is hard, at first, to see why a question so abstract could be worth an inquisition. Yet for the better part of two centuries it convulsed Islamic intellectual life, sent a revered traditionist to the flogging-post, and forced classical Islam to forge one of its most subtle and consequential doctrines. The episode is called the Mihna—literally the "testing" or "ordeal"—and the answer Islam eventually settled on opens a door that few Muslims and few Christians have walked all the way through. For in resolving the dilemma, mainstream Sunni theology committed itself to a claim about God's eternal Word that lies remarkably close to the very logic Christians use to confess Christ, the eternal Word who "was with God, and was God" (John 1:1). This essay tries to tell that story fairly, to state the most sophisticated Muslim resolution at full strength, and then—gently, in the spirit of 1 Peter 3:15—to ask what it concedes.

Stating the Question Carefully

The dispute turns on the divine attribute of kalam—speech. All parties agreed that God speaks; the Quran says so repeatedly, and recounts God addressing Moses directly (Surah 4:164). The question was about the metaphysical status of that speech as embodied in the Quran. Is the Quran, as God's speech, an eternal reality that has always subsisted in God and never came into being? Or is it something God brought into existence—created in time, as the heavens and the earth were created?

Nothing small hung on the answer. If the Quran is uncreated and co-eternal with God, then there is something eternal "alongside" God—and the gravest worry of Islamic theology is precisely that of compromising tawhid, the absolute oneness of God, by admitting a second eternal. If, on the other hand, the Quran is created, then the book a Muslim holds as the literal speech of God becomes, in some sense, a creature—and the warmth of devotion to it cools into something less than divine. Each horn threatened something the faith could not surrender. That is what made it an ordeal.

The Mu'tazila: Createdness in Defense of Oneness

The party that pressed the doctrine of the created Quran were the Mu'tazila, the great rationalist theologians of early Islam. They are easy to caricature as cold rationalists, but their motive here was deeply pious. Their two governing principles were the absolute unity of God (tawhid) and His justice ('adl), and it was the first that drove their position on the Quran.2

The Mu'tazila reasoned that to call God's speech eternal and uncreated is to set up a second eternal being beside Him. Anything genuinely eternal is, by definition, uncaused and self-subsistent—the very marks of deity. To grant the Quran that status, they held, was to fall into a disguised polytheism, a kind of shirk dressed in scripture. Indeed they generalized the worry: the Mu'tazila were reluctant to affirm a set of distinct, eternally subsisting divine attributes at all, lest each become a co-eternal alongside God. God's knowledge, power, and speech, they argued, must be identified with His simple essence, not treated as eternal realities "in" Him.3 On this logic the Quran, as a determinate arrangement of Arabic words revealed at a moment in history, must be among the things God created. It was, to them, the only conclusion that kept God strictly one.

Al-Ma'mun found this congenial—though, as the historian of Islamic thought must note, he was not himself a thoroughgoing Mu'tazilite; he did not share their characteristic teaching on free will, agreeing with them chiefly on this one doctrine.4 In 833 he made the createdness of the Quran the official creed of the state and instituted the test. It ran on, under his successors al-Mu'tasim and al-Wathiq, until roughly the middle of the ninth century.

Ibn Hanbal and the Uncreated Word

The resistance found its hero in Ahmad ibn Hanbal (d. 855), the Baghdad traditionist whose collections of hadith made him one of the most respected men of his age. Hauled before the inquisitors under al-Mu'tasim, he refused to confess that the Quran was created. He was, by the standard accounts, imprisoned and flogged, and he would not yield.5

Ibn Hanbal's objection was not philosophical subtlety but a refusal to say of God's own speech what may only be said of creatures. The Quran calls itself the speech (kalam) of God. God's attributes—His knowledge, His power, His speech—belong to Him eternally; He was never without them. To say His speech was created is to say there was a time when God did not speak, a time when this attribute was absent from Him—and that, to Ibn Hanbal, impugned the perfection and eternity of God Himself. Better to confess the Quran uncreated and accept the mystery than to make God's word a thing He once lacked.6 His endurance turned him into a symbol of fidelity, and when the political winds shifted—the caliph al-Mutawakkil reversed the policy around 848–851—it was Ibn Hanbal's position, not the Mu'tazila's, that emerged as the touchstone of Sunni orthodoxy. The Mu'tazila, by contrast, were gradually marginalized; their school did not survive as a living force in Sunni Islam.7

The Ash'ari Resolution — Stated at Its Strongest

Victory for "uncreated," however, left the Mu'tazila's original worry standing. If the Quran is eternal and uncreated, how is it not a second eternal beside God? The strict literalist might simply refuse the question. But the towering achievement of classical kalam—the systematic theology developed above all by Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari (d. 936), himself a former Mu'tazilite, and his school—was to answer it with real philosophical care. The Ash'ari resolution deserves to be stated at full strength, because a reply aimed at a crude version persuades no one who holds the refined one.

The Ash'aris drew a distinction that the Mu'tazila had not. God's speech, they said, is of two kinds. There is kalam lafzi—"verbal" or "lettered" speech: the actual Arabic sounds, letters, ink, and recitation of the physical Quran. This, they conceded, involves created things; the sounds I make when I recite, the paper and ink of the codex, come into being in time. But beneath and prior to this stands kalam nafsi—God's "inner" or "self" speech: a single, eternal, undivided meaning subsisting in the divine essence, without letters, without sound, without sequence.8 It is this—the eternal inner speech—that is the uncreated Word of God. The Arabic Quran is its expression in time; the eternal meaning it expresses has always been with God.

This let the Ash'aris hold both horns at once. Against the Mu'tazila: the Word of God is genuinely eternal and uncreated, so God was never without His speech. Against the charge of a second god: the eternal Word is not a being separate from God, nor a partner alongside Him; it is an attribute subsisting in His essence. And here the Ash'aris reached for a formula of striking precision, the same formula they applied to the divine attributes generally: the attribute is "neither He nor other than He" (la huwa wa la ghayruhu)—not simply identical with the bare essence, yet not a separate thing standing outside it.9 God's eternal speech is distinct from His knowledge and His power, really an attribute and not merely a name, and yet it does not divide the divine being or add a second eternal entity to the count of gods. There is one God, eternally possessed of His one eternal Word.

One should appreciate how careful this is. It is not crude polytheism, and it is not a verbal dodge. It is a disciplined attempt to say that God can have something eternal and uncreated that is truly His, truly distinct from other things we predicate of Him, and yet does not multiply deities. That is a genuine intellectual accomplishment, and a Christian ought to say so plainly before saying anything else.

The Dilemma This Opens

Now the parallel comes into view—and it is best introduced not as a Christian invention but as something a Christian living under early Islamic rule noticed at once. John of Damascus (d. 749), a Greek-speaking theologian who had served in the Umayyad administration before becoming a monk, devoted the final chapter of his catalogue On Heresies to what he called "the heresy of the Ishmaelites." There he pressed exactly this point: the Muslims, he observed, accuse Christians of "associating" by confessing the divinity of the Word and the Spirit—yet their own scripture calls Christ the "Word of God" and a "Spirit from Him." If God's Word and Spirit are eternal in God (as God's perfections must be), then the one to whom those titles belong shares God's eternity; and if the Muslims insist the Word is created, they make God—at some point—wordless and spiritless.10 John was arguing this before the Mihna had even crystallized the doctrine; he saw the logic latent in the categories themselves.

The Ash'ari resolution sharpens his point rather than blunting it. Consider what mainstream Sunni Islam now affirms:

  1. God has an eternal, uncreated Word.
  2. That Word is genuinely distinct—an attribute, not merely another name for the bare essence.
  3. That Word is "neither God nor other than God."
  4. Yet there is only one God; the eternal Word does not make two.

Set beside that, listen to the prologue of John's Gospel: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God" (John 1:1). The Word is with God (distinct), and is God (not other than God), and there is one God. The Christian doctrine of the eternal Son—"begotten, not made," in the words of the Nicene Creed, of "one substance with the Father"11—is the claim that God's eternal Word is genuinely distinct yet genuinely God, without there being two gods. That is, structurally, the very thing the Ash'ari is at pains to say about the eternal kalam nafsi.

The dilemma, then, can be put as a question rather than a taunt: if an eternal, uncreated Word that is "neither God nor other than God" can subsist with the one God without violating His oneness when that Word is a book, what principled reason remains for saying it must violate His oneness when that Word is the person of Christ? The Muslim who has lived through the Mihna and embraced its orthodox outcome has already accepted the category that the central objection to Christ—"an eternal Word with God would be a second god"—declares impossible. Islam's own hardest-won doctrine supplies the grammar of the doctrine it most rejects.

The Muslim Replies, Answered at Length

A thoughtful Muslim—and the literature is full of them—will not concede the point easily, and the strongest replies must be given their due.

First: "Word" said of Jesus does not mean what "Word" means of the Quran. This is the standard and best response. When the Quran calls Jesus "a Word from God" (Surah 4:171), the classical commentators (Surah 3:47, 3:59) take it to mean that Jesus was brought into being by God's creative command "Be!" (kun)—he is the result of God's word, not the eternal Word itself. So the title marks Jesus as a creature uniquely brought about by divine fiat, not as a sharer in God's eternity.12 This distinction is fair, and a Christian should grant it as the Muslim's considered position rather than pretend the two uses of "Word" are obviously the same.

Yet granting it leaves an asymmetry that is hard to rest in. The same scripture that demotes Jesus to a creature-spoken-into-being also singles him out, as it singles out no other prophet, with the titles "a Word from Him" and "a Spirit from Him." The honor of "the eternal uncreated Word" is reserved for a book; the man the Quran marks with that very vocabulary is reduced to a thing God merely commanded into existence. The reply works only by suppressing the resonance the text itself sets up. The Christian need not insist the Quran teaches Christ's deity—it plainly does not—but may gently ask why the title bears eternal weight in one case and none in the other.

Second: the uncreated Quran is an attribute, not a person—so it is nothing like the Trinity. This is the deeper reply, and it is correct as far as it goes. The Ash'ari eternal Word is an impersonal attribute (kalam), not a distinct hypostasis who knows, loves, and can be addressed. The Christian Son is a person—one who can say "I," who prays to the Father, who became incarnate. The categories are genuinely different, and any honest account must concede the difference. The parallel is not identity; the eternal kalam nafsi is not a Trinitarian person, and to claim Islam secretly teaches the Trinity would be both false and unfair.

But notice precisely what the reply concedes in order to work. It concedes that Islam affirms an eternal, uncreated, really-distinct divine reality that is "neither God nor other than God"—and then argues this is acceptable because it is an attribute rather than a person. The defense, in other words, is not that an eternal Word-with-God is impossible (the original Mu'tazilite worry, and the standard popular objection to Christ); the defense is that this particular eternal Word-with-God happens not to be personal. That is a far narrower claim. It abandons the sweeping principle that any eternal alongside God multiplies gods—a principle Christians are constantly told their doctrine of the Son violates—and replaces it with a fine-grained dispute about whether the eternal Word is personal or impersonal. The Muslim has moved from "an eternal Word with God is impossible" to "an eternal Word with God is fine, so long as it is not a person." The objection to Christ does not survive that move intact; it has been quietly traded for a different and much more modest one.13

Third: this is mere wordplay—exploiting a coincidence of vocabulary. The charge has some sting, and a Christian should be careful not to deserve it. The argument is worthless if it leans only on the happy accident that both faiths use the phrase "Word of God." But the real point is not lexical; it is structural. Both faiths face the same underlying problem: how can the one God have a Word that is genuinely His—genuinely eternal, genuinely distinct—without multiplying gods? Christianity answers with the eternal Son. Islam, in its mainstream Ash'ari form, answers with the eternal, uncreated, "neither He nor other than He" kalam. Both have judged that an eternal Word in God does not entail a second deity. The Christian is not punning; he is observing that Islam's own theologians, under the pressure of their own scripture, built precisely the category they tell Christians is incoherent.

The Honest State of the Question

Several things must be said plainly, lest the argument be overstated—which is the surest way to make it useless.

First, the parallel is an argument, not a claim about what Islam teaches. Islam does not teach the Trinity, does not confess Christ as God, and would regard any suggestion that it does as offensive. The contention is narrower and purely logical: that the orthodox Sunni doctrine of the uncreated Quran undercuts one specific and very common objection to the deity of Christ—the objection that an eternal Word existing with God must be a second god. It says nothing, by itself, about whether Christ is in fact divine; that case rests elsewhere, on the historical evidence of his life, claims, and resurrection.

Second, the experts who would dissent should be named. Many Muslim theologians—and some careful Western scholars of kalam—will insist that the attribute/person distinction is not a dodge but the whole ballgame, and that the disanalogy between an impersonal attribute and a divine person is decisive enough to dissolve the parallel entirely. The Mu'tazila themselves, were they still a living school, would reject the Ash'ari resolution outright and might tell the Christian: we agree the uncreated-Word doctrine is dangerously close to your Trinity, which is exactly why we denied it—take it up with the Sunnis, not with us. That is a fair retort, and it locates honestly where the dispute lives: the parallel bites against mainstream Ash'ari Sunnism, not against every conceivable Islamic theology. The hinge of the whole argument—whether "distinct eternal attribute" and "distinct eternal person" are near enough in structure for the one to illuminate the other—is genuinely contested, and should be presented as a serious consideration, not a knock-down proof.14

Third, the Ash'ari achievement should be honored even by those it does not persuade. The doctrine of kalam nafsi is a model of theological seriousness: faced with two unacceptable extremes, it carved out a careful middle, refusing both to make God once-wordless and to make His Word a rival deity. Christians, of all people, should recognize that move—for the doctrine of the Trinity was forged under the same pressure, refusing both to deny the Son's deity and to confess two gods. The two traditions are, at this point, wrestling with the same mystery: the eternal Word of the one God.

Conclusion: What the Dilemma Does and Does Not Establish

The Mihna was real history, paid for in real suffering, and its outcome is no marginal opinion but the settled creed of Sunni Islam: the Quran is the eternal, uncreated speech of God, an attribute "neither He nor other than He," with God from eternity and yet not a second god. In reaching that conclusion, classical Islam conceded the very category its sharpest objection to Christ declares impossible—that an eternal, uncreated Word can subsist with the one God without dividing His oneness.

This does not prove the Trinity, and it does not show that Islam secretly believes it. The deep disanalogy between an impersonal attribute and a divine person remains, and it must be conceded; whether Christ is the eternal Word made flesh is settled by the evidence of the Gospels, not by Islamic dogmatics. What the dilemma does establish is more modest and still significant: the common Muslim objection that "an eternal Word alongside God would be a second deity" is one that mainstream Islam, on its own deepest reflection, does not actually believe—because it affirms exactly such a Word. The two faiths are not arguing about whether the one God has an eternal Word. They are arguing about what that Word is: a book sent down, or a Person who "became flesh and dwelt among us" (John 1:14). And that—asked with gentleness and respect—is among the most fruitful questions a Christian and a Muslim can share.
