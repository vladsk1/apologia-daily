Does the Bible Predict Muhammad? The Prophecy Claims Examined

Apologia Daily · deep dive · fully sourced

Short on time? Read the quick answer →

The Qur’an makes a remarkable, checkable claim. Twice over it tells Muhammad’s hearers that he was no novelty—that the Jewish and Christian scriptures already spoke of him. He is “the unlettered prophet, whom they find written in what they have of the Torah and the Gospel” (Surah 7:157, Sahih International).1 And in Surah 61:6 Jesus himself is made to announce the coming of “a messenger to come after me, whose name is Ahmad”—Ahmad and Muhammad being two forms built on the same Arabic root, both meaning “praised.”2 If those verses are right, the Bible should contain a recognizable portrait of the prophet of Islam.

This is one of the most fruitful conversations a Christian and a Muslim can have, precisely because it is so concrete. It does not turn on philosophy or on contested ancient documents. It sends both parties to texts anyone can open and read. The proper response is therefore not to dodge the verses but to read them—carefully, in context, and in their original languages. When that is done, a consistent pattern emerges: each passage, taken on its own terms, points somewhere other than seventh-century Arabia, and the central ones point to Christ. The aim of this essay is to show that pattern fairly, giving the Muslim arguments their full strength before answering them, in the spirit of 1 Peter 3:15—“with gentleness and respect.”

How Old Is This Argument?

The search for Muhammad in the Bible is nearly as old as Islam itself. The earliest surviving Muslim attempt to find a New Testament proof-text appears in the Sira of Ibn Isḥāq (d. 150/767), the oldest biography of Muhammad. There Ibn Isḥāq renders Jesus’ words about the coming “Paraclete” from John’s Gospel, transliterating the key word as al-munaḥḥemana—and glossing it as “Muhammad.”3 The historian Sean W. Anthony has shown that Ibn Isḥāq was working from a Christian Palestinian Aramaic version of John, in which the Greek paraklētos had become the Aramaic menaḥḥemānā (“the comforter,” also evoking menaḥem, “comforter/consoler,” a messianic title in some Jewish texts).4 From this eighth-century seed, later Muslim writers assembled a fairly standard list of biblical “announcements” of Muhammad, which is still in circulation today. We will take the major ones in turn.

Deuteronomy 18: “A Prophet Like Moses”

The flagship text is Deuteronomy 18:15–18. Moses tells Israel: “The LORD your God will raise up for you a prophet like me from among you, from your brothers—it is to him you shall listen.” God then confirms it: “I will raise up for them a prophet like you from among their brothers. And I will put my words in his mouth, and he shall speak to them all that I command him” (ESV).5

The Muslim case, at full strength

The Muslim argument here is thoughtful and deserves to be stated without caricature. It has two parts. First, the phrase “from among their brothers.” Israel’s “brothers,” on this reading, are the Ishmaelites—the descendants of Abraham’s other son. The Bible itself describes Ishmael’s line as dwelling “over against all his kinsmen” (Genesis 25:18), so an Arab prophet, a cousin rather than a son of Israel, fits the wording naturally. Second, and more forcefully, the criterion “like Moses.” The Muslim points out that Muhammad resembled Moses far more closely than Jesus did: both Moses and Muhammad were lawgivers who brought a comprehensive code; both led their people in war; both married and had children; both died natural deaths and were buried; both began as ordinary men, not as divine figures. Jesus, by contrast, founded no state, fought no battles, and—on the Christian account—was no mere prophet but the incarnate Son. So if we are asked who, in history, was “like Moses,” Muhammad seems the more obvious answer.6

The reply: the text’s own criteria

The reply is not to dismiss these observations but to test them against what Deuteronomy actually says. Take “brothers” first. The word for “brothers” (Hebrew aḥim) is used throughout Deuteronomy for fellow Israelites, and the decisive parallel sits only one chapter earlier. In Deuteronomy 17:15 God commands that Israel’s king must be “one from among your brothers… You may not put a foreigner over you, who is not your brother” (ESV).7 The very same phrase, in the very same context, is explicitly defined to exclude the foreigner. To read “your brothers” as Ishmaelite Arabs in chapter 18 while it plainly means non-foreign Israelites in chapter 17 is to make the word mean opposite things in adjacent paragraphs. The plainer reading is that the coming prophet, like the coming king, would be an Israelite.

Now “like Moses.” The Muslim list of similarities—lawgiver, general, husband—is real, but it is a list the text never supplies. Deuteronomy gives its own criteria, and they are different. The prophet would have God’s words put in his mouth and speak in God’s name (18:18–19); Jesus says exactly this of himself: “I have not spoken on my own authority, but the Father who sent me has himself given me a commandment—what to say and what to speak” (John 12:49, ESV).8 More striking still is the book’s own closing verdict. Deuteronomy 34:10–11 ends: “there has not arisen a prophet since in Israel like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face, none like him for all the signs and wonders that the LORD sent him to do” (ESV).9 The two marks of a Moses-like prophet are intimate knowledge of God and confirming miracles. These are not incidental to the Christian case: the Gospels present Jesus as doing precisely such signs, whereas the Qur’an itself records that Muhammad’s contemporaries challenged him for a confirming sign and were told none would be given of that kind (see Surah 6:37; 17:90–93). The Qur’an presents itself as the supreme sign, and later tradition does attribute miracles to Muhammad (Surah 54:1, for instance, is read by some as the splitting of the moon); so the point is the narrow one—that the Qur’an does not present Mosaic-type confirming sign-miracles, not that Muhammad is said to have worked no wonders at all.10

Finally, the New Testament settles the question of its own intended referent. In Acts 3:22 Peter quotes Deuteronomy 18 and applies it directly to Jesus; in Acts 7:37 Stephen does the same.11 Whatever one makes of those identifications, they show that the earliest readers of this prophecy—Jews steeped in the Hebrew text—heard it as pointing to a single coming Israelite prophet, the Messiah, not to an Arabian one six centuries later.

Here, honesty requires a concession. Critical scholars such as the Jewish commentator Jeffrey Tigay read Deuteronomy 18 in its first sense not as a single end-time prophet at all, but as a standing institution—a promise that God would always raise up legitimate prophets for Israel, in contrast to the pagan diviners condemned in the surrounding verses.12 If that is the primary meaning, then the passage was not originally a prediction of one future individual. But notice that this reading helps the Muslim case no more than the Christian: a promise of an ongoing line of Israelite prophets, defined against foreign practice, is the last text to fit a non-Israelite. The Christian claim is the more modest and defensible one—that the immediate context and the New Testament’s own application converge on Jesus—not that the verse is a mathematical proof.

The Paraclete of John 14–16

The second great proof-text is Jesus’ promise, in his farewell discourse, of “another Helper”—in Greek, paraklētos. “I will ask the Father, and he will give you another Helper, to be with you forever, even the Spirit of truth” (John 14:16–17, ESV).13 Surah 61:6, with its prophecy of “Ahmad,” has long been read by Muslims as Jesus foretelling this Helper—Muhammad.

The Muslim case, at full strength

Two versions of the argument exist, and the stronger one should be put first. The straightforward version simply notes that the Paraclete is described as a person who is sent, who teaches, who testifies, who will “convict the world concerning sin” (John 16:8)—language that, the Muslim argues, reads more naturally of a coming man than of an impersonal influence. Jesus even says, “if I do not go away, the Helper will not come to you” (John 16:7), suggesting a successor.14 The bolder, linguistic version is the one associated with the convert ʿAbd al-Aḥad Dāwūd (formerly David Benjamin Keldani) in the 1920s: that the original Greek was not paraklētos (“comforter, advocate”) but periklytos—“famed, renowned, praised”—which is a precise Greek equivalent of Aḥmad, “the praised one.” On this view a Christian scribe, deliberately or by error, swapped periklytos for the similar-looking paraklētos, erasing Muhammad’s name from the text.15

The reply: the Greek and the description

Begin with the linguistic claim, because it is the easier to settle and the more often abandoned even by Muslim scholars. There is no manuscript—not one—in which the word periklytos stands in John. Every Greek copy of these verses reads paraklētos; so do the ancient Latin, Syriac, and Coptic versions and the patristic quotations.16 The proposal is a pure conjecture with zero textual evidence behind it. Worse, it is self-defeating in the context of the larger Muslim–Christian debate: to claim the text was altered is to grant that the Bible is corrupted—which is exactly the premise needed to deny that the Bible can foretell anything reliably at all. One cannot cite the Gospel of John as a true prophecy of Muhammad and in the same breath say John’s wording cannot be trusted. (We treat this tension at length in our companion essay on the Islamic Dilemma.)

The personal-language version is more interesting, and it is right that the Paraclete is spoken of as “he,” not “it.” But Jesus does not leave the identity open. He names the Helper four times over, and each time the same way. The Paraclete is “the Spirit of truth” (14:17); “the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name” (14:26); the one who “proceeds from the Father” (15:26).17 Three features of this Helper cannot be made to fit any seventh-century man, however praised. First, he is a Spirit, not a body. Second, his mode of presence is indwelling: “he dwells with you and will be in you” (14:17, ESV)—Muhammad did not live inside the disciples. Third, the timing is imminent and corporate: Jesus tells the eleven the Helper will come to them, and the risen Jesus dates it precisely—“you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit not many days from now” (Acts 1:5)—fulfilled at Pentecost in Acts 2, weeks later, not six centuries on.18 A figure who is a spirit, who indwells believers, and who arrives within days is not a description that an Arabian prophet of the 600s can satisfy.

The strongest critic here is not a polemicist but the historian Sean Anthony, whose work the Christian should engage rather than wave away. Anthony argues that Ibn Isḥāq’s identification was not a clumsy misreading but a sophisticated piece of late-antique exegesis: working from an Aramaic gospel in which paraklētos had become menaḥḥemānā, Ibn Isḥāq drew on a real stream of messianic expectation around the figure of the “Comforter” (menaḥem) and read Muhammad into it.19 The honest reply concedes the sophistication while denying the conclusion: the Aramaic menaḥḥemānā is itself simply a translation of “the Comforter,” the Holy Spirit; that a creative reader could later hear “Muhammad” in its consonants tells us about the ingenuity of the reader, not about the meaning of John.

The Shorter List: Maḥamaddim, Isaiah’s Servant, Haggai’s Desire

Beyond the two flagship texts, a cluster of shorter claims recurs, and they can be answered briefly and calmly.

Song of Songs 5:16. The bride says of her beloved, in Hebrew, kullo maḥamaddim—rendered “he is altogether desirable” (ESV).20 Because the consonants of maḥamaddim resemble “Muhammad,” some Muslims hear the prophet’s name hidden in the verse. But maḥamaddim is a common Hebrew plural noun meaning “delights, desirable things”; the -im ending is simply the Hebrew plural suffix, and the word appears elsewhere in ordinary contexts (for example Lamentations 2:4, of “all that was delightful”). It is a description in a love poem, not a proper name—rather as the English word “commander” contains the sound “command” without naming anyone. This is said not to mock the reading, which is sincerely offered, but to explain the grammar plainly.

Isaiah 42. Isaiah’s “servant” songs speak of a chosen one in whom God delights, who will “bring forth justice to the nations” (42:1, ESV).21 Some Muslims apply this to Muhammad as the prophet to the Gentiles. But Isaiah defines the servant’s manner in the next breath: “he will not cry aloud or lift up his voice… a bruised reed he will not break” (42:2–3)—a gentle, suffering figure, who in the fuller arc of Isaiah 52–53 is “pierced for our transgressions” (53:5, ESV).22 The New Testament identifies this servant as Jesus (Matthew 12:18–21 quotes Isaiah 42 directly of him), and the portrait of one who suffers and bears others’ sins fits the crucified Christ, not a victorious statesman.

Haggai 2:7. The King James Version reads, “the desire of all nations shall come,” and this “desire” is occasionally claimed for Muhammad.23 The claim rests on a translation choice. The Hebrew verb is plural (“they shall come”) while “desire” (ḥemdat) is singular, so most modern versions render it “the treasures of all nations shall come in” (ESV)—the wealth of the nations flowing into God’s house, not a person at all. And the context is the second temple, which God promises to fill with glory—a temple destroyed in AD 70, long before Muhammad. The verse cannot be about a seventh-century man without rewriting both its grammar and its setting.

Two Visions: Isaiah’s Riders and Daniel’s Stone

Two further passages come up less often than Deuteronomy 18 or the Paraclete, but both recur in written dawah, and both reward exactly the same discipline: read the vision through to its end, and it names its own subject.

Isaiah 21: the “rider on a camel”

The argument fastens on a single line in one of Isaiah’s night-visions. The prophet’s watchman is told to look for “riders on donkeys, riders on camels” (Isaiah 21:7).24 On the Muslim reading these are two prophets seen together: the rider on the donkey is Jesus, who entered Jerusalem on a donkey as all four Gospels record, and the rider on the camel is Muhammad, the prophet of the camel-riding Arabs who came after him. The pairing looks tidy—the Messiah and then the final Messenger, foreseen side by side in one verse—and it is offered sincerely.

The tidiness dissolves the moment the line is read with the ones around it. Isaiah 21 opens, “The oracle concerning the wilderness of the sea” (21:1), and it is a vision of the fall of Babylon. God tells the prophet, “Go, set a watchman; let him announce what he sees” (21:6). The watchman is posted to catch the first sign of an approaching army, and the riders—horsemen in pairs, some on donkeys and some on camels—are that army’s couriers, galloping in with news. What is the news? The watchman cries it out two verses later: “Fallen, fallen is Babylon; and all the carved images of her gods he has shattered to the ground” (21:9, ESV). The donkeys and the camels belong to one military scene—the messengers who announce that Babylon has fallen. They are not a timeline of two prophets six centuries apart; they are the mounts of a single report arriving on a single night.

Read in its own context, then, Isaiah 21 is about the collapse of an empire, and it says nothing about a future prophet at all—neither Jesus nor Muhammad is its subject. To lift one clause out of a Babylon oracle and hang a prophetic biography on it is to read past the very sentence the vision supplies as its own meaning.

Daniel 2: the statue and the stone

Daniel interprets Nebuchadnezzar’s dream of a great statue—head of gold, chest of silver, on down to feet of iron and clay—as a succession of kingdoms. Then “a stone was cut out by no human hand,” struck the statue, and “became a great mountain and filled the whole earth” (Daniel 2:34–35).25 Daniel explains that this represents a final kingdom God will set up, one that crushes the others and endures. The Muslim argument identifies that last, world-filling, God-given kingdom with Islam: it arose after the empires of the ancient world, spread across the earth with remarkable speed, and—on this reading—stands as the everlasting kingdom the vision foretold. Put at its strongest, the case points to something genuinely in the text: Daniel does describe a final kingdom, divinely established, that outlasts the rest and covers the earth.

What settles the matter is the two things Daniel says about that kingdom—both of which cut against the identification. The first is its timing. The kingdom of God in this vision arises “in the days of those kings” (Daniel 2:44)26—that is, during the era of the fourth and last empire of the statue, not centuries after it has passed. Interpreters divide over that empire’s identity: the traditional Jewish and Christian reading is Rome, while much critical scholarship, dating Daniel to the second century BC, reads it as the Greek (Seleucid) kingdom; some dispensationalists expect instead a future, revived Roman empire. But the disagreement gives the Islamic reading no foothold—on every one of these timetables the fourth empire is either the ancient one (Rome or Greece) or, for dispensationalists, a still-future revival of it, never a power first appearing in seventh-century Arabia. A caliphate founded in the 600s arrives generations too late for the ancient readings and centuries too early for the futurist one; it fits no window of “the days of those kings.” And even if one relocates the kingdom from the political caliphate to the faith it founded, that faith still arose in the seventh century—too late on the very same timetable.

The second is its permanence. Daniel 2:44 says this kingdom “shall never be destroyed… and it shall stand forever” (ESV). The Aramaic word behind “forever” (ʿalam) carries the sense of unbroken perpetuity; the standard lexicons gloss the Biblical Aramaic term as “perpetuity, antiquity,” and list Daniel 2:44 among its occurrences.27 Whatever kingdom the vision means, it is one that does not end. The historical caliphate does not answer to that description: it arose in the seventh century and was formally abolished in 1924.28 A kingdom with a datable beginning and a datable end—however vast in between—is not the kingdom “that shall stand forever.” So the verdict here, held to what the text shows, is again no: Daniel 2 does not describe a kingdom founded in seventh-century Arabia. On its own terms it points to a kingdom that begins while the ancient empires still stand and never falls—which is why Christians have long read the stone cut without human hands as the kingdom of God inaugurated in Christ, the one kingdom that fits both marks.

The Structural Problem Beneath the Whole Project

Step back from the individual texts, and a deeper difficulty appears—one of logic rather than language. To argue “the Bible foretells Muhammad,” one must treat the Bible as a reliable, God-given witness; otherwise its “prophecies” prove nothing. Yet the standard Muslim response to the embarrassing things the Bible actually does teach—Jesus’ deity, his crucifixion, his resurrection—is to say that the text has been corrupted (taḥrīf). These two moves sit in deep tension. A more sophisticated Muslim may distinguish textual corruption (taḥrīf al-naṣṣ) from corrupted interpretation (taḥrīf al-maʿnā)—holding that the words are intact but Christians misread them—which would let him cite the Bible as prophecy while rejecting its teaching on Christ. But that move only sharpens the asymmetry it hopes to escape: the alleged prophecies of Muhammad are supposed to be clear enough to recognize, while the Bible’s teaching on Jesus’ deity, crucifixion, and resurrection—stated far more plainly and far more often—would have to be so obscure that its first readers and every generation since misread it. A text lucid enough to foretell Muhammad is not opaque enough to hide the cross. This is not a debating trick but a genuine tension, and a charitable conversation can name it without hostility. It is the reason the prophecy argument, pressed hard, tends to lead a thoughtful Muslim back into the pages of the Gospels themselves—which is the best possible place for the conversation to go.

What This Does and Does Not Establish

Honesty requires marking the limits of the case. It does not prove that every Christian messianic reading of the Old Testament is beyond dispute; messianic interpretation involves real interpretive judgment, and serious Jewish and critical scholars read several of these texts differently from how Christians do. The argument of this essay is narrower and firmer: that, read in their own literary and historical context and in their original languages, none of the cited passages naturally refers to Muhammad, and the two load-bearing ones—Deuteronomy 18 and the Paraclete—are, on their own internal criteria, the worst possible fit for a non-Israelite man who was neither a spirit nor a worker of confirming signs. The New Testament writers, reading their own scriptures, applied them to Jesus.

So the honest answer to the question is no: the texts standardly advanced in support of the Qur’an’s appeal to the Torah and Gospel do not, on inspection, predict Muhammad—and are unsupported by any manuscript that has ever been found. That is a significant result, reached not by attacking Islam’s prophet but by doing the one thing the Qur’an itself invites: opening the Bible and reading what is written. For the Christian, the goal of that reading is never to win a point but to introduce a Muslim friend to the prophet like Moses who knew God face to face, the Spirit of truth who dwells within, and the suffering Servant pierced for our transgressions—to let a conversation that began about Muhammad become, gently, a conversation about Christ.

Frequently asked questions

Does the Bible actually predict Muhammad?

The Quran says Muhammad is found written in the Torah and Gospel, so the claim is concrete and checkable. But read in context and in their original languages, the standard texts point elsewhere. The two load-bearing passages, Deuteronomy 18 and the Paraclete of John, are on their own internal criteria the worst possible fit for a non-Israelite who was neither a spirit nor a worker of confirming signs. The New Testament applies them to Jesus.

Is the prophet 'like Moses' in Deuteronomy 18 a prediction of Muhammad?

The Muslim case, that 'from among their brothers' means Ishmaelite Arabs and that Muhammad resembled Moses as a lawgiver, deserves a fair hearing. But one chapter earlier, Deuteronomy 17:15 uses the same phrase to exclude foreigners and require an Israelite. Deuteronomy's own criteria are God's words in the prophet's mouth and confirming signs, and Acts applies the passage directly to Jesus, whom the earliest readers heard as its referent.

Was the 'Comforter' or Paraclete that Jesus promised actually Muhammad?

The claim that the Greek was periklytos ('praised,' matching Ahmad) rather than parakletos has zero manuscript support; every copy reads parakletos. It is also self-defeating, since claiming the text was altered grants the Bible is corrupted. Jesus names the Helper as the Spirit of truth who indwells believers and arrives within days at Pentecost, so the description points to the Spirit given at Pentecost rather than a prophet arriving six centuries later.
