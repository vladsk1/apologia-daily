What is the argument from desire?

It arrives unbidden — a bar of music, woodsmoke on an autumn evening — and the heart leaps toward something it cannot name. C. S. Lewis called it Joy, 'an unsatisfied desire which is itself more desirable than any other satisfaction,' and built an argument around it: 'If I find in myself a desire which no experience in this world can satisfy, the most probable explanation is that I was made for another world.'

Peter Kreeft tightened it into a syllogism: every natural, innate desire corresponds to a real object — hunger to food, thirst to water; there exists in us a desire nothing on earth can satisfy; so its object must exist beyond the world. The roots run back to Augustine's cry, 'You have made us for yourself, and our heart is restless until it rests in you.'

The objections are serious. Wanting something does not make it exist — people long to be immortal or to fly. Any defender must concede this at once: it was never a proof, and Lewis said 'most probable,' not 'necessary.' John Beversluis presses harder, charging that the 'natural' versus 'artificial' desire distinction is drawn precisely so the awkward cases get excluded. The honest reply grants the hard cases but holds where the desire is genuinely innate and species-wide, not a conjured wish.

What survives is quieter and, to many, more haunting. An evolutionary story explains why we keep striving, but strains to explain the specific shape of Joy — a homesickness for a place we have never been, that intensifies precisely when every earthly good is secured. Augustine did not argue that the restless heart proves God; he confessed that it was restless, and that he had finally found its rest.
