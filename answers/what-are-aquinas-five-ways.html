What are Thomas Aquinas's Five Ways?

✓Fact-checked & reviewed for Nicene orthodoxyHow we review →

Thomas Aquinas, the thirteenth-century theologian, offered five short arguments - his "Five Ways" - that reason from ordinary features of the world to the existence of God. They are not five versions of "everything has a cause," a common caricature. Each starts from something we observe and argues that it cannot be explained without a first, self-existent reality.

The first way, from motion: things in the world are being changed from potential to actual, and this cannot regress infinitely, so there must be an unchanged source of all change - what Aquinas calls an Unmoved Mover.

The second, from causation: we see chains of causes here and now, but a series of dependent causes needs a first cause that is not itself caused - otherwise nothing would be happening at all.

The third, from contingency: things that can exist or not exist do not have to be here. If everything were like that, at some point nothing would have existed, and nothing comes from nothing - so there must be a necessary being that cannot fail to exist.

The fourth, from degrees: we rank things as more or less good, true, and noble, which points to a maximum - a source of perfection by which everything else is measured.

The fifth, from the order of nature: unintelligent things consistently act toward ends, as if directed - which points to an intelligence ordering them, the way an arrow is aimed by an archer.

Aquinas is careful about what these show. They don't, on their own, prove every attribute of the Christian God - he builds those out afterward. Each argument concludes to a first, necessary, purely actual reality, "which everyone calls God."

Modern critics press these arguments - the third way's step from "each thing might not exist" to "everything together might fail at once" is the most contested - and defenders answer that Aquinas is talking about here-and-now dependence, not a chain stretching back in time. Whatever one makes of them, the Five Ways remain some of the most influential arguments in the history of thought - and a reminder that belief in God has a long tradition of careful reasoning behind it.
