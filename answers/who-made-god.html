If God made everything, who made God?

It is a fair and natural question, and it feels like it should stop the whole conversation. If everything needs a maker, then surely God needs one too, and the whole idea collapses into an infinite regress. Many thoughtful people have set the matter down right here.

But it rests on a small misunderstanding of what is actually being claimed. The argument for God from the universe's beginning never says everything has a cause. It says something narrower and more careful: whatever begins to exist has a cause. Things don't simply pop into being out of nothing; they come from something. The universe, the evidence suggests, began to exist, so it needs a cause beyond itself. God, by definition, did not begin to exist. He is eternal, without beginning. So he is not an arbitrary exception to the rule; he simply falls outside a rule that was only ever about things that begin.

Asking what made God is a bit like asking what the bachelor's wife is doing tonight. The question presupposes something the case has already ruled out. You are welcome to ask instead whether an uncaused, eternal being is coherent, or whether the universe might itself be eternal, and those are good questions worth pursuing. But once the claim is stated precisely, who made God stops being the knockdown it first appears to be. Whatever is the ultimate explanation of everything must, by its nature, be something that did not need to be made.
