Was Jesus sinless?

Yes - and it is a claim the earliest Christians made about him alone, one ancient literature almost never makes about anyone: that he was sinless. Paul writes that God "made him to be sin who knew no sin" (2 Corinthians 5:21); Hebrews describes one "in every respect tempted as we are, yet without sin" (4:15); Peter says he "committed no sin, neither was deceit found in his mouth" (1 Peter 2:22). What makes this remarkable is who rendered the verdict: Jews steeped in a tradition insisting that all have sinned, writing within a generation of the man they knew. And Jesus himself issued a challenge no honest person would dare - "Which one of you convicts me of sin?" (John 8:46) - to enemies who, at his trial, had to seek false witnesses because they could find no true charge.

This is both a historical claim and a theological confession, and it should be held honestly as both. The skeptic can press a real objection: we draw both the character and the claims from the same Gospels, so the argument leans on their broad reliability. That is fair - it is a cumulative consideration, not a freestanding proof.

But the sinlessness does genuine work. It makes the popular compromise - "a great moral teacher" who merely thought too highly of himself - hard to sustain. A man of penetrating psychological insight, who diagnosed self-deception in others and warned relentlessly against pride, being catastrophically self-deceived at the very center of his self-understanding, and leaving no other mark of it on a life his intimates judged blameless, is deeply improbable. The character fits the confession the Church makes: that this was no ordinary sage, but the Lord.
