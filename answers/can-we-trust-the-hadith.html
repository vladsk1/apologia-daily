Can we trust the hadith?

Most of what Islam practices — the five daily prayers, the rites of fasting and pilgrimage, the bulk of Islamic law, and nearly all we know of Muhammad's life — rests not on the Quran but on the hadith: reports of what the Prophet said, did, and approved. This is Sunni Islam's own self-understanding. So the honest question is whether the method used to certify authentic hadith can bear that weight.

The traditional science is genuinely sophisticated. Every hadith has a text (matn) and a chain of transmitters (isnad), and scholars built biographical dictionaries (the 'science of men') to vet each link's honesty and memory. Al-Bukhari (d. 870) reportedly sifted some 600,000 reports and kept only a few thousand. Modern isnad-cum-matn analysis, pioneered by Harald Motzki, has pushed the datable core of many traditions earlier than the old skeptics claimed.

But two facts frame the problem. The canonical collections were compiled two centuries after Muhammad, across civil wars and sectarian disputes in which a saying of the Prophet could settle any dispute — and the tradition itself admits forgery was rampant. A method that vets the chain is, by its own logic, weakest against a skilled forger, who supplies exactly a clean chain. Even the tradition's defender Jonathan Brown grants isnad criticism could not always catch a skillful fabrication.

The fair conclusion is modest, and asks only one even standard. The mass-transmitted core is well-insulated, but the certainty popularly claimed is harder to sustain across a two-hundred-year gap. By contrast, the gap between Jesus and the Gospels is measured in decades, and the creed of 1 Corinthians 15 sits within a few years of the events. We hold this out, with 1 Peter 3:15, in gentleness and respect.
