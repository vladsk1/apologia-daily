Has the Bible been changed over the centuries?

It's a common worry - a "game of telephone" over two thousand years, where the message degrades a little with every retelling until it bears no resemblance to the original. But that image misunderstands how manuscript copying actually worked, and the surviving evidence shows close to the opposite. The New Testament survives in over 5,800 Greek manuscripts, plus tens of thousands more in Latin, Syriac, Coptic and other early languages - by far the best-attested text of the ancient world.

The "telephone" picture assumes a single chain of copies, each made only from the one before it, with no way to check backward. Real transmission was nothing like that. Copies branched out in many directions and many regions, and earlier manuscripts were never destroyed when newer ones appeared. That means scholars are not stuck at the end of one fragile line; they can lay hundreds of independent copies side by side and compare them. Where they disagree, the disagreement itself usually reveals where and when a slip entered, so the change can be traced and corrected rather than silently passed down.

It would be misleading to pretend the copies are identical - they are not, and there are hundreds of thousands of differences across them. But the honest question is what kind of differences these are. The vast majority are spelling, word order, and obvious scribal slips; only a small fraction affect meaning at all, and of those, not one core Christian doctrine hangs on a disputed line. Even sceptical text critics grant this point. The Dead Sea Scrolls told the same story for the Old Testament: an Isaiah scroll a thousand years older than our previous copies matched them closely. None of this proves the Bible's claims are true - that is a separate question - but it does answer this one. The text was not corrupted beyond recovery; it was carefully preserved, and we can show our work.

Related questions

What do the Dead Sea Scrolls tell us about the Old Testament text?

Copied roughly between the third century BC and the first century AD, the scrolls pushed our oldest Hebrew manuscripts back about a thousand years. When the great Isaiah scroll was compared with medieval copies a millennium later, the text matched closely - differences confined to spelling and minor wording. Strong evidence of careful transmission, not rewriting.

What about passages like the ending of Mark or the woman caught in adultery?

Because the manuscript evidence is so rich, scholars can see that the longer ending of Mark (16:9-20) and the story in John 7:53-8:11 are missing from the earliest copies. Modern Bibles flag them in footnotes for exactly that reason. No doctrine depends on them, and the openness shows the text is studied transparently.

Wasn't the Bible changed at the Council of Nicaea?

No. The Council of Nicaea (AD 325) addressed how the Son relates to the Father and produced the Nicene Creed; it did not edit or rewrite the New Testament. We can verify this because manuscripts that predate Nicaea contain the same writings we read today. The story of Nicaea inventing Scripture has no historical basis.
