Does the Bible condone slavery?

No — not the chattel slavery the question has in mind, and the distinction that shows why is the one the objection usually skips. The engine of the transatlantic slave trade was kidnapping human beings to buy and sell them — and that is precisely what the Mosaic law made a capital crime (Exodus 21:16), while the New Testament lists 'enslavers,' or slave-traders, among the worst lawbreakers (1 Timothy 1:10). What the Old Testament law regulates for Israel is something different: a debt-servitude the desperately poor entered to survive, hedged by mandatory release (Exodus 21:2) and — strikingly for the ancient world — a ban on returning a slave who had fled for refuge (Deuteronomy 23:15-16).

And the Bible's own logic runs against the institution from the start. It opens by declaring that every human being bears God's image (Genesis 1:27); its founding national story is God freeing slaves out of Egypt; Paul tells a master to receive his runaway back 'no longer as a slave, but... as a beloved brother' (Philemon 16) and says that in Christ 'there is neither slave nor free' (Galatians 3:28). Those are depth charges laid under the whole system — which is why the people who led the fight to abolish slavery, like William Wilberforce, did so because they took the Bible seriously — and in the end it was their reading of it, not the slaveholders', that prevailed.

The honest hard case is Leviticus 25:44-46, which permitted Israel to hold foreign slaves as lasting property — real servitude, even without the kidnapping and racial ideology that drove the Atlantic trade. But a law that permits is not a law that commends: nowhere does Scripture call the practice good. It restrained an institution the whole ancient world took for granted, while laying down the very truths — every person an image-bearer, a God who frees slaves, a gospel in which there is 'neither slave nor free' — that its most serious readers would follow all the way to abolition. That is not a Bible that condones slavery; it is one that dismantles it from the inside.
