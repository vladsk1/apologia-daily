Does the Bible condone slavery?

Let's not soften this. The Bible never issues a flat 'thou shalt not own slaves,' and there are passages that regulate slavery rather than abolish it. For anyone who knows the horror of the transatlantic slave trade, that is genuinely unsettling, and a fair reader deserves honesty rather than spin.

Start with a crucial distinction. The engine of the Atlantic trade - kidnapping human beings to buy and sell them - is precisely what the Mosaic law made a capital crime (Exodus 21:16), and the New Testament lists 'enslavers,' or slave-traders, among the worst lawbreakers (1 Timothy 1:10). For fellow Israelites, what the law regulates is closer to indentured debt-servitude - a way for the desperately poor to survive a debt - hedged by mandatory release (Exodus 21:2) and, strikingly for the ancient world, even barring the return of a slave who had fled to Israel for refuge (Deuteronomy 23:15-16).

But honesty means naming the hardest text, not hiding behind that distinction. Leviticus 25:44-46 permitted Israelites to buy foreign slaves and hold them as lifelong, inheritable property - without the kidnapping or the racial ideology of the Atlantic trade, but real and permanent servitude all the same. That is a genuinely difficult passage, and pretending otherwise fools no one.

But follow the Bible's own logic forward. It opens by declaring that every human being - not just kings and masters - bears God's image (Genesis 1:27). Its founding national story is God liberating slaves out of Egypt. Paul tells a slave-owner to receive his runaway back 'no longer as a slave, but... as a beloved brother' (Philemon 16), and announces that in Christ 'there is neither slave nor free' (Galatians 3:28). Those are depth charges laid under the whole institution - and it's no accident that the people who finally led the fight to end slavery, like William Wilberforce, did so precisely because they took the Bible seriously. Scripture doesn't hand us a tidy abolition decree; it plants the truths that made slavery unlivable.
