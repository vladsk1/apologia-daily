Is the virgin birth credible?

✓Fact-checked & reviewed for Nicene orthodoxyHow we review →

The virgin birth is credible - a creedal affirmation resting on the independent testimony of two Gospels, held by faith with good reasons. It is honest to say up front that it is not the kind of claim that can be demonstrated like a theorem or investigated like the crucifixion; it is not a public event open to inspection. Saying that plainly is not a retreat; it is the only honest starting point, and it is where a careful skeptic and a careful believer can actually meet.

Within those limits, though, there are real reasons it carries weight. Matthew and Luke both attest a virginal conception while writing independently - they share almost no wording, yet agree on the core - which is the kind of double, uncoordinated testimony historians take seriously. The tradition is also early: Ignatius of Antioch, around 107 AD, already lists "the virginity of Mary and her giving birth" among the mysteries to be proclaimed, suggesting received tradition rather than late invention. And there is the mark of embarrassment: a pregnancy before marriage invited exactly the charge of illegitimacy that opponents would later throw, hardly a story the church would fabricate to burnish its founder.

Honesty also requires handling Isaiah 7:14 with care, and not overselling it. The Hebrew word there, almah, means a young woman of marriageable age, not specifically a virgin, and its immediate context is a sign to King Ahaz in the eighth century BC. The pre-Christian Greek Septuagint rendered it parthenos, "virgin," and that is what Matthew inherited and quoted. Best treated as typology - a window that illumines the claim - rather than a knockdown prediction. So the case is genuinely modest: real, converging weight from independent and early sources, but not decisive proof. It is affirmed as the Gospels' testimony and the church's doctrine, received by faith with reasons - and it is offered, not imposed.
