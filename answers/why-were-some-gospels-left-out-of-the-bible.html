Why were some gospels left out of the Bible?

It is a fair question, and the popular answer - that a Roman emperor or a church council simply voted the winners in and suppressed the rest - deserves a fair hearing before it is set aside. The early Jesus movement really was more diverse than a tidy picture suggests, there really were competing texts, and the group that became orthodox really did prevail. To pretend otherwise would be to ignore the evidence. So the suspicion that history is written by the winners is not baseless, and it should be granted its due.

But the decisive reason those other gospels were left out was not theology or politics; it was chronology. The four canonical Gospels date to the first century, within living memory of the events. The Gospel of Thomas, the Gospel of Peter, and the Gospel of Judas are second-century or later compositions - the Gospel of Judas, recovered and published in 2006, is generally dated to around the mid-second century, far too late to come from Judas. They borrow the names of apostles who had long since died. The test the early church applied was historical: is this writing genuinely close to the apostles, and has it been read across the churches? The four Gospels passed; the later texts did not.

Worth saying plainly: recognizing which books are early and apostolic is not the same as proving their contents true - that is a further argument, on further grounds. And these Gnostic texts are not worthless curiosities; they tell us much about the movements that produced them. But they are not first-century apostolic testimony, and that, not a suppression campaign, is why they sit outside the canon.
