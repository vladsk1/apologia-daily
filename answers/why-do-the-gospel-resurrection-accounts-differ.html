Why do the gospel resurrection accounts differ?

The gospels differ on the peripheral details of Easter morning — the number of angels, which women came, the order of the appearances — but converge on the core: Jesus was crucified, buried, his tomb found empty, and he appeared alive to his followers. Those surface differences are the fingerprints of independent testimony, not the seamless story colluding witnesses would agree on in advance.

The first thing to notice is what kind of differences these are. They are peripheral: how many angelic figures were seen, who arrived first, which appearance is mentioned. They are not disagreements about the core. All four gospels agree that Jesus was crucified, was buried, that his tomb was found empty by women, and that he was raised - with Matthew, Luke, and John narrating his appearances to his followers. The variation is at the edges; the center is rock-solid.

And that pattern is exactly what historians expect from genuine testimony carried in partly independent streams - Mark and John especially, since Matthew and Luke share material with Mark. If four witnesses to the same event gave word-for-word identical accounts, investigators would suspect collusion, not honesty. Real witnesses notice different details, compress timelines, and emphasize different things. The messy edges of the resurrection accounts are a mark of independent sources, not a mark of fabrication.

Many of the apparent conflicts also ease on a closer read. "Two angels" and "an angel" aren't a contradiction - a writer mentioning one of two isn't denying the other. Different women are named because different writers followed different threads of a larger group. These are the ordinary features of multiple people telling the same true story from different angles.

There's a deeper point too. If the early church had simply invented the resurrection, they had every opportunity to smooth out these details and present one tidy, harmonized account. The fact that they preserved four accounts with visible differences - rather than editing them into artificial agreement - suggests they were handing on testimony they received, not composing propaganda.

So the differences are real, and worth taking seriously. But they cut the opposite way from the objection: they point toward independent witnesses reporting a real event, all converging on the same central claim - the tomb was empty and Jesus was alive.
