Can we trust the New Testament manuscripts?

✓Fact-checked & reviewed for Nicene orthodoxyHow we review →

By any standard historians apply to ancient writings, yes - though not for the reason most often given. The New Testament is, by a wide margin, the best-attested text of the ancient world: it survives in more than 5,800 Greek manuscripts, plus tens of thousands more in Latin, Syriac, Coptic and other early languages, where most classical works come down to us in far fewer and much later copies. But the sheer number is not by itself the argument - a large pile of late copies would prove little on its own. What carries the weight is that some fragments sit within a generation or two of the originals (where a typical classical work has a gap of many centuries between its writing and our earliest copy), and that the copies fan out early across different regions, so the wording can be cross-checked and not merely counted.

Two things make this body of evidence powerful. The first is closeness in time: the smaller the gap between an event and the documents describing it, the less room there is for the record to drift. The second is multiplication. Because the text was copied so widely and so early, the manuscript lines branch out from different regions and centuries. A scribe in Egypt and a scribe in Italy could not coordinate their mistakes, so when their copies agree, that agreement points back to a common ancestor - ultimately the original. The abundance that sceptics sometimes cite as a problem is exactly what lets scholars triangulate the wording the authors first wrote.

The honest objection is that no two handwritten copies are identical, and the surviving manuscripts differ from one another in many places. That is true - but it is mostly a function of sheer volume, and the character of those differences matters far more than the count. The overwhelming majority are spelling variations, slips of the pen, and differences in word order that do not change meaning. Even a determined critic of the text like Bart Ehrman concedes that no essential Christian teaching hangs on a disputed reading. The question was never whether the text was copied - of course it was - but whether it was copied reliably enough to recover what the authors said. No one doubts what Caesar or Tacitus wrote on evidence far thinner than this. By the historian's own measuring stick, the New Testament passes, and passes well.
