Can we trust the New Testament manuscripts?

By any standard historians apply to ancient writings, yes. Compare the numbers: Caesar's Gallic Wars survives in about 10 copies, the average classical work in a few dozen to a few hundred. The New Testament survives in more than 5,800 Greek manuscripts, plus tens of thousands more in Latin, Syriac, Coptic and other early languages - by a wide margin the best-attested text of the ancient world. Some fragments sit within a generation or two of the originals, where most classical works have a gap of eight hundred to a thousand years between the writing and our earliest surviving copy.

Two things make this body of evidence powerful. The first is closeness in time: the smaller the gap between an event and the documents describing it, the less room there is for the record to drift. The second is multiplication. Because the text was copied so widely and so early, the manuscript lines branch out from different regions and centuries. A scribe in Egypt and a scribe in Italy could not coordinate their mistakes, so when their copies agree, that agreement points back to a common ancestor - ultimately the original. The abundance that sceptics sometimes cite as a problem is exactly what lets scholars triangulate the wording the authors first wrote.

The honest objection is that no two handwritten copies are identical, and the surviving manuscripts contain hundreds of thousands of differences between them. That is true - but it is mostly a function of sheer volume, and the character of those differences matters far more than the count. The overwhelming majority are spelling variations, slips of the pen, and differences in word order that do not change meaning. Even a determined critic of the text like Bart Ehrman concedes that no essential Christian teaching hangs on a disputed reading. The question was never whether the text was copied - of course it was - but whether it was copied reliably enough to recover what the authors said. No one doubts what Caesar or Tacitus wrote on evidence far thinner than this. By the historian's own measuring stick, the New Testament passes, and passes well.

Related questions

What is the earliest New Testament manuscript?

The earliest widely accepted fragment is Papyrus 52 (the John Rylands fragment), preserving a few verses of John 18 and usually dated to roughly the first half of the second century - within a lifetime of the original. Larger papyri like P66 and P75 preserve substantial portions of the Gospels from around AD 200.

Do textual variants mean we don't know what the New Testament said?

No. The large number of variants is a side-effect of having so many copies. The overwhelming majority are spelling and word-order differences, and even the sceptic Bart Ehrman grants that no essential doctrine rests on a contested reading. More copies give scholars more points of comparison, which helps recover the original wording.

Does reliable copying prove the New Testament is true?

No - and it is important to keep these separate. Manuscript evidence shows the text was accurately preserved, so we are reading what the authors actually wrote. Whether what they wrote is historically true is a different question, answered by early dating, archaeology, and outside witnesses like Tacitus and Josephus.
