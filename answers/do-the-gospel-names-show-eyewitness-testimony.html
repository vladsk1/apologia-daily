Do the names in the Gospels show eyewitness testimony?

They point powerfully toward it. Names are a watermark of time and place that outsiders find nearly impossible to fake, because writers are not thinking about them. The Israeli historian Tal Ilan catalogued every attested Jewish name in Palestine from 330 BC to AD 200; the New Testament scholar Richard Bauckham, in Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006), laid the Gospel names beside her tables. The fit is striking. The commonest male names in Palestine were Simon and Joseph - exactly the top two in the Gospels and Acts. The nine most popular names account for about 41.5% of men in the external data and about 40.3% in the New Testament. Crucially, the Gospels match Palestine specifically, not the very different diaspora profile (Eleazar, Dositheus, and more Greek names).

Most elegant is a behavior: because a few names were so over-common, real Palestinian Jews disambiguated people (Simon bar-Jonah, Joseph of Arimathea), and the Gospels apply this exactly to the over-common names - the unconscious habit of a culture that genuinely had too many Simons. Josephus, an independent sample, yields the same ratios.

Critics push back seriously. Gregor and Blais (2023) argue the sample is too small; a 2024 reply by Van de Weghe and Wilson found the fit at least as good as Josephus and far better than fiction, though the exchange continues. The Oxford scholar Christopher Tuckett grants the match but notes a well-read diaspora Jew might in principle mimic it. That is fair, and the maximal claim - proof of named eyewitnesses - remains contested. But the narrow conclusion stands firmly: the names carry the fingerprint of early first-century Jewish Palestine, which makes the "late Greek legend" theory very hard to maintain.
