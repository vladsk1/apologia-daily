Is Jesus God, or just a prophet?

'Just a prophet' is the view of Islam and of groups like the Jehovah's Witnesses, and it deserves a real answer - which the New Testament gives decisively. Across twenty-seven documents by some nine or ten independent authors, the writers do something the scholar Richard Bauckham calls including Jesus within the unique identity of the one God of Israel: he shares God's throne, God's work of creation, God's eternal name, and God's worship.

The hardest thing for the 'prophet only' reading to explain isn't a title - it is what the earliest Christians did. In the Hebrew Bible an emissary could bear God's authority; but no prophet, angel, or king ever received the worship reserved for God alone. In Revelation an angel twice refuses John's worship and redirects it to God - yet Jesus receives it without protest. Jesus forgives sins, prompting the objection, 'Who can forgive sins but God alone?' And the very first Aramaic-speaking church prayed to him: Marana tha, 'Our Lord, come!' - an invocation Paul preserves untranslated, carrying us back to the Jerusalem church of the 30s.

That is why the popular idea that Jesus' deity was a late invention does not fit the timeline. The most explosive evidence is also the earliest: the pre-Pauline Philippians hymn, the Aramaic prayer, the pattern of worship in place within a decade or two of the crucifixion. When Thomas touched the wounds and said 'My Lord and my God' (John 20:28), he was not inventing a category. He was naming what the whole New Testament had been saying.
