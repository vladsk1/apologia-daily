Do Muslims and Christians worship the same God?

This is a question people feel strongly about, and it deserves precision rather than a quick yes or no. Both faiths worship the one Creator God of Abraham, the maker of heaven and earth, and both are fiercely committed to his oneness. A Muslim who recoils from the Trinity is defending God's oneness, tawhid, and dreads the sin of shirk, associating a partner with God. That instinct is one Christians should honor, not ridicule, because they too worship one God and reject every rival deity.

Where the two genuinely differ is in what they believe that one God is like in his own inner life. Christians do not believe in three gods; the church has condemned that as heresy for nearly two thousand years. Nor, despite one Qur'anic passage naming God, Jesus, and Mary, has any Christian creed ever placed Mary in the Godhead, and Christians reject that idea as firmly as any Muslim would. The Trinity is not a partner added to God from outside; that would indeed be shirk. It is a claim about what the one God eternally is within himself: an eternal communion of Father, Son, and Spirit in a single, undivided essence. Even the word son is meant relationally, not biologically, Arabic Christians say ibn Allah, never walad Allah, which would imply a physical child that Christians deny as strongly as the Qur'an does.

So the fairest answer is that both aim their worship at the one true God, the Creator, while describing his inner nature in ways that really do differ. That difference is worth exploring honestly and gently, as a conversation between people who both take God's oneness with utter seriousness.
