Is the Mormon Jesus the same as the Jesus of the Bible?

It is a sincere and important question, and it deserves a careful, respectful answer rather than a slogan. The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints loves Jesus and centers its worship on him, and its vision gives the human person an almost breathtaking dignity and destiny. That should be honored, not mocked. But when we ask about the underlying doctrine of God, a real difference appears. In its own words, the LDS Church teaches that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three separate beings united in purpose, with the Father and the Son having tangible bodies of flesh and bone. Its most distinctive teaching, summarized in Lorenzo Snow's couplet, is that God the Father was once a man who progressed to godhood.

The historic Christian faith, shared by Catholics, Orthodox, and Protestants, confesses something different: one God, numerically one divine being, who exists eternally as three coequal, coeternal persons. He did not become God, and there was never a God before him. Isaiah's monotheism is sweeping here: before me no god was formed, nor shall there be any after me. So the disagreement is narrow and sharp: is God one being or several, and did God become God, or is he eternally and unchangeably God?

The honest conclusion is that the two share a name and a deep love for Christ, but describe him within genuinely different accounts of who God is. That is not a reason for contempt; it is a reason for the kind of clear, gentle conversation that takes the difference seriously precisely because the answer matters so much.
