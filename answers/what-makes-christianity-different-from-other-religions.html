What makes Christianity different from other religions?

It's popular to say all religions are basically the same path up the same mountain. But said charitably and accurately, the world's faiths make genuinely different and often contradictory claims about God, the human problem, and the solution - they cannot all be right, though they can all contain truth. Christianity stands apart in two specific ways.

First, it stakes everything on a historical event that could in principle be falsified. Most religions rest on the teachings or private experiences of a founder. Christianity rests on a public claim: that Jesus of Nazareth died and rose bodily, witnessed by named people, in a datable place and time. Paul himself says that if the resurrection didn't happen, the whole faith is false and worthless (1 Corinthians 15:14). No other major religion invites that kind of historical test of its central claim.

Second, it reverses the usual direction of religion. Broadly speaking, the religious impulse is about human beings climbing toward God - through rituals, moral effort, enlightenment, or law-keeping. Christianity says the opposite happened: God came down to us. Salvation is received as a gift of grace, not earned as a wage. As the saying goes, other systems spell religion "do"; the gospel spells it "done."

Those two features are connected. Because the rescue depends on what God did in history - the cross and the empty tomb - rather than on how well we perform, it can be offered freely to anyone.

This isn't a reason to treat people of other faiths with anything but respect; sincere seekers are found everywhere, and much wisdom is shared across traditions. But respect doesn't require pretending the differences don't exist. The honest question isn't "aren't they all the same?" but "which one is true?" - and Christianity is unusual in pointing to evidence in history and offering rescue as a gift rather than an achievement.
