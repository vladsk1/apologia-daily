Does the Big Bang point to God?

It is honest to answer this carefully, because the argument is easy to overstate. The Big Bang does not, by itself, prove that God exists, and any case that claims it does has reached too far. What the evidence supports - a convergence of independent lines never designed to test anyone's theology - is the narrower and still remarkable conclusion that the universe had a beginning; it has not simply existed forever.

From there, a further step of reasoning is available. Whatever begins to exist has a cause - things do not pop into being from nothing. So if the universe began, it has a cause. And the cause of all space, time, and matter cannot itself be spatial, temporal, or material; it would have to be timeless, immaterial, and enormously powerful. That is a striking amount to infer, and it converges with what believers have long meant by God. But notice the word "whatever begins to exist" - the argument does not claim everything has a cause, only what comes into being.

The skeptic's caution deserves respect: the earliest instant lies in a regime where our physics may break down, and even the theorem often cited for a cosmic beginning is a classical result whose co-author, Alan Guth, remains open to a universe with no beginning at all. So a beginning is best held as the more reasonable inference on present evidence, not a demonstration. The Big Bang points in God's direction and clears away the idea of an eternal, self-sufficient cosmos - but getting all the way to the Father Almighty takes more than physics can carry on its own.
