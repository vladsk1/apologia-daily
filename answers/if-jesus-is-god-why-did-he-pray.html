If Jesus is God, why did he pray to God and say he didn't know the day or hour?

This is one of the most honest and forceful objections there is, and it should be felt at full strength. Jesus prays. He obeys. He is sent. He says the Father is greater than I, and he confesses he does not know the day or the hour. How can someone who ranks himself below God, and is ignorant of what God knows, actually be God? Taken together these texts seem to hand us a subordinate being.

The classical Christian answer turns on the incarnation. Philippians 2 says the Son, being in the form of God, emptied himself by taking the form of a servant. Notice what the Greek does not say: it never says he emptied himself of his deity. There is no of anything in the sentence. It tells us how he emptied himself, by adding a genuine human nature, not by subtracting his divinity. The mystery is concealment, not loss: the one who was fully God veiled that glory beneath a servant's form, remaining what he was while becoming what he was not. So the praying, obeying, and not-knowing belong to his real human life and his freely accepted role as servant, not to an inferiority in the divine nature. This is not the ancient Arian idea that the Son is a lesser god.

And the same Jesus who says the Father is greater also says I and the Father are one. Greater speaks of order and role, not of a better nature. The picture that holds all the texts together is not a demoted deity, but God himself stooping to share our life, our limits, and even our death.
