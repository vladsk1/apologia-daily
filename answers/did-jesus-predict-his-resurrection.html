Did Jesus predict his own resurrection?

The Gospels report Jesus predicting, three times in Mark, that he would suffer, be killed, and rise again (Mark 8:31; 9:31; 10:33-34). He answers demands for a sign with the cryptic "sign of Jonah," and speaks of a temple destroyed and raised in three days. If genuinely uttered beforehand, a self-made forecast of resurrection is a striking thing.

But the strongest objection must be faced squarely, and it is the majority critical view: vaticinium ex eventu - "prophecy from the outcome." The Gospels were written decades later by believers who knew how the story ended, so the detailed predictions may well have been sharpened after the fact. Rudolf Bultmann and, today, Bart Ehrman press this hard. Honesty requires conceding a great deal: we cannot reconstruct Jesus' exact words, and the neatest predictions are the ones most vulnerable to hindsight.

Yet the deflationary theory has a soft seam. Invention explains polished predictions well but rough edges poorly - and these sayings are full of rough edges. Why leave the sign of Jonah a riddle the evangelists themselves interpret differently? Why preserve the disciples repeatedly failing to understand? Why let the temple saying stand as a hostile accusation at the trial rather than a triumphant creed? The criterion of embarrassment cuts toward authenticity. Even skeptical historians like Dale Allison grant that Jesus "possibly" foresaw his vindication, and E. P. Sanders ranks the temple episode among the most secure facts we have about Jesus. This is a supporting strand, not a standalone proof - it does not by itself demonstrate detailed foreknowledge. But what survives the sharpest criticism is real and early: the man who died on Friday seems to have walked toward it expecting God to vindicate him beyond the grave.
