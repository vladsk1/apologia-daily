What is abrogation (naskh) in the Quran?

Naskh, abrogation, is the Islamic doctrine that a later word from God can cancel an earlier one. It is not a Christian charge but a mainstream feature of Islamic jurisprudence, grounded in the Quran itself: "We do not abrogate a verse... except that We bring forth one better than it" (Surah 2:106). Classical jurists recognized three types - including the striking category in which a verse's very wording is withdrawn while its ruling remains, the traditional account of the "stoning verse" the caliph Umar said was once recited. Standard examples include the change of prayer direction from Jerusalem to Mecca and the gradual prohibition of wine.

The strongest Muslim defense is that this is not God correcting a mistake but legislating progressively for a changing community, as a wise physician prescribes different treatments in different phases. An obvious question follows - doesn't Christianity also move from an old covenant to a new one (Hebrews 8-10)? It does. But that observation only frames the real question; it does not establish a symmetry.

The tension is narrower and sturdier. The Quran makes maximal claims about itself: perfect consistency ("they would have found within it much contradiction," 4:82), unchangeable words (6:115), and perfect clarity - yet its most revered scholars could never agree how much is abrogated, ranging from over two hundred verses (Ibn Salama) to about twenty (Suyuti) to five (Shah Wali Allah) to zero (some modern Muslims). And the two covenant-shifts are not the same kind of change. Christianity locates its shift in a single public historical event, Christ, and preserves the older Scriptures entire; naskh is a difficulty internal to one book, delivered through one man in two decades, that claims seamless perfection of itself.
