Was Jesus a Muslim?

No — not in the sense the question intends. The religion of Islam is defined by the testimony that Muhammad is God's messenger and by the Quran, both from the seventh century, six hundred years after Jesus. Muslims do call Jesus (Isa) a 'submitter' to the one God, and in that broad sense he submitted to his Father perfectly — but 'submitter to God' is not the same as a follower of Islam. Jesus was a first-century Jew who never heard of Muhammad and kept the Torah, not Islamic law.

More decisively, the Jesus of the historical record did things no Muslim ever could: he forgave sins in his own name (Mark 2:5-7), accepted worship (John 20:28-29), and claimed to share the Father's glory (John 17:5) — which on Islam's own terms would be shirk, impossible for a prophet. A Muslim may reply that the Gospels were corrupted at exactly these points (tahrif) - but the divine-claim material sits in the earliest and best-attested strata, so excising only the inconvenient parts is special pleading. So either we trust the Gospels, and Jesus was no Muslim, or we discard them, and there is no historical 'Muslim Jesus' left at all. His submission to the Father was real — the submission of God the Son, who became human to save usThe Son's submissionIs sayingThe eternal Son, fully God, freely submits to the Father in his incarnate human obedience — the voluntary, loving order of the Trinity's outward work.Not sayingNot that the Son is lesser in being, rank, or divinity than the Father (the Arian / subordinationist error). His submission is that of the God who became man, not of a creature. (Philippians 2:6-8).
