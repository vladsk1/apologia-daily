Isn't Christianity just true because you were raised in it?

✓Fact-checked & reviewed for Nicene orthodoxyHow we review →

Where you're born shapes which beliefs you encounter, but not whether they're true - those are two entirely different questions. It's true that, statistically, most people's religion tends to track their birthplace - born in Pakistan you'd most likely be Muslim; in Thailand, Buddhist; in the American South, some kind of Christian - but that pattern is about how beliefs spread, not whether any of them is right.

The observation is true - but the conclusion doesn't follow, and here's the slip. This is what logicians call the genetic fallacy: where a belief came from tells you nothing about whether it's true. You probably believe the earth orbits the sun because you were raised in a scientific culture, not because you personally ran the experiment - yet that origin doesn't make it false. And the point cuts every direction: a skeptic's atheism is just as predictable from a secular upbringing. Taken seriously, the argument would quietly discredit every belief anyone holds anywhere, including itself. So we should concede that upbringing shapes belief - while denying that this settles what's true.

Which means the real question isn't where you started; it's what's actually the case - and there you simply have to examine the evidence. Christianity is unusual in staking everything on public, investigable claims of history: a real man crucified under Pontius Pilate, and an empty tomb his closest followers were willing to die defending. That's testable in a way 'you only believe it because...' is not. And it's worth noting the faith is spreading fastest today precisely where it isn't inherited - through adult conversion in places like China and Iran - often at real personal cost. As Scripture itself urges: 'test everything; hold fast what is good' (1 Thessalonians 5:21).
