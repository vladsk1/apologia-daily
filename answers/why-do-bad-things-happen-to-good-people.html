Why do bad things happen to good people?

Let's feel the full weight of it first. The kind neighbour gets the cancer diagnosis; the cruel one seems to sail through untouched. A child who never hurt anyone suffers. If God is just, this looks exactly backwards - and that's not a shallow complaint. It's the ache behind Psalm 73, where the writer nearly loses his faith watching the arrogant prosper while he keeps his heart clean for nothing.

Honesty comes first: there's no tidy formula that makes this add up, and anyone who offers you one is selling something. But notice one thing. The moment you say this is genuinely unfair - that the world ought to be otherwise - you've assumed a real moral order, a way things are supposed to be. A blind universe of atoms has no 'ought'; it can host pain, but not injustice. So the very outrage that fuels the question is quietly borrowing from a moral reality that fits a just God better than a godless world does.

Christianity's answer isn't a formula but a person, and a promise. God didn't watch our suffering from a safe distance; in Jesus, the one truly innocent man suffered the worst the world could do. And justice here is deferred, not denied - Scripture promises a day when God sets everything right and 'wipes away every tear' (Revelation 21:4). Job, the righteous man who lost everything, is never actually told why. But he is met by God, and that meeting is offered as enough. If you're the one hurting right now through no fault of your own, you are not being overlooked.
