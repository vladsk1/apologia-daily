Is there evidence that Jesus rose from the dead?

Yes - and the strongest evidence is also the earliest. In 1 Corinthians 15:3-7 the apostle Paul writes, "I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins... that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day... and that he appeared." Paul is quoting an earlier, formal creed that he himself had received. Most critical scholars - including sceptics such as the atheist New Testament professor Gerd Ludemann - date this confession to within roughly two to five years of the crucifixion, far too early to be a slowly growing legend. The bedrock claim of the resurrection is traceable to the first handful of years after the events, not to "decades later" as the worry usually goes.

From that early core, historians work with a set of facts that the large majority accept regardless of their own beliefs. Jesus was crucified under Pontius Pilate and was buried. His tomb was found empty (the most disputed item on this list). Many different people, in different times and settings, sincerely reported seeing him alive afterwards - including a sceptical brother, James, and an outright enemy, Paul, both transformed into bold witnesses. And the first disciples were willing to suffer and die rather than recant what they claimed to have seen. The debate among historians is usually not about whether these things happened, but about what best explains them.

This is where the alternatives have to be weighed fairly. Naturalistic theories each have a serious answer to give. Hallucination is the most discussed - but hallucinations are private experiences, and they do not fit group appearances or account for an empty tomb. Theft or a moved body does not explain why the disciples would knowingly die for what they knew to be false. Legend cannot do the work either, because the creed is dated too early for myth to have crowded out memory. Each theory can address one fact, but each strains to explain the whole set together. The resurrection, by contrast, accounts for the early creed, the empty tomb, the appearances, and the transformed witnesses simply and at once. That is why it remains, on the historian's own terms, the best explanation of the evidence. "He is not here; he has risen" (Matthew 28:6).

Related questions

How early is the evidence for the resurrection?

Earlier than most assume. In 1 Corinthians 15:3-7 Paul hands on a creed he had received - that Christ died, was buried, was raised, and appeared. Most critical scholars, including sceptics like Gerd Ludemann, date it to within about two to five years of the crucifixion. The core claim is traceable to the first years, not a late legend.

What are the naturalistic explanations, and why do they struggle?

The main alternatives are hallucination, theft of the body, and legendary development. Each can address a single fact but strains to explain the whole set. Hallucinations are private and do not fit group appearances or an empty tomb; theft cannot explain the disciples dying for it; legend cannot grow that fast given the early creed. The resurrection explains them together.

Did the disciples just die for a lie?

People die for sincere beliefs all the time, so martyrdom alone does not prove a claim true. The force is different: the disciples were in a position to know whether they had seen Jesus alive. They were not dying for a doctrine handed to them, but for something they claimed to have witnessed first-hand - and held to under lethal pressure.
