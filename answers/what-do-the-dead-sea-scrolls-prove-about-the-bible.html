What do the Dead Sea Scrolls prove about the Bible?

The Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered in caves near Qumran beginning in 1947, are among the most important manuscript finds ever made. They include copies of nearly every Old Testament book, some dated to around 250 BC to 68 AD - roughly a thousand years older than the manuscripts scholars had relied on before.

Their great significance is what they reveal about accuracy of transmission. The Great Isaiah Scroll, dated to about 125 BC, contains the entire book of Isaiah - and it is remarkably close to the medieval Masoretic text that came a thousand years later. The differences are overwhelmingly minor: spelling, word order, and obvious slips of the pen, with nothing that changes the meaning. That is powerful evidence that Jewish scribes copied the text with extraordinary care across the centuries.

This matters for a common objection: "the Bible has been changed so many times over the centuries that we can't know what it originally said." The scrolls directly answer it for the Old Testament - the text was not evolving beyond recognition; it was being preserved faithfully.

They also carry weight for prophecy discussions. Because the Isaiah scroll is firmly dated over a century before Jesus, no one can claim its messianic passages, such as Isaiah 53, were written or edited after the fact to fit him.

One crucial clarification, in fairness: the scrolls demonstrate that the text was accurately preserved - not that its contents are true. Those are two different questions. Showing that we can trust we have what Isaiah wrote is a separate matter from showing that what Isaiah wrote is God's word. The scrolls settle the first question impressively; the truth of the message is argued on other grounds.

So the Dead Sea Scrolls don't prove Christianity by themselves. What they do is remove a specific objection - that the Old Testament text is hopelessly corrupted - and replace it with strong evidence of careful, reliable transmission.
