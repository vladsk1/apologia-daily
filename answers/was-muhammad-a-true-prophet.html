Was Muhammad a true prophet?

First, a fair concession: Muhammad was a real historical figure. He existed, he preached in early-seventh-century Arabia, and he founded the movement that erupted out of Arabia within a few years of his death in 632. To deny that - to make him a myth - would badly overreach the evidence, and the scholarly revisionism that tried it in the 1970s was largely rolled back.

But testing a claim to prophethood means examining the sources, and here honesty is needed. Almost none of the vivid detail - the angel in the cave of Hira, the wording of particular sermons, the miracle reports - comes from the Quran, which says remarkably little about his life. It comes from the sira, the prophetic biography. The earliest, by Ibn Ishaq, was written roughly 120 to 135 years after Muhammad died, survives only through a later editor (Ibn Hisham) who tells us he left out what might 'distress' people, and rests on a tradition its own scholars did not treat as beyond question.

So the granular portrait should be held loosely. The honest plea is simply for a single, even standard applied to both faiths' founding sources. Apply it, and the New Testament - resting partly on eyewitness testimony, and behind it the creed of 1 Corinthians 15:3-7 datable to within a few years of the crucifixion - stands closer to its events than the sira stands to Muhammad. That is not a weapon to wield over anyone made, as we all are, in the image of God. It is an invitation to follow the evidence together, with gentleness and respect.
