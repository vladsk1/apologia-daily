The People Behind the Arguments

Apologists & Scholars

From C.S. Lewis to Alvin Plantinga — know the thinkers, their key arguments, and their best books. Being able to cite scholars by name gives your apologetics real credibility.

WC
William Lane Craig
1949 – present · American
Philosophy Theology Cosmology

Research Professor of Philosophy at Talbot School of Theology. The foremost Christian apologist of the late 20th and early 21st century. Has debated virtually every major atheist philosopher — Hitchens, Dawkins, Dennett, Kagan, Atkins, Krauss — and is widely regarded as having won most of them on argumentation.

Key arguments
Kalam Cosmological Fine-Tuning Resurrection Moral Argument Ontological
"The God of classical theism is not merely one more object in the universe, but the ground of being itself — the metaphysically necessary foundation of all contingent reality."
Essential books
  • Reasonable Faith (1984, 3rd ed. 2008) — his systematic apologetics
  • The Kalam Cosmological Argument (1979) — the definitive treatment
  • On Guard (2010) — accessible introduction to his arguments
  • God Over All (2016) — divine aseity and abstract objects
Evangelical · Molinism reasonablefaith.org →
AP
Alvin Plantinga
1932 – present · American
Philosophy Epistemology Logic

Emeritus Professor of Philosophy at the University of Notre Dame. Widely regarded as the greatest living Christian philosopher. His work on the modal ontological argument, the free will defence against the problem of evil, and his epistemology of religious belief (Warranted Christian Belief) transformed academic philosophy of religion.

Key arguments
Modal Ontological Free Will Defence EAAN Warranted Belief
"It is entirely right, rational, reasonable, and proper to believe in God without any evidence or argument at all." — Warranted Christian Belief
Essential books
  • God and Other Minds (1967) — established him as a major philosopher
  • The Nature of Necessity (1974) — modal ontological argument
  • Warranted Christian Belief (2000) — his epistemological magnum opus
  • Where the Conflict Really Lies (2011) — science, religion, and naturalism
Reformed · Calvinist Notre Dame
CL
C.S. Lewis
1898 – 1963 · British
Literature Philosophy Theology

Professor of Medieval and Renaissance Literature at Oxford and Cambridge. Former atheist who converted to Christianity after sustained argument with J.R.R. Tolkien and Hugo Dyson. The most widely read Christian apologist of the 20th century. His literary training gave him an unusual ability to detect whether texts read like genuine history or like legend — the Gospels, he concluded, read like the former.

Key arguments
Trilemma (Lord/Liar/Lunatic) Argument from Reason Argument from Desire/Joy Moral Argument
"A man who was merely a man and said the sort of things Jesus said would not be a great moral teacher. He would either be a lunatic or else he would be the Devil of Hell." — Mere Christianity
Essential books
  • Mere Christianity (1952) — the best introduction to Christian apologetics ever written
  • The Problem of Pain (1940) — theodicy and suffering
  • Miracles (1947) — includes the Argument from Reason
  • Surprised by Joy (1955) — his conversion autobiography
  • The Abolition of Man (1943) — moral objectivism
Anglican · Mere Christianity Oxford · Cambridge
GH
Gary Habermas
1950 – present · American
History Resurrection Theology

Distinguished Research Professor at Liberty University. The world's foremost expert on the historical evidence for the resurrection of Jesus. Has surveyed over 3,400 scholarly sources — including atheist and agnostic scholars — to develop his Minimal Facts methodology: building the case for the resurrection using only facts accepted by virtually all critical scholars regardless of theological commitments.

Key arguments
Minimal Facts Method Empty Tomb Resurrection Appearances Paul's Conversion
"The resurrection is not an article of faith that historians cannot touch. It is a historical event that meets the criteria historians apply to all ancient events — and it passes."
Essential books
  • The Case for the Resurrection of Jesus (2004, with Licona) — the standard work
  • The Historical Jesus (1996) — ancient evidence for the life of Christ
  • The Risen Jesus and Future Hope (2003) — resurrection and eschatology
Evangelical · Baptist Liberty University
NW
N.T. Wright
1948 – present · British
History Theology New Testament

Former Bishop of Durham, Research Professor at St Andrews. The foremost New Testament scholar of his generation. His eight-volume series Christian Origins and the Question of God includes The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003) — the most comprehensive historical investigation of the resurrection ever written, at 740 pages. A committed Anglican who takes both historical method and orthodox theology with equal seriousness.

Key arguments
Historical Resurrection Theological Novelty Jewish Context Empty Tomb
"The historian, whether or not a believer, is bound to say that the tomb was empty and that the disciples had experiences which they believed were appearances of the risen Jesus." — The Resurrection of the Son of God
Essential books
  • The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003) — the definitive historical treatment
  • Simply Christian (2006) — accessible introduction to Christianity
  • Surprised by Hope (2008) — resurrection and Christian hope
  • The New Testament and the People of God (1992) — historical background
Anglican · Historical-critical St Andrews · Durham
JL
John Lennox
1943 – present · Irish
Science Mathematics Philosophy

Emeritus Professor of Mathematics at Oxford University. Three earned doctorates. Has debated Richard Dawkins three times publicly — widely considered among the most effective Christian debaters on science and faith. His key insight: science and Christianity are not in conflict because science describes the mechanisms God uses; it cannot tell us whether God exists or what he is like.

Key arguments
Science & Faith Mathematical Universe DNA Information Big Bang
"The more I understand science, the more I believe in God." — responding to Dawkins's claim that science leads to atheism
Essential books
  • God's Undertaker: Has Science Buried God? (2007) — the science/faith interface
  • God and Stephen Hawking (2011) — response to The Grand Design
  • Gunning for God (2011) — response to the New Atheists
  • 2084: Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Humanity (2020)
Evangelical · Irish Oxford University
RB
Richard Bauckham
1946 – present · British
History New Testament Theology

Emeritus Professor of New Testament Studies at St Andrews. His Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006) transformed Gospel scholarship by demonstrating that the Gospels are rooted in specific named eyewitness testimony. His God Crucified (1998) established the "divine identity" framework — showing that the New Testament includes Jesus within the divine identity of the one God of Jewish monotheism without abandoning monotheism.

Key arguments
Eyewitness Testimony Naming Patterns Divine Identity High Christology
"The Gospels are not anonymous community products. They are rooted in the testimony of specific named eyewitnesses — people known to their communities and available for verification." — Jesus and the Eyewitnesses
Essential books
  • Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006) — landmark Gospel scholarship
  • God Crucified (1998) — divine identity and New Testament Christology
  • Jesus and the God of Israel (2008) — collected essays on Christology
Anglican · Reformed St Andrews
JM
J.P. Moreland
1948 – present · American
Philosophy Consciousness Theology

Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at Talbot School of Theology. A leading philosopher of mind and philosopher of science. His Consciousness and the Existence of God (2008) is the most comprehensive argument that the existence of consciousness is the single most powerful evidence for theism currently available. His work on the soul, substance dualism, and the philosophy of mind has been enormously influential.

Key arguments
Consciousness Substance Dualism The Soul Argument from Reason
"The existence of consciousness is not a minor puzzle to be resolved by future neuroscience. It is the central fact about the universe that any worldview must account for — and naturalism cannot." — Consciousness and the Existence of God
Essential books
  • Consciousness and the Existence of God (2008) — the argument from mind
  • Scaling the Secular City (1987) — comprehensive apologetics
  • The Soul (2014) — accessible treatment of the philosophy of mind
  • Kingdom Triangle (2007) — integrating mind, spirit, and kingdom
Evangelical · Biola University Talbot School of Theology
FT
Frank Turek
1961 – present · American
Philosophy Theology Apologetics

Co-author (with Norman Geisler) of I Don't Have Enough Faith to Be an Atheist — one of the best-selling apologetics books of the 2000s. Founder of CrossExamined.org. Known for making complex philosophical arguments accessible to general audiences, particularly on university campuses. His CRIMES acronym (Cosmology, Reason, Information, Morality, Evil, Science) provides a memorable framework for the cumulative case for theism.

Key arguments
Cumulative Case Cosmological Moral Argument Resurrection
"Atheism requires more faith than Christianity. To believe the universe came from nothing, life came from non-life, and consciousness from unconscious matter — without any cause — requires extraordinary faith in the improbable."
Essential books
  • I Don't Have Enough Faith to Be an Atheist (2004, with Geisler) — the best popular-level cumulative case
  • Stealing from God (2014) — why atheists need God to make their case
  • Hollywood Heroes (2022) — apologetics through film
Evangelical · CrossExamined crossexamined.org →
JP
John Polkinghorne
1930 – 2021 · British
Science Theology Physics

Former Professor of Mathematical Physics at Cambridge, then ordained as an Anglican priest. Fellow of the Royal Society. One of the most respected voices on the integration of science and faith in the 20th century. His key insight: the universe is not only rationally intelligible (the foundation of science) but also rationally beautiful — and both features are better explained by theism than naturalism.

Key arguments
Fine-Tuning Mathematical Universe Anthropic Fruitfulness Quantum Theology
"Science and theology are both searching for truth... The universe is not only rationally transparent — it is rationally beautiful, a fact that calls for explanation beyond mere physics."
Essential books
  • Belief in God in an Age of Science (1998) — his clearest statement
  • The Faith of a Physicist (1994) — systematic theology through a scientist's eyes
  • Science and the Trinity (2004) — Trinitarian theology and physics
Anglican · FRS Cambridge University
TM
Timothy McGrew
1965 – present · American
Philosophy Epistemology History

Professor and Chair of Philosophy at Western Michigan University. Expert in epistemology, philosophy of science, and historical evidence for Christianity. His entry on "Miracles" in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy and his Bayesian analysis of the resurrection are among the most rigorous academic treatments of these topics. Known for bringing the tools of formal epistemology to bear on historical questions about the Gospels.

Key arguments
Bayesian Resurrection Miracles Gospel Reliability Undesigned Coincidences
"The undesigned coincidences in the Gospels — small details that interlock in ways no collaborating authors could have planned — are among the most powerful evidences for their historical reliability."
Essential books
  • Hidden in Plain View (2017, with Lydia McGrew) — undesigned coincidences in the Gospels
  • The Gospel of John as Eyewitness Testimony (2019) — Johannine scholarship
Evangelical · Reformed Western Michigan University
ML
Michael Licona
1961 – present · American
History Resurrection New Testament

Associate Professor of Theology at Houston Baptist University. His The Resurrection of Jesus: A New Historiographical Approach (2010) — at 718 pages — is the most thorough application of standard historiographical methodology to the resurrection question ever published. Has debated leading sceptics worldwide including Bart Ehrman, John Shelby Spong, and Richard Carrier.

Key arguments
Historiographical Method Resurrection Appearances Paul's Testimony Minimal Facts
"Applying standard historiographical criteria to the resurrection — multiple attestation, enemy attestation, the criterion of embarrassment — the resurrection is the most plausible explanation of the relevant historical bedrock."
Essential books
  • The Resurrection of Jesus: A New Historiographical Approach (2010) — the magnum opus
  • The Case for the Resurrection of Jesus (2004, with Habermas) — popular level
  • Why Are There Differences in the Gospels? (2017) — genre and Gospel harmony
Evangelical · Southern Baptist Houston Baptist University
EF
Edward Feser
1968 – present · American
Philosophy Thomism Metaphysics

Associate Professor of Philosophy at Pasadena City College. The leading contemporary expositor of Thomistic-Aristotelian philosophy and natural theology. A former atheist, his conversion was driven by intellectual engagement with Aquinas. His book The Last Superstition is a sustained argument that the New Atheism is philosophically illiterate — it attacks a straw man of theism rather than the God of classical theology.

Key arguments
Thomistic Cosmological Act & Potency Pure Act Divine Simplicity
"The God the New Atheists attack is not the God of classical theism. Dawkins spends 400 pages refuting a God that Aquinas, Augustine, and Anselm would not have recognized." — The Last Superstition
Essential books
  • Aquinas (2009) — the clearest introduction to Aquinas's natural theology
  • The Last Superstition (2008) — against the New Atheism
  • Five Proofs of the Existence of God (2017) — Aristotelian, Augustinian, Thomistic, Leibnizian, Rationalist
Catholic · Thomist Pasadena City College
BE
Bart Ehrman
1955 – present · American
Sceptic New Testament Textual Criticism

James A. Gray Distinguished Professor of Religious Studies at UNC Chapel Hill. A former evangelical Christian who lost his faith and became an agnostic. The most prominent popular-level sceptic of New Testament reliability. Know his arguments well — you will encounter them. Importantly, he affirms the historical existence of Jesus, the crucifixion, and the disciples' genuine belief in the resurrection, while denying the resurrection itself.

Key objections he raises
Textual Variants Gospel Contradictions Problem of Suffering Hallucination Theory
"Jesus certainly existed... I don't think we can claim that the resurrection is a matter of historical knowledge." — Jesus, Interrupted (but he affirms the crucifixion and disciples' belief)
Key books to know (and respond to)
  • Misquoting Jesus (2005) — textual variants (responded to by Wallace, Metzger)
  • Did Jesus Exist? (2012) — actually defends Jesus's existence against mythicists
  • How Jesus Became God (2014) — sceptical Christology (responded to by Bird et al.)
Agnostic · Know to respond to him Sceptic
RD
Richard Dawkins
1941 – present · British
Sceptic Biology Atheism

Former Professor of the Public Understanding of Science at Oxford. The most famous popular atheist of the early 21st century. A brilliant biologist and science communicator whose philosophical arguments are generally considered weak by professional philosophers — even atheist ones. His "who designed the designer?" objection and his Boeing 747 gambit are important to understand and respond to. His debates with Lennox and McGrath are worth studying.

Key objections he raises
Who Designed the Designer? Boeing 747 Gambit Evolution Explains Design Meme Theory of Religion
"The God of the Old Testament is arguably the most unpleasant character in all fiction." — The God Delusion (this is rhetoric, not argument)
Key books to respond to
  • The God Delusion (2006) — responded to by Lennox, McGrath, Ward, Feser
  • The Blind Watchmaker (1986) — evolution and design (legitimate science)
  • The Selfish Gene (1976) — gene-centred evolution (legitimate science)
Atheist · Know to respond to him Sceptic

Science Philosophy Intelligent Design

  • Darwin's Doubt (2013) — the Cambrian explosion and animal origins

RC
Robin Collins
1961 – present · American
Philosophy Physics Fine-Tuning

Professor of Philosophy at Messiah University. The most rigorous philosophical developer of the fine-tuning argument. His version — using the likelihood principle and Bayesian reasoning — is considered the most sophisticated treatment of fine-tuning in the academic literature. He holds a degree in physics as well as philosophy, giving him the technical credibility to engage with multiverse proposals and quantum cosmology at a serious level.

Fine-Tuning Likelihood Principle Multiverse Response Boltzmann Brain

History New Testament Christology

DA
Douglas Axe
1961 – present · American
Science Biochemistry Intelligent Design

Director of Biologic Institute. His research on protein function — published in the Journal of Molecular Biology in 2004 — provides the most rigorous empirical estimate of the probability of a functional protein arising by random mutation: approximately 1 in 10^77. This research has become foundational for the design inference in biology. Formerly a researcher at Cambridge University's Medical Research Council Centre.

Key arguments
Protein Function Functional Sequences 1 in 10^77 Origin of Life
"The probability of a functional protein sequence arising by chance is approximately 1 in 10^77. If every atom in the observable universe ran an experiment every nanosecond since the Big Bang, this could not happen by chance." — Journal of Molecular Biology (2004)
Essential reading
  • "Estimating the Prevalence of Protein Sequences Adopting Functional Enzyme Folds" — J. Molecular Biology (2004) — the key paper
  • Undeniable (2016) — accessible treatment of design in biology
Evangelical · Biologic Institute

GC
G.K. Chesterton

Literature Philosophy Theology

Paradox of Christianity Moral Argument Tradition & Democracy Against Determinism
"The Christian ideal has not been tried and found wanting. It has been found difficult and left untried." — What's Wrong with the World (1910)
Essential books
  • Orthodoxy (1908) — his spiritual autobiography and apologetic masterpiece
  • The Everlasting Man (1925) — influenced C.S. Lewis's conversion
  • Heretics (1905) — critique of contemporary intellectual fashions
  • St. Thomas Aquinas (1933) — described by Etienne Gilson as the best book on Aquinas ever written
Catholic · Convert from Anglicanism

BP
Blaise Pascal
1623 – 1662 · French
Philosophy Mathematics Theology

Mathematician, physicist, and Christian philosopher. Inventor of probability theory and the mechanical calculator. His Pensées (Thoughts) — published posthumously from fragments — constitute one of the most searching explorations of the human condition and the case for Christianity ever written. His famous "wager" is often misunderstood — it is not an argument for belief but a psychological analysis of why intelligent people resist believing what the evidence already supports.

Key arguments
Pascal's Wager The Misery of Man Diversion (Divertissement) Argument from Desire
"There is a God-shaped vacuum in the heart of each man which cannot be satisfied by any created thing, but only by God the Creator, made known through Jesus Christ." — Pensées
Essential books
  • Pensées (1669, posthumous) — the greatest French apologetics work
  • The Provincial Letters (1656-57) — brilliant polemical writing
Catholic · Jansenist 17th Century
AS
Anselm of Canterbury
1033 – 1109 · Italian-English
Philosophy Theology Historical

Archbishop of Canterbury and Benedictine monk. Father of scholasticism and one of the most important Christian philosophers of the medieval period. His Proslogion (1077-78) contains the original ontological argument — "that than which nothing greater can be conceived." The argument has been debated for nearly 1,000 years and, in its modal form as developed by Plantinga, is still taken seriously by academic philosophers today.

Key arguments
Ontological Argument Necessary Being Cur Deus Homo Divine Attributes
"God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived. If God exists only in the mind, we can conceive of something greater — a God that also exists in reality. Therefore God must exist in reality." — Proslogion (1078)
Essential works
  • Proslogion (1077-78) — the original ontological argument
  • Monologion (1076) — arguments for God's existence from degrees of goodness
  • Cur Deus Homo (1098) — why God became man; the satisfaction theory of atonement
Catholic · Benedictine 11th–12th Century
TA
Thomas Aquinas
1225 – 1274 · Italian
Philosophy Theology Historical

Dominican friar and the greatest theologian of the medieval period. His synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology — contained in the Summa Theologica — became the official theology of the Catholic Church. His Five Ways remain the most influential arguments for God's existence in history. The Thomistic tradition he founded continues to produce rigorous philosophical theology today — Edward Feser is his foremost contemporary expositor.

Key arguments
Five Ways Act & Potency Pure Act Divine Simplicity
"We cannot know what God is, but only what He is not. We must therefore consider the ways in which God does not exist, rather than the ways in which He does." — Summa Theologica
Essential works
  • Summa Theologica (1265-74) — the greatest work of medieval theology
  • Summa Contra Gentiles (1259-65) — systematic apologetics for non-Christians
