Hostile Witnesses

Nine who changed their minds

A changed life is not, by itself, an argument — that case has to be made on its own merits, which is what the rest of this Library tries to do. But the testimony of a witness who had every reason to reject a claim, and embraced it anyway, carries a weight of its own. Here are nine people — an atheist journalist, a devout Muslim, a record-setting trial lawyer, an Oxford don, a North-African rhetorician, a first-rank mathematician, a slave-trading sea captain, a homicide detective, and a feminist professor — who set out either to ignore or to disprove the Christian claim, examined the evidence honestly, and ended up persuaded. We tell their stories with respect for who they were before, never as trophies.

Skeptics & scholars who came to faith

01
Lee Strobel
Atheist journalist who set out to disprove the resurrection — and became one of its best-known defenders
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

Lee Strobel (b. 1952) was an avowed atheist and the Yale-trained legal-affairs editor of the Chicago Tribune, a working investigative journalist whose stock-in-trade was weighing testimony and exposing weak cases. In 1979 his wife Leslie became a Christian. Alarmed and irritated — he expected her to turn into a humorless prude — he resolved to use the investigative and legal skills of his profession to demolish the foundation of her new faith and win her back.1

The turning point

He decided that the whole of Christianity stood or fell on a single historical question: did Jesus really rise from the dead? If the resurrection could be disproved, the rest would collapse. So he set out to disprove it — treating it as the most important investigation of his life rather than a matter of mere feeling.

How they came to faith

The inquiry ran for the better part of two years. He interviewed scholars across disciplines, weighed the manuscript and medical evidence, and tested the naturalistic alternatives one by one — the swoon theory, the stolen-body theory, the hallucination theory. To his own discomfort, he concluded the case for the resurrection was stronger than the case against it. He placed his trust in Christ on November 8, 1981.1

“I’ll admit it: I was ambushed by the amount and quality of the evidence that Jesus is the unique Son of God.”2

What they became after

Strobel left journalism for ministry, serving as a teaching pastor at Willow Creek and later at Saddleback and at Woodlands Church in Texas, and as a professor of Christian thought at Houston Baptist University. In 2020 he founded the Lee Strobel Center for Evangelism and Applied Apologetics at Colorado Christian University. His account of the investigation, The Case for Christ (1998) — structured as a series of cross-examinations of leading scholars — became one of the best-selling apologetics books of its era and was later made into a feature film.3

  • The Case for Christ (1998)
  • The Case for Faith (2000) · The Case for a Creator (2004)
02
Nabeel Qureshi
A devout Muslim who debated Christianity for years — and counted the cost of following the evidence
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

Nabeel Qureshi (1983–2017) grew up in a loving, devout Ahmadi Muslim family and was a committed, well-equipped defender of Islam — able to argue his faith ably and proud of his heritage. He was a serious student headed for medicine, and his faith and family were inseparable from his sense of identity. We tell his story with respect for the community that raised him and the sincerity of the convictions he once held.

The turning point

As a pre-med student at Old Dominion University he befriended a Christian classmate, David Wood, and the two began a years-long, genuinely respectful dialogue over the historical claims of both faiths: the reliability of the Gospels, the evidence surrounding Jesus’ death and resurrection, and the textual history of the Qur’an. Qureshi did not want the conversation to go where it was going — he loved Islam and resisted the conclusion.4

How they came to faith

After extended study, prayer, and what he described as dreams and visions he could not dismiss, he came to faith in Christ in 2005 — at real cost to his family ties, which he never minimized. He told the story in Seeking Allah, Finding Jesus (2014), a New York Times bestseller.4

Following the gospel, Qureshi knew, would cost him nearly everything — his family and the Muslim community that had raised him.5

What they became after

Qureshi earned an MD from Eastern Virginia Medical School, an MA in Christian apologetics from Biola University, and an MA in religion from Duke University. In 2013 he joined Ravi Zacharias International Ministries (RZIM) as an itinerant speaker and apologist, lecturing at more than a hundred universities — including Oxford, Cambridge, Columbia, and Johns Hopkins — and engaging in public dialogues on Islam and Christianity. He died of stomach cancer on September 16, 2017, at the age of 34.6

  • Seeking Allah, Finding Jesus (2014)
  • Answering Jihad (2016) · No God but One: Allah or Jesus? (2016)
03
Sir Lionel Luckhoo
The most successful trial lawyer in recorded history — who put the resurrection on trial and returned a verdict
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

Sir Lionel Luckhoo (1914–1997) was, by the measure of the Guinness Book of Records, the most successful advocate in history — credited with 245 consecutive murder-case acquittals. A Guyanese barrister, diplomat, and statesman, he was knighted twice by Queen Elizabeth II and once served simultaneously as ambassador for two sovereign nations. His whole career was the disciplined weighing of evidence and the persuading of juries.7

The turning point

Luckhoo’s conversion came not from a courtroom but from a meeting on 7 November 1978, sponsored by the Full Gospel Business Men’s Fellowship International, where he made a Christian commitment at the age of 64. Having come to faith, he then did what came naturally to a trial lawyer: he subjected its central historical claim to forensic scrutiny.8

How they came to faith

For years he applied the same standards of proof he used to win acquittals to the historical record of the resurrection — the testimony, the alternative explanations, the burden of proof — and concluded that it met the highest evidentiary bar he knew. His verdict, often quoted, carries the weight of a man who had cross-examined a great deal of testimony in his time.

“I say unequivocally [that] the evidence for the resurrection of Jesus Christ is so overwhelming that it compels acceptance by proof which leaves absolutely no room for doubt.”9

What they became after

After his conversion Luckhoo became a full-time Christian evangelist. He established Luckhoo Ministries, based in Fort Worth, Texas, and became an itinerant speaker who testified about his faith across Guyana, England, Australia, and the United States. He wrote and distributed a series of apologetics booklets — including What Is Your Verdict? and The Question Answered: Did Jesus Rise from the Dead? — pressing the case for the resurrection in the plain language of a man making his closing argument.8

  • What Is Your Verdict? (booklet on the resurrection)
  • The Question Answered: Did Jesus Rise from the Dead?
04
C.S. Lewis
An atheist Oxford don — “the most dejected and reluctant convert in all England” — who became the century’s most-read apologist
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

C.S. Lewis (1898–1963) was a brilliant young atheist and a literary scholar at Magdalen College, Oxford. He had lost his faith in his teens and held his unbelief with conviction, fortified by wide reading and a sharp dialectical mind. He was the last man one would have expected to write hymns of intellectual surrender.

The turning point

His unbelief gave way slowly — through grief, through reading writers like George MacDonald and G.K. Chesterton, and above all through long late-night conversations with his Christian colleagues, chief among them J.R.R. Tolkien and Hugo Dyson. His trained literary instinct mattered: the Gospels, he judged, did not read like the myths he had spent his life studying, but like reportage — myth that had, as he put it, become fact.10

How they came to faith

He moved first to belief in God in the Trinity Term of 1929, reluctantly, and then to Christianity in 1931 after a now-famous nighttime walk and talk with Tolkien and Dyson about myth, fact, and the Gospels.10

“In the Trinity Term of 1929 I gave in, and admitted that God was God… perhaps, that night, the most dejected and reluctant convert in all England.”— Surprised by Joy11

What they became after

Lewis became the most widely read Christian apologist of the twentieth century. His wartime BBC broadcasts were gathered into Mere Christianity (1952); he wrote The Problem of Pain, The Screwtape Letters, the seven Chronicles of Narnia, and the spiritual autobiography Surprised by Joy. In 1954 he was elected the first Professor of Medieval and Renaissance English at Cambridge, a chair he held until shortly before his death in 1963.12

  • Mere Christianity (1952)
  • Surprised by Joy (1955) · The Problem of Pain (1940)
05
Augustine of Hippo
A brilliant, dissolute rhetorician who chased truth through every philosophy of his age — and became the West’s greatest theologian
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

Augustine (354–430), born at Thagaste in Roman North Africa, was a gifted teacher of rhetoric who rose to a prestigious public professorship in Milan. By his own later account he lived a restless, pleasure-seeking young life — ambitious for status, keeping a long-term mistress, and intellectually proud. For nearly a decade he was drawn to Manichaeism, a dualist sect that promised reasoned answers, and later to the Neoplatonist philosophers, who lifted his mind toward the immaterial but could not change his will. He famously prayed, in those years, “Grant me chastity and continence, but not yet.”13

The turning point

The decisive scene came in a Milan garden in the summer of 386. In an agony of inner division — wanting God yet unwilling to let go of his old life — Augustine threw himself down under a fig tree and wept. He heard a child’s voice from a neighboring house chanting, again and again, “Tolle, lege” — “take up and read.” Taking it as a divine command, he opened the letters of Paul and read the first passage his eyes fell on (Romans 13:13–14): a call to put off the works of the flesh and put on Christ. “Instantly,” he wrote, the darkness of doubt vanished.14

How they came to faith

The garden did not happen in a vacuum. Augustine had been moved for months by the preaching of Ambrose, the bishop of Milan, whose allegorical reading of the Old Testament dissolved the objections that had kept him from Scripture; and behind it all stood the decades of tears and prayers of his Christian mother, Monica, who had followed him across the Mediterranean. On Easter Vigil in 387, Ambrose baptized Augustine in Milan. His mother died soon after at Ostia, her lifelong prayer answered.13

“You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our heart is restless until it rests in you.”— Confessions I.115

What they became after

Augustine returned to Africa, was ordained, and from 396 served as Bishop of Hippo Regius until his death in 430, as the Vandals besieged the city. He became the most influential theologian of the Western church, shaping its thought on grace, sin, the Trinity, time, and the relation of faith and reason for the next thousand years — a wellspring claimed by Catholic and Protestant alike. His spiritual autobiography, the Confessions (c. 397–400), is regarded as the first true autobiography in the West; his City of God (c. 413–426), written as Rome reeled from its sack, framed all of history as the story of two loves and two cities.16

  • Confessions (c. 397–400)
  • City of God (c. 413–426) · On the Trinity (c. 400–428)
06
Blaise Pascal
A first-rank mathematician and physicist who met “not the God of the philosophers” in a two-hour night of fire
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was one of the towering intellects of the seventeenth century. A child prodigy in geometry, he founded the mathematical theory of probability in correspondence with Pierre de Fermat, built one of the first mechanical calculating machines (the “Pascaline”) at nineteen to ease his father’s tax work, and carried out celebrated experiments on the vacuum and atmospheric pressure. He was already a nominal, conventional Catholic — orthodox in belief but, by his own reckoning, not gripped by it — and increasingly drawn to the fashionable, worldly society of Paris.17

The turning point

On the night of 23 November 1654, between about half past ten and half past midnight, Pascal underwent an overwhelming religious experience he could describe only as “FIRE.” He recorded it at once on a scrap of parchment — the Mémorial — and thereafter kept it sewn into the lining of his coat, transferring it from garment to garment for the rest of his life. It was discovered only after his death, when a servant noticed the bulge.18

How they came to faith

The Mémorial is not an argument but a cry: the God who met him that night was not the abstract First Cause of the metaphysicians but the living God of the patriarchs, encountered in “certitude, certitude, feeling, joy, peace.” From that night Pascal’s scattered piety became a settled, consuming devotion; he drew close to the rigorous Jansenist community at Port-Royal and reordered his life around his faith.18

“FIRE. God of Abraham, God of Isaac, God of Jacob — not of the philosophers and scholars.”— the Mémorial, 23 Nov. 165419

What they became after

Pascal turned his formidable powers to the defense of the faith. He wrote the Lettres provinciales (1656–57), a polemical and stylistic landmark of French prose, and began an ambitious Apology for the Christian Religion that he did not live to finish — he died in 1662, aged 39, after years of illness. The surviving fragments were gathered and published posthumously in 1670 as the Pensées (“Thoughts”), containing his probing analysis of the human condition and the famous “wager.” It remains among the most-read works of Christian apologetics ever written.20

  • Pensées (publ. posthumously, 1670)
  • Lettres provinciales (1656–57) · the Mémorial (1654)
07
John Newton
A profane slave-ship captain who, “once an infidel and libertine,” became a pastor, an abolitionist, and the author of “Amazing Grace”
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

John Newton (1725–1807) lost his devout mother at six and went to sea young. By his twenties he was, in his own later words, an “infidel and libertine” — profane, mutinous, notorious among his shipmates for mocking faith and morals. Pressed into the Royal Navy and then trading on the African coast, he sank to real degradation, for a time himself effectively enslaved in Sierra Leone, before entering the Atlantic slave trade in which he would eventually captain ships carrying enslaved Africans to the West Indies. We name that evil plainly, as he himself came to.21

The turning point

On 10 March 1748, sailing home aboard the Greyhound, Newton was woken by a violent storm that nearly sank the ship and washed a crewman overboard. As he worked the pumps for his life, he found himself crying out to a God he had spent years deriding — and the ship, against the odds, survived. He marked that date for the rest of his life as the beginning of his turning.21

How they came to faith

His conversion was not instantaneous; by his own admission he continued in the slave trade for several years afterward, his conscience only slowly awakening. But the storm began a long, deepening repentance through years of reading Scripture and Christian writers. Only with hindsight did he grasp the full horror of the trade he had served — and renounce it utterly.22

“Once an infidel and libertine, a servant of slaves in Africa, was, by the rich mercy of our Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ, preserved, restored, pardoned, and appointed to preach the faith he had long laboured to destroy.”— Newton’s self-written epitaph23

What they became after

Newton was ordained in the Church of England in 1764 and served as curate of Olney and later rector of St Mary Woolnoth in London. With the poet William Cowper he produced the Olney Hymns (1779), which included “Amazing Grace.” In 1788 he published Thoughts upon the African Slave Trade, a public confession of his guilt, and became a leading abolitionist witness and a mentor to William Wilberforce — living just long enough to see the slave trade abolished by Parliament in 1807.24

  • “Amazing Grace,” in Olney Hymns (1779)
  • Thoughts upon the African Slave Trade (1788) · An Authentic Narrative (1764)
08
J. Warner Wallace
A cold-case homicide detective who read the Gospels as witness statements — and found the earmarks of truth
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

J. Warner Wallace (b. 1961) spent his career as a Los Angeles-area homicide detective, specializing in cold cases — murders solved years or decades later, often built entirely on the careful evaluation of old eyewitness statements with no living witnesses to cross-examine. His cases have been featured on Dateline more than those of any other detective. He was a committed atheist into his thirties who considered Christianity a fable.25

The turning point

At around age 35, almost on a dare to himself, he began reading the Gospels not as scripture but as witness statements — applying the same forensic-statement-analysis techniques he used to test the reliability of testimony in court.

How they came to faith

He examined the accounts for internal corroboration, incidental and unintended detail, and the presence or absence of late legendary embellishment. To his surprise, the Gospel accounts bore the marks he associated with true eyewitness reports. He became a Christian in 1996.25

Wallace concluded the Gospels bear what he calls “the earmarks of trustworthy testimony” — the same markers he weighed in any cold-case witness.26

What they became after

Wallace became a full-time Christian case-maker, speaker, and author. He is a Senior Fellow at the Colson Center for Christian Worldview and an adjunct professor of apologetics at Talbot School of Theology (Biola University). Through his books, the Cold-Case Christianity podcast, and frequent speaking, he teaches believers to make an evidential case for their faith.25

  • Cold-Case Christianity (2013)
  • God’s Crime Scene (2015) · Person of Interest (2021)
09
Rosaria Butterfield
A tenured professor of queer theory who came to faith through a years-long friendship — not a debate
🌿 Testimony

Who they were

Rosaria Butterfield (b. 1962) was a tenured professor of English and women’s studies at Syracuse University, specializing in queer theory and critical theory. She was in a committed lesbian relationship, an activist, and by her own description deeply skeptical of — even hostile toward — Christianity, which she associated with cruelty toward people like her. We honor her request that her story be told without caricature of the life she left.27

The turning point

In 1997 she wrote a newspaper piece criticizing the religious right. Among the responses was a warm, non-defensive letter from a local Reformed Presbyterian pastor, Ken Smith, who — instead of arguing — invited her to dinner. What followed was not a debate but a years-long friendship.27

How they came to faith

She ate at Ken and Floy Smith’s table again and again, and read the Bible repeatedly out of professional curiosity, expecting to refute it. Slowly her objections gave way. She converted in 1999 at real cost to her career and relationships, and recounted it in The Secret Thoughts of an Unlikely Convert (2012).27

She has described her conversion as feeling, at the time, like “a train wreck” — the upheaval of an entire life, not a tidy decision.28

What they became after

Butterfield left academia and became an author and conference speaker. She is married to Kent Butterfield, a Reformed Presbyterian pastor in North Carolina, and is a mother and grandmother. Her writing on conversion, identity, and Christian hospitality — especially The Gospel Comes with a House Key — has made her a widely read voice in the contemporary church.29

  • The Secret Thoughts of an Unlikely Convert (2012)
  • Openness Unhindered (2015) · The Gospel Comes with a House Key (2018)
