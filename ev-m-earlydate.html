The Early Date of the New Testament

The deep dive tells you what the argument is. This track makes you able to use it — reconstruct it from memory, answer its hardest objections, and hold the line in a real conversation.

The argument in brief

When was the New Testament written, and is it early enough to be trusted? The argument is that a document written within the lifetime of eyewitnesses is too early to be a developed legend that could pass unchallenged. The strongest evidence is not the narrative Gospels but what lies beneath them: in 1 Corinthians 15:3–7 Paul quotes a fixed creed about Jesus' death, burial and resurrection appearances that scholars across the spectrum — including skeptics — date to within just a few years of the crucifixion. The resurrection proclamation is therefore at the foundation, not a late addition.

Other clues point the same way. Paul's letters were written in the AD 50s. Acts never reports the deaths of Paul, Peter or James, nor the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — events a later historian would surely include — which suggests Luke wrote in the early 60s and his Gospel earlier still. By ancient standards even the conventional dating keeps the Gospels within living memory, when hostile witnesses in Jerusalem could have refuted fabrication on the spot.

The honest scope: an early date rules out slow legendary development but not deliberate fabrication — that is answered separately by the witnesses' willingness to suffer for their claims. Many scholars date Mark around AD 70 and the others later; this argument does not depend on the earliest dating, because the creed and letters everyone accepts already anchor the central claims in the 30s and 50s.

Understand it

Not more prose — the load-bearing structure. See the skeleton, then learn to defend each bone.

The argument, formally

Three lines. The whole weight rests on the two premises.

P1

A document written within the lifetime of eyewitnesses is too early to be a developed legend that could pass unchallenged.

P2

The core New Testament documents were written within that eyewitness window — most before AD 70.

∴ C

Therefore, the New Testament is too early to be legend and must be assessed as first-generation testimony.

The logic is airtight — if both premises are true, the conclusion follows necessarily. So every serious objection is really an attack on P1 (whether an early date rules out legend) or P2 (whether the books really are that early). Knowing which premise an objection targets is half the battle.

Then the conceptual step — what does an early date buy you?

Reasoning from the conclusion: if the books are early, hostile witnesses and living eyewitnesses were still around to refute fabrication — which dramatically constrains how much invention is possible.

Defending the premises

Why each premise is more plausible than its denial

Support for P1 — an early date is incompatible with developed legend

Legends need time and distance

A. N. Sherwin-White, the Roman historian, observed that even two full generations are too short for legend to erase a historical core; for the Gospels the interval is far shorter. Where living memory remains, an invented account collides with people who were there.

Hostile witnesses were a live constraint

The first preaching happened in Jerusalem, weeks from the events, to a crowd including the very authorities who executed Jesus. Claims about an empty tomb or public miracles could have been falsified on the spot if they were fiction. Early dating keeps that check in force.

Support for P2 — the NT really was written that early

The silence of Acts

Acts never reports the deaths of Paul, Peter, or James, nor the AD 70 destruction of Jerusalem — events a historian writing later would surely include. The natural read is that Luke wrote before they happened, placing Acts in the early 60s and the Gospel of Luke earlier still.

A creed older than the Gospels

In 1 Corinthians 15:3–7 Paul quotes a fixed creed about the death, burial, and resurrection appearances — a formula scholars across the spectrum date to within a few years of the crucifixion. The resurrection claim is therefore not a late accretion; it is at the foundation.

The hard part — objections, steelmanned

Each one stated as its strongest defender would put it

This is what separates someone who knows the Early Date of the NT from someone who can defend it. We give the objection its best form first — never a strawman — then the reply.

"The Gospels were written 40–70 years later" Common

Strongest form

Even on conservative dating, Mark comes around AD 70 and John near the century's end. Decades are plenty of time for embellishment to creep in before anyone wrote.

Reply

By ancient standards a forty-year gap is remarkably short — our best biographies of Alexander the Great were written four centuries after him and historians still trust their core. More importantly, the gap to the source material is far shorter: Paul's letters (50s) and the creed he cites (within a few years) carry the central claims well inside living memory, long before the narrative Gospels were finalized.

"Jesus predicts the Temple's fall, so it was written after AD 70" Trickier

Strongest form

Mark 13 has Jesus foretelling the destruction of the Temple in detail. The simplest explanation is that the author wrote after 70 and put the prophecy in his mouth — so the Gospels are post-70.

Reply

That argument only works if you assume in advance that genuine prediction is impossible. Set that aside and the text cuts the other way: the prediction is notably unspecific about how the fall happened, and the Gospels and Acts never mention the catastrophe as a past event — an astonishing omission if it had already occurred. The silence fits a pre-70 date far better than a post-70 cover-up.

"Early doesn't mean accurate — people lie fast too" Trickier

Strongest form

Conspiracy theories form within days. An early date rules out slow legend but not deliberate fabrication, so it proves nothing about whether the events happened.

Reply

Correct that early dating targets legend, not lying — and that's the right division of labor. This argument removes one whole class of explanation (gradual mythologizing) so the remaining options are honest report or deliberate fraud. Fraud is then answered separately by the willingness of the witnesses to suffer and die for what they claimed to have seen, and by hostile parties' inability to produce the body. Each argument carries its own part of the load.

"The argument from silence in Acts is weak" Hard

Strongest form

Authors omit things for many reasons. Luke may simply have ended Acts where his narrative purpose was complete; you can't date a book from what it fails to mention.

Reply

An argument from silence is only strong when the omission is one the author would almost certainly have included. Luke records the martyrdoms of Stephen and James son of Zebedee in detail, yet stops with Paul under house arrest and never reports Paul's death, the Neronian persecution, or Jerusalem's fall — events central to his themes. It isn't any single silence but the convergence of several expected-yet-absent events that points to a pre-60s composition.

"Most scholars date the Gospels later than you do" Hard

Strongest form

The mainstream consensus puts Mark around 70 and the others in the 80s–90s. Appealing to a minority early dating is special pleading against the experts.

Reply

The argument doesn't depend on the earliest possible dating of the narrative Gospels. Even the standard consensus places them within living memory of eyewitnesses — and concedes that Paul's letters and the 1 Corinthians 15 creed, which carry the central claims, are demonstrably early. Whether Mark is 65 or 72, the resurrection proclamation is anchored in the 30s. The case stands on the earliest layers everyone already accepts.

Where this comes from — primary sources, not our summary

A. N. Sherwin-White20th c. · Roman Society and Roman Law in the NT
Oxford classical historian who argued, from how legends actually form in the ancient world, that the interval to the Gospels is too short for the historical core to be lost.
John A. T. Robinson1976 · Redating the New Testament
A liberal Anglican bishop who, no apologist, concluded the entire NT may have been written before AD 70 — read him for the silence-on-the-Temple case in full.
Richard Bauckham2006 · Jesus and the Eyewitnesses
Argues the Gospels preserve named eyewitness testimony rather than anonymous community tradition, tightening the link between the events and the texts.
Gary Habermascontemporary · creedal research
Catalogued the broad scholarly agreement that the 1 Corinthians 15 creed dates to within a few years of the crucifixion. (Read his work directly — we paraphrase, but the originals reward the time.)
