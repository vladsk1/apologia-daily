Featured club
Can we show — by the standards of professional historians — that it’s very probable the historical Jesus claimed divine authority? Dr. Mikel Del Rosario builds the case through two parallel “blasphemy accusation” scenes: the paralytic Jesus forgives in Mark 2 (authority to forgive sins), and the Jewish examination in Mark 14 (authority to judge). Each scene runs the same three beats — the core scene, the blasphemy charge, the divine claim. This club walks the book chapter by chapter, then hands you the tools to defend it.

Finish the study · Mastery check

Can you make the whole case?

One capstone for the whole book — eight prompts that walk through the entire case, in two modes. Flashcards: read a prompt, answer it in your head (or out loud to the group), then tap to reveal a model answer drawn from Dr. Del Rosario’s argument. Self-rate quiz: take the same eight prompts one at a time, rate how well you answered each, and get an honest score at the end. If you can walk through all eight, you can make the case.

01 · The method
Why build the case from Mark, not John?
Tap to reveal →
John’s explicit “I am” sayings are exactly what skeptics dismiss as late. Mark is the earliest Gospel — the one even critical scholars grant has the least embellishment. If Jesus claims divine authority even there, the “only in John / the church invented it” objection collapses. It meets the skeptic on their own ground.
02 · The angle
Why focus on Jesus’ enemies, not his followers?
Tap to reveal →
The book’s distinctive move: how did Jesus’ Jewish adversaries hear his claims? Their blasphemy charges are historical evidence that his words and deeds were taken as claims to divine authority — independent of what his followers later came to believe.
03 · Scene 1 · Mark 2
What did Jesus claim, and why was it blasphemy?
Tap to reveal →
Healing the paralytic, he first says “your sins are forgiven” (Mk 2:5). The scribes react: “Who can forgive sins but God alone?” (Mk 2:7). He claimed authority on earth to forgive sins — a divine prerogative — then backed the invisible claim with the visible healing (Mk 2:10).
04 · Scene 2 · Mark 14
What did Jesus claim, and why was it blasphemy?
Tap to reveal →
Asked if he is the Christ, he answers by fusing Daniel 7 and Psalm 110 — the Son of Man seated at God’s right hand, coming on the clouds (Mk 14:62). He claimed authority in heaven to judge — again God’s alone. The high priest tears his robes and calls it blasphemy.
05 · The objections
What are the strongest objections — and the answers?
Tap to reveal →
Hägerland: Jesus never said Mk 2:10 (added later). Casey (and Ehrman): claiming to be Messiah or citing the Son of Man wasn’t blasphemy. Kirk: Jesus is an “idealized human,” not divine. Del Rosario answers each — the authenticity holds; and claiming to share God’s throne and judge is far more than mere messiahship — and more than any “idealized human” was ever granted.
06 · The verdict
How does the book reach its conclusion?
Tap to reveal →
It gathers ten well-evidenced data points from the two scenes, then weighs two explanations — human vs divine authority — against five criteria (plausibility, scope, power, less ad hoc, illumination). The divine-authority reading passes all five and best explains the data.
07 · The calibration
Why “very probable,” not “proof” — and why no need for “I am God”?
Tap to reveal →
History deals in probabilities, not proofs — the honest verdict is “very probable,” the best explanation of the evidence. And the slogan “I am God” isn’t required: claiming to forgive sins on earth and judge from heaven — prerogatives reserved to God — is itself the claim.
08 · The takeaway
The 60-second version for a skeptic?
Tap to reveal →
Even in Mark, the earliest Gospel, Jesus does two things only God could rightly do — forgive sins and claim to judge the world from God’s throne — and his own enemies recognized both as claims to be divine. By ordinary historical standards, the best explanation is that Jesus claimed divine authority. That — not a slogan — is how, the book argues, Jesus said he was God.
