## The chapter's spine / thesis
"Has the NT text been hopelessly corrupted?" **No.** Radical skepticism (Ehrman-style) and naive
certainty (KJV-Only) are *both* off the guardrail; the mainstream, evidence-based middle is that we can
recover the wording of the autographs with high confidence, and that **the number of variants is large
precisely because the number of manuscripts is large** — which is a strength, not a weakness. The
chapter answers three questions in order: (1) the **number** of variants, (2) the **nature** of
variants, and (3) the **theological** significance of variants (the most important, saved for last).
Bottom line: in the places the text is uncertain, **no cardinal doctrine is at stake**, and the core of
the faith — the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ — is textually secure.

## The frame (pp. 140–143) — the two ditches + what's at stake
- **Two errors to avoid:** *radical skepticism* (the offspring of postmodernism — treats uncertainty
  as the only certainty) and *absolute certainty* (the offspring of modernism — e.g. KJV-Only). Wallace
  pictures both as driving off opposite sides of the same road. The mainstream scholarly middle (held
  by evangelical, liberal, Protestant, and Catholic textual critics alike) is the sane position.
- **Skeptics quoted (steelmen to answer, verify each to source):** Bart Ehrman, *Misquoting Jesus*
  (the "we don't even have copies of the copies of the originals… many thousands of places" passage);
  the **Jesus Seminar** (*The Five Gospels*, Funk/Hoover) on the copyist "temporal gap"; plus how the
  skeptical talking points get **borrowed by Muslim apologists** (answering-christianity.com; M. M.
  Al‑Azami) and some **Mormon** writers (Bickmore; Gee; Matthews) to argue NT corruption — though
  **not all Mormon scholars agree** (Richard Lloyd Anderson is cited as a dissenter). *This "the same
  skeptical argument gets repurposed by other worldviews" observation is useful for our Islam and
  worldviews tabs — but represent each fairly; steelman, don't caricature.*
- **The KJV-Only ditch (fn. 9–13):** the *Textus Receptus*/"God wrote only one Bible" position (Jasper
  James Ray; the more extreme David Otis Fuller / Donald Waite strand) is handled as the *other* error —
  a "false certainty." Wallace cites **D. A. Carson, *The King James Version Debate*** as the standard
  critique. *For us: this is denominationally sensitive; we defend the reliability of the text without
  adjudicating the KJV-Only intra-Christian dispute as settled doctrine. Cite Carson; keep our neutral
  tone.*

## Q1 — The NUMBER of variants (pp. 143–152) — index for our own manuscript-count content
- **Definition first:** a "textual variant" = *any* place of wording variation among manuscripts,
  including word order and spelling. Count is driven by the *number of manuscripts*, not the number of
  serious problems.
- **~400,000 variants** is the current best estimate — and it is high **because** there are so many
  manuscripts. Wallace's key illustration: variants are counted across all witnesses, so a single
  wording found in a thousand manuscripts counts as one variant, while a nonsense reading in one
  manuscript also counts as one; more manuscripts ⇒ more raw variants but *better* recovery of the
  original. *(Historical: John Mill's 1707 Greek NT listed ~30,000 variants from ~100 mss and caused a
  storm; Bentley's reply — more mss = more anchors, better recovery — is the still-valid answer.
  Leads: fn. 14, 17.)*
- **Manuscript riches:** ~**5,600+** Greek NT mss (Wallace's figures at the 2013 writing), plus **≥
  20,000** in Latin, Coptic, Syriac, and other versions, plus **>1 million** patristic quotations —
  such that the NT could be substantially reconstructed from citations alone (Metzger–Ehrman). Compared
  with classical authors (Homer aside, most survive in a handful of late copies), the NT is in an
  incomparably better position. **This is the "embarrassment of riches" argument — but apply our
  Bible‑reliability rule: it proves the wording is *recoverable/accurately preserved*, not that the
  content is *true*.** *Leads: fn. 18 (Metzger–Ehrman, *The Text of the NT*, 4th ed., p. 126); fn. 19
  (Ehrman, *Lost Christianities* 219; Epp essay); fn. 25 (Pausanias); classical comparisons pp. 150.*
- **⚠️ Calibration Wallace himself flags (fn. 26–29, pp. 151):** he is *careful* not to overplay the
  raw manuscript-count apologetic (the old "5,600 vs. X for Caesar" chart). Skepticism is really about
  *what the autograph said*, and simply piling up late copies doesn't by itself answer that; the honest
  case rests on the *early* and *good* manuscripts and the *reasoned* reconstruction. **This matches our
  own 2026‑07‑09 fix that scrubbed inflated Caesar/Plato manuscript-count claims from the Resurrection/
  Bible-Reliability cards — Wallace is an ally here, use him to keep the count argument disciplined.**
- **The papyri chart (pp. 148–149):** Wallace's own table of early papyri (𝔓¹, 𝔓¹⁵, 𝔓³⁰, 𝔓³², 𝔓⁴⁵+𝔓⁶⁷,
  𝔓⁶⁴+𝔓⁶⁷, 𝔓⁷⁵, 𝔓⁷⁷, etc.), each with date + contents + Aland "Category I / strict / normal text"
  classification, arguing the earliest manuscripts are *good* ones (drawn from Kurt & Barbara Aland,

## Q2 — The NATURE of variants (pp. 152–160) — the four-category taxonomy (the useful core)
Wallace's headline move: sort the ~400,000 variants by **viability** (could it be original?) and
**meaningfulness** (does it change the meaning?):
1. **Neither viable nor meaningful** — the *vast majority* (~70%+ are spelling alone). Example: the
   movable *nu*; the two spellings of "John" (Ἰωάννης / Ἰωάνης). No impact.
2. **Viable but not meaningful** — real but trivial (word order in inflected Greek — "Jesus loves
   John" can be written 16 ways without changing sense; definite article with proper names).
3. **Meaningful but not viable** — changes the meaning but has no real claim to be original (poorly
   attested). Examples: codex 2882 at Luke 6:22 ("blessed… because of the Son of Man" omitted — a lone
   late witness); a medieval codex at 1 Thess 2:9 ("gospel of God" → "gospel of Christ"). Cannot be
   original, so no threat.
4. **Meaningful AND viable** — the only category that matters, and it is **less than 1%** of all
   variants. Worked examples Wallace walks through:
   - **Romans 5:1** — ἔχομεν ("we have peace," indicative) vs. ἔχωμεν ("let us have peace,"
     subjunctive): a single o/ω letter; either way, no doctrine of justification is touched.
   - **"Jesus Barabbas" (Matt 27:16–17)** — whether Barabbas's first name was "Jesus"; a historical/
     text-critical curiosity, *no* theological issue. (Wallace notes the irony: one Jesus dies in the
     place of another.)
   - **1 Thessalonians 2:7** — νήπιοι ("infants/little children") vs. ἤπιοι ("gentle"): meaningful but
     doctrinally inert.
   - **The two longest variants — the *pericope adulterae* (John 7:53–8:11) and the longer ending of
     Mark (16:9–20)** — Wallace's view: both are almost certainly **not** part of the original text,
     yet **excising them costs no cardinal doctrine**; the woman-caught-in-adultery story is a beloved
     but text-critically late passage, and Mark's authentic ending is debated. *Leads: fn. 38 (Metzger,
     *A Textual Commentary on the Greek NT*, 2nd ed., p. 56); fn. 40 (D. A. Black, ed., *Perspectives on
     the Ending of Mark: Four Views*, B&H 2008); fn. 41. Cross-map to our own honest handling of these
     two passages — we should already footnote them the same careful way.*
- **Scribal harmonization (fn. 32):** scribes tended to *harmonize* Synoptic parallels (make Mark read
  like Matthew), so the *non*-harmonized reading is usually the earlier/authentic one — evidence scribes
  revered the text (they smoothed, they didn't invent). *Leads: Gordon D. Fee, "Modern Textual Criticism
  and the Synoptic Problem"; W. F. Wisselink, *Assimilation as a Criterion…* (Kok, 1989), 87–90.*
- **Internal vs. external evidence** (pp. 158): how critics actually decide — external (which mss, how
  early, how widespread) + internal (which reading best explains the others / fits the author). The
  Phil 1:14 example ("speak the *word*" — many later mss add "of God"). *Method material we can reuse.*

## Q3 — The THEOLOGICAL significance (pp. 160–163) — the payoff, and the orthodoxy-sensitive part
- **The core claim (orthodox, load-bearing):** **no cardinal/confessional doctrine of the Christian
  faith rests on a textually disputed passage.** Even if every "meaningful and viable" variant were
  decided the skeptic's way, no essential — and specifically **the deity, death, and resurrection of
  Jesus Christ** — would fall. This is the chapter's thesis and it's exactly our line.
- **Ehrman himself concedes it (fn. 43–45):** in the appendix Q&A to *Misquoting Jesus*, asked whether
  any essential Christian belief is jeopardized by a textual variant, **Ehrman answered no** — the
  variants don't affect the core. *This is a genuinely strong, quotable concession — verify the exact
  wording/location in the *Misquoting Jesus* appendix before we use it, and attribute it precisely
  (Ehrman is conceding the theological-impact point, not conceding his broader skepticism).*
- **⚠️ ORTHODOXY / NEUTRALITY calibration to carry (inerrancy section, pp. 161–162):** Wallace argues
  inerrancy is about the **autographs**, and that textual variation in the *copies* does not disprove it;
  he cites the ETS doctrinal statement, Calvin, Packer, and Kantzer that "inerrancy" concerns the message/
  witness, allowing for the human language of the Bible. **For our use:** (1) inerrancy vs. the exact
  *form* of inerrancy is an **intra-Christian dispute** — under our denominational-neutrality rule we
  defend the *reliability and authority* of Scripture without adjudicating a specific inerrancy formulation
  as the settled Christian position. (2) Don't let "no doctrine is affected" slide into "so the wording
  doesn't matter" — Wallace doesn't say that; keep his precision. *Leads: fn. 46 (ETS statement); fn. 49
  (Calvin, Torrance trans., Eerdmans 1960, 114; Packer in *Inerrancy and the Church*, ed. Hannah, Moody
  1984, 178–79; Kantzer); fn. 50 (Wallace, "Inerrancy and the Text of the NT," in *Evidence for Faith*,
  ed. Dembski & Licona, Baker 2010).*
- **The "0.001%" debate anecdote (pp. 156–157, fn. 34–37):** in a Wallace–Ehrman debate a questioner
  (Justin Bass) pressed Ehrman on how much of Mark's Gospel is genuinely in doubt; Ehrman first gave a
  sweeping-skeptical answer, then conceded the actually-disputed portion is minuscule (Wallace frames it
  as far less than 1%). *A vivid illustration — but verify the debate details (date, venue: Southern
  Methodist University, Oct 1, 2011; the DVD/csntm reference) before repeating specifics; don't inflate
  the anecdote.*

## The Conclusion (p. 163) — our takeaway line (paraphrase, don't quote)
Bona fide skeptical textual critics have **not** shown the NT text to be hopelessly corrupted; the text
is certain in most particulars, and where it's genuinely in doubt **no cardinal doctrine is at stake** —
and the core (the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ) has not been tampered with across ~1,400 years
of transmission. *Faithful, non-overstated, confidence-earned-by-truth — this is the exact posture we
want on the Biblical Reliability tab.*
