## The book's spine — the 12-point cumulative case

The whole book argues one chain: you don't need blind faith; a step-by-step case leads from
"truth exists" to "the Bible is God's word." The authors call the finished picture the **"box
top"** to the puzzle of life (Introduction). Since ~1996 they taught it as a seminar,
*"The Twelve Points That Show Christianity Is True."* **Verified against the Introduction
(pp. 27–28):**

1. **Truth about reality is knowable.**
2. **The opposite of true is false** (law of non-contradiction).
3. **It is true that the theistic God exists** — evidenced by:
   - **a.** the beginning of the universe — **Cosmological** argument (ch. 3, "SURGE")
   - **b.** the design of the universe — **Teleological / Anthropic** argument (ch. 4)
   - **c.** the design of life — **Teleological** argument (ch. 5–6)
   - **d.** the moral law — **Moral** argument (ch. 7)
4. **If God exists, then miracles are possible** (ch. 8).
5. **Miracles can be used to confirm a message from God** (acts of God confirming a word from God).
6. **The New Testament is historically reliable** — evidenced by:
   - **a.** early testimony · **b.** eyewitness testimony · **c.** uninvented (authentic)
     testimony · **d.** eyewitnesses who were not deceived (ch. 9–11)
7. **The New Testament says Jesus claimed to be God.**
8. **Jesus' claim to be God was miraculously confirmed by:**
   - **a.** his fulfillment of many prophecies about himself;
   - **b.** his sinless life and miraculous deeds;
   - **c.** his prediction and accomplishment of his resurrection (ch. 12).
9. **Therefore, Jesus is God.**
10. **Whatever Jesus (who is God) teaches is true.**
11. **Jesus taught that the Bible is the Word of God** (ch. 14).
12. **Therefore, it is true that the Bible is the Word of God** (and anything opposed to it is
    false).

**Method caveats the authors flag up front (important for how we use this — it keeps us honest):**
- The points are **not "true by definition"** — most are premises that **must be justified by
  evidence**; e.g. point 3 ("the theistic God exists") is backed by argument, not asserted.
- They deliberately **start at complete skepticism** (a person who denies truth exists), because
  if "there is no truth" were correct, nothing downstream could be *true* either.
- Point 12 does **not** mean other religions are "completely false" — the authors grant that
  "nearly all religions have some truth"; the claim is narrower: *if* the Bible is true, then a
  specific claim that *contradicts* it is false. (This matches our own "concede the accurate,
  not the frame" calibration.)

### Introduction — Finding the Box Top
**Thesis / metaphor:** life is a jigsaw puzzle; you need the **"box top"** (the true worldview
picture) to make the pieces fit. The book claims to build that box top step by step. Opens with
Frank Turek's own story (a skeptical OT professor at the University of Rochester, ~mid-1990s) and
a Phillip E. Johnson epigraph. Lays out the **12-point case** (above).

**Biblical mandate for apologetics** the authors ground the whole project in (good primary-source
leads to cite directly in our "why apologetics" material):
- **1 Peter 3:15** — be ready to give a reason/answer (matches our own house verse).
- **2 Corinthians 10:4–5** — "demolish arguments" against the knowledge of God.
- **Isaiah 1:18** — "Come, let us reason together" (God invites reasoning).
- **Matthew 22:37** — love God "with all your **mind**" — reason as part of the greatest
  commandment.
- Their line: Christians are *commanded* to know what they believe and why; faith is not "just

### Ch. 1 — Can We Handle the Truth? (truth is knowable & absolute)
**Thesis:** truth about reality — including religion and morality — is **knowable and absolute**;
relativism is **self-defeating** (points 1–2 of the 12). Opens with *A Few Good Men* ("You can't
handle the truth!") and a Churchill epigraph; notes our "cultural schizophrenia" — we demand truth
in money/medicine/safety yet say we're "not interested in truth" about morality or religion.

**Core argument (all verified, pp. 35–39):**
- **What truth is:** truth is "telling it like it is" — **correspondence** to reality / an accurate
  state of affairs. It is **absolute, narrow, and exclusive** by nature: if something is true, it's
  true for all people, at all times, everywhere; every truth claim excludes its opposite (even
  "everything is true" excludes "everything is *not* true"). So exclusivity isn't unique to
  Christianity — every worldview (humanism, relativism) is equally exclusive ("if H is true,
  anything opposed to H is false").
- **Truth is unaffected** by the *sincerity* (a sincere flat-earther is sincerely mistaken) or the
  *attitude* (arrogance doesn't make a claim false) of the one professing it; even "relative"-seeming
  truths are really absolute.
- **The Road Runner tactic** (the chapter's signature tool): **turn a self-defeating statement back
  on itself.** A self-defeating statement fails its own standard — "there is no truth" (is *that*
  true?), "you can't know truth," "that's just your opinion." Like Wile E. Coyote running off the
  cliff, the claim collapses under its own weight. References **Augustine** ("we love truth when it
  enlightens, hate it when it convicts").
- Frames the four questions for ch. 1–2: What is truth? · Can truth be known? · Can truths about God
  be known? · Who cares?

> ✅ **Guardrail fit:** clean, standard logic (law of non-contradiction, correspondence theory).
> Directly supports "orthodoxy outranks charity" indirectly — truth is exclusive, so being firm on
> doctrine isn't arrogance, it's the nature of truth-claims. Reusable for "isn't it arrogant to say
> one religion is true?" answers. Nothing here strains our guardrails.
_Maps to:_ foundational — "is truth relative?" / "isn't every religion true?" answer pages; the
Ask-Anything system prompt's posture on exclusivity.

### Ch. 3 — In the Beginning There Was a Great SURGE (cosmological argument)
**Thesis:** five converging lines of scientific evidence show the universe **began** (the Big
Bang), so it needs a cause beyond itself. The evidence is packaged as the acronym **SURGE**
(pp. 75–76: "five lines of scientific evidence… prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the universe
did indeed have a beginning").

**SURGE — the five evidences that the universe began** (S directly page-verified p. 76; the full
expansion is the book's well-known five-point Big Bang case — confirm the middle letters on use):
- **S — Second Law of Thermodynamics.** The universe is running out of usable energy; if it were
  eternal it would already be "out of gas" / dark. (Analogies: a car with finite gas; a
  flashlight.) An eternal universe contradicts the most-validated law in nature. *(verified p. 76)*
- **U — Universe is expanding.** Galaxies receding (Hubble); rewind the expansion → a single
  beginning point.
- **R — Radiation afterglow** from the Big Bang (the cosmic microwave background). Lead to verify:
  **Arno Penzias & Robert Wilson**, 1965 discovery (Nobel Prize).
- **G — Great galaxy seeds** — the tiny temperature ripples needed to form galaxies, found by the
  **COBE** satellite (1992). Lead to verify: **George Smoot**'s "like looking at God" remark.
- **E — Einstein's General Relativity** — implied a non-eternal universe with a beginning
  (Einstein initially resisted with a "fudge factor," later conceded).

**The argument (cosmological / Kalam form):** (1) everything that **had a beginning** had a cause;
(2) the universe had a beginning; (3) therefore the universe had a cause — a first cause beyond
space/time/matter: eternal, immaterial, powerful, personal.
> ✅ **Guardrail fit:** their premise is "everything that **had a beginning** had a cause," which
> matches our NON-NEGOTIABLE Kalam rule ("whatever *begins to exist* has a cause," never
> "everything has a cause"). Safe to lean on. Keep the data/inference line: the Big Bang *data* is
> mainstream science; "therefore a personal Creator" is the *philosophical* inference.

**Signature citation — Robert Jastrow** *(verified pp. 82–84)*: self-described **agnostic**
astronomer; director of the **Mount Wilson Observatory**; founder of **NASA's Goddard Institute
of Space Studies**; author of ***God and the Astronomers***. Value for us: a non-believer
conceding the theistic implications of the beginning ("Does the Big Bang point to God?"); his
book's famous closing image of scientists scaling the mountain of ignorance to find theologians
already there. → Cite Jastrow **from his own book** with attribution (it is not PD — 1978/1992 —
so keep to our one-short-quote limit or paraphrase; the *fact* of an agnostic's concession is
freely usable).

### Ch. 4 — Divine Design (teleological / fine-tuning)
**Thesis:** the universe's physical constants are fine-tuned within extraordinarily narrow limits
for life — "the design is in the details" (**the Anthropic Principle**). Change one constant
slightly and life is impossible. Best explanation: an intelligent Designer.
**Illustration:** *Apollo 13* — Earth is like a spacecraft engineered with thousands of precisely
calibrated components; a tiny flaw (the two-inch oxygen-tank drop) cascades to catastrophe;
likewise a tiny change in a cosmic constant makes life impossible *(pp. 104–105)*.

**Signature citation — Fred Hoyle** *(verified pp. 106–107)*: astronomer, **an atheist "whose
atheism was shaken by the Anthropic Principle."** His line (book's footnote 9): a "commonsense
interpretation of the facts suggests that a **super intellect has monkeyed with physics**, as well
as chemistry and biology, and that there are no blind forces worth speaking about in nature." → An
atheist conceding the *fine-tuning data* points to intelligence (he stayed vague on *who*).
Book sources it (note ~168.8/9) partly to **Geisler & Hoffman, eds., *Why I Am a Christian***, p.
142 — a step removed, so cite Hoyle from a primary on use.

> ✅ **Guardrail fit (fine-tuning rule):** Hoyle is used exactly the right way — an *atheist
> conceding the data*, not "scientists agree the universe is designed." Preserve our
> data-vs-inference line when we borrow this: the fine-tuning **measurements** are mainstream;
> **design** is the contested inference. The book (popular-level) leans harder toward asserting
> design than our essays should — keep our calibration.

### Ch. 5 — The First Life: Natural Law or Divine Awe? (origin of life)
**Thesis:** the origin of *first life* from non-life is the design argument's sharpest edge —
because living cells contain **information**. DNA carries **"specified complexity"** *(verified
p. 116)*: the four-letter genetic alphabet (A, T, C, G) is not just complex, it spells a specific
*message* (the genome), exactly as the specific order of letters in a sentence communicates
meaning. **A message requires a mind** — natural law + chance can produce order (repetitive
patterns) but not *specified information*. So the information in the first cell points to an
intelligent Author, not undirected chemistry.

**Key distinctions to reuse (they're precise and defensible):**
- **Order vs. specified complexity vs. randomness** — a crystal is *ordered* (repetition, low
  information); random letters are *complex* but not *specified*; a sentence / DNA is *specified
  complexity* (complex **and** meaningful). Only intelligence is known to produce the third kind.
- Message ⇒ messenger: information is a mind-product wherever else we observe it.

### Ch. 6 — New Life Forms: Goo to You via the Zoo? (macroevolution)
_Captured at thesis level (structure, not page-by-page) — confirm all specifics before use._
**Thesis:** distinguishes **microevolution** (small change within a kind — observed, uncontested)
from **macroevolution** ("goo-to-you-via-the-zoo," one kind becoming a fundamentally new kind —
which they argue is *not* observed and doesn't follow from micro). Argues the extrapolation from
one to the other is the disputed leap, and points to the information problem (ch. 5) and fossil
discontinuities.

**Verified specifics (from in-book highlights, pp. 141, 154):** the chapter's precise claims are

macroevolution, which "is exactly what Darwinists claim from the data." Cites **Michael Behe**

the **irreducible-complexity** argument (Behe, *Darwin's Black Box*).

### Ch. 8 — Miracles: Signs of God or Gullibility?
_Captured at thesis level — confirm specific quotes before use._
**Thesis / role in the case:** this is **points 4–5** of the 12 — the bridge from "God exists" to
the historical case. If a theistic God exists (established in ch. 3–7), then **miracles are
possible** (a God who created the universe from nothing can certainly act within it), and miracles
tied to a truth-claim can **confirm a message from God**. So one cannot rule out the resurrection
*a priori*; it must be examined *historically* (ch. 9–12).

**Main interlocutor — David Hume** (*Of Miracles*, in *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*).
The book treats Hume (with **Kant**) as the fountainhead of modern skepticism *(HUME'S SKEPTICISM
section, p. 56)* and rebuts Hume's argument against miracles — the standard reply that Hume begs
the question / weighs evidence by mere frequency and so would rule out any rare-but-real event.
> ✅ **Guardrail fit:** the move is modest and correct — *if* God exists, miracles are *possible*
> (not "proven here"); the resurrection is then argued on *historical* evidence, not assumed. This
> matches our resurrection method (lead with evidence, not with "miracles must have happened").
_To confirm on use:_ Hume is **PD** (d. 1776) — his *Of Miracles* text can be quoted directly and

### Ch. 7 — Mother Teresa vs. Hitler (moral argument)
**Thesis:** an objective **Moral Law** really exists (Mother Teresa was genuinely *good* and Hitler
genuinely *evil* — not merely matters of taste), and an objective moral law needs a transcendent
**Moral Law Giver**. Third theistic argument, after the cosmological and teleological.

**The syllogism** *(verified p. 170):*
1. **Every law has a law giver.**
2. **There is a Moral Law.**
3. **Therefore, there is a Moral Law Giver.**
"Every prescription has a prescriber… someone must have given us these moral obligations."

**How they defend premise 2 (that the Moral Law is real, not invented)** — the useful part for us:
the Mother-Teresa-vs-Hitler contrast shows we all *know* some acts are really good and others
really evil; moral disagreements presuppose a real standard; we make excuses (which concede the
standard); reformers (e.g. abolitionists) appeal to a law *above* their culture, so morality isn't
merely cultural. The point is **ontological** (what *grounds* real good and evil), not behavioral.

> ✅ **Guardrail fit (morality rule):** the chapter argues moral **duties need a ground**, *not*
> "atheists can't be moral." Keep our phrasing exactly there when we borrow it — concede that
> atheists live morally and know right from wrong; the question is what *grounds* objective duty.
> Avoid the popular overstatement horn.

Lewis, *Mere Christianity*, Book I** — verify any Lewis quote from the book; Lewis is **not** PD in

### Ch. 9 — Do We Have Early Testimony About Jesus?
**Thesis:** the NT witness is **early**, not a late legend. The gap between the events and the
sources is short enough to exclude legendary corruption, and the earliest material predates the
documents that contain it.

**Key evidence + citations (strong for us):**
- **Gary Habermas — 41 early creeds** *(verified p. 245):* Habermas identified **forty-one** short
  creedal sections embedded in the NT — "compact sayings that could easily be remembered," passed
  along **orally before** they were written. Prime example: **1 Corinthians 15:3–8**.
- **The 1 Cor. 15:3–8 creed** — most scholars, **even liberals/skeptics**, accept it as an early
  formula; dated to the events themselves "**or within a few years of them**" *(p. 250)*, i.e. it
  predates Paul's letter and reaches back to the first years after the crucifixion.
- Oral-culture point: a largely oral first-century milieu preserved memorized testimony reliably;
  early Christians had reasons *not* to delay recording (some expected an imminent return).

> ✅ **Guardrail fit (resurrection rule) — textbook:** this is exactly our house method — *lead with
> the 1 Cor. 15:3–7 early creed (~2–5 years)*; never concede "merely written decades later." Safe
> and central. When we cite Habermas's "41 creeds," attribute to **Habermas's own work** (e.g.
> *The Historical Jesus*; Habermas & Licona, *The Case for the Resurrection of Jesus*) and keep our
> dating phrasing ("within ~2–5 years"; "within months" only when attributed, e.g. to Dunn).

### Ch. 10 — Do We Have Eyewitness Testimony About Jesus?
**Thesis:** the NT (esp. Luke–Acts) reads like **eyewitness testimony** and is demonstrably
accurate on the checkable details, which warrants trust on the rest.

**Key evidence + citations:**
- **Colin Hemer — Luke's verse-by-verse accuracy** *(citation verified in notes, p. 425):*
  **Colin J. Hemer**, *The Book of Acts in the Setting of Hellenistic History* (Winona Lake:
  Eisenbrauns, 1990), pp. 376–382. Hemer, a **classical scholar/historian**, chronicles Luke's
  accuracy in Acts "verse by verse, with painstaking detail" — a level of accuracy (names, titles,
  ports, routes, local customs) that "could befit only an eyewitness contemporary of the time and
  events" *(pp. 256)*. (This is the famous catalogue of confirmed historical details in Acts.)
- **Early dating of Acts (before AD 62)** *(p. 240):* Acts ends with Paul **alive** under house
  arrest — no mention of Paul's death (~64–65), the Neronian persecution (64), or the fall of
  Jerusalem (70). Argued conclusion: Acts was written ≤ AD 62; therefore Luke earlier, and Mark
  earlier still — pushing the Gospels close to the events.

> ⚠️ **Guardrail / calibration:**
> - **Reliability rule:** Hemer shows Luke is *accurate on verifiable facts* → *trustworthy as a
>   historian*. Keep our distinction: demonstrated accuracy supports **reliability/preservation**,
>   it does not by itself prove the **miracle claims** — argue those separately (resurrection ch.).
> - The **pre-62 dating** is a **real scholarly argument but not consensus** (many date Acts
>   later). Present it as "a strong case that," not "it is established that." Our essays already
>   hold this line.

### Ch. 11 — Top Ten Reasons We Know the NT Writers Told the Truth
**Thesis:** even granting the NT is early (ch. 9–10), how do we know the writers didn't
*exaggerate or embellish*? The chapter gives **"at least ten reasons"** the writers "did not play
fast and loose with the facts" — essentially the standard historians' **criteria of authenticity**,
applied to the Gospels. Framed against their earlier list of historical tests (the **"principle of
embarrassment"** is historical test #7, p. 231).

**Reasons directly captured (verified, pp. 275–279) — the criterion-of-embarrassment family, our
flagship and most reusable material:**
- **Reason 1 — They included embarrassing details about *themselves*.** The writers depict
  themselves as dim-witted / failing to understand Jesus (**Mark 9:32; Luke 18:34; John 12:16**),
  falling asleep at Gethsemane (**Mark 14:32–41**), doubting even the risen Jesus (**Matt 28:17**);
  Peter is called "Satan" by Jesus, denies him three times, and the disciples hide at the
  crucifixion; **women** (whose court testimony was not valued) are the first to find the empty
  tomb. Inventors of a legend would not fabricate their own humiliation.
- **Reason 2 — They included embarrassing details about *Jesus*.** Jesus is called a "drunkard"
  (**Matt 11:19**), "demon-possessed" (**Mark 3:22; John 7:20; 8:48**), a "madman" (**John 10:20**);
  has his feet wiped by a former prostitute (**Luke 7:36–39**); is crucified though "anyone hung on
  a tree is under God's curse" (**Deut 21:23; cf. Gal 3:13**) — a *cursed, crucified* Messiah is the
  opposite of an invented hero.
- **Reason 3 — They left in *hard / demanding sayings* of Jesus** they'd never invent — e.g. "unless
  you eat my flesh and drink my blood, you have no life in you" (**John 6:53**), after which many
  disciples "turned back and no longer followed him" (**John 6:66**).

**Reasons 5–10 — now page-verified (pp. 281–293):**
- **Reason 5 — They included *resurrection* events they'd never have invented** (p. 281): the
  **burial by Joseph of Arimathea** (a named Sanhedrin member — easily checkable, so not fabricated);
  **women as the first witnesses** — incl. the formerly demon-possessed **Mary Magdalene**
  (Luke 8:2) — in a culture where a woman's testimony "carried no weight in a court of law"; the
  **Jewish "theft" counter-explanation** (Matt 28:11–15), which *concedes the empty tomb* — and which
  **Justin Martyr (~AD 150) and Tertullian (~AD 200)** attest the authorities kept repeating; and
  the **conversion of Jerusalem priests** (Acts 6:7).
- **Reason 6 — They include more than *thirty historically confirmed people*** (p. 283) — real,
  checkable public figures; you don't populate a deceptive fiction with verifiable names.
- **Reason 7 — They include *divergent details*** (p. 284) — e.g. Matthew's one angel at the tomb
  vs. John's two: the mark of **independent** eyewitnesses, not a single harmonized/collusive source.
- **Reason 9 — They *abandoned* long-held sacred beliefs and *adopted* radical new ones** (pp.
  290–292, **Table 11.1**): sacrifice → fulfilled in Christ; Law of Moses → nonbinding; strict
  monotheism → **Trinity**; Sabbath → Sunday; conquering Messiah → sacrificial Messiah; circumcision
  → baptism/Communion. Devout Jews don't overturn this without cause. Cites **J. P. Moreland** on the
  magnitude of the shift.
- **Reason 10 — They were *willing to die* for their testimony** (pp. 292–293): **11 of the 12**
  apostles were martyred (John exiled to Patmos); "no sane person dies for what they *know* is a
  lie." Uses the **Watergate argument** — cites **Chuck Colson** (the conspirators cracked within
  weeks under mere threat of embarrassment/prison; the apostles faced execution and none recanted —
  Peter, who denied Jesus 3×, would not die for a hoax) and **Justice Antonin Scalia**.

**Reasons 4 & 8 — not isolated this pass** (the remaining two of the ten; standard Geisler–Turek
set includes "they carefully **distinguished Jesus' words from their own**," they **challenged
readers / hostile witnesses to verify** the facts, and they **described miracles plainly** without
embellishment — confirm which two fill slots 4 & 8 on a further read).

> ✅ **Guardrail fit:** the criterion of embarrassment + enemy-attestation + divergent-details are
> mainstream, defensible historical tools — excellent, on-brand material, and Table 11.1 / the
> martyrdom + Watergate argument are directly reusable. **Care points:** (1) the "women's testimony
> was legally inadmissible" claim is commonly overstated — our `/answers/why-were-women-the-first-witnesses`
> handles it carefully; keep the *cultural-devaluing* form. (2) "11 of 12 martyred" — the martyrdom
> **traditions** vary in strength; our resurrection material already leans on the safer point (the
> apostles *sincerely believed and suffered* for it), per Habermas/Licona. Cite the verifiable core,
> not every martyrdom legend.

### Ch. 12 — Did Jesus Really Rise from the Dead?
**Thesis:** with the NT established as early + reliable eyewitness testimony (ch. 9–11) and
miracles shown to be possible (ch. 8), the resurrection is examined as a **historical** question,
and it is the best explanation of the evidence (empty tomb, post-death appearances, the sudden
transformation and martyrdom of the disciples, the rise of the church). The authors then run the
skeptical alternatives and show each fails.

**Verified this pass — the "SKEPTICAL THEORIES" refutation section (pp. 301–302):**
- The chapter lists "the explanations for the Resurrection most frequently offered by skeptics" and
  refutes them one by one, leading with the **Hallucination Theory** ("were the disciples deceived?
  Perhaps they sincerely thought they had seen the risen Christ but were really experiencing
  hallucinations").
- **Key rebuttal captured:** hallucinations have "**fatal flaws**" — chiefly, **hallucinations are
  not collective**: "dreams are not collective experiences. Individuals have dreams — groups do
  not." The NT reports Jesus appearing to **groups** (and to 500 at once, 1 Cor 15:6), which
  hallucination cannot explain.

**Positive evidence — the empty tomb + the appearances (both must be explained):** *(verified pp.
302–303)* the authors stress that the **empty tomb alone did not convince** most disciples — it was
the **appearances** that did — so *both* facts require explanation. They tabulate the appearances in
**Table 12.1, "The Order of the Twelve Appearances of Christ,"** each with Scripture and evidence
type — a directly reusable reference:
1. Mary Magdalene (John 20:10–18) · 2. Mary Magdalene & the other Mary (Matt 28:1–10) · 3. Peter
(1 Cor 15:5) & John (John 20:1–10) · 4. Two on the Emmaus road (Luke 24:13–35) · 5. Ten apostles
(Luke 24:36–49; John 20:19–23) · 6. Eleven apostles (John 20:24–31) · 7. Seven apostles (John 21) ·
8. All apostles (Matt 28:16–20; Mark 16:14–18) · 9. **500 brethren (1 Cor 15:6)** · 10. James
(1 Cor 15:7) · 11. All apostles (Acts 1:4–8) · 12. **Paul (Acts 9:1–9; 1 Cor 15:8)**.
On **6 of the 12**, Jesus was **physically touched and/or ate food** — ruling out "vision/ghost."

**Skeptical theories refuted (verified pp. 301–303):**
- **Hallucination Theory** — two "fatal flaws": (1) hallucinations are **individual, not collective**
  (groups don't share one); (2) the **empty tomb** — if it were mass hallucination, authorities
  could have simply **produced the body** and ended Christianity, but couldn't.
- **Wrong-Tomb / moved-body theory** — would require the Jewish and Roman authorities to have
  "**collective amnesia**" about what they did with the body; and even a wrong tomb "**does not
  explain how the risen Jesus appeared twelve different times.**"
- (The book also engages the **Swoon** and **Stolen-Body** theories in this chapter — capture their
  exact rebuttals on a further read if we need them; standard replies: crucifixion is lethal / the
  disciples' martyrdom refutes a knowing fraud.)

> ✅ **Guardrail fit (resurrection rule):** exactly our method — build the *historical* case
> (empty tomb + appearances + transformed disciples), then eliminate alternatives; don't presume the
> miracle. Aligns with `library/minimalfacts.html` / `library/emptytomb.html`. Cite the appearances
> from **1 Cor 15:3–8** (the early creed) as the anchor; Table 12.1 is a ready appearance-list.
_Maps to:_ Resurrection tab (`ev-s2`), `library/minimalfacts.html`, `library/emptytomb.html`,
`/answers/is-there-evidence-jesus-rose-from-the-dead`.

### Ch. 13 — Who Is Jesus: God? Or Just a Great Moral Teacher?
**Thesis:** Jesus **claimed to be God** (not merely a wise teacher), and — having risen (ch. 12) —
his claim is vindicated. The chapter title frames the classic **Lewis "trilemma"** (if he claimed
deity, he was **Lord, liar, or lunatic** — "great moral teacher" is *not* an available option for
someone making such claims).

**Verified this pass (in-book highlight, p. 341):** a centerpiece is Jesus applying to himself the
title **"Son of Man"** and declaring he would come "**on the clouds** [of heaven]" — the allusion
to **Daniel 7:13–14** (a divine, worshipped figure), spoken at his **trial before the Sanhedrin**
(cf. **Mark 14:61–64**), which the high priest treats as blasphemy. This is the book's key
"Jesus claimed deity" datum.

**The trilemma, verified in full (p. 346):** "to be God, one of only three possibilities could be
true: he was either **a liar, a lunatic, or the Lord**."
- **Liar** "doesn't fit the facts" — Jesus taught the highest ethics and would not have died for a
  claim he knew was false.
- **Lunatic** doesn't fit — his teaching is among "the most profound… ever recorded," and even his
  **enemies** granted he was a man of integrity who taught the truth (**Mark 12:14**).
- **∴ Lord.** The chapter develops the argument citing philosopher **Peter Kreeft** (Boston College).

**Other deity evidence the chapter marshals (standard — the specific "I AM"/John 8:58, forgiving
sins in Mark 2, worship accepted, Thomas's "My Lord and my God" John 20:28 — confirm exact set on a
further read).**

> ✅ **Guardrail fit (orthodoxy / deity of Christ):** central to our Jesus tab and non-negotiable
> guardrail (full deity of Christ). Safe and important. When we use the trilemma, **keep our
> completeness — answer the "legend/liar-legend" fourth horn** (that's why ch. 9–11 on early +
> eyewitness testimony must come first; the trilemma only bites once the deity claims are shown to
> be authentically Jesus', not later invention). Our essays already handle the "was it a legend?"
> objection — pair the two.

`/answers/did-jesus-claim-to-be-god`.

### Ch. 14 — What Did Jesus Teach About the Bible?
**Thesis (point 11 of the 12):** since Jesus is God (ch. 13), what he taught is true (point 10) —
and **Jesus taught that Scripture is the Word of God**. The chapter walks through the attributes of
the **Old Testament** that Jesus affirmed, then extends the authority to the **New Testament**,
closing the loop to point 12.

**Attributes of Scripture Jesus affirmed — verified (p. 357), a numbered list with the proof-texts
(directly reusable):**
1. **Authoritative** — Jesus answered Satan in the wilderness three times with "**it is written**"
   + the OT (**Matt 4:4, 7, 10**). The book notes **"on 92 occasions Jesus and his apostles"** said
   "it is written" (or equivalent) and quoted the OT — treating it as the settled source of truth.
2. **Imperishable** — (the standard proof-text is **Matt 5:18**, "not the smallest letter… will…
   disappear"; confirm the book's exact wording on a further read).
3. **Infallible** — "**the Scripture cannot be broken**" (**John 10:35**, NKJV), which Jesus cited
   when about to be stoned; and "**your word is truth**" (**John 17:17**).
4. **Inerrant** — against the **Sadducees**, Jesus rested an argument on Scripture's precision
   (**Matt 22** — the resurrection debate, even the tense "I *am* the God of Abraham").

**Extension to the New Testament (standard — confirm exact form on a further read):** Jesus
**pre-authenticated** the apostles' writings by commissioning them and promising the Spirit to
teach them and bring his words to their remembrance (**John 14:26; 16:13**). So the whole Bible's
authority rests on **Jesus' own endorsement**, not a circular claim.

> ✅ **Guardrail fit:** this is the capstone move of the whole book — denominational-neutral (all
> Christians affirm Jesus' authority over Scripture). Keep our reliability-vs-truth care: the
> historical case (ch. 9–12) establishes Jesus' identity; *his* endorsement is then the ground for
> Scripture's authority. The John 10:35 / Matt 5:18 / Matt 4 proof-texts are clean and reusable.
