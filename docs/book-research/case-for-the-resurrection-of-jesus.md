## Usage rules (same as every note in this folder)
- **Never reproduce the book's prose.** Everything here is our paraphrase of the argument's *shape*.
- **Nothing here is quotable until verified.** Every citation below is a **lead**, unverified until
  confirmed against the **primary source** and run through `apologia-citations` → `apologia-argument`
  → `apologia-orthodoxy`. Copyrighted text never enters `/sources`.
- Watch for **popular-level overstatement** (this is a lay-apologetics book) and hold it to the
  argument-specific rules in `CLAUDE.md` — especially the Resurrection rule (lead with the
  1 Cor 15:3–7 creed dated to within ~2–5 years; never "merely written decades later").

## Why this book matters to us
It is the standard popular presentation of **Habermas's "minimal facts" method** — the approach our
Resurrection tab (`ev-s2`) and the `minimalfacts` / `emptytomb` / `paulconv` essays already lean on —
and it is our own **"Further study"** recommendation on several `/answers/*` resurrection pages. A
verified map lets us cite the underlying primaries (creed, scholars, skeptical concessions) directly
and in our own voice, rather than the book.

## Spine / thesis
The resurrection is the central, load-bearing claim of Christianity (Paul: if it didn't happen, the
faith is worthless), and it can be defended **historically** — from a small set of facts that even
critical, non-Christian scholarship grants — rather than by first assuming the Bible is inspired.
The book's aim is explicitly **equipping the ordinary believer to give a reasoned defense**
(1 Peter 3:15 — the same verse that governs this site).

## Chapter map (captured so far)

### Introduction to Part 1 — "Let the Discussion Begin" (pp. 23–24)
- **Beat:** the popular media picture of Jesus is skewed toward the **Jesus Seminar**, a *self-selected*
  fringe group mistaken by the public for mainstream scholarship. Sets up the book's method: argue from
  facts the broad scholarly field grants, not from the Seminar's radical conclusions.
- **Primary-source leads to verify (do NOT quote from the book — chase these to the source):**
  - **Peter Jennings / ABC News, *The Search for Jesus*** (television documentary, aired **July 2000**);
    the book's claim: of ~7 NT scholars interviewed, 4 were Jesus Seminar members, and Seminar
    co-founder **John Dominic Crossan** got ~¼ of the airtime. → verify the doc, air date, and the
    airtime characterization before any use.
  - **Luke Timothy Johnson** (Emory University) — that the Jesus Seminar is **"self-selected,"** not a
    genuine cross-section of the field. Likely from *The Real Jesus* (HarperSanFrancisco, 1996) →
    verify exact wording + page.
  - **Craig L. Blomberg** (Denver Seminary) — that the Seminar reflects only the field's
    **"radical fringe,"** with flawed methodology and unnecessarily skeptical conclusions → verify
    source (essay/interview?) + wording.
  - **John Dominic Crossan** — his own admission that his views do **not** represent most scholars →
    find the primary (Crossan's own writing/interview), not the book's paraphrase.
- **Guardrail fit:** the "Jesus Seminar ≠ consensus" point is legitimate and useful, but keep it
  **charitable and accurate** — it's a claim about *representativeness*, not a licence to caricature
  critical scholarship. Steelman the other side.

### Chapter 1 — "Unwrapping the Gift: Evangelism and the Resurrection of Jesus" (pp. 25–26+)
- **Beat:** defines the **gospel** (from Acts and Paul) as centring on a minimum of **three essential
  facts — (1) the deity of Jesus, (2) his substitutionary death, (3) his resurrection** — then argues
  the resurrection is the *crucial, load-bearing* issue: without it the whole message collapses.
- **Primary-source leads (Scripture — verify wording/reference in a standard translation before use):**
  - **Matthew 28:19–20** — the Great Commission.
  - **Romans 10:8–9** — confess "Jesus is Lord" + believe God "raised him from the dead."
  - **1 Peter 1:3–4** — a living hope / indestructible inheritance "through the resurrection of Jesus
    Christ from the dead."
  - **1 Corinthians 15:14–19** — "if Christ has not been raised… your faith is worthless… you are
    still in your sins"; those who died in Christ are lost. *(This is our tab's anchor — but note our
    rule: lead the historical case with the **1 Cor 15:3–7 creed**, ~2–5 years, which this chapter's
    framing sets up.)*
  - **1 Corinthians 15:32** — "let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die."
  - **1 Peter 3:15** — "make a defense… with gentleness and reverence" (the book's stated objective;
    our site's governing verse).
- **Guardrail fit:** the "three essential facts" gospel definition is squarely orthodox (deity +
  atoning death + resurrection). When we use the 1 Cor 15 material, apply the **Resurrection rule**:
  lead with the early **creed** (vv. 3–7) and its dating, not "written decades later."

### Chapter 1 continued — the resurrection as the external/historical test (pp. 27–28)
- **Beat:** Christianity uniquely stakes its truth on a **public, historical, external** test — the
  resurrection — rather than a private/subjective one. If Jesus did not rise he was a false prophet;
  if he did, it vindicates his claim. This is contrasted with religions whose confirmation test is
  internal/experiential.
- **Primary-source leads to verify (chase to the primary, do NOT quote the book):**
  - **Qur'an 2:23** — the "produce a *sura* like it, and call your witnesses besides Allah" challenge,
    used as an example of a *subjective/literary* test. → verify the wording against a standard Qur'an
    translation + the surah:ayah; represent Islam **accurately and charitably** (our neutrality +
    over-concession rules), not as a strawman.
  - **Moroni 10:4–5** (Book of Mormon) — the "ask God with a sincere heart… the Holy Ghost will
    manifest the truth" promise, as an example of a *subjective feelings* test; plus the book's claim
    of archaeology / **Book of Abraham** problems for Mormonism → verify each of those secondary claims
    separately before any use (they carry footnotes 13–14, not yet captured).
  - **Romans 8:16** — the Spirit's inward witness (used to affirm that inward assurance is real but is
    *not* the external evidential test).
- **Guardrail fit:** usable framing, but **high-caution**: the Islam/Mormonism contrast must pass the
  over-concession *and* accuracy tests — represent each faith's own test fairly, concede the
  observation not a caricature, and keep our own Resurrection rule (external case still leads with the
  1 Cor 15 creed).

### "Did Jesus Predict His Resurrection?" (pp. 29–30)
- **Beat:** four reasons the passion/resurrection predictions are historically authentic:
  1. If the resurrection is historical, dismissing the predictions is beside the point.
  2. **Criterion of embarrassment** — the disciples are repeatedly shown *not* understanding or
     believing (Mark 8:31–33; 9:31–32; 14:27–31; empty tomb disbelieved: John 20:2, 13–15; "idle
     tale": Luke 24:10–12; John 20:9; Thomas: John 20:24–25). Unlikely to be invented.
  3. **Criterion of dissimilarity** — Jesus' self-reference as **"Son of Man"** in the predictions
     (Mark 8:31; 9:31; 10:33–34) fits neither the later NT epistles (which don't use the title of
     Jesus) nor the Jewish expectation (Daniel 7:13–14 is not a *suffering* figure) → points to
     authenticity.
  4. **Multiple attestation** — the predictions appear across sources: Matt 12:38–40; 16:1–4, 21;
     17:23; 20:19; Mark 8:31–32; 9:31; 10:33; Luke 9:22; John 2:18–21 (cf. Mark 14:58; Luke 11:29–30).
- **Primary-source leads to verify:**
  - **Craig L. Blomberg, "Who Did Jesus Think He Was?"** (essay/chapter — the book cites it for the
    Son-of-Man authenticity point) → find the actual publication + page.
  - **John P. Meier, *A Marginal Jew*, vol. 1** (New York: Doubleday, 1991), **p. 171** → verify the
    citation supports the dissimilarity/Son-of-Man point.
  - The Scripture lists above → spot-check each reference and its use.
- **Guardrail fit:** embarrassment + dissimilarity + multiple attestation are standard, defensible
  historical-Jesus criteria — good, verifiable material for `minimalfacts`. Represent the criteria as
  *supporting authenticity with probability*, not proof.

### "Proof does not aim at 'absolute historical certainty'" (pp. 30–31)
- **Beat:** history deals in **probability, not 100% certainty**; the honest claim is a *high
  probability* on a **spectrum** (the book's line-graph: Very Doubtful → … → Very Certain), not
  mathematical proof. Analogy: we can't prove with 100% certainty that Washington was the first
  president, yet reasonably affirm it. Applied to the resurrection: the historian weighs criteria and
  reaches the best-supported conclusion.
- **Guardrail fit:** this is exactly our register (confidence **earned**, not overstated) — a good
  antidote to "the resurrection is *proven*." **BUT** the page also leans on "the Bible has been
  verified by archaeology" (footnote 17): apply the **Bible-reliability rule** — archaeology/
  manuscripts establish *accurate preservation and specific historical points*, **not** that all
  content is true; argue truth separately. Flag footnote 17's sources for the citations pass.

### Chapter 1 close — "reasonable certainty," evidence + gospel (pp. 32–35)
- **Beat:** the historian seeks the scenario that **best explains all the data** (inference to the
  best explanation); **historicity is the "default"** when the reasons to accept significantly
  outweigh the reasons to reject. Evidence is a legitimate part of gospel-sharing (1 Pet 3:15): the
  method adapts to the audience while the message stays fixed; the Spirit, not the argument, converts.
- **Primary-source leads to verify:**
  - **Annette Gordon-Reed** (law professor, New York Law School) — on plausibility / burden of proof /
    "proof that makes the truth of an accusation more probable than not" (footnotes 19–20) → find the
    actual work + wording.
  - **Graham Twelftree** — "a position is demonstrated when the reasons for accepting it
    *significantly outweigh* the reasons for not accepting it… a finding of historicity is essentially
    a default position" (footnote 21) → verify source + exact wording.
  - **Scripture:** Rom 8:16 (Spirit's inward witness); John 6:44 & Rom 3:11 (God draws / no one seeks);
    **Acts 17:2** (Paul reasons from the Scriptures to Jews) and **Acts 17:16–31** (at Athens he cites
    the pagans' own poets) — a solid, verifiable "adapt the method, not the message" point.
- **Guardrail fit:** the evidence-vs-faith balance (evidence *supports*, the Spirit *saves*) is orthodox
  and squarely on-mission (confidence **earned**, not manufactured). The Acts 17 material is accurate
  and useful. Popular illustrations here (the Niagara-Falls wheelbarrow) are fine as *illustration* —
  never cite them as argument.

  case** (classically: multiple independent attestation, enemy attestation, the principle of
  embarrassment, eyewitness testimony, and early testimony) — but so far only the chapter opening +

- **Guardrail fit:** these criteria are standard and defensible; frame them as *raising the probability*
  of a fact, not as proof.
