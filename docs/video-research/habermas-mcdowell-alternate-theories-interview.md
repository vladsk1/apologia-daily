## Thesis (our words)
The companion volume to the positive case: a survey and refutation of **naturalistic/alternative theories** of
the resurrection, plus the **philosophical meta-critiques** that try to rule out miracles *before* the
evidence is weighed. Habermas's method throughout: grant only the minimal facts every critical scholar
accepts, and ask the skeptic to name a *single* naturalistic theory that accounts for all of them.

## Argument map (beats → all already certified on-site)
- **Meta-critiques (cut the legs off before the evidence):**
  - **Hume on miracles** — the *a priori* objection. Habermas's line: Hume's part-one definition (a miracle =
    a violation of a law of nature) plus "a man rising from the dead has never been observed" is a **circular
    definition + circular argument** (the CS Lewis point: "we know miracles don't happen because… miracles
    don't happen"). Distinguish an *a priori* **objection** (legitimate, must be answered) from an *a priori*
    **rejection** (dismiss-without-looking; circular). Leads: Hume, *Enquiry* §10; the 18th-c. "pastor-
    philosopher" critics; Harvey Cox's "the humean mark on my brow is too strong" (~1968).
  - **"History can't evaluate miracles"** — answered by *getting the right toolset* (a historian who is also
    trained in philosophy/theology), citing agnostic historians who engage it (**J. K. Elliott**, **Michael
    Grant**); cf. Ben Shaw's dissertation (historian *qua* historian).
- **Naturalistic theories (each fails the facts-grid):**
  - **Swoon / apparent death** — the Baylor medical study (asphyxiation-dominant; *see the Vol. 1 note*),
    the broken-ankles/spear-wound Roman sources (**Quintilian**: the body released to the family after a
    finishing blow), and — decisively — **D. F. Strauss's** critique (a half-dead Jesus could not convince the
    disciples he was "the risen Lord of Life"; the swoon Jesus is *living* but not *raised*). Leads: Paulus /
    Schleiermacher held the swoon *before* Strauss killed it; Schweitzer's *Quest* narrates the German debate.
  - **Stolen body** — "the worst theory of all." Disciples-stole-it (**Reimarus**, Wolfenbüttel Fragments) is
    held by virtually no one; an *anonymous* thief only explains the empty tomb, never the appearances; and
    there is no data for it.
  - **Wrong tomb** (**Kirsopp Lake**) — a half-sentence aside, not a developed theory. **⚠ Correction to the
    video's framing:** do NOT say "Lake affirmed the resurrection" — Lake was a *critical/liberal* scholar
    whose 1907 book worked to *undercut* the empty-tomb narrative and did not affirm a bodily resurrection.
    The only defensible narrow point is that **Lake accepted the disciples' experiences as genuine (not
    fraud)** — phrase it that way, never as "even a believer proposed it."
  - **Copycat / dying-and-rising gods** — the two-question frame: were there pre-Christian dying-rising gods,
    and did Christians borrow? **Tryggve Mettinger** (*The Riddle of Resurrection*) grants a *few* but says
    Christians did **not** borrow and that he's one of a handful who accept the category; **Jonathan Z. Smith**
    argues the category dissolves on inspection ("leave and come back, or die and don't return — but not die
    *and* rise"); **Wolfhart Pannenberg**: even a myth can't *start* the movement ("an idle venture"). Adonis
    /Attis "resurrection" motifs are **2nd–3rd c. AD** — *after* Christianity. **All already in our certified
    `pagan-myths-copycat` brief + `uniqueness.html`.**
  - **Hallucination** (**Gerd Lüdemann**) — define carefully (illusion ≠ hallucination ≠ mental illness); the
    "no group hallucinations" point; **Antony Flew's "natural-miracle objection"** ("no group hallucinations
    / no resurrections"; Flew debated Habermas in **2003**, moved to **deism in 2004**) answered by the
    *multiple group* appearances of 1 Cor 15 (the Twelve, the 500, all
    the apostles) — a skeptic must posit *many* never-documented group hallucinations vs. *one* resurrection.
  - **Illusion / UFO / Marian apparitions** — dispelled by investigation (the empty tomb rules out
    "hallucination"; Marian apparitions are *dis*analogous — a few visionaries "see," thousands only watch the
    watchers; and Habermas's concession-strategy: *if* Mary really appeared, Christianity is confirmed anyway).
- **The agnostic dodge + "back doors":** you can't refute the facts and then sit in agnosticism without a
  positive case; near-death experiences (→ an afterlife) and the design/God arguments raise the prior
  probability of resurrection. (Standard; also his close: the discrepancies/"Swiss-cheese Bible" reply.)
