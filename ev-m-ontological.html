Understand it

Not more prose — the load-bearing structure. See the skeleton, then learn to defend each bone.

The argument, formally

Three lines that reason from a concept to a reality.

P1

It is possible that a maximally great being (necessary, omnipotent, omniscient, perfectly good) exists.

P2

A necessary being possible in any world exists in every world — that is what necessary means.

∴ C

Therefore a maximally great being exists — in this world too. God exists.

The modal version is logically valid (it uses the standard modal system S5). So the entire argument rests on one claim: the possibility premise (P1). Almost every objection is really a challenge to whether a maximally great being is genuinely possible.

Then the conceptual step — what is distinctive here?

This is the only argument that is purely a priori — it reasons from the very concept of God to his existence, with no appeal to the cosmos or to experience. If a maximally great being is even possible, necessity does the rest.

Defending the premises

From Anselm’s insight to the modern modal form

Anselm’s original insight

That than which nothing greater can be conceived

Anselm: God is the greatest conceivable being. If such a being existed only in the mind, a greater could be conceived — one existing in reality too. So the greatest conceivable being must exist in reality.

The modern upgrade

The medieval form had real flaws (Kant’s worry below). Plantinga’s modal version repairs them by working from necessary existence and possible worlds rather than treating existence as a perfection.

Why the possibility premise carries it

Validity is not in dispute

Granted P1, the rest follows by standard modal logic: a necessary being that exists in some possible world exists in all of them, including the actual one.

The burden shifts

Maximal greatness shows no evident incoherence (unlike a largest number). So the burden falls on the objector to show that God is impossible — not merely that he is non-actual.

The hard part — objections, steelmanned

Each one stated as its strongest defender would put it

This argument feels like a trick to many people — so the objections matter more than usual. Strongest form first, then the reply.

"Gaunilo’s perfect island" Common

Strongest form

The same logic conjures a greatest conceivable island, a perfect pizza, anything — which is absurd. So the form is broken.

Reply

Islands have no intrinsic maximum — there is always room for one more palm tree — and their great-making features are contingent, not necessary. Maximal greatness applies only to a being with a real upper bound on its essential properties, including necessary existence. An island cannot have necessary existence, so the parody does not generalise.

"Existence is not a predicate" Hard

Strongest form

Kant argued that existence adds nothing to a concept. Exists is not a great-making property, so the argument equivocates.

Reply

The modal version sidesteps Kant entirely. It does not treat existence as a perfection added to God; it works from necessary existence as a modal property and the logic of possibility and necessity. Whether or not exists is a predicate, exists-necessarily behaves differently — and the modal argument is valid.

"Run it in reverse for God’s impossibility" Hard

Strongest form

It is possible that no maximally great being exists. By parallel reasoning, then it exists in no world — so God is impossible. Symmetry sinks the argument.

Reply

This is the strongest objection, and it shows the argument cannot coerce a skeptic — it is only as strong as the possibility premise. But the two intuitions are not equal: maximal greatness shows no evident contradiction, whereas the reverse premise must assume God is impossible, a far stronger and less obvious claim. The argument shifts the burden; it does not force assent.

"You are defining God into existence" Common

Strongest form

You cannot conjure a real thing from a definition. This is a verbal trick.

Reply

The argument does not define God as existing; it argues that if a necessary being is even possible, the modal logic entails its actuality — a substantive logical result, not a stipulation. The real action is the possibility premise, which is an open metaphysical claim, not a definition.

"Possible just means I can imagine it" Trickier

Strongest form

Epistemic possibility (for all I know) is not metaphysical possibility (true in some world). Imagining God does not make him metaphysically possible.

Reply

Agreed — the premise needs metaphysical possibility, which is exactly why the argument is modest and does not claim to compel everyone. But maximal greatness shows no internal contradiction, which is decent grounds for metaphysical possibility — and far better grounds than we have for its impossibility.

"It only gives a bare perfect being" Trickier

Strongest form

Even if sound, the conclusion is an abstract maximally great being, not the Trinity or the God of the Bible.

Reply

True, and honestly so: this argument establishes a necessary, omnipotent, omniscient, morally perfect being — classical theism in outline. Identifying that being with the God revealed in Christ is the work of other arguments and revelation. Natural theology builds a cumulative case; no single argument delivers the whole portrait.

Touch the originals

Where this comes from — primary sources, not our summary

Anselm of Canterbury11th c. · Proslogion
The original argument: God as that than which nothing greater can be conceived, who must therefore exist in reality, not only in the mind.
Immanuel Kant18th c. · Critique of Pure Reason
Source of the famous objection that existence is not a predicate — which the modal version is built to evade.
Alvin Plantingacontemporary · The Nature of Necessity
Recast the argument with possible worlds and necessary existence, making it valid and locating its whole weight on the possibility premise.
Norman Malcolm20th c.
Revived interest in Anselm’s second formulation, arguing from necessary existence. (We paraphrase — read the originals.)

    SK
    Skeptic
    "This is the worst one — you cannot just define God into existence with word games. By that logic I can define a perfect island into existence too."
    One strong way to answer — in a 1 Peter 3:15 tone

    "I get why it feels like a trick — but the modern version is not defining God into being, and the island parody actually fails for an interesting reason. An island has no intrinsic maximum: there is always room for one more palm tree, so greatest possible island is incoherent, like largest number. And an island’s good features are contingent — it could have not existed. The argument runs on necessary existence, which an island cannot have, so it does not generalise. What it really turns on is one question: is a maximally great being even possible? If it is possible in any world at all, then being necessary, it exists in every world — including ours. It will not force a skeptic, because you can dig in and say I think it is impossible. But that is now the claim you would have to defend — and maximal greatness shows no contradiction, while calling it impossible is a much bigger bet."
