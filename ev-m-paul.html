The Conversion of Paul

The deep dive explains the argument. This track makes you able to wield it — rebuild it from memory, meet its hardest objections, and stay clear and calm in a real conversation.

The argument in brief

The conversion of Paul argues that the resurrection best explains how a violent enemy of the church became its most tireless missionary. By his own testimony in undisputed letters — written within about twenty-five years of the crucifixion — Saul of Tarsus had persecuted and tried to destroy the early Christians. He even appears as a hostile outsider in the very early creed he later quotes in 1 Corinthians 15:3–7, where he places himself last in the line of those to whom the risen Jesus appeared: "last of all he appeared also to me."

What needs explaining is the reversal. Paul changed instantly and permanently, surrendering his standing, his security, and finally his life, and he gave one reason: he had seen the risen Jesus. Explanations that work for grieving insiders — wishful thinking, expectation, group enthusiasm — are unavailable here, because every one of Paul's biases ran the opposite way. In a courtroom, testimony against one's own interest carries special weight.

The honest scope: a sincere conversion proves that Paul believed he had encountered the risen Christ, not, on its own, that the encounter was veridical. The argument's claim is that no hostile-psychology account fits the data as well as Paul's own — and it gains further force alongside the appearances and the empty tomb.

Understand it

Not more prose — the load-bearing structure. See the skeleton, then learn to defend each bone.

The argument, formally

The prosecution became the chief witness — explain that.

P1

Saul of Tarsus was a violent opponent of the church — arresting believers, approving executions — by his own testimony and that of others.

P2

He reversed instantly and permanently, at the cost of status, safety, and ultimately his life, giving one explanation: he saw the risen Jesus.

∴ C

Therefore something happened on the Damascus road that hostile psychology cannot supply — and his own explanation remains the best one.

The strength is hostility: every bias Paul had ran against conversion. Explanations that work for sympathetic insiders — grief, expectation, wish-fulfilment — are unavailable here.

Then the evidential weight — what a hostile witness is worth

In any courtroom, testimony against interest is gold. Paul gained nothing and lost everything: his standing under Gamaliel, his zealot credentials, his security. Five times whipped, three times beaten, stoned, and finally executed — all attested in his own undisputed letters, written within twenty-five years of the cross.

Defending the premises

Why each premise is more plausible than its denial

Support for P1 — the persecution is bedrock

His own confession

In Galatians and Philippians — letters virtually no scholar disputes — Paul confesses he violently persecuted the church and tried to destroy it. People do not invent shameful pasts.

Independent attestation

Acts records it independently, and Paul notes the Judean churches knew him only by reputation: the one who persecuted us now preaches the faith.

Support for P2 — the reversal demands a cause

Instant and total

No gradual disillusionment, no contact with Christian preaching that moved him — he was mid-campaign, letters of arrest in hand. The cause he gives is an appearance: last of all he appeared also to me.

Paid for in full

His catalogue of sufferings (2 Corinthians 11) is the receipt. Men abandon comfortable lies; they do not adopt fatal ones they know to be false.

The hard part — objections, steelmanned

Each one stated as its strongest defender would put it

Each objection gets its strongest form first — never a strawman — then the reply.

"Paul had a guilt-driven hallucination" Common

Strongest form

Tormented by conscience over persecuting good people, Paul cracked under psychological pressure — a classic conversion hallucination on a desert road.

Reply

The guilt is read into the text — Paul says the opposite: as to righteousness under the law, blameless; he persecuted in zealous good conscience. Hallucination also cannot explain his companions perceiving the event, or why a vision of the man he knew to be a cursed deceiver would read as God’s vindication rather than torment.

"Temporal lobe epilepsy explains Damascus" Trickier

Strongest form

Light, falling, a voice — the symptoms match a seizure. Paul had a medical event and interpreted it religiously.

Reply

Retro-diagnosis from four verses is not medicine. Seizures do not deliver coherent dialogues, redirect a life permanently into hardship, or generate the theology of Romans. And the content problem remains: a Pharisee’s brain misfiring would not produce the conclusion that a crucified blasphemer is the risen Lord — every prior in him screamed against it.

"People convert dramatically all the time" Common

Strongest form

Sudden conversions are a known phenomenon in every religion — Paul is one more data point, not a miracle.

Reply

Ordinary converts are persuaded by preaching, community, or crisis — channels Paul lacked and despised. He converted by what he insisted was an objective appearance, in the middle of persecuting, against every interest. And his conversion does not stand alone: it locks together with the tomb, the appearances to others, and James.

"Paul never met Jesus — his vision proves nothing about a body" Hard

Strongest form

Even granting an experience, Paul saw a heavenly light, not a walking corpse. His testimony supports visions, not bodily resurrection.

Reply

Paul’s theology of resurrection is emphatically bodily — 1 Corinthians 15 argues at length for the transformed body, and as a Pharisee no other category existed for him. He lists his experience in the same series as the appearances to Peter and the Twelve, and the Jerusalem apostles — who had eaten with the risen Jesus — accepted his claim as the same kind of event.

"Acts contradicts itself about the Damascus road" Trickier

Strongest form

The three Acts accounts differ — companions hear the voice in one, do not in another. Contradictory testimony is unreliable testimony.

Reply

Minor variation in retellings is the signature of real testimony, not fabrication — colluding inventors harmonise. The core is identical in all three and in Paul’s own letters, which are the primary evidence anyway: persecutor, appearance, reversal. The case never rested on Acts alone.

"He wanted power in a new movement" Common

Strongest form

Maybe Paul saw a leadership vacancy: big fish in the small Christian pond beats junior Pharisee.

Reply

The Christian pond offered no power — it was an illegal, despised sect whose members he had been jailing. The career move bought him whippings, shipwrecks, prison, and a Roman execution, while he worked with his hands and begged no money. Ambition has never looked like 2 Corinthians 11.

    "Paul obviously had some inner crisis — guilt, heatstroke, a seizure, take your pick. Private experiences on desert roads do not raise anyone from the dead."
    One strong way to answer — in a 1 Peter 3:15 tone

    "Pick whichever mechanism you like — each has the same two problems. First, the content: hallucinations draw on what the mind expects, and everything in Saul’s mind said crucified men are cursed by God. A misfiring Pharisee brain does not conclude that the blasphemer is the risen Lord; it concludes torment. Second, the aftermath: medical episodes fade; this one produced an instant, permanent reversal that he paid for with whippings, prison, and execution, while insisting to his death it was no inner state but an appearance — the same kind the eyewitnesses in Jerusalem had. Explain the experience however you wish; you still have to explain why your explanation never occurred to the one man with every motive to embrace it."
