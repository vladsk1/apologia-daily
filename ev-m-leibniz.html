The Leibniz Contingency Argument

The deep dive explains the argument. This track makes you able to wield it — rebuild it from memory, meet its hardest objections, and stay clear and calm in a real conversation.

The argument in brief

The Leibnizian cosmological argument starts from a famous question Gottfried Leibniz pressed: "Why is there something rather than nothing?" Its engine is the Principle of Sufficient Reason — the idea that whatever exists has an explanation of its existence, either in the necessity of its own nature or in an external cause. The universe, by contrast, looks contingent: it exists, but there seems to be no reason it had to exist, and it could have been otherwise. If the universe is contingent, it cannot be the explanation of itself, so its explanation must lie in a being that exists necessarily — a being whose non-existence is impossible. That necessary, self-existent ground is what believers call God.

Unlike the Kalam argument, this one does not depend on the universe having a beginning. Even an eternal universe would still be contingent and still require an explanation for why it exists at all. The whole case therefore turns on two points: whether the Principle of Sufficient Reason holds, and whether the universe is genuinely contingent rather than itself necessary.

In fairness, a skeptic can call the universe a "brute fact" needing no explanation, or treat the cosmos itself as the necessary being. The argument does not claim to be a knock-down proof; it claims that a necessary, personal ground is a more satisfying explanation than an unexplained brute fact, and it leaves further questions about that being's character to other arguments.

Understand it

Not more prose — the load-bearing structure. See the skeleton, then learn to defend each bone.

The argument, formally

Three steps from a simple principle to a necessary being.

P1

Whatever exists has an explanation of its existence — in the necessity of its own nature or in an external cause.

P2

The universe is contingent — it exists, but it could have failed to exist.

∴ C

Therefore the universe’s explanation lies in a necessary being — which is God.

The form is valid — deny the conclusion and you must reject a premise. So every objection lands on either the Principle of Sufficient Reason (P1) or the claim that the universe is contingent (P2). Spot which one, and you are halfway to the answer.

Then the conceptual step — what must this necessary being be?

A being whose explanation lies in its own nature cannot be contingent, cannot depend on anything, and cannot fail to exist. That rules out matter and energy, which come in arbitrary amounts and could always have been otherwise.

Defending the premises

Why each premise is more plausible than its denial

Support for P1 — the Principle of Sufficient Reason

The bedrock of reason

Every rational inquiry assumes things have explanations — that is what it means to look for a reason at all. Deny the principle and science, history, and ordinary thought collapse into magic.

A modest version

The argument needs only the modest claim that whatever exists has some explanation — in its own nature or in a cause. That avoids overreach while still ruling out sheer brute existence.

Support for P2 — the universe is contingent

It could have been otherwise

Nothing about the universe is self-explanatory: the constants, the amount of matter, the laws could all have been different, or absent. A thing that could have failed to exist does not carry the reason for its own existence.

Necessity belongs elsewhere

Matter and energy are exactly the kind of thing that comes in arbitrary quantities and configurations. A truly necessary being could not be otherwise — which is not how the physical world looks.

The hard part — objections, steelmanned

Each one stated as its strongest defender would put it

This is what separates someone who has heard of the argument from someone who can defend it. Each objection gets its strongest form first — never a strawman — then the reply.

"The universe itself is the necessary being" Common

Strongest form

Why add God? Maybe the universe just exists necessarily — it is the brute necessary thing, and no further explanation is required.

Reply

The universe shows every mark of contingency: different constants, a different amount of matter, or no universe at all were all genuinely possible. A necessary being could not be otherwise in any possible world — and the universe plainly could. Matter is the wrong kind of thing to be necessary.

"The universe is just a brute fact" Hard

Strongest form

Russell’s line: the universe is just there, and that is all. We can simply reject the Principle of Sufficient Reason for the universe itself — some things have no explanation.

Reply

A brute fact is an admission of defeat, not an explanation — and we accept it nowhere else in rational life. Applying the principle everywhere inside the universe but suspending it for the universe itself is the taxicab fallacy: using the rule until it becomes inconvenient. The modest principle leads to no absurdity, so there is no principled reason to exempt the universe.

"Explaining each part explains the whole" Trickier

Strongest form

If you explain every contingent thing by a prior contingent thing — even an infinite chain — you have explained everything. Asking for a further explanation of the whole collection is a fallacy.

Reply

Even an infinite chain of contingent things is itself contingent. Explaining each link by the previous link never answers why there is any chain at all rather than nothing. A contingent series, however long, still needs a non-contingent ground outside the series.

"The Principle of Sufficient Reason destroys free will" Hard

Strongest form

If every fact has a sufficient reason that necessitates it, then nothing is contingent and no choice is free — the principle leads to modal collapse and refutes itself.

Reply

The argument uses the modest principle (everything has an explanation), not the strong rationalist one (every fact is necessitated). An explanation can be a free personal choice, which explains an outcome without forcing it. So contingency and freedom survive intact.

"Why a necessary being and not a necessary number?" Trickier

Strongest form

Grant a necessary explanation — why think it is God rather than some abstract object, like a number or a set, existing necessarily?

Reply

Abstract objects are causally impotent — a number cannot cause a universe. The necessary being must be able to produce contingent reality and, to bring about a contingent universe, plausibly chooses to. That points to a necessary mind, not an inert abstraction or a brute particle (which would itself be contingent).

"The explanation is unknown, not God" Common

Strongest form

Even granting the universe has an explanation, you cannot leap straight to God. It could be some unknown necessary cause we have no concept of.

Reply

The argument does not start from the word God. It reasons to the properties the explanation must have — necessary, self-existent, the cause of all space, time, and matter, plausibly personal — and notes these are exactly the core attributes classical theism ascribes to God. It is an inference to what the ground must be like, not a label slapped on a mystery.

Where this comes from — primary sources, not our summary

Gottfried Leibniz18th c. · Monadology
Framed the Principle of Sufficient Reason and the question behind the whole argument: why is there something rather than nothing?
Samuel Clarke18th c. · Leibniz–Clarke correspondence
Developed the contingency argument in detail and defended it against early objections.
Richard Taylor20th c. · Metaphysics
His translucent-ball illustration: a ball found in the forest needs an explanation — and enlarging it to the size of the universe does not remove the need.
William Lane Craigcontemporary
Gave the clean three-line formulation used here and defends the link from a necessary being to God. (We paraphrase — the originals reward the time.)
