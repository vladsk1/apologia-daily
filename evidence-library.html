Cosmological arguments

01
The Kalam Cosmological Argument
Everything that begins to exist has a cause — William Lane Craig
Beginner
Free
The argument in brief
  1. Everything that begins to exist has a cause.
  2. The universe began to exist.
  3. Therefore, the universe has a cause outside itself.

The Kalam is one of the most discussed arguments in modern philosophy of religion, popularised by philosopher William Lane Craig. The first premise is intuitive — nothing comes from nothing. The second is supported by both philosophical reasoning and modern cosmology: the Big Bang indicates the universe had an absolute beginning. The cause must be spaceless, timeless, immaterial, and enormously powerful — properties consistent with theism.

Common objections — overview
Q Who made God? — The Kalam only applies to things that begin to exist. God, as a necessary and eternal being, never began to exist and therefore requires no cause.
Q Quantum events happen without causes. — Quantum indeterminacy doesn't mean events are uncaused — they still occur within a pre-existing framework of physical laws and energy, not absolute nothingness.
Pro — Deep Dive
Full analysis, extended objections & conversation guide
Historical & philosophical background

The Kalam Cosmological Argument has an ancient pedigree. The medieval Islamic philosopher Al-Ghazali (1058–1111 AD) formulated the argument in his work Tahafut al-Falasifa (The Incoherence of the Philosophers), arguing that an actually infinite past is philosophically impossible and therefore the universe must have had a beginning. The argument draws its name from the Arabic word for "speech" or "word" — the Kalam tradition of Islamic philosophical theology. In the twentieth century, philosopher William Lane Craig revived and rigorously defended the argument in his landmark 1979 work The Kalam Cosmological Argument, marshalling both philosophical and scientific evidence for the second premise. Today it is arguably the most discussed argument for God's existence in analytic philosophy of religion, with major philosophers including J.P. Moreland, Alexander Pruss, and Robert Koons defending variations of it.

Evidence for Premise 1 — Everything that begins to exist has a cause

This premise has both intuitive and philosophical support. Intuitively, we have never observed anything come into existence without a cause — the principle that something cannot come from nothing (ex nihilo nihil fit) has been foundational to rational thought since Parmenides. Philosophically, if things could begin to exist without a cause, it becomes inexplicable why anything and everything doesn't pop into existence uncaused. The very fact that we don't observe this confirms the premise. The objection that quantum mechanics disproves it misunderstands quantum physics — even quantum fluctuations occur within a pre-existing vacuum state governed by physical laws. They are not examples of something coming from absolute nothingness.

Evidence for Premise 2 — The universe began to exist

This is the most scientifically substantiated premise in all of natural theology. There are two independent lines of support:

Philosophical: An actually infinite number of past events is impossible. If the past were infinite, we could never have arrived at the present moment — an infinite series has no final term. Furthermore, an actually infinite collection leads to mathematical absurdities (Hilbert's Hotel paradoxes) that make such a collection metaphysically impossible in the real world.

Scientific: The standard Big Bang cosmological model, confirmed by Hubble's observation of cosmic expansion (1929), the discovery of cosmic microwave background radiation (1964), and the Borde-Guth-Vilenkin (BGV) theorem (2003), demonstrates that the universe — including space, time, matter, and energy — had an absolute beginning. The BGV theorem is particularly significant: Alan Guth and Alexander Vilenkin are themselves secular cosmologists, and Vilenkin has stated plainly that "all the evidence we have says that the universe had a beginning." The theorem proves that any universe that has been on average expanding throughout its history cannot be past-eternal — it must have a boundary in the past.

What must the cause be like?

If the cause of the universe must exist outside space, time, matter, and energy — since it brought all of these into existence — then it must be: spaceless, timeless, immaterial, uncaused, and of unimaginable power. Remarkably, this cause must also be personal. A non-personal, abstract cause (like a mathematical law) cannot bring something into existence from nothing — only a personal agent with will and intention can choose to create. This profile corresponds precisely to the God of classical theism. As Craig argues, from the Kalam alone we can establish the existence of a transcendent, personal Creator of the universe.

Extended objections — 8 challenges answered thoroughly
Q Who made God? — The Kalam only applies to things that begin to exist. The argument does not say "everything has a cause" — it says "everything that begins to exist has a cause." God, by definition, is a necessary and eternal being who never began to exist. He is the uncaused First Cause. This is not special pleading — it follows directly from the logic of the argument itself. If God also began to exist, he would need a cause, leading to an infinite regress. The argument terminates in a being that necessarily has no beginning.
Q Quantum mechanics shows things can happen without causes. — This objection misunderstands quantum physics. Quantum vacuum fluctuations do not occur in absolute nothingness — they occur within a pre-existing quantum vacuum, which is itself a structured, energy-laden field governed by the laws of physics. This is not nothing. Furthermore, even if subatomic events are genuinely indeterministic, they still occur within a framework of pre-existing physical reality. Nothing in quantum mechanics suggests that entire universes — including space, time, matter, and physical laws — can pop into existence from absolutely nothing.
Q The universe could be eternal — perhaps in a cyclical model. — The BGV theorem (Borde, Guth, Vilenkin 2003) applies to all models in which the universe has on average been expanding — including cyclical, bouncing, and multiverse models. Even Guth himself acknowledges that the theorem shows these models require a beginning. There is no viable cosmological model that is genuinely past-eternal. The Second Law of Thermodynamics also rules out an eternal past — an eternally old universe would have reached maximum entropy long ago.
Q Even if there's a cause, why must it be the Christian God? — The Kalam establishes the existence of a transcendent, personal, enormously powerful Creator. It does not by itself establish every Christian doctrine. This is not a weakness — it is how cumulative case apologetics works. The Kalam establishes theism; the resurrection evidence establishes Christian theism specifically. Each argument does its part. Craig himself is clear that the Kalam gets us to generic theism, and other arguments fill in the specifics.
Q The cause could be impersonal — like the laws of physics. — Abstract objects and physical laws cannot cause anything — they have no causal power. The number seven cannot cause a universe to exist. Only concrete, active agents can initiate causal sequences. Furthermore, a timeless, non-personal cause would produce a timeless effect — yet the universe began at a point in time. Only a personal agent who freely chooses to create can explain why the universe began when it did rather than from eternity.
Q This is just a God of the gaps argument. — A God of the gaps argument says "we don't know what caused X, therefore God." The Kalam is the opposite — it argues positively from what we do know: that the universe began, that beginnings require causes, and that the cause must have specific properties. The argument is driven by evidence (the BGV theorem, cosmic expansion, thermodynamics), not by ignorance. If new evidence showed the universe had no beginning, the argument would be defeated — it is empirically engaged, not immune to evidence.
Q Time began with the universe, so "before" the universe is meaningless. — This objection actually supports the theistic conclusion. If time itself began with the universe, then the cause of the universe must be timeless — it existed without any temporal relations before creation. This is precisely what classical theism claims about God: he is eternal, existing outside of time. The objection strengthens rather than undermines the argument's conclusion.
Q Stephen Hawking showed the universe has no boundary — no beginning. — Hawking's "no-boundary proposal" uses imaginary time to model the universe without an initial singularity. However, as Hawking himself acknowledged, imaginary time is a mathematical tool — in real time, the universe still has a beginning. Furthermore, the Hartle-Hawking model is itself a quantum cosmological model that requires pre-existing physical laws and mathematical structures. It does not describe creation from absolute nothingness.
How to use this argument in a real conversation
1
Start with the simple form. Don't lead with jargon. Try: "Here's something I find genuinely compelling — scientists now agree the universe had a beginning. Whatever caused the universe to begin must exist outside space and time. That sounds a lot like God to me." This lands the core idea without sounding like a lecture.
2
Anticipate "who made God?" immediately. This is the most common response and you want to be ready: "The argument only applies to things that begin to exist — God, by definition, is eternal and never had a beginning. So he doesn't need a cause any more than the universe would if it had always existed." Practise saying this naturally.
3
Name-drop the secular scientists. "What's interesting is that this isn't just a religious claim — cosmologists Alexander Vilenkin and Alan Guth, neither of them Christians, published a theorem proving the universe had to have a beginning. Vilenkin has said publicly that all the evidence points to a beginning." Secular sources carry more weight with sceptics.
4
Don't overclaim. Be honest that the Kalam establishes a Creator — not necessarily every Christian doctrine. "This argument gets me to a powerful, personal Creator outside the universe. I think other evidence — like the resurrection — fills in the rest of the picture." Intellectual honesty builds trust and keeps the conversation going.

Leibniz's Contingency Argument
Why does anything exist rather than nothing?
Intermediate
The argument
  1. Everything that exists has an explanation of its existence.
  2. If the universe has an explanation, that explanation is a necessary being.
  3. The universe exists.
  4. Therefore the universe has an explanation — a necessary, uncaused being.

Developed by philosopher Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz and championed in modern form by William Lane Craig and Alexander Pruss. The universe is contingent — it could have not existed. Contingent things require an explanation outside themselves. The only satisfying explanation for the entire physical universe must be something that exists necessarily by its own nature — a being that could not have failed to exist.

Common objections answered
Q The universe could just exist without explanation. — This violates the Principle of Sufficient Reason, which is foundational to all rational inquiry. Accepting brute inexplicability for the universe would undermine science itself.
Q Why can't the necessary being be the universe itself? — A necessary being cannot be physical — the laws of physics could have been otherwise. The universe is contingent by nature; a necessary being must transcend it.

03
The Thomistic Cosmological Argument
There must be an unmoved mover — Aquinas's Five Ways
Advanced
The argument
  1. Things in the world are in motion or undergoing change.
  2. Whatever is moved is moved by another — nothing moves itself.
  3. An infinite regress of movers is impossible.
  4. Therefore there must be a first unmoved mover — Pure Act itself.

Thomas Aquinas's First Way draws on Aristotle's metaphysics of act and potency. Every change in the universe is a transition from potential to actual — but potential cannot actualise itself. The chain of causes cannot regress infinitely; it must terminate in something that is Pure Act — that exists necessarily and moves without being moved. This is what all people call God.

Common objections answered
Q Why can't the chain be infinite? — An actually infinite regress of simultaneous causes cannot explain current motion. The question is not about a temporal series but a hierarchical series of causes operating right now.

Teleological arguments

04
The Fine-Tuning Argument
The physical constants of the universe are exquisitely calibrated for life
Beginner
The argument
  1. The fine-tuning of the universe for intelligent life is either physical necessity, chance, or design.
  2. It is not physical necessity, and the odds against chance are astronomically prohibitive.
  3. Therefore the fine-tuning is best explained by design.

Physicists have discovered that the constants of nature — the strength of gravity, the mass of electrons, the cosmological constant — are calibrated to life-permitting values with extraordinary precision. If any were different by even a fraction, no stars, planets, chemistry, or life would be possible. Cosmologist Robin Collins and philosopher William Lane Craig argue this points powerfully to intentional design.

Common objections answered
Q The Multiverse explains it. — The Multiverse is speculative and itself requires fine-tuning to produce life-permitting universes. It also raises deeper questions about why a multiverse-generating mechanism exists at all.
Q We can only observe a life-permitting universe. — The Anthropic Principle explains why we observe fine-tuning but not why fine-tuning exists. A firing squad that all miss is not explained by "if they hadn't missed, I wouldn't be here to wonder."

The Moral Argument
If God does not exist, objective moral values do not exist
Beginner
The argument
  1. If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist.
  2. Objective moral values and duties do exist.
  3. Therefore God exists.

On atheism, moral values reduce to evolutionary preferences or cultural constructs — they may be useful, but they're not objectively binding. This deep moral intuition points to a transcendent moral lawgiver who grounds objective moral reality.

01
The Minimal Facts Argument
Five facts accepted by virtually all historians — Gary Habermas
Beginner
The five minimal facts
  1. Jesus died by crucifixion.
  2. His disciples believed he rose and appeared to them.
  3. Paul, a persecutor of Christians, was suddenly converted.
  4. James, a sceptic and Jesus's brother, was suddenly converted.
  5. The tomb was empty.

Historian Gary Habermas identified a set of facts about the post-crucifixion events that are accepted by the vast majority of New Testament scholars — including sceptics — on the basis of standard historical criteria. The resurrection is the best explanation of all five facts taken together. No other hypothesis — stolen body, swoon theory, hallucination theory — adequately accounts for all of them simultaneously.

Common objections answered

Q The disciples stole the body. — This doesn't explain Paul's conversion — he was actively persecuting Christians. It also doesn't explain why the disciples would willingly die for what they knew was a lie.

The Historical Jesus

01
The Historical Existence of Jesus — Non-Christian Sources
Secular historians confirm the existence of Jesus independently of the New Testament
Beginner
Free
The argument in brief
  1. Multiple non-Christian sources from the 1st and 2nd centuries independently confirm Jesus existed.
  2. These sources — Roman, Jewish, and secular — had no motive to fabricate a Jewish religious figure.
  3. The independent corroboration of Jesus's existence meets the standard of proof applied to any other ancient figure.
  4. Therefore the claim that Jesus never existed fails by any consistent standard of historical methodology.

The "Jesus mythicist" position — that Jesus never existed — is rejected by virtually all professional historians, including secular and atheist scholars. The historical existence of Jesus is confirmed by multiple independent non-Christian sources including Tacitus, Josephus, Pliny the Younger, Lucian of Samosata, and Mara bar Serapion. Combined with early Christian sources, the evidence for Jesus as a historical figure is stronger than for many accepted figures of antiquity.

Common objections — overview
Q The Josephus passages were interpolated by Christians. — Scholars acknowledge the longer Testimonium Flavianum passage was likely embellished, but the shorter reference to James "the brother of Jesus called Christ" is widely accepted as authentic. Tacitus's reference in the Annals is also considered genuine.
Q Paul never mentions a historical Jesus, only a spiritual one. — Paul explicitly mentions Jesus's crucifixion under Pilate, his burial, his post-resurrection appearances, and names specific eyewitnesses including Peter and James the Lord's brother whom Paul personally met (Galatians 1:18-19).
Pro — Deep Dive
Full analysis of every non-Christian source with scholarly assessment
Tacitus — Annals 15.44 (c. 116 AD)

The Roman historian Tacitus, writing around 116 AD, describes Nero's persecution of Christians following the great fire of Rome in 64 AD. He writes that the Christians derived their name from "Christus, who was executed at the hands of the procurator Pontius Pilate in the reign of Tiberius." This passage is considered authentic by virtually all scholars — Tacitus was not sympathetic to Christianity, describing it as a "destructive superstition," making fabrication or interpolation extremely unlikely. It independently confirms the crucifixion under Pilate, exactly as the Gospels record.

Josephus — Antiquities of the Jews (c. 93 AD)

The Jewish historian Josephus refers to Jesus twice in his Antiquities. The shorter reference — describing the stoning of "James the brother of Jesus called Christ" — is accepted as authentic by the vast majority of scholars across the ideological spectrum. The longer Testimonium Flavianum passage is more complex: most scholars believe it contains authentic Josephan material that was later embellished by Christian scribes. The Arabic version of Josephus, discovered in the 10th century, preserves what appears to be a less-embellished form of the passage, still referring to Jesus as a wise man who performed surprising deeds.

Pliny the Younger — Letter to Trajan (c. 112 AD)

The Roman governor Pliny the Younger wrote to Emperor Trajan around 112 AD describing his investigation of Christians in Bithynia. He records that Christians "sing hymns to Christ as to a god" and meet on a fixed day before dawn. While this doesn't confirm biographical details about Jesus, it confirms that within 80 years of the crucifixion, Christians in Asia Minor were worshipping him as divine — far too early for legend to have entirely displaced historical memory.

Mara bar Serapion — Letter from prison (c. late 1st century)

A Syrian philosopher writing from prison to his son refers to "the wise king of the Jews" who was executed but whose teachings live on — most scholars identify this as a reference to Jesus. This represents an independent non-Roman, non-Jewish source confirming the broad outlines of Jesus's execution and the survival of his movement.

The criterion of embarrassment and enemy attestation

Two important historical criteria further confirm the historical Jesus. The criterion of embarrassment notes that early Christian sources contain details that would have been embarrassing if invented — Jesus's baptism by John (implying subordination), his crucifixion as a criminal (a stumbling block to Jews), his Galilean origins, and the flight of his disciples. No one inventing a religious founder would include these details. Enemy attestation notes that even Jesus's enemies — Jewish leaders and Roman authorities — never denied he existed; they disputed his claims, not his existence. The Talmud contains references to Jesus that are hostile but presuppose his historical reality.

Evidence from Prophecy

04
Messianic Prophecy — The Statistical Case
The probability of one person fulfilling hundreds of specific Old Testament prophecies by chance is astronomically small
Beginner
Free
The argument in brief
  1. The Old Testament contains hundreds of specific prophecies about a coming Messiah written centuries before Jesus's birth.
  2. Jesus of Nazareth fulfilled these prophecies in remarkable detail — including his birthplace, lineage, entry into Jerusalem, betrayal price, manner of death, and resurrection.
  3. The probability of one person fulfilling even a small subset of these by chance is mathematically negligible.
  4. The most reasonable explanation is that these prophecies were divinely inspired and Jesus is their fulfilment.

The Old Testament contains over 300 references to the coming Messiah, written between 400 and 1,500 years before Jesus's birth. Mathematician Peter Stoner calculated that the probability of one person fulfilling just 8 of these prophecies by chance was 1 in 10 to the power of 17. Fulfilments include: born of a virgin (Isaiah 7:14), born in Bethlehem (Micah 5:2), entering Jerusalem on a donkey (Zechariah 9:9), betrayed for 30 pieces of silver (Zechariah 11:12-13), hands and feet pierced (Psalm 22:16), and rising from the dead (Psalm 16:10).

Common objections — overview
Q Jesus or his followers manipulated events to fulfil prophecies. — Some prophecies could theoretically be engineered, but many could not — his birthplace, lineage, manner of death (crucifixion was a Roman punishment not invented when Psalm 22 was written), and the specific betrayal price of 30 silver coins were not under his control.
Q The prophecies were written after Jesus to match his life. — The Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered in 1947, contain Isaiah and other prophetic texts dating to at least 100 BC — centuries before Jesus. The Septuagint Greek translation of the Old Testament was completed around 200 BC. Both pre-date Jesus and confirm the antiquity of the prophecies.
Pro — Deep Dive
Key prophecies examined in detail with historical context
Isaiah 53 — The Suffering Servant (written c. 700 BC)

Isaiah 53 is the most remarkable messianic prophecy in the entire Old Testament. Written approximately 700 years before the crucifixion, it describes a servant who is "pierced for our transgressions" and "crushed for our iniquities," who is "led like a lamb to the slaughter," who makes "his grave with the wicked and with the rich in his death," and who "will see the light of life" after his suffering. Every detail corresponds precisely to the crucifixion and resurrection of Jesus — the piercing, the silence before accusers, burial in a rich man's tomb (Joseph of Arimathea), and subsequent vindication. This is not a vague prophecy but a detailed narrative of substitutionary suffering and death that is unmistakably fulfilled in the Gospel accounts.

Psalm 22 — David's cry (written c. 1000 BC)

Psalm 22 begins with the words "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — the exact words Jesus cried from the cross (Matthew 27:46). The Psalm continues: "I am a worm and not a man, scorned by everyone, despised by the people. All who see me mock me... they say 'He trusts in the LORD; let the LORD rescue him.'" It describes enemies who "pierce my hands and my feet" and who "divide my clothes among them and cast lots for my garment." All of these details are recorded in the Gospel crucifixion accounts. Crucifixion did not exist as a method of execution when David wrote this Psalm — and yet the description of hands and feet being pierced corresponds precisely to crucifixion.

Micah 5:2 — Born in Bethlehem (written c. 700 BC)

Micah 5:2 specifies that the ruler of Israel will come from Bethlehem Ephrathah — a small village, not the larger Bethlehem of Zebulun. This is noteworthy because Jesus's family were from Galilee; the birth in Bethlehem resulted from the Roman census requiring Joseph to return to his ancestral city. This is precisely the kind of detail that would be historically verifiable and could not be engineered after the fact — the Gospel accounts include it because it actually happened, and it happens to fulfil a 700-year-old prophecy.

Zechariah 11:12-13 — 30 pieces of silver (written c. 520 BC)

Zechariah 11:12-13 specifies that the shepherd of Israel would be valued at 30 pieces of silver, and that this money would be thrown into the temple and used to buy a potter's field. Matthew 27:3-10 records precisely this sequence of events — Judas's betrayal price of 30 silver coins, his throwing them into the temple, and the priests using the money to buy the potter's field. The specificity of the amount, the location (the temple), and the subsequent purchase make coincidence an implausible explanation.

The Dead Sea Scrolls — confirming pre-Christian dating

The discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls in 1947 was one of the most significant archaeological finds in history for this argument. Among the scrolls was a complete copy of Isaiah dating to approximately 125 BC — over 100 years before Jesus's birth. The text of Isaiah 53 in the Dead Sea Scroll is virtually identical to the Masoretic text used in modern Bibles. This definitively refutes the objection that the prophecies were written after Jesus to match his life.

How to use this in conversation
1
Start with Isaiah 53. Read it aloud without identifying it and ask "who does this describe?" Many people who are unfamiliar with the passage will immediately say "Jesus" before you've told them it was written 700 years before his birth. The text speaks for itself.
2
Address the "written after" objection immediately. Mention the Dead Sea Scrolls — "We actually have a physical copy of Isaiah dating to 125 BC. We can see it in a museum in Jerusalem. It matches today's Bible almost perfectly and it was written 100 years before Jesus was born."
3
Use the uncontrollable details. Focus on prophecies Jesus could not have engineered — his birthplace, his lineage, the manner of his death (crucifixion was a Roman method not invented when Psalm 22 was written), the specific betrayal price. These are the most compelling.

The Deity of Christ

07
Did Jesus Claim to Be God? — The Evidence from the Gospels
Jesus made implicit and explicit claims to divine identity that his Jewish audience understood clearly
Beginner
Free
The argument in brief
  1. Jesus made multiple claims — explicit and implicit — to divine identity in the Gospel accounts.
  2. His Jewish audience understood these claims as claims to divinity, responding with accusations of blasphemy.
  3. C.S. Lewis's trilemma argues that Jesus was either Lord, liar, or lunatic — good teacher is not an available option given what he claimed.
  4. The evidence best supports the conclusion that Jesus genuinely believed himself to be God — and that the resurrection vindicates that claim.

The claim that Jesus never said he was God is historically untenable. While Jesus rarely used the explicit formula "I am God," he made multiple claims that his Jewish audience immediately understood as claims to divine identity — and responded to with charges of blasphemy. These include forgiving sins (a divine prerogative in Jewish theology), accepting worship, claiming pre-existence before Abraham, applying the divine name "I AM" to himself, and claiming to be the final judge of all humanity at the last day.

Common objections — overview
Q Jesus never directly said "I am God, worship me." — This misunderstands how divine claims were made in a Jewish context. When Jesus said "Before Abraham was, I AM" (John 8:58), the audience immediately picked up stones — they understood the claim. The divine name formula "I AM" (ego eimi) echoes Exodus 3:14 unmistakably.
Q The divine claims were added by later editors. — The divine claims appear in the earliest strata of the New Testament including Paul's letters (written 20 years after the crucifixion) and the pre-Pauline creeds embedded within them. The high Christology of John 1 reflects genuine early Christian worship, not late theological development.
Pro — Deep Dive
Every major divine claim examined with Greek text and scholarly analysis
Claim 1 — Forgiving sins (Mark 2:5-7)

When Jesus told the paralysed man "your sins are forgiven," the scribes immediately responded: "Why does this man speak this way? He is blaspheming. Who can forgive sins but God alone?" They were correct in their theology — only God can forgive sins against God. Jesus's claim was not to relay God's forgiveness but to personally pronounce it — a claim to divine authority that he then validates by healing the man. This is not a late theological interpolation — it appears in Mark, widely considered the earliest Gospel.

Claim 2 — "Before Abraham was, I AM" (John 8:58)

The Greek text reads "prin Abraam genesthai, ego eimi" — "before Abraham came into being, I am." The phrase ego eimi is the Greek equivalent of the Hebrew YHWH's self-designation in Exodus 3:14: "I AM WHO I AM." The shift from the expected "I was" (past tense) to "I am" (present tense) is grammatically and theologically significant — Jesus is claiming timeless, self-existent being, not merely prior existence. His audience's immediate response — picking up stones to execute him for blasphemy — confirms they understood exactly what he was claiming.

Claim 3 — Accepting worship (Matthew 28:17; John 20:28)

In Jewish theology, worship is owed to God alone — this is the first of the Ten Commandments. When Thomas declares "My Lord and my God!" (John 20:28) and falls before the risen Jesus, Jesus does not correct him. When the disciples worship Jesus in Matthew 28:17, Jesus receives it without correction. Compare this with Peter (Acts 10:25-26) and the angel in Revelation (22:8-9) who both immediately refuse worship and redirect it to God. Jesus's acceptance of worship is a profound implicit claim to divine identity.

Claim 4 — Final judge of all humanity (Matthew 25:31-46)

In Matthew 25, Jesus describes himself as the Son of Man who will sit on "his glorious throne" and judge all nations, separating people "as a shepherd separates the sheep from the goats." In Jewish theology, the final judgement belongs to God alone. Jesus places himself in the seat of divine judge over all humanity — including the nations, not just Israel. The parable is not attributed to God acting through Jesus — Jesus himself is the judge. This is an unmistakable claim to divine prerogative.

C.S. Lewis's Trilemma — Lord, Liar, or Lunatic

C.S. Lewis's famous argument in Mere Christianity remains one of the most incisive responses to the "Jesus was just a good teacher" position. Lewis argues: "A man who was merely a man and said the sort of things Jesus said would not be a great moral teacher. He would either be a lunatic — on the level with a man who says he is a poached egg — or else he would be the Devil of Hell. You must make your choice. Either this man was, and is, the Son of God: or else a madman or something worse. You can shut Him up for a fool, you can spit at Him and kill Him as a demon; or you can fall at His feet and call Him Lord and God. But let us not come with any patronising nonsense about His being a great human teacher. He has not left that option open to us."

The early high Christology — against the "late development" theory

Some scholars argue that the divine claims of Jesus were a late theological development added decades after his death. Larry Hurtado's landmark scholarship in Lord Jesus Christ (2003) and How on Earth Did Jesus Become a God? (2005) demolishes this thesis. Hurtado demonstrates that the devotional pattern of treating Jesus as a divine figure alongside God the Father — including prayer to Jesus, hymns to Jesus, and the invocation of Jesus's name — is present in the earliest recoverable layer of Christianity, within years of the crucifixion. This "mutation" in Jewish monotheism is unprecedented and demands an explanation. The most historically plausible explanation is that the earliest Christians genuinely believed they had encountered a risen divine figure.

Manuscript evidence

01
The Manuscript Reliability Argument
The New Testament is the most well-attested document in ancient history
Beginner
The key facts
  1. There are over 5,800 Greek New Testament manuscripts — more than any other ancient document.
  2. The earliest manuscripts date within decades of the originals.
  3. Scholars can reconstruct the original text with over 99% confidence.
  4. No other ancient text comes remotely close to this level of attestation.

We accept the works of Julius Caesar, Plato, and Tacitus as historically reliable despite having only a handful of manuscripts copied centuries after the originals. The New Testament, by contrast, has thousands of manuscripts, the earliest fragments dating to within a generation of the events. Textual criticism can confidently reconstruct the original documents. The question of reliability is settled by the same standards applied to all ancient history.

01
The Argument from the Big Bang
Modern cosmology confirms the universe had an absolute beginning
Beginner
The key facts
  1. The standard Big Bang model shows the universe began from an initial singularity.
  2. The Borde-Guth-Vilenkin theorem proves any universe that has been expanding must have had a beginning.
  3. Space, time, matter, and energy all came into existence at this point.
  4. Whatever caused the universe must be outside space, time, and matter.

For most of history, atheists preferred an eternal universe — no beginning meant no need for a Creator. Modern cosmology has changed everything. The BGV theorem, proposed by atheist cosmologists themselves, demonstrates that the universe cannot be past-eternal. It had a beginning. This is exactly what Genesis 1:1 claimed thousands of years ago — and exactly what a theistic worldview predicts.
