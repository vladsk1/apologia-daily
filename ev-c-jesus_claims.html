The Case, Plainly Pro

Did Jesus Claim to Be God?

Apologia Daily · the positive case · plain English

There is a quiet fear that keeps a lot of us from ever looking closely at this question. We worry that if we go digging, we will find that Jesus never really claimed to be God at all — that the whole thing was something the church added later, and that we have been standing on air. So we avoid the question. But the good news is this: when you actually look at what Jesus said and did, the ground under your feet turns out to be firmer than you feared, not softer. Let me walk you through it, slowly, the way I wish someone had walked me.

Start with one thing that will make the rest click into place. In first-century Israel, the most important truth a faithful Jew held was that God is one. They guarded that with their lives. So Jesus did not typically walk around saying the flat sentence “I am God” — partly because, in that world, it would have sounded like the wrong claim (like claiming to be the Father himself). Instead, something more striking happened. Jesus quietly did the things that only God has the right to do. He didn’t announce it. He acted it. And the people who guarded God’s oneness most fiercely were the first to notice.

He forgave sins — and everyone knew what that meant

Picture a crowded house in Galilee. Friends tear open the roof and lower a paralyzed man down to Jesus. Everyone expects a healing. Instead Jesus says, “Son, your sins are forgiven” (Mark 2:5). And the religious scholars in the room go cold. Their reaction is the tell: “Who can forgive sins but God alone?” (Mark 2:7).

Here is what is easy to miss. They were right. Sin is ultimately an offense against God, so only God can truly absolve it. And Jesus does not correct them. He does not say, “Relax, I only meant God forgives you.” He heals the man precisely to prove he has that authority. He steps into God’s place — calmly, as though it were his to occupy.

He spoke as if the Law and the Sabbath were his

Think about how a prophet talks. A prophet says, “Thus says the LORD” — pointing away from himself to God. But listen to Jesus in the Sermon on the Mount: “You have heard that it was said… but I say to you” (Matthew 5:21–22). He sets his own word over the sacred Law of Moses — not to throw it out, but as one who has the standing to declare what it truly means.

Then he calls himself “lord of the Sabbath” (Mark 2:28) — master of the very day God set apart at creation. No prophet talks like that. This is a man speaking as though he owns the house.

The “I AM” and the throne of God

In John’s Gospel, Jesus says something that stops the room: “Before Abraham was, I am” (John 8:58). That is not clumsy grammar. It reaches back to the burning bush, where God gives Moses his name: “I AM” (Exodus 3:14). His hearers understood exactly what he was reaching for — they picked up stones to kill him for blasphemy.

Now watch the most decisive moment of all: his trial. The high priest asks him directly, “Are you the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?” Jesus answers, “I am, and you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power, and coming with the clouds of heaven” (Mark 14:61–62). “Son of Man” sounds humble — but Jesus is quoting Daniel 7, where a heavenly figure comes on the clouds (which in the Old Testament is God’s own way of arriving) and receives everlasting dominion and the worship of all peoples. Jesus places himself in that scene, seated beside God’s throne. The high priest tears his robes and the court condemns him.

Notice what that verdict tells us. Merely claiming to be the Messiah — a human king — was not a crime. It tore no robes. Something bigger was heard. The blasphemy charge is, in effect, his enemies’ own testimony that they understood him to be claiming far more than any man should.

And when someone worshiped him, he received it

After the resurrection, doubting Thomas sees the risen Jesus and falls before him: “My Lord and my God!” (John 20:28). A faithful Jew — or a mere prophet — would have recoiled in horror and said, “Do not worship me; worship God.” Jesus does the opposite. He receives it, and gently blesses those who will believe without seeing.

See the pattern, and let it settle

Step back and look at the whole shape. One man forgives sins as only God can. He sets his word above the Law and claims the Sabbath. He takes up the “I AM.” He seats himself on God’s throne in Daniel’s vision. He accepts worship. Any one of these alone, a skeptic might explain away. But together they form a pattern that points in a single direction: Jesus consistently, deliberately, put himself in the place that belongs to God alone. He was fully human — hungry, weeping, dying — and yet he acted throughout as one who stands on God’s side of the line between Creator and creature.

That is why this closes the door on “Jesus was just a good moral teacher.” A good teacher points you to God. This man kept pointing you to himself.

None of this is hidden from scrutiny. Thoughtful people have raised sharp, serious questions — about the wording in John, about what “Son of Man” really meant, about whether the church added the high claims after Easter. Those questions are real, and they deserve honest answers. The deep dive takes them on one by one, fairly and at full strength. But the place to start is not the objections. It is the pattern itself — and once you have seen it, you can walk into the harder questions unafraid.
