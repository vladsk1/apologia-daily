The Moral Argument

The deep dive explains the argument. This track makes you able to wield it — rebuild it from memory, meet its hardest objections, and stay clear and kind when it gets personal.

The argument in brief

The moral argument for God runs in two steps: if God does not exist, then objective moral values and duties do not exist; but objective moral values and duties do exist; therefore God exists. The crucial first point is widely conceded even by atheist thinkers. Friedrich Nietzsche saw that abandoning God meant abandoning the binding force of traditional morality, and philosopher J.L. Mackie argued that objective values would be "queer" entities that fit awkwardly into a purely natural universe.

An important clarification, because this argument is easy to caricature: it is not the claim that atheists cannot be moral. Plenty of atheists live admirably and know right from wrong. The claim is narrower — it asks where genuinely binding moral obligations come from. If morality is only an evolved feeling or a social agreement, then it describes what we happen to prefer, not what we truly ought to do; it would have no authority over someone who simply disagreed. Real duties seem to require a foundation beyond human opinion — a transcendent moral lawgiver.

In fairness, atheist philosophers have offered grounds for objective morality without God, and that debate is live. But the believer can point out that our deepest conviction — that torturing the innocent for fun is really wrong, everywhere, for everyone — fits far more naturally in a universe grounded in a good God than in one of blind matter.

Understand it

Not more prose — the load-bearing structure. See the skeleton, then learn to defend each bone.

The argument, formally

Three lines — and the whole case turns on the second premise.

P1

If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist.

P2

Objective moral values and duties do exist.

∴ C

Therefore, God exists.

Valid as it stands: accept both premises and the conclusion follows. Most debate targets P2 (do objective values really exist?) or tries to ground them without God. Know which move your interlocutor is making.

Then the conceptual step — what must ground morality?

Duties bind persons; a real moral law that stands over everyone needs a foundation beyond human opinion, beyond evolution, beyond any one culture — a transcendent personal source whose own nature is the standard of good.

Defending the premises

Why each premise is more plausible than its denial

Support for P1 — no God, no objective morality

On naturalism, ought vanishes

If humans are accidental byproducts of a mindless process, our moral feelings are survival mechanisms, not perceptions of real obligations. A universe of pure matter contains no oughts woven into it — only what is.

Even an atheist saw it

J.L. Mackie, an atheist, agreed that objective moral values would be deeply strange entities — the kind of thing that fits a theistic universe far better than a naturalistic one.

Support for P2 — objective moral values exist

We know some things are really wrong

Torturing a child for fun, or the Holocaust, is not merely unfashionable — it is wrong, full stop. This moral knowledge is as secure as our knowledge of the external world.

No one can live the denial

Even those who deny objective morality protest real injustice done to them. Moral experience is properly basic — we are right to trust it unless given strong reason not to.

The hard part — objections, steelmanned

Each one stated as its strongest defender would put it

This is where a defender is made. Each objection in its strongest form first — never a strawman — then the reply.

"The Euthyphro dilemma" Hard

Strongest form

Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good? Either morality is arbitrary (God could command cruelty) or there is a standard above God — making God unnecessary.

Reply

A false dilemma. Goodness is grounded in God’s own nature — love, justice, mercy — not in arbitrary commands nor in a standard outside him. God commands in accordance with what he is. So morality is neither arbitrary nor independent of God; God himself is the standard.

"Evolution explains morality" Common

Strongest form

Cooperation and altruism helped our ancestors survive, so morality is a biological adaptation — fully explained without God.

Reply

Evolution might explain why we have moral beliefs, but not whether any are true. If our sense of ought is just a survival trick, it carries no real authority — which debunks morality rather than grounding it. That objective duties exist is the opposite of what a mere survival mechanism would give us.

"Atheists are moral too" Common

Strongest form

Plenty of atheists live good lives. Clearly you do not need God to be moral.

Reply

Completely agreed — and the argument never says otherwise. That is a question of epistemology (who can know and follow the moral law — anyone). The argument is about ontology (what makes it a real law at all). Atheists keep the moral law as well as anyone; the question is what grounds it.

"Morality is just cultural" Trickier

Strongest form

Different societies hold different values, so morality is relative to culture, not objective.

Reply

Disagreement does not disprove truth — cultures disagree about science too, yet facts remain. Much variation is over facts (who counts as a person), not core values; and we rightly judge slavery and genocide wrong across all cultures, which assumes a standard above culture.

"Objective morality can be grounded without God" Hard

Strongest form

Secular moral realists like Shafer-Landau ground objective morality in non-natural moral facts or in human flourishing — no deity required.

Reply

These views either smuggle in a purpose to human life (a teleology) that fits theism better than naturalism, or leave moral facts floating as unexplained brute necessities. Why would a mindless universe contain binding obligations addressed to persons? Duties hold between persons — which points to a personal ground.

"What about the evil commanded in the Bible?" Hard

Strongest form

The God said to ground morality is depicted commanding violence — which undercuts the claim that he is the standard of good.

Reply

A serious challenge that deserves honest engagement, and one Christians themselves debate. But note it is a different argument — it presupposes an objective standard by which to judge those acts wrong, which is exactly what the moral argument says requires God. It is a question about this God’s character, not about whether objective morality needs a transcendent ground.

    "You are absolutely right that you do not need to believe in God to live a good life, and I would never suggest otherwise. But notice that is a different question. If right and wrong are just evolution and empathy, then torturing a child is wrong really only means most of us feel a deep distaste for it — which can tell the torturer he is outvoted, not that he is mistaken. When you say cruelty is genuinely wrong, not merely unpopular, you are reaching for a standard above human opinion. Where does that standard come from?"
